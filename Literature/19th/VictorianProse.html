CHAPTER 24

VICTORIAN PROSE

Carlyle and Mill as Extremes:

  • Carlyle: Transcendental, idiosyncratic, authoritarian.
  • Mill: Empirical, rational, democratic.
  • Carlyle: Romantic inheritance.
  • Mill: Enlightenment influence but admired Carlyle, Wordsworth, Coleridge.
  • Both critiqued contemporary civilization.

Carlyle's View: Rejected Christian dogma. Emphasized virtues of renunciation, self-discipline. Advocated a spiritual view of reality.

Mill's View: Agnostic, but allowed for religion's moral utility. Suggested Christ (as man) offered a divine moral truth.

Oxford Movement and Tractarianism:

  • Began in the 1830s with Tracts for the Times (1832–1841).
  • Advocated for primitive Christianity as preserved by Anglican tradition.
  • Sought balance between Protestant individualism and Catholic dogmatic abuse.
  • Many of its leaders eventually converted to Roman Catholicism and the movement played a significant role in the development of Anglo-Catholicism within Anglicanism

Newman and Catholic Influence:

  • John Henry Newman: Key Tractarian, leader of Oxford Movement.
  • Tract 90 (1841): Interpreted Anglican Thirty-Nine Articles as Catholic.
  • Controversy led to Newman's 1845 conversion to Roman Catholicism.

This phase of English religious history influenced key Victorian thinkers and writers, highlighting tensions between faith, reason and secular modernity.

John Henry Newman (1801–90)

English Catholic theologian, academic, philosopher, historian, writer, poet. Former Anglican priest; converted to Catholicism; became a cardinal. Key figure in 19th-century English religious history; nationally known by mid-1830s. Canonised Catholic saint in 2019; member of the Oratory of St. Philip Neri. Leader of the Oxford Movement: sought to restore pre-Reformation Catholic beliefs and rituals in the Church of England. Key question: how to resist "liberalization" of the Church? Movement had some success; Newman’s Tract 90 (1841) controversial. Newman later wrote: “I was on my death-bed, as regards my membership with the Anglican Church.” Wrote popular hymns:

  • "Lead, Kindly Light"
  • "Firmly I believe, and truly"
  • "Praise to the Holiest in the Height" (Gerontius).

Fifth saint of the City of London: preceded by Thomas Becket, Thomas More, Edmund Campion, Polydore Plasden. Leading figure in the Oxford Movement, aimed to revitalize the Anglican Church with Catholic principles. Converted to Roman Catholicism in 1845, marking the movement's decline.

Matthew Arnold’s Reflection: Praised Newman’s spiritual and literary presence, calling him a "religious music" of subtle, mournful charm.

Works

Anglican period

The Arians of the Fourth Century (1833); Tracts for the Times (1833–1841); Lyra Apostolica (poems mostly by Newman and Keble, collected 1836); Select Treatises of St. Athanasius (1842, 1844); Lives of the English Saints (1843–44)

Catholic period

Loss and Gain (novel – 1848): philosophical novel. Depicts Oxford University culture and a student’s conversion to Roman Catholicism. First work Newman published after his 1845 conversion to Catholicism. Popular: nine editions and thirteen printings during Newman’s lifetime. Dialogical, resembling Plato’s dialogues: intellectual debates on religion. Characters offer partial truths that shape Charles’s conclusions. Written in response to Elizabeth Harris’s anti-Catholic novel From Oxford to Rome.  Plot: Charles Reding, new Oxford student, follows Anglican Church and avoids factions. Initially Latitudinarian: "Measure people by what they are, not what they are not." Conversations with Sheffield lead him to seek doctrinal truth and Church authority. Disillusioned by Protestant private judgment and party disputes. Questions the Thirty-nine Articles, critical for his degree. Encounters Willis, a Catholic convert, sparking deeper doubts. Jennings, suspecting his influence on others, sends him away to study. Reding confesses doubts to sister Mary; she loses trust. After deciding to convert, family expresses anger and resentment. Encouraged by a Catholic priest (possibly Newman) on his way to London. Confronted by emissaries of other sects but joins Catholic Church at Passionists Convent.

The Idea of a University (1852 and 1858): Liberal education cultivates intellect, independent of religious or moral goals. A "gentleman" emerges from intellectual excellence, but education alone doesn't guarantee sanctity or conscience. Advocated intellectual discipline for understanding and integration of knowledge.

Callista (novel – 1855): Set in mid-3rd century Sicca Veneria, Roman province of Africa.  Context: Christian persecution under Emperor Decius. Main character: Callista, a talented Greek sculptor, works with her brother Aristo for their employer Jucundus, crafting pagan statues. Agellius: young Christian, wants to marry Callista but struggles with faith and pagan family (brother Juba, stepmother Gurta, pagan uncle Jucundus). Christian priest Caecilius (revealed as St. Cyprian of Carthage): father figure to Agellius, strengthens his faith. After a locust plague, popular rage ignites against Christians, leading to persecution. Agellius flees Sicca Veneria. Callista drawn to Christianity; faces choice when forced to offer incense to pagan gods. Converts to Catholicism, accepts martyrdom. Novel commissioned as a "prequel" to Fabiola (1854) by Cardinal Nicholas Wiseman.

Apologia Pro Vita Sua (religious autobiography – 1864; revised edition, 1865): (Latin for "A defence of one's own life"). Explains Newman's religious journey from Anglicanism to Catholicism. Written in 1864 as a response to Charles Kingsley's attack on Newman's honesty.  Originally published in pamphlet form; revised version released in 1865. Widely successful, cleared Newman’s name after years of accusations. Newman described writing it as arduous, often emotional and "a terrible trial." Structure: Parts 1-2 ("Mr. Kingsley's Method of Disputation" & "True Mode of Meeting Mr. Kingsley"): shorter, lighter, gain reader sympathy. Parts 3-6: autobiographical, covering Newman’s life, Oxford Movement and development of Via Media theory. Part 7 ("General Answer to Mr. Kingsley"): theological views at the time. Appendix: "Answer in Detail to Mr. Kingsley's Accusations," refuting 39 charges ("blots"), mirroring Anglican Thirty-nine Articles.

The Dream of Gerontius (1865): Newman's longest poem. Focus: prayer of a dying man; angelic and demonic responses. Written after Newman's conversion from Anglicanism to Roman Catholicism. Explores Catholic beliefs: death, Purgatory, Paradise, judgment, union with God. Newman: poem "written by accident – published by accident." Inspired by Dante's Divine Comedy: journey through realms of the dead. Divided into seven "phases." Phase 1: Gerontius's final moments in life. Later phases: soul's journey through Heaven's courts.

Letter to the Duke of Norfolk (1875): Approx. 150 pages; response to Protestant-Catholic debates post-First Vatican Council. Rebuts PM Gladstone's claim that Catholics lack "mental freedom." Defends Catholics: "We do not deserve his injurious reproach... captives and slaves of the Pope." Addressed to the Duke of Norfolk, from a prominent recusant family post-Reformation.

John Ruskin (1819–1900)

English polymath of the Victorian era: writer, lecturer, art historian, critic, draughtsman, philanthropist. Wrote on art, architecture, political economy, education, geology, botany, ornithology, literature, myth and museology. Anticipated modern concerns: environmentalism, sustainability, ethical consumerism and craft. Gained fame with Modern Painters (1843), defending J.M.W. Turner and advocating "truth to nature" in art—rooted in experience and observation. From the 1850s, supported the Pre-Raphaelites, influencing their work. Saw the whole system of Victorian industry as enslaving. One of the first English writers to express over and over again his horror at what industrialism had done to the face of England. First publication: Poem On Skiddaw and Derwent Water (1829). Originally titled Lines Written at the Lakes in Cumberland: Derwentwater. Early essays: The Poetry of Architecture serialized in Loudon's Architectural Magazine (September 1837–December 1838) under pen name Kata Phusin (Greek for "According to Nature"). Fiction: The King of the Golden River (Ruskin’s only work of fiction, written earlier but published December 1850).

Architecture and criticism: The Seven Lamps of Architecture (1849): Ruskin’s first work under his name. Chapter “The Nature of Gothic” (from The Stones of Venice, Vol. II): Praised Gothic ornament, critiqued industrial capitalism and division of labor. Gothic Revival: Inspired architects to develop "Ruskinian Gothic" buildings.

Tolstoy: Described Ruskin as "one of the most remarkable men of all countries and times." Quoted and adapted Ruskin’s ideas.

Proust: Admired Ruskin and helped translate his works into French.

Gandhi: Influenced by Unto This Last, paraphrased it as Sarvodaya ("The Advancement of All").

 Key theories (per Kenneth Clark):

  • Art engages intellect, morals, memory and feeling.
  • Great art conveys truths about life, vision and religion.
  • Beauty lies in functional perfection, with parts cohering (the "Law of Help").
  • Good art arises from enjoyment, freedom and societal need.
  • Great art reflects shared purpose and faith.

Terms coined:

  • Pathetic fallacy: Attributing human emotions to nature (Modern Painters III, 1856).
  • Illth: Opposite of wealth ("ill-being").

Possible inspiration for Drawling Master or Gryphon in Alice's Adventures in Wonderland (1865).

Light, Descending (biographical novel by Octavia Randolph, 2014). His unfinished autobiography is Præterita: Outlines of Scenes and Thoughts Perhaps Worthy of Memory in My Past Life (3 vols)

The King of the Golden River (1841): written for Effie Gray, became a Victorian classic in 1851. The story is set in the Treasure Valley, Styria, Austria, where the evil Black Brothers, Hans and Schwartz, mistreat the Southwest Wind, causing the valley's prosperity to vanish. Forced into goldsmithing, they melt their brother Gluck's heirloom, releasing the King of the Golden River, a golden dwarf. The King offers them a chance to turn the river into gold by throwing holy water into it, but they fail due to their selfishness. Gluck succeeds by helping others and the river becomes golden again, restoring the valley’s fertility. The tale also serves as an aetiological myth explaining the valley's change and the fate of the Black Brothers.

Modern Painters (1843–1860), a five-volume work, began when he was 24. Based on material from Switzerland (1842). Argues that modern landscape painters are superior to old masters.  Primarily defends J. M. W. Turner’s later work. Art should document nature accurately, with Turner evolving from detailed depictions to exploring natural forces and atmosphere. Reflects John Neal’s "Landscape and Portrait-Painting" (1829), distinguishing "things seen by the artist" from "things as they are." Volume two (1846) focused on symbolism in art, influencing the Pre-Raphaelite Brotherhood. Ruskin completed three more volumes, with the final one in 1860.

The Seven Lamps of Architecture (1849): an extended essay, outlines his principles of architecture, later expanded in The Stones of Venice. The 'lamps' represent Ruskin's architectural ideals, contributing to the Gothic Revival movement. While offering little new to the debate, it summarized and popularized contemporary thought, gaining approval from ecclesiologists like the Cambridge Camden Society, who criticized modern architects in church design. The lamps are: Sacrifice, Truth, Power, Beauty, Life, Memory, Obedience. Ruskin's work is referenced in Lewis Carroll's 1857 poem Hiawatha's Photographing:

“He had learned it all from Ruskin
(Author of The Stones of Venice,
Seven Lamps of Architecture,
Modern Painters, and some others);
And perhaps he had not fully
Understood his author's meaning”

The Stones of Venice (1851–1853):  three-volume treatise. It analyzes Venetian art and architecture in detail, covering over eighty churches. Ruskin explores Venetian architecture from the Byzantine, Gothic and Renaissance periods. The work also offers a general history of Venice.

Unto This Last (1860-62): essay critical of economics. Title is a reference to the Parable of the Workers in the Vineyard, which includes the phrase "unto this last." The essay critiques the ethics of economics and the exploitation of labor.

Fors Clavigera: Letters to the Workmen and Labourers of Great Britain (1871-84): published as pamphlets, addressed social issues and moral intervention, inspired by Ruskin's mentor, Thomas Carlyle. "Fors Clavigera" refers to three powers of human destiny: Force (Hercules' club), Fortitude (Ulysses' key) and Fortune (Lycurgus' nail). These represent the ability to act decisively, based on Shakespeare's "tide in the affairs of men." Ruskin believed the letters embodied the Third Fors, aiming to influence social change at the right moment.

Sesame and Lilies: Two Lectures delivered at Manchester in 1864 (1865); The Crown of Wild Olive: Three Lectures on Work, Traffic and War (1866)

Thomas Henry Huxley (1825–1895)

English biologist and anthropologist, was a key advocate of Darwin’s theory of evolution, earning the nickname "Darwin’s Bulldog." One of the great figures of Victorian controversy. He is best known for his role in the 1860 Oxford debate with Samuel Wilberforce, advocating evolution. Though initially hesitant about some of Darwin’s ideas, Huxley publicly supported them and worked to popularize science. He coined the term "agnosticism" in 1869 and argued for the limits of human knowledge. His work on education emphasized practical scientific knowledge, challenging traditional views. Huxley’s influential 1893 Romanes Lecture had a significant impact in China, influencing translations of Darwin's work.

An essayist and reformer, he believed in the power of science and education for human progress, contrasting with thinkers like Matthew Arnold. Huxley’s works, such as Science and Culture and Other Essays (1881), explored science’s role in society, education and its conflict with religion.

Huxley's essays and addresses often explored the intersection of science and literature. He argued that scientific rationalism and the imaginative sense of fact were closely related. In his essay "On the Physical Basis of Life” (1869) Huxley used literary devices like metaphor to support his claim that non-fiction could be considered literature.

The Nineteenth Century (1882); American Discourses (1885)

Huxley is referenced as the tutor of Edward Prendick in H. G. Wells' The Island of Dr Moreau (1896).

William Morris (1834–1896)

Poet, artist, craftsman and socialist. Inspired by John Ruskin, but went beyond him, turning to active socialism. Key figure in the Arts and Crafts movement, he revived British textile arts. Rejected industrialism and mourned its impact on society and nature. Romanticized medieval England: clean cities, community and artistic life. Admired medieval architecture, Chaucer and Icelandic sagas. Promoted art as “the expression of joy in labor.” Advocated handicrafts over industrial production. Criticized Victorian capitalism for prioritizing profit over beauty and social welfare. “Art made by the people for the people” was his ideal. Transition to Socialism. Believed industrialism led to the dehumanization of labor and social inequality. Shifted from romantic medievalism to political activism, seeing socialism as necessary. Member of the Social Democratic Federation (SDF) (1883). Co-founded the Socialist League (1884), advocating revolutionary socialism. In his essay “How I Became a Socialist” (1894), gave his final definition of socialism “a condition of society in which there would be neither rich nor poor…”. In 1890 founded the Kelmscott Press, designing his own fonts (printed his own works and medieval classics).

A major literary contributor, he helped establish modern fantasy. Known for translating Icelandic Sagas, including The Saga of Gunnlaug Worm-Tongue and Grettis Saga. Morris's Beowulf was one of the first translations of the Old English poem into modern English. Most famous as a poet. His first volume of poetry, The Defence of Guenevere (1858), was the first Pre-Raphaelite poetry book published. His poem "The Haystack in the Floods" is well-known, set during the Hundred Years' War. He wrote Christmas-themed poems like "Masters in this Hall" (1860) and "The Snow in the Street." His later works included "prose romances," such as The Wood Beyond the World and The Well at the World's End. These novels, set in invented fantasy worlds, marked a milestone in fantasy literature. Morris's prose style was praised as "lyrical and enchanting." He was finishing The Sundering Flood at the time of his death.

Key Works:

The Earthly Paradise (1868–1870): an epic poem with retold myths from Greece and Scandinavia. Frame story about Norse wanderers seeking the Earthly Paradise. They return disillusioned and find a Greek colony. In the city, they share stories at feasts. Twelve sections, each representing a month, with two stories per month. One classical, one medieval. Prologue introduces Norsemen fleeing plague, seeking paradise. They return aged after long travels. In Greek city, find ancient gods still worshipped. Stories alternate between Greek and Norse. Form includes rhyme royal, heroic couplets, iambic tetrameter. The stories are told in March through February, with lyrics about nature’s changes between sections. Morris’ later works were published as "by the author of The Earthly Paradise."

The Story of Sigurd the Volsung and the Fall of the Niblungs (1877): an epic poem of over 10,000 lines by William Morris. It tells the tragic story, based on the Volsunga Saga and Elder Edda, of Norse hero Sigmund, his son Sigurd (equivalent to Siegfried in the Nibelungenlied and Wagner's Ring of the Nibelung) and Sigurd's wife Gudrun. Inspired by Morris's youth fascination with the Volsung legend, it was his favorite poem. Praised by critics, T. E. Lawrence, and George Bernard Shaw, it influenced fantasy writers like Andrew Lang. Morris discovered the story in Benjamin Thorpe’s Northern Mythology, which became a key influence on his work.

Hopes and Fears For Art (1882): Advocated for art that uplifts society. Criticized industrial capitalism for creating “squalor” and “pleasureless work.” Echoed Ruskin’s call for beauty in everyday life but with a socialist foundation (Ruskin’s chapter on Gothic in The Stones of Venice).

A Dream of John Ball (1888): Focuses on the Great Revolt of 1381 ("Peasants' Revolt") Features rebel priest John Ball, accused of being a Lollard. Famous quote: "When Adam delved and Eve span, who was then the gentleman?". Novel involves a time-travel encounter between medieval and modern worlds Contrasts ethics of medieval vs. contemporary society. Time-traveller explains decline of feudalism and rise of the Industrial Revolution. Ball realizes his dreams for an egalitarian society remain unfulfilled. Narrator explains future rise of poor men becoming lords, blinded to exploitation. Highlights hope for personal gain sustaining inequality and social order

A Tale of the House of the Wolfings, and All the Kindreds of the Mark Written in Prose and in Verse (1889): a fantasy novel blending prose and verse, influencing Tolkien's The Lord of the Rings. Set in a Germanic Gothic tribe, the story portrays the House of the Wolfings in a romantically idealized, archaic style. The tribe lives in the Mark, a forested area divided into Upper-mark, Mid-mark and Nether-mark, worships Odin and Tyr and sacrifices horses. The tribe faces attacks from imperial Rome. Two War Dukes, one from the House of the Wolfings and the other from the House of the Laxings, lead the tribe. Thiodolf, the Wolfing leader, is unknowingly cursed by a magical mail-shirt. He is supported by his lover, the Wood Sun and their daughter, the Hall Sun, who are linked to the gods.

The Roots of the Mountains (1889): a fantasy romance novel. The story is set in Burgdale, a small Germanic settlement, home to Dalemen, Woodlanders and Shepherds. These societies are threatened by the Sons of the Wolf (descendants of the Wolfings) and the invading Dusky Men (Huns). The novel focuses on the integration of the Sons of the Wolf into their new home and the love stories of five characters, four of whom marry. The Roots of the Mountains inspired elements in Tolkien's The Lord of the Rings, particularly the Dunedain subplot. Morris also planned a sequel, The Story of Desiderius, which was never completed.

News from Nowhere (or, An Epoch of Rest) (1890): combines utopian socialism and soft science fiction. First serialized in Commonweal starting 11 January 1890. The narrator, William Guest, falls asleep after a Socialist League meeting and wakes in a future society with common ownership and democratic control. No private property, big cities, authority, money, marriage, divorce, courts, prisons, or class systems. The agrarian society thrives on the people's enjoyment of nature and work.

The Story of the Glittering Plain (1891): a fantasy novel explores socialist themes. Set in a northern European-like past, it follows Hallblithe of the House of the Raven on a quest to rescue his fiancée, the Hostage, from pirates. This leads him to the utopian Land of the Glittering Plain (Acre of the Undying or Land of the Living Men), where inhabitants are believed to be immortal. Morris developed this genre further in later works like Child Christopher and Goldilind the Fair and The Well at the World's End.

The Wood Beyond the World (1894): a seminal fantasy novel. The story follows Golden Walter, whose wife betrays him, leading him to embark on a voyage that takes him to an unknown land where he meets a Lady, her Maid and a Dwarf. After a series of events, including the Lady's seduction of Walter and the Maid’s escape, Walter learns the truth about a murder involving the Lady, the Maid and the King's Son. Together, they travel through dangerous lands, including one inhabited by Bear-People, where the Maid saves them by claiming to be their goddess. Eventually, Walter is crowned King in Stark-Wall through a strange custom and marries the Maid, who loses her magical powers. He reforms the kingdom, abolishing prisons and supporting the poor, but when his bloodline ends, the monarchy is abolished.

Other fantasy novels include: Child Christopher and Goldilind the Fair (1895); The Well at the World's End (1896); The Water of the Wondrous Isles (1897)

The Sundering Flood (1897) (published posthumously): Morris' final fiction, completed in rough draft, with the ending dictated on his deathbed. Edited posthumously by his daughter May, it was published in 1897. Plot: Lovers Osberne Wulfgrimsson and Elfhild are separated by the Sundering Flood, an impassable river. After Elfhild is kidnapped by the Red Skinners, Osberne joins Sir Godrick’s army, helps overthrow a tyrannical king and merchant plutocracy and later finds Elfhild in the Wood Masterless. They return to Osberne's home, Wethermel and live happily. The book features the first fantasy map, a genre convention.

Golden Wings and Other Stories (1976): A collection of fantasy short stories by Morris, originally published in The Oxford and Cambridge Magazine (1856). Later republished in various collections. In 2010, an expanded version, The Hollow Land and Other Contributions to the Oxford and Cambridge Magazine, included these stories along with essays, reviews and poems. The book features an introduction by Alfred Noyes and an afterword by Richard B. Mathews. Contents: Introduction (Alfred Noyes). First story "The Story of the Unknown Church". Last (8th) "Frank's Sealed Letter". Afterword (Richard B. Mathews)

Walter Horatio Pater (1839-1894)

English essayist, art critic and fiction writer, was known for his stylistic mastery. Known for musically languorous prose, developed an impressionistic, aesthetic philosophy.

He wrote on Coleridge in Coleridge's Writings (1866), Winckelmann in Winckelmann (1867) and William Morris in The Poems of William Morris (1868), expressing admiration for romanticism. Satirized by Mallock in The New Republic (1876), Pater responded with A Study of Dionysus (1876). In 1889, he published Appreciations, a collection of essays including Style (1888), Aesthetic Poetry and a revised essay on The Poems of William Morris. His unfinished novel Gaston de Latour (1896), set in 16th-century France, was edited and published by Shadwell. Pater’s critical method was subjective and relativist, contrasting with Matthew Arnold’s objective criticism.

Pater’s works influenced Oscar Wilde, especially in The Picture of Dorian Gray (1890). Pater’s ideas also inspired Wilde’s The Critic as Artist (1891) and influenced later writers like Maugham and Stoppard. Pater appears in The Invention of Love and is referenced in Of Human Bondage (1915) and Another Country (1982). A poem by Billy Collins, "The Great Walter Pater," and a reference in Conan Doyle's The Tragedy of the Korosko (1898) acknowledge his impact.

Studies in the History of the Renaissance (1873): later revised to The Renaissance: Studies in Art and Poetry (1877), advocated for Aestheticism. Philosophy: “Art for art’s sake”—focused on individual impressions of beauty over objective truths. Famous Conclusion: Life is fleeting; seize exquisite moments of passion and knowledge. “To burn always with this hard, gemlike flame… is success in life.” Misinterpreted as advocating hedonistic sensationalism, especially by disciples like Oscar Wilde. Revised editions: Removed and later restored controversial passages to clarify his philosophical intent.

Marius the Epicurean (1885): subtitle “His Sensations and Ideas” a historical and philosophical novel set in Rome (161-177 AD). It follows Marius, a young Roman seeking a fulfilling philosophy or religion amidst a time of change. After childhood in Etruria, Marius befriends the hedonistic Flavianus, who introduces him to literature. As Marius grows, he adopts Heraclitus’s flux theory and Epicureanism but becomes disillusioned with Stoicism, especially after witnessing the cruelty of the amphitheater. Seeking meaning, Marius encounters a new faith and, after being falsely arrested for Christianity, dies among poor believers. Pater suggests Marius is "a soul naturally Christian," reflecting on love in his final moments. The novel was conceived as part of a trilogy, with the second novel unfinished at Pater’s death.

“The Child in the House” (1878): Published in Macmillan’s Magazine; reprinted in Miscellaneous Studies (1895). Portrait of the young Florian Deleal.

Appreciations (1889): Essays on literature and criticism. Defined the role of the artist as a transcriber of inner impressions rather than external facts. Art and literature: Aim for a unity of form and content, akin to music. Distinguished “good art” from “great art,” emphasizing that great art must also have depth, hope and purpose.

Philosophy:

Impressionism:

  • Art and life are subjective; individual impressions shape our experience.
  • “Each mind keeps as a solitary prisoner its own dream of a world.”

Solipsism:

  • Acknowledged limitations of individual perception.
  • Experience is shaped by personal isolation within the “thick wall of personality.”

Aestheticism:

  • Life should be lived as art, with attention to beauty and fleeting moments of intensity.
  • Not purely hedonistic but aimed at refining the sensibility.

Victorian Contrasts:  Compared to Ruskin and Morris: Pater’s focus was inward, unlike their broader societal reforms. Saw the world as something to be absorbed by individual sensibility rather than transformed.

James Anthony Froude (1818-94)

English historian, novelist, biographer, editor of Fraser's Magazine. Raised in the Anglo-Catholic Oxford Movement; initially intended to become a clergyman. Contributed to Newman's Lives of the English Saints (Saint Neot). Early semi-autobiographical works: Shadows of the Clouds (1847, as "Zeta") and The Nemesis of Faith. Expressed doubts about Anglican doctrine in The Nemesis of Faith (1849); scandalous novel ended his religious career. It was burned in Oxford by William Sewell; condemned as "a manual of infidelity" (Morning Herald).

Turned to history, influenced by Thomas Carlyle. Focused on the sixteenth century, seeing it as a pivotal period in history. Emphasized the idea that history repeats itself, with divine justice prevailing in the long run. Saw history as a drama (“It is Nature’s drama-not Shakespeare’s but a drama none the less”) Famous historical work: History of England from the Fall of Wolsey to the Defeat of the Spanish Armada (1856–1870). After Thomas Macaulay's death (1859), became England's most famous living historian.

Wrote essays on Africa included in Short Studies on Great Subjects (1867-83): offered an opinionated yet curious Protestant perspective on life and literature.

Traveled extensively in British colonies (South Africa, Australia, New Zealand, U.S., West Indies). Travel writings: Oceana, or England and Her Colonies (1886) and The English in the West Indies, or The Bow of Ulysses (1888), mixing anecdotes and imperialist views. Controversy over colonial writings: Afro-Trinidadian intellectual J. J. Thomas coined “Froudacity” in his critique Froudacity: West Indian Fables by J. A. Froude.

The Nemesis of Faith (1849): an epistolary philosophical novel exploring the crisis of faith experienced by its protagonist, Markham Sutherland. Partly autobiographical, the novel reflects Froude’s own doubts about Christianity, influenced by the Oxford Movement, German Higher Criticism and contemporary scientific and historical developments. Sutherland, pressured into becoming a clergyman, struggles with doctrines like Eternal Punishment and biblical inerrancy. His doubts lead to accusations of heresy, forcing his resignation. In Como, he befriends Helen Leonard and their emotional connection (though non-physical) intensifies their shared guilt, culminating in the accidental death of Helen's daughter. Sutherland retreats to a monastery, dying in doubt, while Helen enters a convent but remains unreconciled with the Church. The novel was condemned by religious figures, publicly burned by William Sewell and led to Froude’s resignation from Oxford. While praised by George Eliot and criticized by Thomas Carlyle, it remains significant for its exploration of Victorian skepticism and the Oxford Movement, though less widely read today.

Later works: English in Ireland in the 18th Century (1873): defended British rule, favored Protestantism, justified Cromwell; deeply unpopular among Irish Catholics.

Life of Carlyle (1882, 84): controversial biography of Thomas Carlyle, published with personal writings of Thomas and Jane Welsh Carlyle, exposing their marital issues, causing enduring gossip.

Wrote historical novel The Two Chiefs of Dunboy (1889): least popular mature work, turned an Irish hero into a villain.

Bishop Stubbs & S. R. Gardiner: Scholars who contributed significantly to English constitutional history and the study of the seventeenth century. They focused on documenting facts and research rather than writing literature.

Historical Approach Shifts: As the century progressed, historians increasingly embraced scientific and objective investigation. Leopold von Ranke’s influence encouraged this trend, focusing on document-based historical analysis. This shift was later challenged by G. M. Trevelyan, who, in the early 20th century, revived a more narrative, engaging style of historical writing.
