 Poems (1842)

1.      "Locksley Hall": a dramatic monologue narrates the emotions of a rejected suitor upon coming to his childhood home, an apparently fictional Locksley Hall. Tennyson was a guest of the Arundel family in their stately home named Loxley Hall, in Staffordshire, where he spent much of his time writing whilst on his visits. According to Tennyson, the poem represents "young life, its good side, its deficiencies, and its yearnings". Each line follows a modified version of trochaic octameter in which the last unstressed syllable has been eliminated; University of Toronto library identifies this form as "the old 'fifteener' line," quoting Tennyson, who claimed it was written in trochaics because the father of his friend Arthur Hallam suggested that the English liked the meter. The protagonist asks his friend to leave him alone as he starts to muse about his childhood upon reaching Locksley Hall where he has grown up. Then it becomes an interior monologue. Everything reminds him of his beloved who is now married. He bursts in anger and starts to feel she will forget him when she has a child. Then he crtitiques mother-child relationship. Finally, he seeks escape from these thoughts but asks what he should do. Then he turns his thoughts to a utopian future with marshals and navy. He starts to see the beauty of civilisation and the beauty of the noble savage. In the end,  he rejects the ideal of the noble savage, preferring the progress that civilisation has made. He also immediately thereafter turns his back on Locksley Hall, and marches forth to meet his comrades.  "Locksley Hall Sixty Years After" (1886) was the sequel of the poem which describes how the industrialized nature of Britain has failed to fulfil the expectations of the poem of 1842.

4.      "Ulysses":  dramatic monologue in  blank verse. Facing old age, mythical hero Ulysses describes his discontent and restlessness upon returning to his kingdom, Ithaca, after his far-ranging travels and after fighting in the Trojan War. Despite his reunion with his wife Penelope and his son Telemachus, Ulysses yearns to explore again. "To strive, to seek, to find, and not to yield". "Life piled on life / Were all too little, and of one to me / Little remains". His son Telemachus will inherit the throne that Ulysses finds burdensome. "He works his work, I mine". Ulysses turns to his fellow mariners and calls on them to join him on another quest, making no guarantees as to their fate but attempting to conjure their heroic past.  Matthew Arnold remarked, "these three lines by themselves take up nearly as much time as a whole book of the Iliad” because the  iambic pentameter is often interrupted by spondees: “Yet all experience is an arch wherethro' / Gleams that untravell'd world, whose margin fades / For ever and for ever when I move.”

In Memoriam A.H.H. (1850) 133 cantos in tetrametric lyrical verse anonymously published as “In Memoriam A.H.H. 1833”. An elegy for his Cambridge friend Arthur Henry Hallam, also explores the random cruelty of Nature seen from the conflicting perspectives of materialist science and declining Christian faith in the Victorian era. The poem thus is an elegy, a requiem, and a dirge for a friend, a time, and a place. Divided in three parts: (i) the prologue, (ii) the poem, and (iii) the epilogue. The epilogue concludes "In Memoriam" with an epithalamium, a nuptial poem for the poet's sister, Cecilia Tennyson, on her wedding to the academic Edmund Law Lushington, in 1842. “Are God and Nature then at strife” The conclusion of the poem reaffirmed Tennyson's religiosity, his progress from doubt-and-despair to faith-and-hope, which he realised by mourning the death of his friend, Arthur Henry Hallam. “'Tis better to have loved and lost / Than never to have loved at all.”

Sordello (1840) in 6 books; a failure. Jane Welsh Carlyle, wife of Thomas Carlyle after reading the poem said she "could not tell whether Sordello was 'a book, a city, or a man'". Tennyson remarked: "There were only two lines in it that I understood, and they were both lies; they were the opening and closing lines, 'Who will may hear Sordello's story told,' and 'Who would has heard Sordello's story told!'". Algernon Swinburne and Ezra Pound praised it. It's a fictionalised version of the life of Sordello da Goito, a 13th-century Lombard troubadour depicted in Canto VI of Dante Alighieri's Purgatorio. The setting is northern Italy in the 1220s, dominated by the struggle between the Guelphs (partisans of the Pope) and the Ghibellines (partisans of the Holy Roman Emperor). Sordello is a Ghibelline. Beginns by him summoning the shades of all dead poets to listen to the story he has to tell, especially Shelley (whose name isn’t said). The prince of Gulleps, Count Richard of St Boniface is captured by Taurello Salinguerra. The poet Sordillo starts to pine about Palma, his beloved. He ponders about his childhood when he was orphan page in the court and how he was enchanted by forests, castles caves etc. And he one day got to know of Palma and Richard’s affair. He starts daydreaming. He wanders towards Mantua and hears an aged troubador Eglamor sing but performs poorly so he continues his performance and wins the prize and Palma’s scarf. Eglamor dies the same night. Eglamor's jongleur, Naddo, becomes Sordello's jongleur. Upon learning about his origins, he finds his father was an archer who saved Adelaide and Palma from a fire set by lord Ecelin. He gives up the plan of becoming a "man of action", and devotes himself to minstrelsy but grows bored. Thus, invents new language for his expression but encounters public incomprehension and personal fatigue. Sordello is deeply divided between his conceptions of poet as profession and poet as destiny. Ecelin II has resolved to retire to a monastery. Sordello is appointed to welcome Taurello with song, but the baffled troubadour, lacking inspiration, wanders back to Goito. Spends a year in daydreaming there and starts to see himself as a narcissist who ruined his career. Later, he’s summoned to Verona to sing at Palma's wedding with Count Richard. He goes but she confesses her love for him and asks him to marry her. This would make him the head of the House of Romano; and Taurello consents to it. Ferrara is destroyed and Sordellois disgusted with both the Guelphs and the Ghibellines. He tells Palma his plans of building City of God in which Christendom can be reunited. Sordello has already concluded his dream is impracticable. He plans to convince Taurello to take up the Guelph cause and keep the Emperor away from Lombardy. It fails as his pride takes away his empathy. It’s revealed that Sordello is Taurello's son, the child he thought had perished at Vicenza. Taurello begins his mad project to ignore both Emperor and Pope and build a new centre of power on the house of Romano. Sordello is torn between his affection for Guelphs and his being leader of Ghibellined. This chasm is deeper and he dies. Sordello's career is inflated by chroniclers and he is misremembered as a statesman and hero. Nothing authentic remains of his life, apart from a fragment of the Goito lay, his first and least remarkable song.

Poems and Ballads, First Series (1866; second series 1878; 3rd 1889) dedicated to fellow Pre-Raphaelite, Edward Burne-Jones; first poem is: “A Ballad of Life”; "The Triumph of Time": The theme, which purports to be autobiographical, is that of rejected love. First lines: “Before our lives divide for ever, / While time is with us and hands are free,”. “Hymn to Proserpine”: addressed to the goddess Proserpina, the Roman equivalent of Persephone, but laments the rise of Christianity for displacing the pagan goddess and her pantheon.  "The Garden of Proserpine": 12 "octave stanza" of eight lines depicting the Victorian Crisis of Faith is an underlying issue that Swinburne uses strong, emphasising diction and metaphors to convey Proserpine's feelings challenging Christianity and asserting his values of paganism and masochism. First line: "Here, where the world is quiet". Some other lines: "who gathers all things mortal; with cold immortal hands" and "Here life has death for neighbour". It brings up various questions commonly accompanying the Crisis of Faith of the Victorian era involving what happens after death. Swinburne was a strong advocate of aestheticism, and believed that art should be able to exist independently of political and moral ideologies. Swinburne once announced that his poetic theory "insists upon the uninhibited exploration of all issues and experiences relevant to comprehensively prophetic treatment of the human condition". This poem celebrates the finality of death and the nothingness that lies beyond Persephone's welcoming arms, making a stark contrast to the beliefs of leading religions during this time. During his years as a journalist, John Morley famously described that Swinburne was always either "the vindictive apostle of a crushing and iron shod despair or else the libidinous laureate of a pack of satyrs" and that his poem "The Garden of Proserpine" displayed what kind of person Morley thought he was. "Dolores: Notre-Dame des Sept Douleurs” regards the figure of the titular "Dolores, Our Lady of Pain". The speaker of the poem is the voice of a besotted lover, faced with, and lamenting, Swinburne's particular ruthless and grim representation of the sacred feminine, embodied here as the Lady of Pain. In these respects, the poem shares its central themes with "Satia te Sanguine" from the same 1866 collection, as does it similarly share its sadomasochistic imagery with that poem and many others within Swinburne's corpus. It conflates the cruel yet libidinous pagan goddess figure of Dolores, the Lady of Pain with Mary, Mother of Jesus and associates the poem itself, through its parenthetical titular text (Notre-Dame des Sept Douleurs, i.e., "Our Lady of Seven Sorrows") with the Seven Dolours of the Virgin. It laments the passing of the worship of classical deities in favour of Christian morality: “What ailed us, O gods, to desert you | For creeds that refuse and restrain?” Arthur Clement Hilton parodied it in his poem "Octopus", which substitutes the character of the Lady of Pain for that of the titular mollusk. Opens: “Cold eyelids that hide like a jewel | Hard eyes that grow soft for an hour". “The Sea-Swallows”. Other poems like "The Leper", "Laus Veneris", and "St Dorothy" evoke a Victorian fascination with the Middle Ages and are in Medieval tone and style. Swinburne wrote in a wide variety of forms, including Sapphic stanzas (comprising 3 hendecasyllabic lines followed by an Adonic.

In France, Swinburne was highly praised by the Symbolist poet Stéphane Mallarmé. In the United States, horror fiction writer H. P. Thomas Hardy (1840 to 1928)

In the twentieth century Hardy published only poetry. Death of his wife Emma in 1912

Wessex Poems and Other Verses (1898) fifty-one poems set against the bleak and forbidding Dorset landscape. Hostile criticism and dry humour in it. Two notable early poems from the collection (1860s) were "Hap" and 'Amabel' - the latter exploring the theme of sexual attraction impacted by age taken up by The Well-Beloved. 'She at His Funeral' was a tribute to Hardy's friend Horace Moule; while the bitter "Neutral Tones" and the cheerful 'Sergeant's Song' show further aspects of Hardy's range of poetic subjects. The poem “Neural Tones” is famous: It is about the end of a relationship, and carries strong emotional appeal despite its "neutral tones". Opening: “We stood by a pond that winter day, / And the sun was white, as though chidden of God,”

Poems of the Past and the Present (1901) second collection. It is divided into five headings. The opening section is “War Poems” which deals with the treatment of the Boer War, especially one poem Drummer Hodge. In it also "The Darkling Thrush": a poem about the turn of the century. Originally titled "By the Century's Deathbed". The first two stanzas describe a bleak winter landscape at dusk, and the feeling of lifelessness that it produces. In stanza three, the melancholy atmosphere is transformed when "an aged thrush, frail, gaunt, and small" suddenly launches into "a full-hearted evensong of joy illimited." The final stanza muses that since there was no apparent cause for such an ecstatic outburst, the bird's singing must have been inspired by "some blessed Hope, whereof he knew and I was unaware." The use of the word "darkling" recalls the same word in Matthew Arnold's Dover Beach (1867), a poem about loss of faith. Opening: “I leant upon a coppice gate / When Frost was spectre-grey,”. "The Ruined Maid" : a satirical poem about a woman who loses her purity or virginity during the Victorian Era, which is looked down upon. This poem displays how the ruined maid sees herself, but also how society sees her. It is in the form of a dialogue (couplets) between the ruined lady (‘Milia) and the working-class person (with a dialect). Amilia informs them: "O didn't you know I'd been ruined?" said she. ***

"The Man He Killed" (1902) dramatises a battle scene between two men. He deals with an internal struggle as his thoughts are regretful before he even shoots. The narrator muses that, in a different context, he would have befriended the combatant. Nevertheless, he kills the man and attempts to reassure himself by explaining the reasons for shooting him. In the end, he has no real justifiable reason and wonders at how "quaint and curious war is" to make one kill a man as easily as becoming friends at an inn. Opening “'Had he and I but met / By some old ancient inn,”.

The Dynasts (1904 to 1908) in 3 parts, closet drama in verse and prose which Hardy considered as "an epic-drama of the war with Napoleon, in three parts, nineteen acts and one hundred and thirty scenes". The three parts were published in 1904, 1906 and 1908. He described the work as "the longest English drama in existence". George Orwell wrote that Hardy had "set free his genius" by writing this drama and thought its main appeal was "in the grandiose and rather evil vision of armies marching and counter-marching through the mists, and men dying by hundreds of thousands in the Russian snows, and all for absolutely nothing." It opens with the time when Napoleon repeated his coronation ceremony at Milan and took up the crown of Lombardy, through January 1806, the time of the death of William Pitt the Younger. The principal historical events entail Napoleon's invasion plans for England, which are abandoned when French Admiral Pierre-Charles Villeneuve sails for the south, the Battle of Trafalgar, and subsequently the Battle of Ulm and the Battle of Austerlitz.

The poem is about a trampwoman and her 3 companions, one her lover she calls her "fancy man", a man named Jeering John with whom she flirts to tease her lover and an older lady named Mother Lee. She sits in John’s lap to tease him and he tells her is the child his or John’s. She just nods in ambiguity to tease him. He stabs John and is hanged later; she also has a miscarriage. "After a Journey", "The Voice" are best in the collection. Claire Tomalin argues that Hardy became a truly great English poet after the death of his first wife Emma, and with these elegies he wrote "the finest and strangest celebrations of the dead in English poetry." "Without Ceremony," "Beeny Cliff," and "At Castle Boterel", together represent experiences that Hardy and Emma had shared prior to their marriage.
