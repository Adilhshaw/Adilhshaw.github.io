Chapter 23

Prose of Early and Middle Nineteenth century

Late 18th/early 19th-century writers: Shift in writer-society relationship.

  • Autobiographical writing varied in style and purpose; reflects new literary trends.
  • Egotism variations:
  • Wordsworth: Prelude shows “egotistical sublime” (self as illuminating case study).
  • Byron: Childe Harold projects melodramatic, exhibitionist defiance and alienation.
  • Pope: Satirical persona; egotism tied to art and social norms.
  • Milton: Fierce personal identification with cause.
  • Graveyard poets (18th century): Brooding, meditative egotism.
  • Romantic egotism differed from Augustan literature: personality as a tool of self-expression, often defiant or showman-like, rather than tied to a system or collective cause.
  • Familiar essay: Evolved in the Romantic period to focus on individuality and personal reflection.

Romantic Familiar Essay:

  • 19th century: Familiar essay became a literary norm.
  • Rooted in earlier models: Sir Thomas Browne, Steele and Cowper.
  • Exploitation of personality as a dominant style emerged with Lamb.
  • Influence: Popularized personal, whimsical and nostalgic writing but led to weaker imitations

Charles Lamb (1775 – 1834)

English essayist, poet and antiquarian. Best known for Essays of Elia and Tales from Shakespeare (co-authored with his sister Mary Lamb). Called "the most lovable figure in English literature" by biographer E.V. Lucas. Created a portrait of his father as "Lovel" in “Elia on the Old Benchers”. Born in London, enrolled at Christ's Hospital (1782), a charity boarding school. A thorough record of Christ's Hospital is to be found in several essays by Lamb as well as The Autobiography of Leigh Hunt and the Biographia Literaria of Samuel Taylor Coleridge. Developed a lifelong friendship with Samuel Taylor Coleridge. Despite the school's harshness, Lamb fared well, partly because he could often return home. He later reflected on this in his essay “Christ's Hospital Five and Thirty Years Ago”, referring to himself as "L." Suffered a stutter, which barred him from pursuing a clerical career.

Romantic interest in Ann Simmons, immortalized as "Alice M" in his essays and “Rosamund Gray” (a story of a young man named Allen Clare who loves Rosamund Gray but their relationship comes to nothing because of her sudden death). The essays "Dream Children", "New Year's Eve" and several others, speak of the many years that Lamb spent pursuing his love that ultimately failed.

Struggled with mental health: spent six weeks in an asylum (1795). Sister Mary Lamb, also mentally ill, fatally stabbed their mother in 1796. Charles took responsibility for Mary's care for life. In 1800, Mary relapsed and Charles returned her to the asylum, expressing his melancholy and loneliness in a letter to Coleridge, stating, "I almost wish that Mary were dead."

On his deathbed, Coleridge sent a mourning ring to Charles Lamb and his sister Mary. Lamb never married. In 1819, at age 44, he fell in love with actress Fanny Kelly and proposed to her; she declined. His mother’s death, killed by Mary during a mental health crisis, deeply influenced his writing. Lamb’s first publication: Four sonnets by “Mr Charles Lamb of the India House” included in Coleridge's Poems on Various Subjects (1796).

Worked for East India Company while writing in various genres. Published John Woodvil (tragedy) in 1802. Mr H (farce) performed at Drury Lane (1807) but booed. Co-wrote Tales from Shakespeare (1807) with sister Mary; Charles adapted tragedies, Mary comedies; a bestseller for Godwin's "Children's Library." Proposed to actress Fanny Kelly in 1819; rejected; remained a bachelor.

Essays of Elia* (1823) published; criticized by Southey in 1823 in Quarterly Review for irreligion. Lamb wrote a letter "Elia to Southey" in response.

The Last Essays of Elia* published in 1833, just before his death. Co-published Blank Verse with Charles Lloyd (mentally unstable son of the founder of Lloyds Bank); Lamb's most famous poem "The Old Familiar Faces." Original version of "The Old Familiar Faces" referenced his mother’s death (“I had a mother, but she died, and left me,/Died prematurely in a day of horrors”); removed in the 1818 collection.

Compiled Specimens of the English Dramatic Poets Who Lived About the Time of Shakespeare (1808). Essays: "On the Tragedies of Shakespeare Considered with Reference to their Fitness for Stage Representation" (Reflector, 1811) and "The Londoner" (criticized fascination with nature). Praised Hogarth’s images as "readable books" in Reflector essay (1811). Hazlitt described Lamb as preferring "bye-ways to highways."

Christian faith central to life; Wordsworth called him a firm Christian in the poem "Written After the Death of Charles Lamb", Alfred Ainger, in his work Charles Lamb, writes that Lamb's religion had become "an habit". His religious poems include: "On The Lord's Prayer", "A Vision of Repentance", "The Young Catechist", "Composed at Midnight", "Suffer Little Children, and Forbid Them Not to Come Unto Me", "Written a Twelvemonth After the Events", "Charity", "Sonnet to a Friend" and "David" express his religious faith, while his poem "Living Without God in the World" (attack on atheism).

Lamb’s quote, "Lawyers, I suppose, were children once," serves as the epigraph for To Kill a Mockingbird. Plays a significant role in The Guernsey Literary and Potato Peel Pie Society.

William Hazlitt (1778 – 1830)

English essayist, drama and literary critic, painter, social commentator and philosopher. Now considered one of the greatest critics and essayists in the history of the English language.  A vigorous and less mannered essayist than Charles Lamb. Initially trained as a painter but shifted to literary journalism after failing in art. Maintained his radicalism throughout life, inspired by the French Revolution, unlike other men of letters who later abandoned their early political idealism. Faced savage reviews, notably from William Gifford of the Quarterly Review, who mixed personal and political attacks with literary criticism. Advocated for prose that was colloquial yet disciplined, avoiding “cant or slang phrases” (“On Familiar Style”). His influence on the English essay was considered healthier than Lamb’s; R.L. Stevenson benefited from imitating his style.

Broad range of topics: Literature, painting, prize-fights, natural landscapes, “coffee-house politicians,” Milton’s sonnets and Sir Joshua Reynolds’s Discourses. Explored formal subjects like the fear of death while sharing Lamb’s interest in character oddities, though he disliked oddity for its own sake. Valued intellectual breadth, writing, “I hate to be surfeited with anything, however sweet.” Celebrated “gusto,” demonstrating a relish for life and the skill to convey it vividly.

Illustrated Romantic ideals: a catholic taste, a dislike of strict rules, and an appreciation for varied styles. Promoted objective criticism but often infused it with personal insight: “In works of genius, we prefer him who bows to the authority of nature.” Critical of theatrical Shakespeare; preferred reading plays over seeing them performed, particularly Hamlet. Wrote political pamphlet Free Thoughts on Public Affairs (1806). Compiled The Eloquence of the British Senate (1807).  Published A New and Improved Grammar of the English Tongue (1809). Compiled Memoirs of the Late Thomas Holcroft (1809).  Hired by The Morning Chronicle as a parliamentary reporter (1812). Critiqued Wordsworth's The Excursion in The Examiner (1814). Napoleon's defeat at Waterloo devastated him (18 June 1815). Collected The Round Table essays (1817):

  • Topics: Shakespeare (Midsummer Night's Dream), Milton (Lycidas), Hogarth (Marriage à-la-mode), beauty, acting (championed Edmund Kean), social issues (sects, Methodism, fame).

Essays explored paradoxes of human nature:

  • "On the Love of Life": Life valued through passions, not enjoyments.
  • "On Pedantry": Interest in trivial pursuits brings happiness.
  • "On Different Sorts of Fame": Immediate applause vs. lasting recognition.
  • "On Good-Nature": Good nature as the most selfish virtue.

Major Works

The Round Table (1817): First collection of literary sketches.  Collection of essays by William Hazlitt and Leigh Hunt. Hazlitt: 40 essays. Hunt: 12 essays.

Characters of Shakespeare’s Plays (1817): first book-length study of all Shakespeare’s plays (35 genuine, 32 chapters). Reaction against neoclassical criticism (e.g., Samuel Johnson); influenced by A. W. Schlegel and early Romantic views. Preface emphasizes uniqueness of Shakespeare's characters; chapters include “Doubtful Plays” and “Poems and Sonnets.” Focus on characters with personal, psychological insights ("It is we who are Hamlet") and commentary on dramatic structure and poetry. Comments on acting and stage portrayal of characters; tragedy (Macbeth, Othello, Lear, Hamlet) seen as central to Shakespeare’s genius. Tragedy valued for expressing strongest passions; imagination tied to intensity of natural impressions (Lear conclusion). Comedies appreciated but weighted less heavily; contrasts Johnson, who preferred Shakespeare’s comedies. Influenced 19th-century criticism, culminating in A.C. Bradley’s work.

Lectures: On the English Poets (1818); On the English Comic Writers (1819); On the Dramatic Literature of the Age of Elizabeth (1820).

Essay Collections:

  • Table Talk (1822): Essays on diverse topics published in two volumes (first in April 1821). Praised by Duncan Wu as Hazlitt's "pinnacle" and, with The Plain Speaker (1826), his masterpiece. One easy "On the Pleasure of Painting": Planned as a reflection on Hazlitt’s father (died 1820); expanded into views on art and satisfaction from painting. Concludes with Hazlitt painting his father’s portrait in Wem’s Unitarian chapel. Personal tone, use of "I" shows mastery of familiar essay form. Another essay "The Indian Jugglers": Philosophical musings on greatness, genius; ends with Hazlitt's obituary of John Cavanagh, noted fives player (died 1819).
  • The Spirit of the Age (1825): Profiles of his contemporaries. Collection of 25 character sketches. Subjects: thinkers, social reformers, politicians, poets, essayists, novelists. Highlights trends in thought, literature, politics of the time. Many subjects were Hazlitt’s acquaintances; essays first published in The New Monthly Magazine (1824). Topics include Jeremy Bentham, William Godwin, Coleridge, Sir Walter Scott, Lord Byron and others. Ends with an untitled section on James Sheridan Knowles. Widely regarded as Hazlitt's masterpiece and a landmark of 19th-century criticism.
  • The Plain Speaker (1826): Reflected his candid style and independent spirit.

Liber Amoris (1823): Autobiographical account of a tortured love affair with a vulgar woman; controversial for its emotional exposure.

Thomas Penson De Quincey (1785 – 1859)

English writer, essayist and literary critic. Best known for Confessions of an English Opium-Eater (1821), which is considered to have inaugurated Western addiction literature. Opposed the abolitionist movement in Britain, accusing anti-slavery campaigners of pursuing "schemes of personal aggrandizement" (articles in The Edinburgh Post, 1827–1828). Suffered from trigeminal neuralgia, a severe facial pain disorder, starting in his youth. First used opium in 1804 to alleviate the pain of neuralgia. Contributed regularly to Hogg's Weekly Instructor starting in 1850, a periodical published by James Hogg. James Hogg published Selections Grave and Gay (1853–1860), a multi-volume collection of De Quincey’s works, edited and revised by De Quincey, though not complete. The second edition of Confessions (1856) was prepared for inclusion in Selections Grave and Gay. A more comprehensive collection, The Works of Thomas De Quincey, was compiled by scholar and editor David Masson in 14 volumes (1889–1890). Due to the volume and spread of his writings, further collections followed: The Uncollected Writings (1890, 2 vols.), Posthumous Works (1891–1893, 2 vols.), The 1803 Diary (1927), and New Essays by De Quincey (1966). Philosophy: Influenced by German metaphysics (especially Kant and Jean Paul Richter); not deeply versed in Kant’s philosophy but saw transcendentalism as key to understanding the self. Imaginative Works: Reconstructs historical events (e.g., Flight of a Tartar Tribe) and ordinary topics with a dreamlike quality (e.g., The English Mail-Coach). Comparison with Landor: De Quincey’s prose is more personal and emotionally charged, unlike Landor’s formal, aristocratic style focused on political liberty and historical imagination.

Confessions of an English Opium-Eater (1821): an autobiographical account by Quincey about his laudanum addiction. First published anonymously in The London Magazine in 1821, later released in book form in 1822 and revised in 1856. Part I: "Preliminary Confessions" covers De Quincey's childhood and time as a homeless runaway in London (1802-1803). Part II: The Pleasures of Opium discusses the early positive phase (1804-1812). Introduction to the Pains of Opium and The Pains of Opium describe his negative experiences with insomnia, nightmares, and physical symptoms. Famous passage in The Pleasures: "Oh! just, subtle, and mighty opium!" modeled after Sir Walter Raleigh’s "O eloquent, just and mightie Death!" Represents De Quincey’s first attempt at "impassioned prose", later continued in Suspiria de Profundis (1845) and The English Mail-Coach (1849). Adapted into the play The Opium Eater by Andrew Dallmeyer. Parodied in the 2002 video game The Elder Scrolls III: Morrowind with Confessions of a Dunmer Skooma-Eater.

On the Knocking at the Gate in Macbeth (1823): Essay first published October 1823 in The London Magazine, as part of Notes from the Pocket-Book of a Late Opium Eater, signed "X.Y.Z."  First part: Malthus, a much longer essay debunking the economist's theory of overpopulation. De Quincey reflects on being perplexed by the knocking at the gate in Macbeth (Act II), after King Duncan is murdered. The knocking creates an effect of "peculiar awfulness" and "depth of solemnity" that puzzled him since childhood, as it couldn't be explained by existing literary criticism. De Quincey dismisses human understanding as the "meanest faculty" of the mind, as it can override other senses. The Ratcliff Highway murders, committed by John Williams, provided a new understanding for De Quincey. In the first murder, the servant knocked while the murderer was still inside, which helped De Quincey connect it to Macbeth. He realizes that murder is a "coarse and vulgar horror" from the victim's perspective, but Shakespeare’s Macbeth makes readers sympathize with the murderer. De Quincey describes the moment of someone stirring from fainting as the most affecting part of the scene, because it marks the return of life after a suspension. Macbeth and Lady Macbeth step out of the human realm, transfiguring into murderers; Lady Macbeth says she is "unsexed." After the murder, Macduff’s knock signals the return of the world and the beginning of life resuming. De Quincey describes this knock as the moment when the “pulses of life are beginning to beat again,” which makes the Macbeths' act seem even more awful in comparison. The essay ends with a rhapsody on Shakespeare’s gifts. This essay, less than 2,000 words long, is called “De Quincey’s finest single critical piece” and “one of the most penetrating critical footnotes in our literature.” Charles Lamb quipped at a dinner party that De Quincey had written something better than anything he could write, but added, “I could not write anything better.” De Quincey views his response to Macbeth in relation to his other classic essay, On Murder Considered as One of the Fine Arts.

Walladmor (1825)

On Murder Considered as One of the Fine Arts (1827): Triology of satirical essays on a gentleman's club celebrating homicide from an aesthetic perspective. Based around the 1811 Ratcliff Highway murders by John Williams. On Murder (1827): Satirical essays on a club celebrating homicide from an aesthetic view, using the Ratcliff Highway murders as a key theme. The lecture, titled The Williams' Lecture on Murder, dismisses moral concerns, discussing murder as art. History of murder, from Cain to modern crimes like the Ratcliff Highway murders and the crimes of John Williams and Thurtell. Concludes with a primer on choosing victims for murder, citing Aristotle's catharsis, ending with a personal anecdote about killing a cat. Second Paper (1839): Letter defending the aesthetic view of murder, warning against indulging in it. Story of "Toad-in-the-hole", a member who returns after the Ratcliff murders and celebrates with a dinner, which includes the disappearance of a secretary. Club toasts various murder-related topics and Toad-in-the-Hole is kicked out for his madness. Postscript (1854): De Quincey defends the extremism of satire.  Recap of the Ratcliff Highway murders: John Wilson kills Mr. Marr's family and the Williamson family, then is caught and hangs himself. Ends with a brief summary of the M'Keans' failed robbery and their execution.

Suspiria de Profundis (1845): (Latin for "sighs from the depths") is a collection of essays in the form of prose poems. Examines memory and hallucinogenic drug use, rooted in De Quincey's opium addiction. Published in fragmentary form in Blackwood's Magazine in 1845, then revised and expanded in De Quincey’s collected works (1854 and after). Left incomplete in its original publication; portions not published until Posthumous Works in 1891. A sequel to Confessions of an English Opium-Eater (1821), focusing on the visionary experiences caused by opium. De Quincey called it "impassioned prose," now considered prose poetry. Fritz Leiber’s novel Our Lady of Darkness (1977) quotes from Levana, a section of Suspiria and references the third Mother.

The English Mail-Coach (1849): essay first published in Blackwood's Edinburgh Magazine. Divided into three parts: Part I: "The Glory of Motion" describes the mail coach system and the experience of riding outside. Includes digressions on Chaucer, the Thames and the comparison of a coachman to a crocodile. Mentions "Fanny of the Bath Road" and the spreading of news about English victories in the Napoleonic Wars. Part II: "The Vision of Sudden Death" recounts a near-accident when De Quincey, opium-intoxicated, nearly collided with a gig. Part III: "Dream Fugue, Founded on the Preceding Theme of Sudden Death" explores De Quincey's opium-induced dreams, elaborating on themes from Parts I and II. Describes five dreams:

  1. Encounter with young women on a man-of-war.
  2. Storm at sea, near collision with a frigate, mysterious woman among the shrouds.
  3. Woman overwhelmed by shifting sands on a beach.
  4. Triumphal car racing through the night, passing "Waterloo and Recovered Christendom."
  5. A cosmic cathedral filled with music, the mysterious woman and death's specter.

A "Postscript" provides a conceptual frame for the essay.

Thomas Love Peacock (1785–1866)

English novelist, poet, East India Company official. Close friend of Shelley, they influenced each other’s work. Satirical novels with characters discussing and criticizing philosophical opinions of the time. Published volumes of poetry The Monks of St. Mark (1804) and Palmyra (1806). Published The Philosophy of Melancholy (1812), met Shelley same year. The Four Ages of Poetry (1820) argued that science was ending poetry’s relevance, prompting Shelley’s Defence of Poetry. Wrote satirical poem “A Day at the India Office” about work life in the India Office. Known for combining mock-Gothic and Aristophanic comedy. Intellectual love for Ancient Greece, used punning names for characters representing philosophy. Novels present a variety of types, satirizing intellectual and social views of the time: Epicurean clergyman, romantic young lady, transcendental philosopher, political pamphleteer, optimistic believer in progress and Malthusian pessimist. Not merely a reactionary, Peacock critiqued both Toryism and romantic idealism. Remained an ironic observer, relishing absurdity while exposing it. Novels include poems with rhythmic energy, captivating the ear.

Novels:

Headlong Hall (published 1815 but dated 1816) [revised slightly, 1837]:  a novella (a mix of farce, parody and caricatured dialogue), his first long work of fiction. Set in Squire Harry Headlong Ap-Rhaiader's estate in Wales. Features eccentrics with monomaniacal obsessions, similar to Crotchet Castle. Visitors include Mr. Foster (perfectibilian), Mr. Escot (deteriorationist), Mr. Jenkins (statu-quoite) and Reverend Doctor Gaster (a turkey-stuffing expert). Additional guests: phrenologist Mr. Cranium, philosopher Mr. Panscope, musician Mr. Chromatic and novelist Miss Philomela Poppyseed. Focus on dinner, Christmas celebrations and debates among the guests. By the end, four couples become engaged and are married by Reverend Gaster. Guests promise to reunite in summer.

Melincourt (1817): It is based on the "idea of an orang-outang mimicking humanity" An orangutan called Sir Oran Haut-ton is put forward as a candidate for election as a Member of Parliament. Personal attacks on Coleridge (Mr. Mystio), Southey (Mr. Feathernest) and Wordsworth (Mr. Paperstamp).

Nightmare Abbey (1818) [revised slightly, 1837]: satirizes Gothic fiction and Romanticism's morbid trends. The novella is Peacock’s most popular work. The plot centers on Christopher Glowry, a melancholic widower and his son, Scythrop, who lives in the gloomy mansion, Nightmare Abbey. Glowry’s servants are named Mattocks, Graves and Skellet. Scythrop is recovering from a failed romance, writing in a tower and distracted by two women: flirtatious Marionetta and Celinda Toobad, who hides from an arranged marriage. Mr. Cypress, a misanthropic poet, visits before going into exile and a ghostly figure causes panic, later revealed to be the somnambulant steward, Crow. Scythrop is caught between Marionetta and Celinda. Celinda reveals her true identity and both women renounce him.  Scythrop contemplates suicide but is dissuaded by his father’s promise to intercede. Mr. Glowry returns with letters from both women, announcing their marriages to other men. Scythrop resigns himself to misanthropy, hoping to gain recognition.

Maid Marian (1822): Peacock wrote most of it in 1818 at Marlow, except for the last three chapters. He wrote to Percy Bysshe Shelley about the "brilliant summer" of 1818, where his imagination was filled with "rivers, castles, forests, abbeys, monks, maids, kings, and banditti." In 1819, Peacock joined the East India Company, delaying the novella's completion and publication.  Due to the delay, it was mistaken for an imitation of Ivanhoe, although it was written earlier. Differs from other works, more romantic and lively, with a different take on Robin Hood.

The Misfortunes of Elphin (1829): a historical romance set in 6th century Wales. Features Taliesin, princes Elphin and Seithenyn and King Arthur. Based on early Welsh materials and bardic poetry, with satire of Tory attitudes. PLOT: Elphin warns Seithenyn of failing embankments, but a storm floods Caredigion. Elphin marries Angharad, finds Taliesin in a salmon weir and raises him. Elphin inherits the throne but is kidnapped by Maelgon, king of Gwyneth. Taliesin frees Elphin, vindicates Angharad, and enters a poetic contest with Maelgon’s bards. Taliesin warns of doom, including an attack by King Arthur. Rhun attempts to abduct Angharad again but is imprisoned by Taliesin. Taliesin seeks King Arthur, uncovers Gwenyvar's abduction by Melvas and negotiates her release. Taliesin wins a bardic contest in Caer Lleon and Arthur grants him a boon. Taliesin’s request for Elphin’s release is granted and Maelgon submits to Arthur’s will. Taliesin marries Melanghel, is made Chief of the Bards of Britain.  Narrative based on three ancient tales: Gwythno’s kingdom flood, Taliesin’s birth and Gwenyvar’s abduction. Sources include the Black Book of Carmarthen, Samuel Rush Meyrick’s History of Cardigan and T.J. Llewelyn Prichard’s The Land Beneath the Sea. Arthur and Gwenyvar story derived from Vita Gildae by Caradoc of Llancarfan and Ritson’s Life of King Arthur.

Crotchet Castle (1831) [revised slightly, 1837]: Like Headlong Hall, Peacock features eccentric characters with monomaniacal obsessions, using their interactions for humor and social satire.  "Crotchet" means a "perverse and peculiar notion." Reverend Doctor Folliott, a middle-aged clergyman, is the author's voice in the novel; suspicious of the "March of Intellect" and Lord Brougham's work. Two romantic courtships: Mr. Chainmail (obsessed with history) and Susannah Touchandgo (daughter of a disgraced banker); Captain Fitzchrome (moderate income) and Lady Clarinda Bossnowl (seeking a financially rewarding marriage). Action begins at Mr. Crotchet's villa on the Thames, continues with a river and canal journey to Wales and ends at Mr. Chainmail's pseudo-medieval dwelling, with a parody of the Captain Swing riots.

Gryll Grange (1861) [serialised first during 1860]: final novel by Thomas Love Peacock. Gregory Gryll, owner, believes he's descended from Gryllus, who opposed Ulysses and preferred animal life. Gryll has no heir but adopts his niece, who rejects numerous suitors. Plot centers on her finding a man to her liking. PLOT: Starts with dinner conversation at Gryll’s, where Dr. Opimian suggests an Aristophanic comedy involving Gryllus' spirit. Dr. Opimian meets Mr. Algernon Falconer, a bachelor living with seven sisters in a tower. Falconer, an eccentric, avoids the world to maintain mental equanimity. Dr. Opimian visits Falconer, encountering Harry Hedgegrow, who loves one of the sisters. A lightning strike near the tower brings Mr. Gryll and daughter Morgana to Falconer’s house. Christmas at Gryll Grange: Lord Curryfin courts Morgana, but she shows little interest and he turns to Alice Niphet. Falconer falls for Morgana but hesitates to propose due to his attachment to the seven sisters.  Harry Hedgeworth courts Dorothy, one of the sisters, while the others fall for the remaining girls.  Morgana gives Falconer an ultimatum to propose in 28 days. Curryfin and Alice Niphet’s mutual attraction leads to a proposal. Aristophanic comedy is performed, with spirit-rappers summoning Gryllus, who dismisses modern advancements. Competitive examination is held, where historical figures fail to answer irrelevant questions. Novel ends with Falconer proposing to Morgana and all nine marriages are celebrated.

William Cobbett (1763 – 1835)

English pamphleteer, journalist, politician and farmer from Farnham, Surrey. Led an agrarian faction seeking Parliamentary reform, abolition of rotten boroughs, foreign activity restrictions and higher wages to ease poverty. Supported lower taxes, returning to gold standard, reversing commons enclosures. Opposed borough-mongers, tax-eaters and stockbrokers. Radicalism contributed to Reform Act 1832, gaining him a seat in Parliament for Oldham. Best-known book: Rural Rides (1830) presented rural England's people and land in an impactful, radical manner. Wrote Observations on the Emigration of Dr. Priestley (1794) attacking Priestley. Wrote A Bone to Gnaw for the Democrats (1795), attacking pro-French Democratic Party.

First work under Peter Porcupine pseudonym: A Kick for a Bite (1795). Launched The Censor in January 1796, replaced by Porcupine's Gazette after 8 issues. Criticized Spanish King Charles IV after Spain allied with France in 1800, in Porcupine's Gazette. Founded The Porcupine in 1800; sold it in 1801.  Published Complete Collection of State Trials (1804-1812), sold shares to T.C. Hansard in 1812. Convicted of treasonous libel (1810) for opposing flogging of militiamen. Wrote Paper Against Gold in prison.  Published Political Register (1816), reached circulation of 40,000. Lived on Long Island, wrote Grammar of the English Language and The American Gardener (1821).  Published A Treatise on Cobbett's Corn (1828) and Cottage Economy (1822). Wrote The Woodlands (1825) on silviculture.

In 1823, wrote Letter to William Wilberforce, attacking support for the Combination Act, influencing its repeal in 1824. Published History of the Protestant Reformation (1824-1826), challenging traditional Protestant narrative. Criticized Malthus in Advice to Young Men (1829).  Charged with seditious libel in 1831 for Rural War, endorsing Captain Swing Riots. Known for his agrarian democracy views, influenced by Industrial Revolution’s impact on England.

Sydney Smith (1771–1845)

English wit, writer and Anglican cleric. Founder and first editor of Edinburgh Review. Known for energetic parochial work, writing, philosophy. Chiefly remembered for his wit in conversation and as a Whig reformer. Lectured at the Royal Institution. Famous for rhyming recipe for salad dressing: “Two boiled potatoes, strained through a kitchen sieve,/ Softness and smoothness to the salad give;…To add a double quantity of salt.”

 Published first book Six Sermons (1800), preached in Charlotte Street Chapel, Edinburgh.

Published Peter Plymley's Letters (1807-1808), was a spirited attack on religious intolerance, advocating for Catholic emancipation (Roman Catholics were not fully enfranchised until 1829). Peter Plymley published as A Letter on the Subject of the Catholics to my brother Abraham; ten letters in total. Peter Plymley's authorship was secret but later rumors spread.

 A Letter to the Electors upon the Catholic Question (1826) was a vigorous polemic. Three Letters to Archdeacon Singleton on the Ecclesiastical Commission (1837-1839). Petition and Letters on the repudiation of debts by the state of Pennsylvania (1843) notable polemics.

Suffered from "low spirits" and gave advice on managing the condition. Smith’s work was characterized by a tone of humor, shrewdness and good-heartedness. Put Enlightenment traditions at the service of early nineteenth-century moderate Whig reformism.

Thomas Babington Macaulay (1800 – 1859)

British historian, poet and Whig politician. Represented Victorian Whig ideals shaped by Adam Smith’s laissez-faire, utilitarianism and Victorian liberalism. Served as Secretary at War (1839-1841) and Paymaster General (1846-1848). Played a key role in shaping India’s education policy. Published an influential essay on Milton in Edinburgh Review (1825). Did not study classical literature at Cambridge, but later studied it during his time in India.

In 1827, Macaulay criticized Colonel Thomas Moody’s analysis of indentured labor in a series of letters to The Morning Chronicle and in the Edinburgh Review. His father, Zachary Macaulay, was an evangelical Whig and anti-slavery activist. Macaulay was rumored to have fallen in love with Maria Kinnaird, the wealthy ward of Richard 'Conversation' Sharp.

Critique of Southey (1830): Opposed Southey’s nostalgia for pre-industrial England and paternalistic governance. Highlighted the benefits of machinery and industrial progress. Argued discontent in England was proof against revolution, contrasting with suppressed unrest in authoritarian regimes.

His closest emotional bonds were with his younger sisters: Margaret, who died while he was in India, and Hannah, to whose daughter Margaret ("Baba") he was also attached. In 1834, Macaulay went to India and served on the Supreme Council from 1834 to 1838.  His Minute on Indian Education (1835) introduced Western-style education in India, recommending English as the medium of instruction in secondary education. Urged Lord William Bentinck to reform education on utilitarian lines and deliver "useful learning," which he equated with Western culture.

Macaulay believed that the native literature of India and Arabia was inferior to European literature, stating that a single shelf of a good European library was worth the whole native literature of India and Arabia.  His education policy recommendations were implemented in Bentinck’s English Education Act (1835). Macaulay's later years in India focused on creating a Penal Code, serving as the leading member of the Law Commission. After the Indian Mutiny of 1857, Macaulay’s proposed criminal law was enacted.

In Indian culture, the term "Macaulay's Children" refers to Indians who adopt Western values, sometimes used disparagingly, implying cultural disloyalty. Macaulayism is also associated with Dalit empowerment in post-colonial India, where some see his support for Western education as a way to challenge traditional caste hierarchies.

Lays of Ancient Rome (1842):  collection of narrative poems with heroic and tragic themes. Four poems on Roman history: Horatius, The Battle of Lake Regillus, Virginia, The Prophecy of Capys. Two historical ballads: Ivry (1824) and The Armada (1832).

  • Horatius: Publius Horatius and two companions defend the Sublician Bridge against Etruscan army of Lars Porsena. Bridge collapses; Horatius jumps into the river, survives and becomes a hero. Includes famous lines: “How can man die better than facing fearful odds...”
  • The Battle of Lake Regillus: Romans defeat Latin League led by exiled king Lucius Tarquinius Superbus. Castor and Pollux, twin gods, intervene in the battle.
  • Virginia: Appius Claudius claims Virginia as a runaway slave. Her father, Virginius, kills her to protect her honor. Sparks plebeian revolt; leads to fall of decemvirs and new laws empowering plebeians.
  • The Prophecy of Capys: Capys predicts the triumphs of Romulus' descendants. References future Roman victories in Pyrrhic and Punic wars.
  • Ivry: Celebrates Henry IV of France’s 1590 victory over the Catholic League at Ivry. Highlights his Protestant leadership and later Catholic conversion (1593).
  • The Armada: Describes news of the Spanish Armada (1588) reaching Plymouth. Focuses on beacon fires alerting England of invasion.

Critical and Historical Essays (1843): essay collection widely read for their engaging prose and confident arguments.

The History of England from the Accession of James the Second (1848): Macaulay’s most famous work. Planned as a five-volume work covering English history from 1685 to 1783, but completed only up to 1702:

  • Volumes 1–2 (1848): Covers James II’s reign, the Glorious Revolution and early reign of William III and Mary II (1685–1714).
  • Volumes 3–4 (1855): Extends to the Peace of Ryswick (1697).
  • Volume 5: Posthumously completed by his sister, Lady Trevelyan, bringing it to William III's death (1702).

Macaulay’s political writings championed a Whig interpretation of history, emphasizing progress, liberty and the triumph of constitutional government over superstition and autocracy. In 1888, Acton praised Macaulay for spreading Liberal values, calling him the most representative Englishman of his time. Piers Brendon called Macaulay “the only British rival to Gibbon.”

In 1857, Macaulay was raised to the peerage as Baron Macaulay of Rothley in the County of Leicester, though he seldom attended the House of Lords. Macaulay died of a heart attack on 28 December 1859, aged 59. He was buried on 9 January 1860 in Westminster Abbey (Poets’ Corner), near a statue of Addison. As he had no children, his peerage became extinct upon his death.

Style and Philosophy:

  • A master of debate, using logic, historical facts and rhetorical flourish.
  • Valued literature for its aesthetic rather than philosophical depth; memorized works like Paradise Lost.
  • Regarded as a Philistine by Matthew Arnold but recognized for brilliance and energy.

Social Conditions and Reform:

  • Saw the "condition of England question" as central: disparity between industrial wealth and working-class misery.
  • Recognized shifts from agrarian nostalgia to industrial acceptance post-1820.
  • Supported parliamentary reforms like the 1832 Reform Act (middle-class franchise) and later expansions of suffrage.

In the novel Marathon Man and its film adaptation, the protagonist was named Thomas Babington in honor of Macaulay.

Thomas Carlyle (1795- 1881)

Born on December 4 in Ecclefechan, Dumfriesshire, Scotland. Attended the University of Edinburgh; excelled in mathematics; invented the Carlyle circle. 1821: Experienced a "spiritual conversion" during a period of distress. Published Goethe's Faust (1822)

Wrote Life of Schiller (1823). Translated Goethe’s Wilhelm Meister's Apprenticeship (1824) and Travels (1825)

 Specimens of German Romance (1827); Wotton Reinfred (unfinished); Jean Paul Friedrich Richter (Edinburgh Review, 1827); Signs of the Times (1829); Thoughts on History (1830); Characteristics (1831)

Published Sartor Resartus serially in Fraser's Magazine (1833–34). Sartor Resartus first published in book form (1836): Full Title is Sartor Resartus: The Life and Opinions of Herr Teufelsdröckh in Three Books. "Sartor Resartus" translates as "The Tailor Re-tailored" (other versions: "The Tailor Patched," "The Tailor Made Whole Again"). Plot: Framed as a review by an unnamed English Editor of Teufelsdröckh’s fictional book Clothes: Their Origin and Influence. Teufelsdröckh, Professor of "Things in General" at Weissnichtwo ("Know not where") University, explores Transcendentalism. Editor critiques Teufelsdröckh’s philosophy while presenting his autobiographical fragments. Parody of Hegel and German Idealism. Influences: Jonathan Swift's A Tale of a Tub (admired by Carlyle); Goethe’s works (Faust, Wilhelm Meister, Werther); Laurence Sterne’s Tristram Shandy (quoted extensively). First English appearance of "Speech is silver, silence is golden" and "meaning of life." Martin Luther King Jr. paraphrased a line from Book 2, Chapter 7 in a 1957 sermon: "God sits in His heaven and does nothing."

1837: Completed and published The French Revolution: A History (Three-volume work chronicles events from the Revolution's start to the Reign of Terror (1793–94) and its aftermath in 1795);  Published Memoirs of Mirabeau, The Diamond Necklace (January-February) and Parliamentary History of the French Revolution (April).

1838: Published essays Sir Walter Scott and Varnhagen von Ense's Memoirs.

1839: Published Petition on the Copyright Bill, On the Sinking of the Vengeur and Chartism.

On Heroes, Hero-Worship, and the Heroic in History (1841): Six lectures from May 1840 on historical figures; emphasized heroic leadership's importance. Treats hero as “divinity” (the Gods of Norse mythology), as prophet (Mohammad), as port (Dante and Shakespeare), as priests (Luther and Knox), as man of letters (Johnson, Rousseau and Burns) and as King (Cromwell, Napolean). Core Belief: "Great Men" should lead, revered by others; society evolves, thrives, weakens and dies, replaced by superior breeds. Heroes: Courage > love; creators of progress, models to imitate, embodying life's cruelty as necessary and good. Death of God: Heroes create new values to replace old ones; humanity as "miracle of miracles." Heroic Vitalism: Political (aristocratic radicalism) and metaphysical (supernatural naturalism) theory opposing democracy as favoring the unfit. Comparison: Heroes akin to Aristotle's "magnanimous man" but flawed; creative energy amid contradictions defines heroism. Valetism: Criticism of heroes' flaws shows lack of imagination; heroes transcend conventional judgments. Cultural Impact:

  • Elizabeth Barrett Browning referenced On Heroes in Aurora Leigh (1856).
  • George Bernard Shaw drew inspiration from the work for several of his plays, including Arms and the Man (1894), The Man of Destiny (1897), Man and Superman (1905) and Back to Methuselah (1920).
  • Mahatma Gandhi read The Hero as Prophet and learned about Muhammad's greatness, bravery, and austere life.

1841: Published Fractions (collection of poems, 1823–1833).

Past and Present (1843): Written in 7 weeks during Carlyle’s work on Oliver Cromwell’s Letters and Speeches. Inspired by Chronicles of the Abbey of Saint Edmund's Bury by Jocelin of Brakelond. Carlyle contrasts the monk’s reverence for work and heroism with 19th-century British society’s leadership. Influenced medievalism and the Pre-Raphaelite Brotherhood.

Book 1: Proem

  • Carlyle critiques the Condition of England.
  • Despite England’s resources, the poor are starving and lack work (e.g., Manchester Insurrection).
  • Argues for an "Aristocracy of Talent" and the need for a heroic English society to recognize true leaders.

Book 2: The Ancient Monk

  • History of Samson of Tottington, a 12th-century monk, from Jocelin's account.
  • Samson, a lowly monk, became Abbot of Bury St. Edmunds and revived the abbey’s fortunes.
  • Carlyle contrasts this genuine leadership with modern British society’s lack of true governors.
  • Critiques modern English leadership as shallow, promoting "sham leaders".
  • His history is action-focused, not dry details, contrasting with the fictional historian Dryasdust.

Book 3: The Modern Worker

  • Critiques modern society: the plight of the worker and lack of meaning in industrial life.
  • Attacks Corn Laws, laissez-faire economics and aristocracy for neglecting society.
  • Condemns industrial society for reducing life to money and empty talk, calling it 'Midas-eared Mammonism'.

Book 4: Horoscope

  • Proposes solutions for societal reform: combining aristocracy and priesthood to provide guidance.
  • Advocates for a rejuvenation of spirit to elevate the working class, away from the ‘anarchy of supply and demand’.
  • Envisions a society where wise leaders bring order and structure to the masses.

Revised and reprinted as a pamphlet in 1853, titled "Occasional Discourse on the Nigger Question." Sparked a debate between Carlyle and John Stuart Mill. Carlyle Coined "Dismal Science" to describe political economy. Mill’s reply, The Negro question, also published anonymously in Fraser's Magazine. Mill criticized Carlyle’s views on human nature, the poor and societal inequality. Mill argued that self-defeating actions are a result of class oppression.

Hudson's Statue: The best-known pamphlet, attacking plans for a monument to bankrupt financier George Hudson ("railway king"). Pamphlets: No. 1: The Present Time…No. 8: Jesuitism.

Shooting of Niagara: and After (1867): his response to Disraeli’s Reform Bill of 1867. Distinguishes between Speculative Hero and Practical Hero, both will war against the “cheap and nasty” products of Democracy.

Philosophy: Developed "Natural Supernaturalism" in Sartor Resartus (1833–34). Proposed the "Great Man Theory" in On Heroes (1841).

Historiography: Viewed history as a "Prophetic Manuscript" with a cyclical progression.

Influence: Founded the London Library (1841). Contributed to the National Portrait Galleries in London and Scotland.

Personal Life and Challenges:

  • Struggled with spiritual doubt, dyspepsia and health issues; found relief in sea-bathing.
  • Disagreed with Anthony Panizzi; criticized him in the Westminster Review.
  • Reputation damaged posthumously by Froude's revelations about his marriage to Jane Welsh.

Reception:

  • Emerson called him "head of English letters."
  • Darwin praised him as "worth listening to."
  • Ruskin regarded Carlyle as his "master."
  • Reputation declined in the 20th century due to associations with Prussianism and fascism but revived since the 1950s through modern scholarship.

 John Stuart Mill (1806-1873)

Utilitarian philosopher, son of James Mill, critical of mechanical happiness theories.

Key Works: On Liberty (1859), The Subjection of Women (1869). Advocated for individual freedom, equality and reform of political institutions. A secular thinker, he opposed supernatural explanations but valued the historical and psychological aspects of religion. Mill's thought contrasted with Carlyle’s hero-worship, focusing instead on reason, liberty and social progress.

Believed in individual liberty vs. state/social control; sometimes leaned toward socialism. Proponent of utilitarianism, following Jeremy Bentham. Member of Liberal Party, early feminist, advocated for women's suffrage. Published The Subjection of Women, second to call for women's suffrage in Parliament (1832).

Early education: studied logic, Aristotle; helped father with Elements of Political Economy (1821). Experienced deep sadness at 20, contemplated suicide, found joy through Wordsworth's poetry. Supported Carlyle's work, provided materials for French Revolution. Corresponded with Comte, influenced by his sociological views. Colonial administrator at the East India Company (1823–1858), defended Company rule in India.

Godfather to Bertrand Russell. Agnostic and sceptic regarding religion.

A System of Logic, Ratiocinative and Inductive (1843): In this work, he formulated the five principles of inductive reasoning that are known as Mill's Methods.

On Liberty (1859): essay applies Mill's utilitarian ethics to society and state. Explores the relationship between authority and liberty. Emphasizes individuality as key to higher pleasures (summum bonum of utilitarianism). Warns of potential tyranny of the majority in democracies. Opposed foreign intervention in "barbarous" countries. Married Harriet Taylor after 21 years, her influence shaped his work. Advocated for women’s rights, called himself an "amanuensis" to his wife. Published On Liberty after her death in 1858.

Thoughts on Parliamentary Reform (1859)

Considerations on Representative Government (1861): Mill advocates for representative government as the ideal system. Argues representatives should not make legislation. Representatives should act as platforms for public debate, reflecting population's views. Their role is to highlight needs, discuss public matters and critique government officials. Mill emphasizes the importance of checking public officers by withdrawing support if necessary.

Utilitarianism (1863)

The Subjection of Women (1869): essay developed with wife Harriet Taylor Mill, who died in 1858. Mill finished it after completing On Liberty (1859). Argues for gender equality, challenging European norms. Attacks the idea that women are naturally inferior to men. Claims we don't know women's capabilities because they've never been allowed to try. Argues that speculative physiology isn't valid evidence.
