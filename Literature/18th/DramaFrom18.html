CHAPTER 27

DRAMA FROM THE BEGINNING OF THE EIGHTEENTH CENTURY

Early 18th century: Reaction Against Restoration Drama

Restoration Drama dominated by wit, cynicism and amorality, designed for courtly audiences.

  • Jeremy Collier: Short View of the Immorality and Profaneness of the English Stage (1698) criticized Restoration drama, aligning with rising bourgeois morality.
  • Theatrical audiences grew diverse, including the middle class, leading to shifts in themes and styles.

Moral and Sentimental Comedy

Sentimental comedy emphasizing essential goodness but lacking wit or dramatic complexity.

Sir Richard Steele (1671 – 1729)

Anglo-Irish writer, playwright, politician, co-founder of The Spectator with Joseph Addison. First Work: The Christian Hero (1701), moral pamphlet critiquing perceived vs. actual masculinity; ridiculed for hypocrisy due to Steele's indulgent lifestyle (drinking, dueling, debauchery). Married Mary Scurlock (1707); wrote 400+ letters to her. Had illegitimate daughter, Elizabeth Ousley, later adopted. Wrote prologue for John Vanbrugh’s The Mistake (1705). Prefaced Addison’s The Drummer (1716); referenced as "Dick the Scholar" in Thackeray's The History of Henry Esmond.  Member of Whig Kit-Kat Club*; associated with Child’s Coffee-house, St. Paul’s Churchyard.

*Early 18th-century English club in London; political and literary focus. Strong Whig alignment. Originated at Shire Lane tavern, run by Christopher Catt. Named after "Kit Cats" (mutton pies served there). Notable members: Writers: William Congreve, John Locke, Sir John Vanbrugh, Joseph Addison. Politicians: Duke of Somerset, Earl of Burlington, Duke of Newcastle-upon-Tyne, Earl of Stanhope, Viscount Cobham, Abraham Stanyan, Sir Robert Walpole.

Journals:

  • The Tatler (1709): His 1st public journal. Edited as “Isaac Bickerstaff”; published thrice weekly (Tuesdays, Thursdays, Saturdays); aimed to critique societal cunning, vanity, affectation; Steele wrote 188 of 271 essays; closed in 1711 due to Tory attacks. 
  • The Spectator (1711): Co-founded with Addison; notable for essays on culture and society.
  • The Guardian (1713): Another collaboration with Addison.

Comedies:

The Funeral (1701): Successful, performed at Drury Lane, gained royal and Whig attention.

The Lying Lover Or, The Lady's Friendship (1703): (Early sentimental comedy, stage failure):  Steele's second play, written during garrison duty in Harwich, War of the Spanish Succession. Combines Restoration and sentimental comedy; transitional work. Published version dedicated to Duke of Ormonde, Irish politician and soldier.

The Tender Husband (1705): Co-written with Addison.

The Conscious Lovers (1722): a five-act sentimental comedy with a prologue and epilogue by Leonard Welsted, who also penned the preface emphasizing morality and manners over the vulgarity of contemporary comedies. The plot revolves around Sir John Bevil urging his son, Bevil Jr., to marry Lucinda, the wealthy daughter of Mr. Sealand. However, Bevil Jr. loves Indiana, a poor woman raised by her aunt Isabella, who is secretly Mr. Sealand’s daughter from his first marriage. Meanwhile, Mrs. Sealand wants Lucinda to marry her wealthy cousin Cimberton, whose fortune depends on his Uncle Geoffrey's consent. To delay Lucinda’s marriage, Bevil Jr. and his friend Myrtle (in love with Lucinda) disguise themselves as Geoffrey’s lawyers, stalling the proceedings. Mr. Sealand visits Indiana to assess Bevil Jr.’s morality, recognizes her as his daughter through a bracelet and insists she marry Bevil Jr. Lucinda is paired with Myrtle after Cimberton withdraws upon learning her dowry is halved. The play concludes with Sir John extolling virtue, honesty and providence as the keys to happiness.

Richard Cumberland (1731 –1811)

English dramatist and civil servant, authored 54 plays, including 35 standard plays, 4 operas and a farce, favoring "sentimental comedy" that combined domestic plots, moral lessons and humor; his first play, The Banishment of Cicero (1761), was followed by the musical drama The Summer's Tale (1765), compressed into Amelia (1768) and his first sentimental comedy, The Brothers (1769), was inspired by Fielding's Tom Jones. His hit play The West Indian (1771) became notable for its sympathetic depiction of marginalized characters. As a secret negotiator with Spain during the American War of Independence, he sought peace, while his critical journal The London Review (1809) aimed to rival the Quarterly. Co-authored Exodiad (1807) and the novel John de Lancaster with Sir James Bland Burges; defended his grandfather Bentley in Letter to the Bishop of Oxford (1767); proposed Church revenue reforms in Letter to Richard Watson (1783); vindicated Lord Sackville in Character of Lord Sackville (1785); criticized Dr. Parr in Curtius Rescued from the Gulf; also wrote a version of 50 Psalms of David.

HIS COMEDIES

The Brothers (1769): comedy. Cumberland's breakthrough work. Complicated plot: villain with virtuous young brother. Set partly in Cornwall. Dedicated to UK Prime Minister, Duke of Grafton.

The West Indian (1771): a comedy by Richard Cumberland, premiered at Drury Lane Theatre in 1771. The plot centers on Belcour, a West Indian plantation owner who travels to Britain to win his father's approval and marry his sweetheart Louisa. The character of Belcour, a libertine with generous instincts, was likely influenced by David Garrick; he ultimately succeeds in winning favor. A popular character, Irishman Major O'Flaherty, appears again in Cumberland's 1785 play The Natural Son.

The Fashionable Lover (1772): It is a sentimental comedy. The plot centers on Augusta Aubrey. She leaves her ward's house and encounters Lord Abberville. Lord Abberville attempts to seduce her.

The Choleric Man (1774); Calypso (1779); The Natural Son (1785), in which Major O'Flaherty who had already figured in The West-Indian, makes his reappearance; The Impostors (1789), a comedy of intrigue The plot closely resembled that of The Beaux' Stratagem by George Farquhar; The School for Widows (1789): It was never published; The Armourer (1793), originally Richard II but rewritten as The Armourer to pass censorship; The Box-Lobby Challenge (1794), a protracted farce. It is a farcical comedy of manners set amongst the working class.

The Jew (1794), a drama, highly effective when the great German actor Theodor Döring played "Sheva". First English play with a Jewish moneylender as the hero. PLOT: Charles, a young clerk, is fired due to his sister Eliza's secret marriage to Sir Stephen Bertram's son, Frederick. Frederick seeks help from Sheva, a Jewish moneylender, assuming him to be miserly. Sheva is kind, sympathetic and recalls Charles's father, Don Carlos, saving him in Cádiz, Spain. Sheva gives Frederick the loan and secretly invests in Eliza’s name to prove her worth to Sir Stephen. Charles, unaware of the marriage, challenges Frederick to a duel out of pride. The duel ends quickly when Charles is lightly wounded and Sheva's generosity is revealed. The play concludes happily.

The Wheel of Fortune (1795): a five-act play, referenced in Jane Austen's Mansfield Park (1814). Set primarily in London, the first act takes place in Roderick Penruddock's rural cottage. Roderick, living in isolation for 20 years, learns from comic lawyer Timothy Weazel that he is the heir to Sir George Penruddock's estate. This inheritance, alongside the ruin of Roderick’s enemy Woodville at the gaming tables, sparks Roderick's plan for revenge. He searches for Woodville in London and meets Henry Woodville. Arabella and Woodville’s friend Sydenham appeal to him, leading Roderick to reconsider his actions. His conscience causes him to abandon revenge, forgive and grant a fortune to Henry Woodville and Emily.

The Dependent (1795); First Love (1795); False Impressions (1797); A Word for Nature (1798): It is also known by the alternative title of The Passive Husband.

The Sailor's Daughter (1804): a comedy play. After a young woman, Julia, is orphaned following the Battle of Copenhagen she enjoys a series of adventures until settling down with her long-lost guardian Captain Sentamore. The play is set in Bath.

A Hint to Husbands (1806), which, unlike the, rest, is in blank verse. The prologue was written by James Bland Burgess. The play was not a success and lasted for only five nights.

Other works:

The Note of Hand (1774), a farce. Set at Newmarket Racecourse, the play mocked prominent Whig politicians, including Charles Fox and the Duke of Devonshire. This likely led to a dispute between Cumberland and Richard Brinsley Sheridan, a Whig. Sheridan later created the character Sir Fretful Plagiary in his 1779 play The Critic, based on Cumberland.

The Princess of Parma (1778); The Battle of Hastings (1778), a tragedy

The Carmelite (1784), a romantic domestic drama in blank verse, in the style of John Home's Douglas, furnishing some effective scenes for Sarah Siddons and John Kemble as mother and son

The Mysterious Husband (1783): It is a Domestic drama with a tragic ending. Along with several other Cumberland plays it was influenced by the 1768 gothic play The Mysterious Mother by Horace Walpole

The Days of Yore (1796), a drama; The Clouds (1797); Joanna of Montfaucon (1800); The Jew of Mogadore (1808)

Satirical Reactions to Sentimental Comedy:

Oliver Goldsmith: The Good-Natured Man (1768): Critiqued sentimental trends despite moments of sentimentality. She Stoops to Conquer (1773): Revived robust comedy, featuring lively humor and simpler plots. (SEE FILE 18 NOVEL FROM RICHARDSON TO AUSTEN)

George Colman (1732 – 1794)

English dramatist and essayist, often referred to as "the Elder" to distinguish him from his son, George Colman the Younger. He co-founded The Connoisseur (1754–1756) with Bonnell Thornton, a periodical that reached 140 issues but was criticized for lacking substance. Colman’s first play, Polly Honeycomb (1760), was a great success and his comedy The Jealous Wife (1761), inspired partly by Tom Jones, made him widely famous. In 1766, he produced The Clandestine Marriage with David Garrick, but a quarrel arose when Garrick refused to take the role of Lord Ogleby. The following year, Colman purchased a fourth share in the Covent Garden Theatre. A riot broke out during the third performance of his play The Oxonian in Town (1767), which was reportedly sparked by a claque of card-sharpers.

HIS PLAYS:

Polly Honeycomb (1760): a comedic play about novel-reading’s influence on young women and 18th-century society. Colman’s first play, it helped establish his reputation, later followed by The Jealous Wife. Polly Honeycombe, a young woman who reads novels, shapes her expectations based on them. She believes novels teach a girl about life. Her father plans to marry her to Ledger, a wealthy man, but Polly prefers Scribble, an attorney’s clerk, who shares her novelistic view of life. When her father discovers her intentions, he locks her in her room, a typical novel twist. Scribble, hiding there, helps Polly escape. Ledger finds them, but Polly and Scribble have already started the marriage process by pronouncing their bans under the 1753 Marriage Act. The play ends with Polly and Scribble happily leaving, and Honeycombe and Ledger frustrated.

The Jealous Wife (1761)

The Clandestine Marriage (1766): a comedy by George Colman the Elder and David Garrick. It is both a comedy of manners and a comedy of errors. The idea was inspired by William Hogarth’s Marriage à-la-mode series. Plot: Mr. Sterling, a merchant, wants his elder daughter to marry Sir John Melvil. Melvil, however, loves Sterling's younger daughter, Fanny. Fanny is in love with Lovewell, a humble clerk and they secretly marry. To avoid Melvil, Fanny is proposed to Melvil’s elderly uncle, Lord Ogleby. The truth is revealed and Fanny and Lovewell are forgiven.

The Oxonian in Town (1767): A satire, the work depicts a naive student of Oxford University travelling south to London where he becomes mixed up with shady company, only to be rescued by a fellow undergraduate from Oxford

The Suicide (1778)

The Manager in Distress (1780): A theatre manager learns that his actors have all left him, meaning he has to cancel his season of plays, only for them to turn up after all. The play is full of in-jokes including references to Colman himself.

The Genius of Nonsense (1780); Tit for Tat (1786); The Village Lawyer (1787)

Mid-18th Century: The Rise of Sentimental and Domestic Tragedy

As comedy shifted to moral tones, tragedy also evolved, focusing on pathos and middle-class values. Moralizing Tragedies of Joseph Addison and James Thomson. Pathetic Tragedies of Nicholas Rowe and Domestic Tragedies of George Lillo

Joseph Addison (1672 – 1719)

English essayist, poet, playwright, politician. Eldest son of Lancelot Addison. Co-founded The Spectator with Richard Steele. Known for simple prose style, marking the end of 17th-century classical mannerisms. Addressed a poem to John Dryden in 1693.  Published Lives of English Poets (1694). Wrote The Campaign (1704) (commemorative poem for the Battle of Blenheim), commissioned by Lord Treasurer Godolphin. Published Remarks on Italy (1705) (account of travels in Italy in 1701–1703). Met Jonathan Swift in Ireland, spent a year there. Helped form the Kitcat Club, renewed friendship with Steele. Contributed to Steele's Tatler in 1709. Wrote libretto for Thomas Clayton's opera Rosamond (disastrous premiere) in 1707.

Produced Cato (1713, tragedy), praised by both Whigs and Tories. Wrote The Drummer (1716 comedy). Wrote The Spacious Firmament on High, published in The Spectator in 1712. Alexander Pope criticized Addison in Epistle to Dr. Arbuthnot (1735), calling him "Atticus" and comparing him to an adder.

Wrote Dialogues on Medals, translated into French by Simon-Jérôme Bourlet de Vauxcelles (1733–1802). Wrote Adventures of a Shilling, an early example of the it-narrative or object narrative, in 1710. Left an incomplete work, Of the Christian Religion. Portrayed by William Thackeray in The History of Henry Esmond.

Lord Macaulay’s tribute to Addison published in 1866, seven years after Macaulay's death in 1859.

Cato, a Tragedy (1713): Based on Marcus Porcius Cato Uticensis, a Stoic and republican hero. Prologue by Alexander Pope, epilogue by Samuel Garth. Plot: Set during Caesar's Civil War in Utica, Tunisia. Cato's court includes his children (Marcus, Portius, Marcia), Numidian prince Juba, his servant Syphax and Roman senators. Cato, firm against Caesar, plans to resist until the end. Sempronius, resentful of Cato, plots to betray him to Caesar for Marcia’s hand. Juba, loyal to Cato, falls in love with Marcia but refuses to betray Cato. Marcus and Portius are unaware of Lucia and Portius' affair. Sempronius urges Juba to fight Cato, but Juba remains honorable. In Senate, Cato rejects peace with Caesar, resolves to fight. Cato refuses Juba's request to marry Marcia, leading to tensions. Sempronius' mutiny fails when soldiers remain loyal to Cato. Juba kills Sempronius after his betrayal. Marcus dies defending Cato, Cato praises his bravery. Cato contemplates suicide, laments Caesar's victory and kills himself. On his deathbed, he blesses Marcia to marry Juba and Portius to marry Lucia.

Inspired quotes during the American Revolution (e.g., Patrick Henry's "Give me liberty, or give me death!" references Cato, Act II, Scene 4: "liberty or death."). Almeida Garrett’s Catão (1821) inspired by Addison’s play.  Cited in David Copperfield by Charles Dickens. Dickens’ Wilkins Micawber (1850) quotes Cato: "Plato, thou reasonest well."

The Drummer (1716): Comedy play also titled The Drummer, or, The Haunted House. Richard Steele wrote a preface. Inspired by the real-life Drummer of Tedworth, but set in a contemporary context. Supports the Hanoverian Succession. PLOT: Lady Truman is told her husband Sir George died in battle, inheriting his estate. She is pursued by two suitors, Mr. Tinsel and Mr. Fantome. The house is disturbed by ghostly drumming at night. The drumming scares off the suitors. It’s revealed Sir George is alive and behind the drumming.

Nicholas Rowe (1674 – 1718)

English dramatist, poet, miscellaneous writer. Appointed Poet Laureate in 1715. First editor of Shakespeare's works. Translated Lucan's Pharsalia (called one of the greatest English poems by Samuel Johnson).

Plays:

The Ambitious Stepmother (1700): First play, set in Persepolis, well received. Set in Biblical times, with subtext on British succession issues leading to the 1701 Act of Settlement. Plot centers on Artemisa, who schemes against her stepson, Artaxerses, at the Persian court. Dedicated to Edward Villiers, Earl of Jersey, Lord Chamberlain of His Majesty’s Household.

Tamerlane (1701): history play, tragedy. Portrays Timur, 14th-century conqueror, founder of Timurid Empire. Rowe, a staunch Whig, uses story as an allegory for William III (Tamerlane). Bayezid I (Ottoman leader) in the play represents Louis XIV of France (William III's opponent). Earlier version, Tamburlaine, by Christopher Marlowe (Elizabethan era), different focus. Marlowe's Tamburlaine set in English Renaissance context. Prologue spoken by actor Thomas Betterton. Play dedicated to Whig politician Lord Hartington, future Duke of Devonshire. After Addison's Cato it was the second most-performed tragedy on the 18th century stage, aside from Shakespeare.

The Biter (1704): Comedy, unsuccessful; returned to tragedy with Ulysses (1705). PLOT: At a country fair taking place near Croydon, the wealthy merchant Sir Timothy Tallapoy has arranged marriages for both himself and his daughter Angelica. However Angelica is really in love with another man, who also arrives at the fair pursued by Lady Stale an amorous, older widow.

Ulysses (1705): a tragedy. Set during Ulysses' return to Ithaca after 20 years. Greek court in turmoil; suitors vying for Penelope’s hand. Key suitors: Eurymachus (King of Samos), Antinous (Ithacan noble). Telemachus, Ulysses' son, in love with Semanthe (daughter of Eurymachus). Semanthe dreams Telemachus will replace her dead father, but loves him. Eurymachus threatens Penelope and her son, Telemachus; she nearly kills herself but is stopped by Aethon (Ulysses in disguise). Ulysses reveals himself, plans to kill the suitors; Telemachus guards Penelope. Ulysses and Telemachus kill Eurymachus after he threatens Penelope. Telemachus is blamed for Eurymachus’ death by Semanthe, causing them to part. Antinous kidnaps Penelope, turning Ithacans against Ulysses. Eurymachus’ body is paraded; Semanthe is made queen of Samos. Antinous leads Ithacan army against Ulysses, but Telemachus gains support from Samian army, revealing Antinous' betrayal. Final battle: Ulysses and followers defeat Antinous and his forces. Play ends with Ulysses reflecting on life’s inevitable pain and suffering.

The Tragedy of Jane Shore (1714):  historical tragedy. It was his penultimate play, and was inspired by the life of Jane Shore the mistress of Edward IV. Rowe dedicated the published work to Charles Douglas, 3rd Duke of Queensberry

Lady Jane Grey (1715): tragedy. Depicts Lady Jane Grey’s brief reign as English queen after Edward VI's death. Focuses on her defeat and execution by Mary I in 1554. Dedicated to Caroline, Princess of Wales, comparing her to Lady Jane as a rightful Protestant heir.  Rowe appointed Poet Laureate shortly after. Rowe’s final play.

Adaptations and translations:

The Fair Penitent (1702/3), an adaptation of Massinger and Field's The Fatal Dowry. The eponym "Lothario," meaning "a man who seduces women," stems from the character in this play. Influenced Samuel Richardson’s Clarissa Harlowe.

Lucan (1718), a paraphrase of the Pharsalia

Callipaedia (1710), translation of Claude Quillet

George Lillo (1691–1739)

English playwright, tragedian and London jeweller. Revived domestic (bourgeois) tragedy genre. First stage work: Sylvia, or The Country Burial (ballad opera, 1730).  Famous for The London Merchant (1731). Wrote at least eight plays (1730–1739), including:

The London Merchant (Or The History Of George Barnwell) (1731): famous tragedy; depicts apprentice's downfall via a prostitute. Unusual for featuring middle/working-class characters; hugely popular in the 18th century. Inspired by a 17th-century Shropshire murder ballad. Lillo emphasized moral instruction in his preface: praised tragedy as "excellent and useful." Title possibly twists Francis Beaumont's Knight of the Burning Pestle; both honor common citizens in drama. Adapted into the novel George Barnwell (1798) by Thomas Skinner Surr. Satirized in Dickens' Great Expectations as an apprentice morality tale. Referenced in Mill's On Liberty to discuss punishment for wrongdoing (Barnwell's murder). PLOT: Sarah Millwood, a manipulative prostitute, targets naive clerk George Barnwell, seduces him and coerces him into stealing from his employer, Thorowgood. Overcome by guilt, George confesses to his friend Trueman but flees, turning to Millwood, who persuades him to rob and murder his wealthy uncle. George kills his uncle, who forgives him before dying. Thorowgood learns of the crimes through Millwood’s servant, Lucy and Millwood betrays George to the police when he fails her. Both are arrested; Millwood, unrepentant, blames society, while George, remorseful, accepts his fate with forgiveness and spiritual solace. The play ends with a moral reflection on avoiding ruinous choices.

The Christian Hero (1735): tragedy based on the life of Skanderbeg who led a rebellion against the Ottoman Empire in the fifteenth century

Fatal Curiosity (1737): tragedy alternative title Guilt Its Own Punishment. the work attacks the government and Imperial strategy of Robert Walpole. It was shown with Henry Fielding's The Historical Register as an afterpiece.

Marina (1738): tragedy, a reworking of Shakespeare's Pericles, Prince of Tyre

Late 18th Century: Revival of Satirical and Robust Comedy (Comedy of Manners)

Saw a backlash against excessive sentimentality, return to satire and wit.

Richard Brinsley Butler Sheridan (1751 – 1816)

Anglo-Irish playwright, writer and Whig politician. He owned the Theatre Royal, Drury Lane in London and wrote several famous plays, including The Rivals (1775), The Duenna (1775), The School for Scandal (1777) and A Trip to Scarborough (1777). His works remain central to the Western literary canon. His mother, Frances Sheridan, was also a playwright and novelist, known for The Memoirs of Miss Sidney Biddulph (1761).

Sheridan's first play, The Rivals, debuted in 1775. Shortly after, he collaborated with his father-in-law, composer Thomas Linley, on the successful opera The Duenna. His most famous work, The School for Scandal, is considered one of the greatest comedies of manners. He followed it with The Critic (1779), a satirical update of The Rehearsal. Sheridan's The Camp (1778) commented on the French invasion threat. In 1825, Thomas Moore's sympathetic biography, Memoirs of the Life of Richard Brinsley Sheridan, influenced perceptions of him.

Sheridan was known for his womanizing, with affairs including Frances Crewe (to whom he dedicated The School for Scandal), Lady Crewe and Harriet Spencer. The affair with Harriet Spencer was particularly disastrous when her abusive husband, Viscount Duncannon, caught them in the act. The character Jack Absolute from The Rivals inspired Chris Humphreys (C.C. Humphreys) to write The Blooding of Jack Absolute, Absolute Honour and Jack Absolute.

PLAYS:

The Rivals (1775): comedy of manners, five acts, considered a masterpiece. “Malapropism” coined after Mrs. Malaprop’s misuse of words. Setting: 18th-century Bath, known for wealth, fashion and "taking the waters". Less exclusive than London, ideal for the characters. The plot revolves around Lydia Languish, a romantic young woman and Jack Absolute, who pretends to be the poor Ensign Beverley to win her love. Lydia’s guardian, Mrs. Malaprop, a moralistic widow known for her malapropisms, disapproves of the romance. Lydia has two other suitors: Bob Acres, a buffoonish country gentleman and Sir Lucius O'Trigger, a poor Irishman manipulated into thinking Lydia is interested in him. Jack learns that his father, Sir Anthony, has arranged a marriage with Lydia but pretends to accept to avoid conflict. Jack reveals his true identity to Lydia, but she rejects him for Beverley. Meanwhile, Faulkland, Jack’s friend, is overly jealous of Julia, leading to misunderstandings. Sir Lucius convinces Acres to duel Beverley, unaware it's Jack. At the duel, Acres hesitates, but Sir Lucius insists. Jack is ready to duel Sir Lucius, but Acres backs out when he learns Beverley is Jack. The duel proceeds and Sir Lucius realizes he’s been duped when Lydia confesses her love for Jack. Mrs. Malaprop admits she’s Delia and Sir Lucius is embarrassed. Sir Anthony comforts Mrs. Malaprop, Julia reconciles with Faulkland and Acres invites everyone to a party.

St Patrick's Day (1775): also called The Scheming Lieutenant. Sheridan completed it in two days. Plot: Set on St. Patrick's Day, Lieutenant O'Connor, a farcical Irish hero, loves Lauretta, daughter of Justice Credulous. O'Connor and his men are billeted in the town where the Justice lives, but Lauretta's jealous father prevents them from meeting often. O'Connor's men complain about poor treatment at inns and suggest eloping with Lauretta to end the matter. Dr. Rosy, a sentimental man mourning his wife, helps O'Connor with a plan to elope. Lauretta secretly favors O'Connor, confides in her mother, who warns against marrying a soldier. Justice Credulous hires a servant to protect Lauretta from the Lieutenant's advances. O'Connor disguises himself as "Honest Humphrey Hum," a naive rustic and is entrusted with guarding Lauretta. O'Connor reveals his true identity, leading to an angry confrontation with the Justice, who threatens to shoot him. O'Connor devises a new plan, disguising himself as a German quack and falsely claiming that the Justice has been poisoned. The Justice, terrified, seeks Dr. Rosy, who affirms the poisoning and insists on O'Connor's help for an antidote. O'Connor demands three thousand pounds and permission to marry Lauretta. Justice Credulous, believing Lauretta will never consent, agrees to O'Connor's terms. The Justice is outwitted when Lauretta agrees to the marriage and the play ends with the couple's union. Justice Credulous reluctantly withdraws his objections and the affair becomes a source of lifelong domestic conflict.

The Duenna (1775): a three-act comic opera. Admirers: Samuel Johnson, William Hazlitt, George Byron (called it "the best opera ever written"). Set in Seville, focuses on Don Jerome's wealthy family. Don Ferdinand loves Donna Clara, whose father wants to send her to a convent. Don Ferdinand bribes her maid to gain access to her chamber, but she refuses to run away with him and leaves on her own.  Don Ferdinand's sister, Donna Louisa, loves Don Antonio, a poor but gallant man. Don Jerome wants Donna Louisa to marry Isaac Mendoza, a cunning, greedy man, who is also a fool. The Duenna has two modern reworkings: Roberto Gerhard's The Duenna and Sergei Prokofiev's Betrothal in a Monastery.

A Trip to Scarborough (1777): based on Vanbrugh's The Relapse (1696), with much bawdy content removed. Tom Fashion arrives penniless in Scarborough with his servant Lory, who won’t leave until paid, planning to beg money from his wealthy brother, Lord Foppington. He learns that Foppington is set to marry Miss Hoyden, the daughter of Sir Tunbelly Clumsey, though they've never met, communicating through go-between Dame Coupler. Tom plans to marry her for her fortune with help from Colonel Townley and Loveless, who is angry at Foppington for wooing his wife, Amanda. Townley tests Berinthia’s fidelity by pretending to woo Amanda, which backfires when Loveless persecutes Berinthia. As their issues resolve, Tom moves forward with his plan, gaining letters from Dame Coupler and ingratiating himself with Sir Tunbelly, who agrees to the marriage. Tom bribes Hoyden’s nurse to marry quickly, ahead of Foppington’s arrival. Tom is exposed as an impostor, but the marriage is already complete, securing the fortune and frustrating his brother.

The School for Scandal (1777): a comedy of manners. Lady Sneerwell, a wealthy widow, plots to ruin Maria’s romance with Charles by spreading rumors about Charles and Lady Teazle. Sir Peter Teazle complains about his wife’s spending and praises Joseph, his respectable ward, who courts Maria. Sir Oliver, the brothers' rich uncle, returns from the East Indies and plans to test his nephews’ characters in disguise. Sir Peter pressures Maria to marry Joseph while Sir Oliver, disguised as Mr. Premium, finds Charles living recklessly, selling family valuables to pay debts. Lady Teazle secretly flirts with Joseph, but later regrets it. Sir Oliver forgives Charles after he refuses to sell a portrait of Sir Oliver. Joseph, disguised as Mr. Stanley, deceives Sir Peter about his wealth, but when Sir Oliver reveals his true identity, Joseph’s hypocrisy is exposed. Lady Sneerwell’s malicious schemes are unraveled, clearing Charles. Maria and Charles are reconciled and Joseph and Lady Sneerwell are disgraced. The play ends with Lady Teazle withdrawing from high society and Charles indicating reform, with a humorous epilogue by George Colman the Elder reflecting on Lady Teazle’s regrets and a parody of Shakespeare's Othello.

William Hazlitt praised Sheridan's comedies: "everything in them tells; there is no labour in vain". Edmund Gosse called the play "perhaps the best existing English comedy of intrigue". Charles Lamb: play grew from Congreve and Wycherley; criticized "sentimental incompatibilities" but acknowledged "buoyant gaiety".

The Critic (1779): Sir Fretful Plagiary is a character mocking the vanity of authors, particularly dramatist Richard Cumberland. Based on George Villiers' The Rehearsal, it portrays the chaos when author Mr. Puff invites Sir Fretful Plagiary, critics Dangle and Sneer to a rehearsal of his play The Spanish Armada.

Pizarro (1799): historical tragedy inspired by August von Kotzebue's play Die Spanier in Peru. The epilogue was written by William Lamb, the future Prime Minister of the United Kingdom.

Clio's Protest (written 1771, published 1819)

He also wrote a selection of poems and political speeches during his time in parliament.

Pantomime: Influenced by masque, commedia dell’arte and dance; evolved into a distinct genre.

 Rev John Home (1722–1808)

Scottish minister, soldier, author. Wrote play Douglas, a key Scottish school text until WWII, now largely forgotten. Co-founder of the Royal Society of Edinburgh (1783).

PLAYS:

Agis, A Tragedy (1747): a blank verse tragedy. It is a classical drama, based on the life of Agis, king of Sparta and is founded on Plutarch's narrative.

Douglas (1756): a blank verse tragedy. Famous Lines: Opening of Act II: "My name is Norval; on the Grampian Hills / My father feeds his flocks; a frugal swain." Referenced in Mansfield Park (Jane Austen) and The Mill on the Floss (George Eliot).  Recited by Young George Osborne in Vanity Fair (Thackeray), moving his aunt to tears.  Cited by Harry Walmers in Dickens’ The Boots at the Holly Tree Inn.  Wackford Squeers in Dickens’ Nicholas Nickleby calls his son "a young Norval." Referenced by twins Philip and Dolly in Shaw's You Never Can Tell. PLOT: opens with Lady Randolph mourning her brother. She reveals a secret marriage to Douglas, her father’s enemy and a son she sent away with her maid, lost in a storm and presumed dead. The son, left to die as an infant, is saved by a shepherd, Old Norval, and grows up as Young Norval. Unaware of his heritage, he is the son of Lady Randolph and Douglas, briefly reunited with his mother. Sir Malcolm exposes him, but Young Norval earns an army commission. He saves Lord Randolph’s life, gaining favor but inciting the envy of Glenalvon, Lord Randolph’s heir. Glenalvon spreads lies, deceiving Lord Randolph into killing Norval, but not before Norval kills Glenalvon. Lady Randolph, heartbroken, takes her life; only Lord Randolph survives.

The Siege of Aquileia (1760): Five-act verse tragedy. Home's third play. Setting: Year of the Six Emperors (238). Theme: Conflict between domestic loyalties and public honour.

The Fatal Discovery (1769) tragedy; Alonzo (1773) tragedy ; Alfred (1778) tragedy. His final play.

Thomas William Robertson (1829 – 1871)

English dramatist, stage director. Developed naturalism in British theatre. Society (1865): Major success, established Robertson as a playwright. Wrote five more plays for Prince of Wales's Theatre (1865–1870), focused on social issues, contrasted melodrama. Plays were called "problem plays" for addressing social issues with "cup and saucer" realism. Robertson’s works contrasted with Victorian melodramas through unexaggerated acting and settings based on contemporary life.

A Night's Adventure (1851): Comic drama set in Jacobite era, unsuccessful, closed after 4 nights.

The Cantab (1861): Farcical sketch, attracted Bohemian literary set, joined Savage, Arundel, Reunion Clubs. David Garrick (1864): Adaptation of Mélesville's comedy Sullivan, professional success.  Ours (1866): Comedy, during its run contributed short stories to a collection edited by Tom Hood. Shadow-Tree Shaft (1866): Drama, staged at the Princess's Theatre. A Rapid Thaw (1866): Adaptation of Sardou's comedy, staged at St James's Theatre.

David Garrick (1856): Comic play. Title: "Adapted from the French of Sullivan, based on a German dramatization of a fictional incident in Garrick's life". Initially conceived as a novel: David Garrick: A Love Story. Play based on a fictional incident in David Garrick’s life. PLOT: Set in 1740s London, it follows Ada, a young woman in love with Garrick, who rejects an arranged marriage to Richard Chivy. Ada’s father, Mr. Ingot, asks Garrick to cure Ada’s infatuation. Garrick agrees but promises not to romance her. At a dinner, Garrick realizes Ada is the girl he admires, but he pretends to be a drunk gambler to help her get over him. Ada is heartbroken but agrees to marry Chivy. Chivy, drunk, tells Ingot about Garrick’s fake story, unaware it’s about Ada and Ada is cheered. Chivy also mentions a duel between Garrick and a man who insulted the girl and her father. Ada goes to Garrick’s house to stop the duel, but Chivy follows her and she hides. Garrick plays along, leaving for the duel with Chivy. Ingot finds Ada and disowns her, causing her to faint. Garrick wins the duel, comforts Ada and convinces Ingot that he loves her. Ingot overhears and agrees to the marriage. One memorable moment is Garrick’s comedic drunken act at the dinner, quoting Coriolanus and breaking a window.

Society (1865): comedy-drama, milestone in Victorian drama. Known for realism in sets, costumes, acting and dialogue. Robertson both wrote and directed the play.  Introduced naturalism, depicting everyday life and characters realistically. Inspired future playwrights like Shaw and Gilbert.

Caste (1867): a comedy-drama about class distinctions. Based on Robertson’s short story The Poor Rate Unfolds a Tale (1866). PLOT:  Captain George D'Alroy, a nobleman’s son, falls in love with Esther Eccles, a ballet dancer from a poor family. His friend, Captain Hawtree, warns against the match due to caste, emphasizing social law. Esther’s drunkard father and her sister Polly add to the tension, while Esther gets a job in Manchester and George proposes. After marrying, George must leave for India and his mother, the Marquise de St. Maur, disapproves of the marriage. A year later, Esther, now widowed by false news of George’s death, struggles with poverty and returns to live with Polly. The Marquise attempts to take Esther’s baby but leaves empty-handed. George, who survived captivity, returns and the Marquise reconciles with Esther. Meanwhile, Hawtree, now a Major, is thwarted by caste in his romantic hopes. Caste hailed by Bernard Shaw as epoch-making, praised for freshness, humanity.

His other realistic plays: Ours (1866), Play (1868), School (1869), M.P. (1870).

Night's Adventure, A (1851, comic drama); Constance (1865, comic opera); Breach of Promise, A (1867, farce); For Love; Or Two Heroes (1867, drama); Dreams (1869, drama); Birth (1870, comedy) Not At All Jealous (1871, farce); War (1871, drama)

Henry Arthur Jones (1851 – 1929)

English dramatist. Often mirrored Ibsen’s ideas but from a conservative perspective. Right-wing views; engaged in debates with left-wing writers like George Bernard Shaw and H.G. Wells. My Dear Wells: a Manual for Haters of England (1921) is a collection of letters to H.G. Wells, originally published in The New York Times. He responded to Shaw's anti-war manifesto Common Sense About the War with harsh criticism, calling Shaw a "freakish homunculus" with a negative lineage. Debut play: It's Only Round the Corner, at age 27. First notable work: The Silver King (1882), a melodrama co-written with Henry Herman.

Financial success of The Silver King enabled him to write Saints and Sinners (1884), “problem play” about middle-class life and religion which caused a stir due to its religious themes.

Defended himself in an article in Nineteenth Century (1885) using a quote from Molière's Tartuffe.

Plays like The Middleman (1889) and Judah (1890) further established his reputation. Plays had 28 Broadway productions, most recently Mrs. Dane’s Defence (1928). Published essays on dramatic art, collected in The Renaissance of the English Drama (1895).

Oscar Wilde mocked his playwriting: "There are three rules for writing plays. The first rule is not to write like Henry Arthur Jones; the second and third rules are the same." In old age, remarked that A Doll's House should end with Helmer relieved to be rid of Nora, lifting a glass to Heaven "Thank God I'm well rid of her". His plays did not wholly escape from conventions of melodramatic tradition, seen intermittently.

His Wife (1881); Cherry Ripe (1881); Home Again (1881); Hard Hit (1887); Heart of Hearts (1887); Wealth (1889); The Middleman (1889); The Dancing Girl (1891); The Crusaders (1891, problem play); The Bauble Shop (1893); The Case of the Rebellious Susan (1894 problem play): Tackled moral dilemmas with wit and insight. Prefaced by an admonitory letter to Mrs Grundy.

The Liars (1897): centers on Lady Jessica Nepean, who flirts to boost her vanity, not because of dissatisfaction with her husband, Gilbert. At a houseparty, she flirts with Edward Falkner, who falls in love with her. Sir Christopher Deering tries to intervene, but Jessica enjoys Falkner’s affection. When Gilbert leaves, Jessica meets Falkner for dinner. Gilbert’s brother, George, sees them and wires Gilbert. Jessica and Rosamund cover up by sending Gilbert a letter. Gilbert arrives to find a web of lies, which he soon uncovers. Jessica, now in love with Falkner, plans to run away. Sir Christopher reconciles the couple, blaming Gilbert and advising him to flirt with Jessica himself.

Mrs. Dane’s Defence (1900): is a society play in four acts, set in the fictional village of Sunningwater, about 25 miles from London. The plot revolves around Mrs. Dane, a widow betrothed to Lionel, the adopted son of Sir Daniel, a prominent judge. Rumors spread that Mrs. Dane is actually Felicia Hindermarsh, involved in a scandalous affair with a married man in Vienna. Before allowing the marriage, Sir Daniel seeks to investigate her past and clear her name, enlisting Lady Eastney and others in the process. Mrs. Dane presents convincing evidence of her innocence, but during a cross-examination, she accidentally reveals her true identity as Felicia Hindermarsh when she slips up about a cousin. Sir Daniel's professional inquiry uncovers the truth and though Lady Eastney intervenes to keep the secret, Mrs. Dane’s marriage to Lionel is called off. In the end, Mrs. Dane is exiled from the village, restoring her reputation but marking her social punishment. The play reflects the late Victorian/Edwardian "fallen woman" trope, where women with a scandalous past are punished, though in this case, through exile rather than death or suicide, which distinguishes it from other works of the period.

Arthur Wing Pinero (1855–1934)

English playwright drawn to theatre from an early age, becoming a professional actor at 19. He wrote his first play in 1877 and, by 1884, had written 15 plays, including three successes, prompting him to leave acting for full-time writing.

He gained fame with farces like The Magistrate (1885) and later turned to serious topics in the 1890s. The Second Mrs Tanqueray (1893), about a woman’s scandalous past, was controversial but profitable. Other successes included Trelawny of the "Wells" (1898), a romantic comedy about theatre reflected his admiration for Tom Robertson’s theatrical realism and The Gay Lord Quex (1899), a comedy of manners. His libretto for The Beauty Stone (1898) failed, leading him to focus on society dramas and comedies. Between 1884 and 1893, he produced six farces and five comedies, including The Schoolmistress (1886), Dandy Dick (1887), The Cabinet Minister (1890) and The Amazons (1893). His serious works, like The Profligate (1889), explored moral double standards. Pinero, often compared to Shaw, emphasized characters over didactic themes. Criticized by Shaw for focusing on personal impact of social issues over didactic arguments. Early 20th-century successes included Iris (1901), The "Mind the Paint" Girl (1912) and The Big Drum (1915). His creativity waned after personal tragedies, including his stepson’s suicide in 1913 and the First World War. Pinero remains celebrated for his contributions to farce, social drama and problem plays.

 Important Plays:

The Magistrate (1885): a farce centered on Mr. Posket, a magistrate and his tangled domestic life. Posket is married to Agatha, a widow who lied about her age (36 → 31) and her son Cis’s age (19 → 14) to seem younger. Cis, precocious and mischievous, flirts, gambles, and smokes, unaware of his real age. Agatha’s sister Charlotte, recovering from a broken engagement, is staying with them. Colonel Lukyn, Posket’s old friend and Cis’s godfather, knows Agatha’s true age. Agatha secretly visits Lukyn to prevent him from exposing her, while Cis convinces Posket to join him at Hôtel des Princes. There, Lukyn dines with Captain Vale, Charlotte’s ex-fiancé. Agatha and Charlotte meet Lukyn, leading to a reconciliation between Charlotte and Vale. A police raid ensues; Posket and Cis escape but are pursued through chaotic streets, while Agatha and others are arrested. The next day, Posket unknowingly sentences Agatha to prison but learns of her deception at home. The farce resolves with forgiveness and highlights societal pretensions through absurd humor and misunderstandings.

The Schoolmistress (1886): a farce set at a girls' boarding school while the headmistress is away. Miss Dyott, married to the impecunious Hon. Vere Queckett, secretly plans to perform in a comic opera to support him. Dinah Rankling, a pupil, is secretly married to Reginald Paulover, whose proposal for her hand is rejected by her parents. In her absence, Queckett invites friends, including Lieut. John Mallory and Rear-Admiral Rankling, to a supper. The invitation grows unexpectedly large as Peggy Hesslerigge manipulates Queckett into including her schoolmates. Queckett spends money meant for rent and servants on the party. Mallory and Rankling arrive, unaware that Dinah is secretly married and a fire breaks out during the party. The pupils escape via a window as the fire brigade arrives, and Mrs. Queckett returns in full opera costume, leading to a series of reconciliations and resolutions.

The Second Mrs. Tanqueray (1893): Problem play, uses "Woman with a past" plot, common in 19th-century melodrama. Referenced in Hilaire Belloc's Matilda (from Cautionary Tales for Children). Plot: Opens with widower Mr. Tanqueray dining with upper-class friends. Friends uneasy about his remarriage to Paula Jarman, a woman with a bad reputation. Marriage reveals mismatch, struggles to bond Paula with Tanqueray's proper daughter, Ellean. Conflict arises when Paula's past intersects with Ellean's fiancé, her former ruinous lover. Paula tells Tanqueray; fiancé dismissed, causing alienation between father, daughter and stepmother. Ellean, learning the truth, resolves to reconcile but finds Paula has died by suicide.

Trelawny of the "Wells" (1898): Comic play. Story of Rose Trelawny, a melodrama star at Barridge Wells Theatre (modeled on Sadler's Wells). Rose quits acting to marry Arthur Gower, pleasing his conservative family (Sir William and Lady Trafalgar). Finds their life dull; her vibrant personality clashes with them. Returns to theatre but loses her flair for melodrama due to her "real world" experience. Arthur secretly becomes an actor at Bristol Old Vic. Rose inspires Sir William with memories of actor Edmund Kean. Sir William supports Tom Wrench, a playwright championing realist drama. Rose stars in Tom’s realist play, her seriousness suiting the style. Arthur cast as her co-star; they reconcile on stage.

The Gay Lord Quex (1899), a comedy, explores the struggles of a reformed philanderer, the Marquess of Quex, trying to embrace monogamy. The plot, controversial for its morality, centers on Sophy Fullgarney, a manicurist engaged to the palmist Frank Pollitt ("Valma"). Sophy, who is against her foster-sister Muriel Eden’s marriage to the notorious Quex, prefers the charming Captain Bastling as a suitor. At a country house, the Duchess of Strood, an old flame of Quex, invites him to a private drink, leading Sophy to spy on them. Although innocent, the encounter appears scandalous. Quex, caught by Sophy, bribes her to remain silent, but she remains unmoved, insisting he isn’t truly reformed. He locks the door to avoid a scandal, convincing Sophy that his reformation is genuine. Sophy, in turn, agrees not to oppose his marriage to Muriel. Meanwhile, Sophy exposes Captain Bastling’s dissolute character, causing Muriel to choose the reformed Quex over him.

The Notorious Mrs. Ebbsmith (1895): Title character: Agnes, 33, a widow, criticizes social conventions, especially marriage. Plot: Agnes and Lucas (a Conservative politician) live together in Venice; not married, but assumed so by others. Lucas abandoned his wife and career for Agnes. They met when Agnes nursed him after malaria. Agnes, once an unhappy wife, became a radical lecturer, then a nurse. Lucas’s relatives, especially his uncle (the Duke), disapprove of their relationship. The Duke proposes a fake reconciliation with Lucas's wife and a traditional domestic life for Agnes. Agnes is horrified but reluctantly agrees. Gertrude, shocked by Agnes’s “immorality,” starts to understand her philosophy and urges Agnes to reject the arrangement. Agnes decides to go with Gertrude and Amos (a spiritual man) to Yorkshire. Lucas rejects the Duke’s proposal, but the Duke urges Gertrude and Amos to stop interfering. Sybil (Lucas’s wife) confronts Agnes, asking her to return to Lucas to prevent his downfall. She once loved Lucas but now despises him. Agnes agrees but is then persuaded by Gertrude to reject this path. Sybil breaks down, renounces the idea, and repents. Lucas offers to resume their free-love relationship, but Agnes realizes she’s weak and corruptible. She abandons her radical ideals, feels unable to judge Sybil and decides to leave with Gertrude and Amos. She urges Lucas to pray.

His two adaptations of serious French works, The Ironmaster (1884) and Mayfair (1885)

Other plays: Sweet Lavender – The Weaker Sex (1888); Lady Bountiful (1891) The Benefit of the Doubt (1895); The Thunderbolt (1908); Mid-Channel (1909); The Times (1891) and The Princess and the Butterfly (1897)

Sir William Schwenck Gilbert (1836 – 1911)

English dramatist, librettist, poet and illustrator. Known for collaboration with composer Arthur Sullivan on 14 comic operas, including H.M.S. Pinafore, The Pirates of Penzance and The Mikado (became one of the most frequently performed works in musical theatre history).

Wrote over 75 plays and libretti, numerous short stories, poems and lyrics (both comic and serious). Early careers: government clerk, lawyer, then focused on writing light verse (Bab Ballads), theatre reviews and illustrations, often for Fun magazine. Began writing burlesques and comic plays in the 1860s, developing a "topsy-turvy" absurdist style.

Developed a realistic method of stage direction and gained a reputation as a strict theatre director. In 1890, quarreled with Sullivan and D'Oyly Carte over Savoy Theatre expenses, leading to the "carpet quarrel." Gilbert won a lawsuit against them, but the dispute strained their partnership.

 Gilbert's first professionally produced play: Uncle Baby.

1865-66: Collaborated with Charles Millward on several pantomimes, including Hush-a-Bye, Baby, On the Tree Top (1866), Harlequin Fortunia, King Frog of Frog Island and The Magic Toys of Lowther Arcade.

Created "fairy comedies" at Haymarket Theatre, starting with The Palace of Truth (1870), based partly on a story by Madame de Genlis. Pygmalion and Galatea (1871), one of seven plays that year, his greatest hit up to that point. Other successful plays: The Wicked World (1873), Sweethearts (1874), Broken Hearts (1875).

Pushed theatre satire boundaries: The Happy Land (1873), political satire with caricatures of Gladstone, banned briefly; The Realm of Joy (1873), set in a theatre lobby, critiquing the Lord Chamberlain; Charity (1874), critique of Victorian attitudes toward sex outside marriage.

Rosencrantz and Guildenstern (1874) burlesque, defined Gilbert’s theory of comic acting: "no such antick fellow as your bombastical hero". Engaged (1877), inspired Oscar Wilde's The Importance of Being Earnest, a romantic drama parody with satirical elements.

Savoy Operas: H.M.S. Pinafore (1878); The Pirates of Penzance (1879); Patience (1881); Iolanthe (1882); Princess Ida (1884), based on The Princess farce; The Mikado (1885); Ruddigore (1887); The Yeomen of the Guard (1888); The Gondoliers (1889).

Major influence on the English language: Phrases like "A policeman's lot is not a happy one", "short, sharp shock", "What never? Well, hardly ever!", "let the punishment fit the crime".

Knighted in 1907 for services to the theatre. Died of a heart attack while trying to rescue a young woman he was giving a swimming lesson to at his home. Inspired dramatists like Oscar Wilde and George Bernard Shaw. His lyrical facility and mastery of meter raised comic opera to new artistic heights, according to The Cambridge History of English and American Literature.

Dulcamara (or The Little Duck and the Great Quack) (1866): Gilbert's first solo success, follows Nemorino's pursuit of Adina, who rejects him in favor of Belcore, a soldier. Doctor Dulcamara arrives selling a magical "Elixir" to help men win back their sweethearts. Nemorino, desperate, buys the potion but is still ignored. Belcore becomes engaged to Adina and Nemorino enlists in the army for money. Later, Adina questions her choice of Belcore and starts to warm to Nemorino, who finally wins her heart. Dulcamara's potion works, revealing that Adina is his daughter, Nemorino's cousin, Belcore is Tomaso's son and Beppo is Dulcamara's long-lost mother. Everyone is related and agrees to live together happily, ending the comedy.

The Merry Zingara (1868): a parody of Michael Balfe's The Bohemian Girl; Robert the Devil (1868): a parody of Meyerbeer's Robert le diable. One of Gilbert's most successful early plays; The Pretty Druidess (1869): a parody of Norma – the last of Gilbert's five "operatic burlesques"

The Palace of Truth (1870): first of Gilbert's three act blank verse "Fairy Comedies, adapted from Madame de Genlis's tale. The Queen is jealous of the King’s monthly visits to the Palace of Truth, where visitors unknowingly speak the truth. The King reveals this to the Queen and plans to test the love between the Prince and Princess by bringing them to the palace. Inside, everyone speaks the truth, leading to chaos, including the Prince confessing his past love affairs. The Princess breaks off their engagement after the Prince admits love for Mirza. The talisman the King possesses to prevent truth-telling fails and Mirza reveals she swapped it with a false one. The King’s lies are exposed and the enchantment ends when the Queen breaks the talisman, with everyone reflecting on the truths revealed.

Pygmalion and Galatea (1871). Gilbert's most successful work up to this time. A reinterpretation of the Pygmalion myth in which the innocent former statue, Galatea, is unable to bear the cynicism and jealousies of the real world. In Gilbert's Pygmalion story, sculptor Pygmalion, a married man, creates statues of his wife, Cynisca, including one, Galatea, which comes to life. Cynisca encourages his interest in Galatea, but when the statue falls in love with Pygmalion, jealousy arises. Galatea’s innocent, wayward behavior disrupts their lives. Realizing the complications she caused, Galatea decides her original, statue form was happier and returns to it.

Creatures of Impulse (1871): based on Gilbert's 1870 short story called "A Strange Old Lady"; Randall's Thumb (1871): A comedy

The Happy Land (1873): This work was briefly banned for its sharp satire of government ministers. It also travesties The Wicked World; The Realm of Joy (1873): it satirises the audience for scandalous plays and the Lord Chamberlain's censorship of plays; Rosencrantz & Guildenstern (1874): Gilbert's burlesque of Hamlet.

Charity (1874). Concerns Victorian attitudes towards sex outside of marriage. Anticipates the 1890s "problem plays" of Shaw, Ibsen.

Tom Cobb or, Fortune's Toy (1875): possibly one of Gilbert's best comedies. 3-act farce Plot Summary: Tom Cobb, a penniless young man, fakes his death to avoid debts. A family claims his supposed fortune, paying him to maintain the ruse. He is then mistakenly betrothed to Caroline Effingham, who was once jilted by the man whose name Tom assumed. Eventually, Tom is revealed to be the heir to the wealth and marries Caroline.

Broken Hearts (1875): The last of Gilbert's "fairy comedies", this was one of Gilbert's favourite plays; Engaged (1877): Probably the most famous of Gilbert's non-Sullivan works for the theatre; Gretchen (1879): One of Gilbert's favorites – his take on the Faust legend; Foggerty's Fairy (1881): Gilbert's Back to the Future play.

Patience; or, Bunthorne’s Bride (1881): a comic opera in two acts by Arthur Sullivan (music) and W.S. Gilbert (libretto), satirizing the aesthetic movement of 1870s–80s England, along with fads, vanity, hypocrisy, romantic love, rural simplicity and military pomp. The aesthetic movement, rooted in “Art for Art’s Sake,” prioritized beauty over moral or social concerns in literature and art. This opera, the sixth of fourteen Gilbert and Sullivan collaborations, follows the antics of a group of lovesick maidens infatuated with Bunthorne, a sham aesthetic poet who secretly loves the simple milkmaid Patience. Patience, never having loved, meets childhood crush Archibald Grosvenor, an ideal aesthetic poet. They love each other but part as her love for his perfection would be selfish. Bunthorne raffles himself to his admirers, but Patience selflessly offers to marry him, only for the maidens to abandon Bunthorne for Grosvenor. In Act II, Lady Jane laments aging and Dragoons adopt aestheticism to win back fiancées. Bunthorne forces Grosvenor to become “commonplace,” leading the maidens to embrace ordinariness and return to their lovers. Patience marries Grosvenor, the Duke weds Jane and Bunthorne is left alone.

Brantinghame Hall (1888): a drama. Gilbert's biggest flop, it sent producer Rutland Barrington into bankruptcy.

 The Fortune Hunter (1897): Not a good play; its reception provoked WSG to announce retiring from writing for the stage.

 The Hooligan (1911): Gilbert's last play, written in a new, serious style.

Late 19th Century: Victorian Aestheticism

1890’s aesthetic movement flourished most vigorously. Wilde belonged to the fin de siècle aesthetic movement which believed in art less as an escape from than a substitute for life

Oscar Wilde (1854 – 1900)

Irish poet, playwright, aestheticism proponent. Major works: The Picture of Dorian Gray (novel), An Ideal Husband (1895), The Importance of Being Earnest (1895). Known for epigrams, wit, plays; became leading London playwright in early 1890s.

Born in Dublin; influenced by aestheticism through tutors Walter Pater and John Ruskin. Devastated by sister Isola’s death at age 9 (febrile illness); wrote "Requiescat" in her memory. Half-sisters Mary and Emily died (1871) in fire accident at a dance; dresses caught fire.

Attended Magdalen College, Oxford; studied Greek culture under J.P. Mahaffy, who called Wilde his "best pupil" but later distanced himself. Became a key figure in aesthetic and decadent movements.

Early play Vera; or, The Nihilists (1880) failed; distributed privately printed copies. Achieved fame with comedies in the 1890s. Criticized and caricatured in press, subject to anti-Irish and homophobic depictions. Advocated aestheticism; "art for art's sake." Close relationship with Robert Ross, who allegedly "initiated" Wilde into homosexuality.

Relationship with Lord Alfred Douglas led to feud with Douglas’s father, Marquess of Queensberry. Sued Queensberry for libel (1895); trial revealed evidence against Wilde. Arrested, tried twice, convicted of gross indecency with other males; sentenced to two years hard labor (1895–97).

Wrote De Profundis in prison (1905, abridged posthumous publication); spiritual reflection on suffering. Released in 1897, exiled to France; never returned to Britain or Ireland. The Ballad of Reading Gaol (1898) commemorated harsh prison life.

1887: Wilde became editor of The Lady's World, prominently featured his name on the cover, renamed it The Woman's World.  1888: Published The Happy Prince and Other Tales (fairy stories). 1891: Published two collections, Lord Arthur Savile's Crime and Other Stories and A House of Pomegranates (dedicated to Constance Mary Wilde).

The Portrait of Mr. W. H. (1889) published in Blackwood's Edinburgh Magazine, theorized Shakespeare's sonnets inspired by actor Willie Hughes.

Wrote Pen, Pencil and Poison (satirical biography of Thomas Griffiths Wainewright) for The Fortnightly Review, edited by Frank Harris. Two aesthetic essays were dialogues: The Decay of Lying and The Critic as Artist.

Lady Windermere's Fan: A Play About a Good Woman (1892): a four-act comedy by Oscar Wilde. The story follows Lady Windermere, who suspects her husband, Lord Windermere, of having an affair with Mrs. Erlynne. When she confronts him, he denies the accusation but invites Mrs. Erlynne to Lady Windermere's birthday ball. Believing her husband unfaithful, Lady Windermere decides to leave him for Lord Darlington, who has confessed his love for her. Mrs. Erlynne, discovering this, follows Lady Windermere to Lord Darlington's house and persuades her to return home. To protect Lady Windermere’s reputation, Mrs. Erlynne allows herself to be discovered in a compromising position at Lord Darlington’s, giving Lady Windermere the chance to leave unnoticed. It is revealed that Mrs. Erlynne is Lady Windermere’s estranged mother, who abandoned her family 20 years earlier. Mrs. Erlynne sacrifices her own reputation to save her daughter’s marriage.

Salomé (1893): one-act tragedy. Depicts Salome's seduction attempt of Jokanaan (John the Baptist), her dance, Jokanaan's execution and her death. Jokanaan imprisoned by Herod Antipas for criticizing Herodias, Herod’s second wife. Salome, fascinated by Jokanaan’s voice, persuades the captain to free him. Salome attempts to seduce Jokanaan, but he rejects her, calling her the “daughter of Sodom.” The captain, unable to bear Salome's desire for Jokanaan, kills himself. Herod becomes fixated on Salome, rejecting her advances. Jokanaan continues denouncing Herodias, sparking a dispute among Jews about God. Herod promises Salome anything in exchange for a dance; she performs the dance of the seven veils. Salome requests Jokanaan’s head on a silver platter. Herod reluctantly agrees, horrified by the request. Salome kisses Jokanaan’s severed head, speaking of the bitter taste of love and blood. Herod orders her execution, crushed by shields.

A Woman of No Importance (1893): is "a new and original play of modern life", in four acts. Regarded as the least successful of his four drawing room plays. Set in 1893, it revolves around a social gathering at Lady Hunstanton’s estate, where gossip and small talk dominate. The plot unfolds as Gerald Arbuthnot is offered a promising position as Lord Illingworth's secretary, a career opportunity that seems to guarantee his success. However, tension arises when Gerald’s mother, Mrs. Arbuthnot, reveals that Illingworth is actually Gerald’s estranged father, whom she once loved but was abandoned by. Illingworth, a cynical and flirtatious man, has no interest in his son, and the play delves into the repercussions of past decisions, with Mrs. Arbuthnot’s sacrifice and strength juxtaposed against Illingworth’s cold disregard. In the final act, after a failed attempt by Gerald to have Illingworth marry his mother, Mrs. Arbuthnot rejects Illingworth's offer and slaps him, declaring him a "man of no importance." The play explores themes of hypocrisy, class and personal integrity and despite its initial failure, remains a poignant critique of societal and personal values.

An Ideal Husband (1895): a four-act play by Oscar Wilde, explores themes of blackmail, political corruption and the tension between public and private honor. Set in Sir Robert Chiltern’s house, the play begins with a party where Sir Robert, a junior government minister, is blackmailed by Mrs. Cheveley, who threatens to expose his past crime of selling a Cabinet secret to Baron Arnheim. She demands his support for a fraudulent canal project and when Sir Robert submits to her demands, his wife, Lady Chiltern, insists he must remain morally impeccable. In the second act, Goring advises Sir Robert to confess, revealing his past connection with Mrs. Cheveley and urges Lady Chiltern to be more forgiving. Mrs. Cheveley exposes Sir Robert's past to Lady Chiltern, who denounces him. In Act III, Goring receives a letter from Lady Chiltern asking for help, which could be misunderstood as a love note. Mrs. Cheveley attempts to blackmail Goring into marriage in exchange for Sir Robert’s incriminating letter, but Goring traps her by revealing her theft of a brooch and forcing her to trade the letter for her release. In Act IV, Goring proposes to Mabel and Sir Robert denounces the canal scheme in Parliament. Lady Chiltern’s letter, misinterpreted by Sir Robert, leads to their reconciliation. Goring convinces Lady Chiltern not to let her husband resign and Sir Robert finally accepts Goring’s marriage proposal for Mabel. H.G. Wells, in The Pall Mall Gazette, criticized the play, calling it disappointing compared to Lady Windermere's Fan and The Woman of No Importance. Shaw praised Wilde, calling him "our only thorough playwright." In 1996, critic Bindon Russell described An Ideal Husband as Wilde's most autobiographical play, reflecting his own double life and scandalous secrets. Gertrude Chiltern was seen as a portrait of Wilde's wife, Constance.

The Importance of Being Earnest (1895): his last of four drawing-room comedies. It’s a farcical comedy about two young men who lead double lives, both using the name "Ernest" to avoid social obligations. They both court two young women, who are both in love with "Ernest." Wilde scholars cite W. S. Gilbert's 1877 farce Engaged as a key influence on the play. Shaw described the play as "extremely funny" but "heartless," a view he held lifelong. Set in 1895, it is a comedy about mistaken identities and societal expectations. Jack Worthing lives a double life, posing as "Ernest" in London and "Jack" in the country, while Algernon Moncrieff also leads a double life, pretending to have a sick friend to avoid obligations. Jack proposes to Gwendolen, but she will only marry him if his name is Ernest. Meanwhile, Jack’s young ward, Cecily, is charmed by Algernon, who pretends to be Ernest. Both women later discover they are engaged to men who are not who they claim to be. The confusion is resolved when Jack learns he is actually Ernest, the long-lost son of Lady Bracknell’s sister and his marriage to Gwendolen is approved. The couples are united and Jack concludes that he now understands the "vital Importance of Being Earnest."

The Picture of Dorian Gray a philosophical fiction and gothic horror novel, first published as a novella in Lippincott's Monthly Magazine (1890), then revised into a full novel in 1891. Wilde’s only novel, it faced criticism for its themes of immorality but later became a classic of Gothic literature.  In response to criticism, Wilde wrote a famous preface (1891) defending the autonomy of art, published separately as a literary manifesto. PLOT: In Victorian England, Dorian Gray, influenced by Lord Henry's hedonism, wishes his portrait ages instead of him. He rejects actress Sibyl Vane after she fails to perform and her suicide follows. Dorian lives a life of indulgence for 18 years, guided by a corrupt French novel. After killing Basil, who discovers the monstrous portrait, Dorian forces scientist Alan Campbell to dispose of the body, leading to Campbell's suicide. Dorian's guilt leads him to seek redemption, but he realizes his efforts were driven by vanity. In the end, he stabs his portrait, only for his servants to find his withered corpse while the portrait becomes youthful again.

The Harlot's House (1885): 36-line poem in terza rima. Focuses on prostitution, interpreted as Aesthetic or Decadent style. Poem opens with a walk down a city street at night. The poet and companion stop outside "the Harlot's house". Uproarious noise and dance music are heard. Shadows of dancers seen on blinds, appearing like automatons or skeletons. Occasionally, a dancer (a "horrible Marionette") steps outside to smoke. Poet comments, "The dead are dancing with the dead". The companion, however, enters the house: "Love passed into the house of Lust". The dance ends, and dawn comes. "Dawn with silver-sandalled feet" creeps down the street like a frightened girl

The Sphinx (1894): 174 lines, written over 20 years, from his Oxford days to 1894 publication. Written in the Decadent style, influenced by French writers like Huysmans, Baudelaire and Rollinat. Initially poorly received, now regarded as Wilde's finest Decadent work, a quintessential fin-de-siècle piece. The poem begins with the narrator describing the Sphinx in his room, calling her "exquisite grotesque" (half woman, half animal). Narrator contrasts her antiquity with his own "twenty summers" and asks about witnessing Classical history and Egyptian mythology. Questions the Sphinx's lovers, naming various Egyptian animals, mythical beasts, and gods. States Ammon was her lover and details their sexual liaison, Ammon’s beauty and the eventual ruin of that splendor. Suggests the Sphinx return to Egypt to find Ammon’s remains, but Ammon and her divine lovers are not dead. The Sphinx could resume her passions with gods or beasts in Egypt, but the narrator dismisses her, disgusted by her sensuality and his own desires: "You make my creed a barren sham, you wake foul dreams of sensual life." Narrator turns to his crucifix, asking to be left with Christ, who "weeps for every soul who dies and weeps for every soul in vain."  Written originally in four-line stanzas (ABBA rhyme scheme), later altered to two 16-syllable lines with internal rhymes.

The Ballad of Reading Gaol (1898): written in exile in Berneval-le-Grand (France) and Naples (Italy) after his release from Reading Gaol on 19 May 1897. Poem has 109 sestets (six-line stanzas), written in iambic tetrameter and trimeter (8-6-8-6-8-6 syllables), with rhyme scheme ABCBDB. Some stanzas feature internal rhymes in 8-syllable lines. Divided into 6 untitled sections: 16, 13, 37, 23, 17 and 3 stanzas. A 63-stanza version, divided into 4 sections (15, 7, 22, 19), is included in posthumous editions of Wilde's poetry, edited by Robert Ross, for ease of declamation.

"Yet each man kills the thing he loves / By each let this be heard. / Some do it with a bitter look, / Some with a flattering word. / The coward does it with a kiss, / The brave man with a sword!". Line references Shakespeare's The Merchant of Venice ("Do all men kill the things they do not love?").

IMPORTANT PLAYS:

The Room (1957) first play, earliest example of Pinter's "comedy of menace", Pinteresque dialogue, tragicomedy, Pinter wrote The Room over two or four days in 1957. The play begins with Rose engaging in a "one-person dialog" with her silent husband, Bert, as she serves him breakfast in the evening. Rose frequently changes topics, contrasting the warm room with the cold weather outside and creates unease with her constant movement and self-answering questions. Mr. Kidd, the old landlord, enters and questions Bert about leaving, with Rose answering for him. Their conversation is disjointed and irrational, as they talk over each other. Bert, a truck driver, eventually leaves in his "van." Rose's attempt to take out the garbage is interrupted by Mr. and Mrs. Sands, who are looking for a flat and Mr. Kidd. Riley, a blind black man who has been waiting in the basement, arrives with a mysterious message from Rose's "father." The play ends violently when Bert returns, finds Rose stroking Riley's face, delivers a sexually suggestive monologue about his van and then beats Riley, possibly to death. Rose then cries, "Can't see. I can't see. I can't see."

The Birthday Party (1957) first full-length play, comedy of menace. The Birthday Party centers on Stanley Webber, a former piano player living in a rundown boarding house run by Meg and Petey Boles in a seaside English town. On what is supposedly Stanley's birthday, two sinister strangers, Goldberg and McCann, arrive and turn his seemingly ordinary birthday party into a nightmare. Act 1 Meg prepares breakfast while Stanley, disheveled and suspicious, learns of the arrival of "two gentlemen." When Goldberg and McCann arrive, Stanley attempts to eavesdrop but hears only vague talk about "the job." After they leave, Meg gives Stanley a toy drum as his birthday present. Act 2 Stanley’s behavior becomes erratic as McCann prevents him from leaving. Goldberg and McCann interrogate Stanley, who denies it is his birthday and questions their motives. Meg arrives in party attire, and the party begins with toasts and games. Stanley, increasingly agitated, attacks Meg and attempts to rape Lulu during a blackout. Goldberg and McCann then corner the maniacal Stanley. Act 3 Petey has breakfast and expresses concern about Stanley’s condition to Goldberg, who is preparing to take Stanley to an unseen character, Monty. Goldberg briefly loses his confidence but recovers. Lulu confronts Goldberg about events from the previous night and is driven away by McCann. Stanley is brought in, broken and unable to respond to questions. Goldberg and McCann lead him to a car, with Petey reluctantly allowing Stanley's departure. Meg returns, unaware of the truth, as Petey remains silent about Stanley’s fate.

The Dumb Waiter (1957) one-act play. Two hit-men, Ben and Gus, wait in a basement room for their assignment. Ben reads a newspaper while Gus ties his shoes and tries to make tea, leading to an argument over "light the kettle" versus "put on the kettle." Food orders come through a dumbwaiter, though the basement isn't a kitchen. Gus leaves to get water and the dumbwaiter's speaking tube signals that their victim has arrived. Ben prepares for the target, but Gus returns without his jacket, waistcoat, tie, or gun. The curtain falls as the two men stare at each other in silence.

The Caretaker (1959) three acts a tragicomedy. Act I Scene 1 Aston invites Davies, a homeless man, into his flat after rescuing him from a bar fight. He plans to retrieve his papers from Sidcup once he finds suitable shoes. They discuss sleeping arrangements and the leaky roof. Davies gets into bed while Aston fiddles with an electrical plug. Scene 2 In the morning, Davies denies making noise during the night, blaming the neighbors and reveals his fear of foreigners. Aston, trusting Davies, leaves the room. Davies starts rummaging through Aston’s belongings but is caught by Aston’s brother, Mick, who seizes Davies in a choreographed struggle, ending Act One with Mick’s question, "What's the game?" Act II Scene 1 Mick interrogates Davies, calling him "Jenkins" and accusing him of being a robber and "stinking the place out". The plot then transitions to its falling action. Act III Scene 1 Two weeks later, Mick discusses redecorating the flat. Davies criticizes Aston’s behavior and tries to ingratiate himself with Mick, betraying Aston. Aston gives Davies another pair of shoes and Davies talks again about going to Sidcup for his papers. Scene 2 That night, Davies insults Aston, who asks him to leave. Davies, mistakenly thinking Mick will side with him, leaves angrily. Scene 3 Davies returns, complaining about Aston and asking Mick for support. Mick sides with Aston, forces Davies to admit his real name and pays him off. Aston and Mick exchange smiles. Davies, begging to stay, is rebuffed by Aston, who remains unresponsive as Davies pleads, ending the play in silence

The Homecoming (1964) two-act play. Set in North London, the play has six characters. Five of these are men of the same family: Max, a retired butcher; his brother Sam, a chauffeur; and Max's three sons: Teddy, a philosophy professor in the United States; Lenny, a pimp who only makes discreet references to his "occupation" and his clientele and flats in the city (London); and Joey, a brute training to become a professional boxer and who works in demolition. The sixth character is a woman, Ruth, Teddy's wife. The play concerns Teddy's and Ruth's "homecoming,". After six years in the U.S., Teddy brings his wife, Ruth, to his working-class family in North London for the first time. Ruth finds the environment familiar and more engaging than their academic life in America. Act One: The play begins with a power struggle between Max, the father, and his middle son, Lenny. Max and his sons engage in verbal sparring, with Max feminizing his brother Sam and claiming to have "given birth" to his sons. Teddy and Ruth arrive, revealing they married in London and moved to the U.S. Their mutual discomfort and Ruth’s flirtation with Lenny expose marital issues. Ruth, calling Lenny "Leonard," strikes a nerve. After a charged conversation with Lenny, Ruth exits and Max, discovering Teddy and Ruth's presence, initially mistakes Ruth for a prostitute but attempts reconciliation when he learns she is his daughter-in-law. Act Two: The act starts with the men sharing cigars, symbolizing phallic imagery. Max reminisces about family life and his butcher past but ends abruptly with cynicism. Ruth reveals details about her past and views on America, prompting Teddy to suggest returning home. Ruth's history as a model comes out during a private conversation with Lenny. Teddy, worried about what Ruth might have revealed, witnesses Lenny’s advances on Ruth and Joey’s inappropriate behavior. Max proposes keeping Ruth as a prostitute, discussing it with varying degrees of seriousness. Teddy refuses to contribute and hints that Ruth would age quickly. Ruth, understanding the proposal, negotiates terms, including a three-room flat and a maid. Sam collapses after revealing a secret about Jessie and MacGregor. Ruth accepts the proposal and Teddy, frustrated by Sam’s condition, decides to leave for the airport alone. In the final scene, Ruth sits relaxed in a chair, almost regal, while Sam lies motionless, Joey rests his head on Ruth’s lap and Lenny observes. Max, feeling ignored, asks Ruth to kiss him. Ruth continues to touch Joey’s head while Lenny stands by, leaving the play's resolution ambiguous, in line with Pinter's dramatic style.
