Anthony Ashley Cooper, 3rd Earl of Shaftesbury (1671 – 1713)

English peer, Whig politician, philosopher, writer. Supported England's participation in the War of the Spanish Succession. Early associate: John Toland; later found him troublesome as Toland published Inquiry concerning Virtue without permission.

Letters from Shaftesbury to Benjamin Furly, his two sons, clerk Harry Wilkinson included in Original Letters (published by Thomas Ignatius Maria Forster, 1830, enlarged 1847). Was a patron of Michael Ainsworth, a young Dorset man, maintained at University College, Oxford. His Letters to a Young Man at the University (1716) addressed to Ainsworth.

Major works completed 1705 to 1710; collected in Characteristicks of Men, Manners, Opinions, Times (first edition 1711, anonymous, 3 vols.). Philosophical focus on ethics, religion, aesthetics; highlighted the concept of the sublime as an aesthetic quality.

 Volume I contents:

  • A Letter Concerning Enthusiasm (1708), advocating religious toleration, based on letter to John Somers (Sept 1707).
  • Sensus Communis: An Essay on the Freedom of Wit and Humour (first published 1709).
  • Soliloquy: or, Advice to an Author (1710).

Volume II contents:

  • Inquiry Concerning Virtue and Merit (based on work from 1699); founder of moral sense theory.
  • The Moralists, a Philosophical Rhapsody (1709); regarded as most ambitious treatise.
  • Propounded a system of natural theology, for theodicy; believed in one God with universal benevolence, moral government, future state of man.

Volume III titled Miscellaneous Reflections; included previously unpublished works.

Notable work from Naples: A Notion of the Historical Draught or Tablature of the Judgment of Hercules. As a moralist, opposed Thomas Hobbes; followed Cambridge Platonists, rejecting Hobbes's ethical egoism. First published work: anonymous Preface to the sermons of Benjamin Whichcote (1698) in which he criticized Hobbes and Christian moralists' carrot-and-stick arguments. Pope referenced character Theocles from The Moralists in Dunciad (IV.487–490). Voltaire claimed Shaftesbury's influence on An Essay on Man in philosophical letter "On Pope."

Other works include:

  • The Danger of Mercenary Parliaments (1698) With the collaboration of John Toland.
  • The Sociable Enthusiast. A Philosophical Adventure Written to Palemon. [1704?]
  • A Letter Concerning Enthusiasm, To My Lord ***** (1708).

Francis Hutcheson (1694 – 1746)

Irish philosopher, born in Ulster to Scottish Presbyterian family. Known as a founding father of the Scottish Enlightenment. Remembered as author of A System of Moral Philosophy. Continued good humour discussion of ethics. While living in Dublin, published anonymously:

Inquiry concerning Beauty, Order, Harmony and Design (1725); Inquiry concerning Moral Good and Evil; together, these form Inquiry into the Original of our Ideas of Beauty and Virtue; Essay on the Nature and Conduct of the Passions and Affections (1728); Illustrations upon the Moral Sense.

Also during Dublin residence:

Thoughts on Laughter (1725), a criticism of Thomas Hobbes; Observations on the Fable of the Bees; Contributed six letters to Hibernicus' Letters, a periodical (Dublin, 1725–1727; 2nd ed. 1734).

Unlike Smith, not a system builder; influenced students through personality and lecturing. Referred to as "the never to be forgotten Hutcheson" by Smith, alongside David Hume. Other published works during lifetime:

  • Considerations on Patronage (1735); Philosophiae moralis institutio compendiaria, ethices et jurisprudentiae naturalis elementa continens (1742); Metaphysicae synopsis ontologiam et pneumatologiam (1742; published anonymously).

After death, son Francis published A System of Moral Philosophy, in Three Books (2 vols., 1755). Only remaining work attributed to Hutcheson: small treatise on Logic (1764).

His Compendium of Metaphysics republished at Strasbourg in 1722.

Standpoint: negative aspect opposed to Hobbes and Mandeville; positive aspect in agreement with Shaftesbury. Key agreements with Shaftesbury: analogy of beauty and virtue, moral sense functions, benevolent feelings as natural, virtuous actions promote general welfare. "Moral sense" central to Hutcheson's ethical system. In Inquiry into the Original of our Ideas of Beauty and Virtue developed the theory of the moral sense into a neat utilitarianism (“The virtue, then, of agents, or their benevolence, is always directly as the moment of good produced in like circumstances and inversely as their abilities: or B=M/A”).

KEY PHILOSOPHERS AND CONCEPTS

Shaftesbury: Nature is the source of religion for modern men; revelation was for primitive societies.

Principle of Plenitude: Creator’s benevolence shown by diversity of creatures; leads to “great chain of being” from reptiles to angels.

Alexander Pope: Essay on Man — argues for a rational order:

  • “All discord, harmony not understood/ All partial evil, universal good”
  • Man has a necessary place in the chain; acceptance of things as they are.

Soame Jenyns: Free Enquiry into the Origin and Nature of Evil (1752):

  • Claims human suffering is necessary for universal happiness.
  • Suggests superior beings may torment humans like humans torment animals; universe   as torture chamber.

Dr. Johnson: Critiques Jenyns; believes in the reality of human suffering:

  • Rejects idea that the poor are as happy as the rich; “Life must be seen before it can be known.”
  • Emphasizes that life involves endurance and little enjoyment.

Joseph Butler: Analogy of Religion (1736):

  • Defends Christian orthodoxy against deism.
  • Argues that evidence of design in nature is as ambiguous as biblical revelation.

Bernard Mandeville: Presents paradoxical views on morality and vice.

Bernard Mandeville, or Bernard de Mandeville (1670 – 1733)

Anglo-Dutch philosopher, political economist, satirist, writer, and physician. Became famous for The Fable of the Bees. Showed ability at Erasmus school in Rotterdam wrote Oratio scholastica de medicina (1685). At Leiden University, produced thesis De brutorum operationibus (1689) in which he advocated Cartesian theory of automatism among animals. Lord Macclesfield (chief justice 1710–1718) introduced him to Joseph Addison described by Mandeville as "a parson in a tye-wig." 

 The Fable of The Bees: or, Private Vices, Publick Benefits (1714) Contains satirical poem The Grumbling Hive: or, Knaves turn'd Honest (first published anonymously in 1705). Includes prose discussion called “Remarks” and essay An Enquiry into the Origin of Moral Virtue. In 1723, a second edition was published with two new essays. In The Grumbling Hive, Mandeville describes a bee community thriving until they decide to live by honesty and virtue. Bees abandon personal gain; economy collapses, leading them to live simple lives in a hollow tree. Mandeville's implication: without private vices, no public benefit; caused scandal after 1723 edition. Stated belief: "Private Vices by the dextrous Management of a skilful Politician may be turned into Publick Benefits." Book written as political satire on England's state in 1705; Tories accused John Churchill, 1st Duke of Marlborough, of personal motives in the War of the Spanish Succession. Moral of the book: “fools only strive/ To make a great an honest hive”. Utopian belief that a strong society can exist without vices is criticized. Prose Argument: Hobbesian view on virtues and vices; Moral virtues seen as offspring of pride; Desire for praise drives decent behavior.

Critique of Virtue: Mandeville argues that actions perceived as virtuous often stem from selfish motives.

Wrote Modest Defence of Publick Stews (1724), arguing for public, state-controlled brothels. Jonathan Swift's A Modest Proposal (1729) likely alludes to Mandeville's title.

Was a Lifelong vegetarian; embraced Gassendi's concept of non-human animal sentience. Howard Williams authored a chapter on Mandeville in The Ethics of Diet (1882); argued Fable of the Bees contains sincere criticism of meat-eating. Henry Stephens Salt, in his 1892 seminal book on animal rights, Animals' Rights: Considered in Relation to Social Progress, included the Fable of the Bees in the chapter, a "Bibliography of the Rights of Animals"

Other work: A Search into the Nature of Society (1723), appended to later versions of Fable, which startled public. Last works: Free Thoughts on Religion, the Church, and National Happiness (1720) and An Enquiry into the Origin of Honour and the Usefulness of Christianity in War (1732); did little to reassure the public.

George Berkeley (1685 – 1753) – known as Bishop Berkeley (Bishop of Cloyne of the Anglican Church of Ireland) 

Anglo-Irish philosopher; primary achievement: advancement of theory of "immaterialism" (later termed "subjective idealism"). Immaterialism denies existence of material substance; familiar objects (tables, chairs) are ideas perceived by the mind. Objects cannot exist without being perceived. He is also known for critique of abstraction, an important premise in argument for immaterialism.

In 1709, published An Essay Towards a New Theory of Vision; discussed limitations of human vision and advanced the theory that proper objects of sight are not material objects, but light and colour. Criticized views of the Optic Writers (Molyneux, Wallis, Malebranche, Descartes). This foreshadowed his chief work A Treatise Concerning the Principles of Human Knowledge (1710) which after poor reception, he rewrote it in dialogue form as Three Dialogues Between Hylas and Philonous (1713).  In Three Dialogues, Philonous (Greek: "lover of mind") represents Berkeley's views; Hylas ("hyle", Greek: "matter") embodies opponents, especially John Locke.

The tract A Discourse on Passive Obedience, or the Christian doctrine of not resisting the Supreme Power (1712) is considered Berkeley's major contribution to moral and political philosophy. In it Berkeley defends the thesis that people have "a moral duty to observe the negative precepts (prohibitions) of the law, including the duty not to resist the execution of punishment."

Argued against Isaac Newton's doctrine of absolute space, time and motion in De Motu (On Motion, 1721). His arguments anticipated views of Ernst Mach and Albert Einstein.

In 1732, published Alciphron, a Christian apologetic against free-thinkers. In 1734, published The Analyst subtitled A DISCOURSE Addressed to an Infidel Mathematician, a critique of the foundations of calculus; influential in the development of mathematics

His last two publications were Siris: A Chain of Philosophical Reflexions and Inquiries Concerning the Virtues of Tarwater, And divers other Subjects connected together and arising one from another (1744) and Further Thoughts on Tar-water (1752)

Berkeley posited two kinds of things: spirits and ideas. Spirits: simple, active beings that produce and perceive ideas; Ideas: passive beings produced and perceived by spirits. He opposed rationalism and "classical" empiricism. Was identified as "subjective idealist" or "empirical idealist” who believed reality constructed entirely of immaterial, conscious minds and their ideas; everything that exists is dependent on the subject perceiving it, except the subject themselves. Refuted existence of abstract objects, notably opposing Plato. According to Berkeley, "an abstract object does not exist in space or time and which is therefore entirely non-physical and non-mental"

Berkeley's razor: rule of reasoning proposed by philosopher Karl Popper in his study of Berkeley's key scientific work De Motu. He considered it similar to Ockham's razor but "more powerful." It represents extreme empiricist view of scientific observation; states scientific method provides no true insight into the nature of the world. Another essay of the same book titled "Three Views Concerning Human Knowledge," Popper argues that Berkeley should be considered an instrumentalist philosopher.

Lord Byron's Don Juan references Berkeley’s immaterialism in the Eleventh Canto: “When Bishop Berkeley said 'there was no matter,'/ And proved it—'t was no matter what he said”

Herman Melville humorously references Berkeley in Chapter 20 of Mardi (1849)

James Joyce references Berkeley's philosophy in the third episode of Ulysses (1922): “Who watches me here? …The good bishop of Cloyne took the veil of the temple out of his shovel…”

James Boswell in Life of Samuel Johnson (1763) records Johnson's opinion on Berkeley's philosophy: Johnson struck his foot against a large stone, saying, “I refute it thus.”

David Hume (born David Home; 1711 – 1776)

Scottish philosopher, historian, economist and essayist. Known to his friends as le bon David. Best known for influential system of empiricism, philosophical skepticism and metaphysical naturalism. Began with A Treatise of Human Nature (1739–40); aimed to create a naturalistic science of man that examined psychological basis of human nature. Followed John Locke in rejecting existence of innate ideas; concluded all human knowledge derives solely from experience. He argued that inductive reasoning and belief in causality cannot be justified rationally; instead, they result from custom and mental habit. Was an opponent of philosophical rationalists; believed that passions, rather than reason, govern human behaviour. Famously stated: "Reason is, and ought only to be the slave of the passions." Hume was a sentimentalist; held that ethics are based on emotion or sentiment rather than abstract moral principles. First to clearly expound the is–ought problem; the idea that a statement of fact alone cannot lead to a normative conclusion of what ought to be done.

Had little respect for professors; told a friend in 1735: "there is nothing to be learnt from a Professor, which is not to be met with in Books."

Came close to a mental breakdown; began with a coldness he attributed to "Laziness of Temper" lasting about nine months. Developed scurvy spots; physician diagnosed him with the "Disease of the Learned." Wrote the massive six-volume The History of England. Judged An Enquiry Concerning Human Understanding and An Enquiry Concerning the Principles of Morals as his greatest literary and philosophical achievements.

Published An Abstract of a Book lately Published to summarize main doctrines of the Treatise, without revealing authorship. In last year of life, wrote brief autobiographical essay titled "My Own Life," summarizing his life in fewer than five pages.

Immanuel Kant, in Prolegomena to Any Future Metaphysics (1783), credited Hume with awakening him from his "dogmatic slumber."

Arthur Schopenhauer stated: "there is more to be learned from each page of David Hume than from the collected philosophical works of Hegel, Herbart and Schleiermacher taken together."

Albert Einstein (1915) inspired by Hume's positivism when formulating theory of special relativity.

Søren Kierkegaard adopted Hume's idea that reason reveals ignorance, using it to argue for necessity of religious faith (fideism).

Jerry Fodor (2003) described Hume's Treatise as "the founding document of cognitive science."

Edmund Burke (1729-1797)

Born in Dublin, Ireland. Political philosopher and statesman, highly regarded by Samuel Johnson for his conversational skills. Member of The Literary Club. Opposed Rousseau’s influence and the general theorizing on the “Rights of Man,” advocating instead for an organic view of the state and a cautious approach to political change.

Believed in the importance of continuity and tradition in politics, though less pessimistically conservative than Samuel Johnson. Opposed the British government’s policies toward the American colonies, the conduct of the East India Company in India, and the French Revolution.

Burke’s views were often complex and non-linear, as he shifted positions over time but remained grounded in consistent principles regarding society, the state, and history.

The well-known maxim that "The only thing necessary for the triumph of evil is for good men to do nothing" is widely misattributed to Burke.

His purple passage about the French Queen is a splendid piece of rhetoric.

His speeches on India: On the Nabob of Arcot’s Debts (1785)

Major Works:

A Vindication of Natural Society (1756): A satire on Bolingbroke’s arguments against Christianity. Argues that Bolingbroke’s atheistic views were disruptive to civil society.

A Philosophical Enquiry into the Origin of Our Ideas of the Sublime and Beautiful (1757): First complete philosophical separation of the beautiful and sublime into distinct categories. Influenced thinkers like Denis Diderot and Immanuel Kant. Beautiful: well-formed, aesthetically pleasing. Sublime: powerful, overwhelming, potentially destructive. Preference for Sublime signals transition from Neoclassical to Romantic era. Kant critiqued Burke for not explaining the causes behind the mental effects of beauty and sublimity. Kant argued Burke only compiled data for future explanation.

Thoughts on the Cause of the Present Discontents (1770): a political pamphlet. Subject is nepotism of King George III and Court influence on the House of Commons. Influential in defining political parties and their roles in government. Burke argued parties are groups united to promote national interest based on shared principles. Burke emphasized: "When bad men combine, the good must associate."

On American Taxation (1774): was a speech by Edmund Burke in the House of Commons on April 19, 1774. Burke advocated for the full repeal of the Townshend Revenue Act of 1767. Parliament had repealed five of the six duties, leaving the tax on tea. The speech occurred during debates on the Coercive Acts. Rose Fuller proposed repealing the tea tax to reduce resistance to the new acts. Burke supported Fuller's motion. Historian Robert Middlekauff praised the speech for its wit and insight into Britain's failed colonial policies.

A Letter to the Sheriffs of Bristol (1777)

Reflections on the Revolution in France (1790): Contrast between French Revolution and British unwritten Constitution. Argument with British supporters of French Revolution. Intellectual attack on French Revolution, key text of modern conservatism. Advocates monarchy, aristocracy, property, hereditary succession and wisdom of the ages. Defines Burke's transformation of traditionalism into political conservatism.

Thomas Paine (1737-1809)

English-born American Founding Father, political philosopher, inventor and French Revolutionary. Authored Common Sense (1776) and The American Crisis (1776–1783), key in inspiring U.S. independence from Britain. Promoted Enlightenment ideals, human rights, and influenced colonial patriots. Wrote Rights of Man (1791) defending the French Revolution against Edmund Burke’s criticism. Convicted in absentia for seditious libel in England (1792) due to Rights of Man. Advocated Deism, reason, freethought and criticized religion, especially Christianity (The Age of Reason). Denounced George Washington in a 1796 pamphlet, calling him a hypocrite and incompetent.

Published Agrarian Justice (1797), proposing a one-time inheritance tax on landowners for a guaranteed minimum income. Returned to the U.S. in 1802, died in 1809. Only six attendees at his funeral due to ostracism from his criticism of Christianity and national leaders.

Common Sense (1775–1776): 47-page pamphlet by Thomas Paine advocating American independence from Britain. Published anonymously on January 10, 1776, it urged egalitarian government and revolution.

The American Crisis (1776–1783): Series of pamphlets by Paine during the American Revolution. 13 pamphlets from 1776–1777 and 3 more from 1777–1783. First published on December 19, 1776, under the pseudonym "Common Sense."

Rights of Man (1791): Book by Paine defending the French Revolution and arguing for political revolution when governments fail to protect natural rights. Responds to Edmund Burke's Reflections on the Revolution in France.

The Age of Reason (1794–1807): Paine’s work arguing for deism, critiquing institutionalized religion, and the Bible. Published in three parts. Part I presents Paine’s creed; Parts II and III analyze the Bible's lack of divine legitimacy.

CULTURAL INFLUENCE FROM FRANCE:

Winds from France influenced not only Tom Paine. Rousseau’s ideas shaped English sentimental novels; “child of nature” trope emerged. William Godwin (1756–1836)

English journalist, philosopher, novelist. Considered one of the first exponents of utilitarianism, first modern anarchist proponent. Famous works: An Enquiry Concerning Political Justice (critique of institutions) and Caleb Williams (mystery novel attacking privilege). Target of conservative backlash due to radicalism and marriage to Mary Wollstonecraft (feminist). Wrote candid biography of Wollstonecraft posthumously; she died in childbirth. Father of Mary Shelley (Frankenstein author), married Percy Bysshe Shelley. Second wife: Mary Jane Clairmont; together ran The Juvenile Library, published children's works under pseudonyms. Disillusionment with French Revolution slowed his influence.

NOVELS:

Damon and Delia, A Tale (1784); Imogen: A Pastoral Romance From the Ancient British (1784)

Things as They Are; or, The Adventures of Caleb Williams (1794): Three-volume novel critiquing tyranny and abuse of power. Popularizes ideas from Godwin's Political Justice (1793). Explores how legal institutions harm individuals, even innocents. Original preface removed for alarming booksellers.

Volume I: Caleb Williams: Poor, self-educated orphan; employed by Ferdinando Falkland. Falkland: Wealthy, noble, prone to rage; valued for reason and justice. Story of Barnabas Tyrrel: Tyrannical neighbor, oppresses tenants, rivals Falkland. Tyrrel imprisons niece Emily Melvile; her distress leads to her death. Tyrrel attacks Falkland at Emily's funeral; later murdered. Falkland cleared due to reputation; two tenants wrongly convicted, hanged. Falkland becomes emotionally unstable.

Volume II: Caleb suspects Falkland of Tyrrel's murder. Falkland admits guilt; forces Caleb to silence under death threat. Frames Caleb for theft; Caleb flees but returns to face trial. Corrupt trial convicts Caleb; imprisoned, suffers with other inmates. Escapes with servant’s help; begins life on the run.

Volume III: Caleb robbed by thieves, rescued by their captain, debates morality of crime. Betrayed, narrowly escapes authorities multiple times. Jones (or Gines): Rival thief, tracks and betrays Caleb. Falkland sends money to Caleb, tries to bribe him into silence. Caleb confronts Falkland in court, exposes his tyranny.

Published Ending: Court confrontation: Caleb vindicated but expresses pity for Falkland. Both forgive; Falkland dies soon after. Caleb reflects on shared humanity of oppressor and oppressed. Feels hollow victory, blames himself for Falkland’s death.

Original Manuscript Ending: Falkland accuses Caleb of revenge in court; Caleb silenced by magistrate. Final scene: Caleb in prison, under Jones’s watch, descends into madness. Forgets Falkland; sees happiness as oblivion, like a gravestone.

Stage Adaptation 1796: Adapted by Richard Brinsley Sheridan as The Iron Chest. Avoided censorship by presenting as an operatic version

St. Leon: A Tale of the Sixteenth Century (1799): influenced Gothic novel St. Irvyne; or, The Rosicrucian (1811) by Percy Bysshe Shelley. Influenced Frankenstein (1818), dedicated to Godwin, written by Mary Shelley, his daughter. PLOT: Count Reginald de St. Leon loses wealth gambling, driven to guilt and near madness. Gains elixir of life and wealth-multiplication secret. Alienated from humanity, becomes a wanderer. Raised by his mother after his father’s death in infancy. Joins Italian wars (1521–6), knighted by Francis I. Returns to France, inherits estate, succumbs to gambling and debt. Marries Marguerite, a cultured, accomplished woman. Loses fortune, family moves to Switzerland, tries farming. Crops destroyed by storm, denied Swiss relief due to foreign status. Evicted, survives with help of a neighbor, later betrayed. Reclaims farm through legal battle. Stranger Zampieri reveals secrets of immortality and gold-making. Reginald hides secret from wife, uses wealth to repurchase estate, but suspicions arise. Family distrusts him. Arrested, bribery attempts fail, escapes with Hector’s help. Moves to Pisa; accused of sorcery. Mob burns house, kills Hector. Marguerite miscarries, dies in Barcelona. Daughters raised by family friend; estranged son Charles. Reginald adopts aliases to avoid disgrace. In Hungary, seeks to rebuild economy, faces unrest and suspicion. Betrayed and imprisoned by ally Bethlem Gabor. Escapes during a siege, reunites with Charles. Forges Pandora’s inheritance to enable Charles’ marriage. Misunderstandings arise; alienated from son again. Reflects on regrets, solitude and cost of immortality.

The Looking Glass: A True History of the Early Years of an Artist (1805)

Fleetwood (1805): subtitled Or, The New Man of Feeling, is an eponymous tale where the hero shares the title's name. It critiques Rousseau’s ideas on the virtue of natural man, opposing the ideals in Emile. Casimir Fleetwood lives in Merionethshire, North Wales, in the early-mid 18th century on a large estate near Cader Idris. Raised as an only child, he travels to Switzerland and visits his father’s friend, Monsieur Ruffigny, a Rousseau-like figure. After learning of his father’s death, Ruffigny accompanies him back to Wales, sharing his life story during the journey. At 45, Fleetwood marries Mary Macneil, whose family perished in a shipwreck. He struggles with the marriage due to the age gap. Fleetwood invites his distant cousins, Kenrick and Gifford. Gifford schemes to discredit Kenrick and become Fleetwood’s heir. When Mary becomes pregnant, Gifford’s insinuations lead Fleetwood to suspect her of an affair with Kenrick. He accuses Mary of adultery, divorces her, and leaves for France. In Paris, Fleetwood is attacked by men but rescued by Kenrick. Louisa Scarborough’s father reveals evidence of Mary and Kenrick’s innocence and exposes Gifford as the instigator and shooter. Fleetwood reconciles with Mary and meets his son. Gifford is executed in France as a highwayman and swindler. Fleetwood wills his possessions to Mary, £18,000 to Kenrick, and settles in the Pyrenees on £400 a year. Kenrick marries Louisa, concluding the story.

The Enquirer (1797); Memoirs of the Author of A Vindication of the Rights of Woman (1798): is William Godwin's biography of his late wife Mary Wollstonecraft.

Life of Geoffrey Chaucer (1804); The Pantheon: Or, Ancient History of the Gods of Greece and Rome (1814); Lives Of Edward and John Philips, Nephews and Pupils Of Milton (1815); Life of Lady Jane Grey, and of Lord Guildford Dudley, Her Husband (1824); History of the Commonwealth (book) (1824–1828); Thoughts on Man, his Nature, Productions, and Discoveries, Interspersed with some particulars respecting the author (1831); Lives of the Necromancers (1834): was the final book written by Godwin. The book concerns paranormal legends from western and Middle Eastern history.

Mary Wollstonecraft (1759–97)

Godwin’s wife, feminist thinker. Known as one of the founding feminist philosophers, influencing feminist thought. Best known for A Vindication of the Rights of Woman (1792). Life marked by unconventional relationships: Henry Fuseli, Gilbert Imlay (daughter Fanny Imlay). Married William Godwin, a philosopher and anarchist. Died at 38 after giving birth to her second daughter, Mary Shelley, 11 days prior. Tombstone inscription: "Mary Wollstonecraft Godwin, Author of A Vindication of the Rights of Woman: Born 27 April 1759: Died 10 September 1797." Godwin published Memoirs of the Author of A Vindication of the Rights of Woman (1798), criticized for lack of sensitivity towards her death. Robert Southey accused Godwin of heartlessness in publishing memoirs.

Satires like The Unsex’d Females were published about her life. Poems about her: "Wollstonecraft and Fuseli" by Robert Browning and by William Roscoe (“Hard was thy fate in all the scenes of life/As daughter, sister, mother, friend, and wife;/But harder still, thy fate in death we own,
Thus mourn'd by Godwin with a heart of stone”).

Inspired modern feminists like Ayaan Hirsi Ali, who cited her in Infidel and Caitlin Moran, who described herself as "half Wollstonecraft."

Mary: A Fiction is her only complete novel.

Vindication of the Rights of Men (1790): critique of Burke; deemed too harsh by Godwin.

WORKS:

Thoughts on the Education of Daughters: With Reflections on Female Conduct, in the More Important Duties of Life (1787): first published by Joseph Johnson. The conduct book offers advice on female education to the emerging British middle class. It focuses on morality, etiquette and female conduct while also providing basic child-rearing instructions, such as how to care for an infant.

Mary: A Fiction (1788): only complete novel. Written while Wollstonecraft worked as a governess in Ireland, after her dismissal and career shift. The novel follows the tragic "romantic friendships" of the heroine, Mary, with a woman and a man. Emile (1762) by Rousseau is a key influence on Mary: A Fiction. Wollstonecraft critiques marriage, seeing geniuses as “enchained” by it. PLOT: Mary’s mother, Eliza, is self-absorbed and neglects her daughter, who educates herself. Mary inherits the family fortune after her brother dies. Mary's mother requests she marry Charles, a wealthy man, before dying; Charles leaves immediately after the wedding. Mary befriends Ann, a local girl, and helps her family, but Ann does not return Mary’s romantic feelings. Ann falls ill; Mary helps her move to Lisbon, where they meet Henry, who is also ill. After Ann's death, Mary and Henry fall in love but are separated. Mary cares for Henry until his death from consumption. Charles returns from Europe, but Mary is emotionally and physically deteriorating. The novel ends with Mary’s failing health, implying she will die young.

Original Stories from Real Life; with Conversations Calculated to Regulate the Affections, and Form the Mind to Truth and Goodness (1788): her only complete children's literature work. The book starts with a frame story about the education of two young girls by Mrs. Mason, their maternal teacher. It includes a series of didactic tales. The two most influential pedagogical works of 18th-century Europe were John Locke's Some Thoughts Concerning Education (1693) and Jean-Jacques Rousseau's Emile. Wollstonecraft responds to Locke and Rousseau, presenting her own pedagogical theory in Original Stories and other educational works.

A Vindication of the Rights of Men, in a Letter to the Right Honourable Edmund Burke; Occasioned by His Reflections on the Revolution in France (1790): a political pamphlet. Response to Edmund Burke’s Reflections on the Revolution in France (1790), defending constitutional monarchy, aristocracy, and the Church of England. Wollstonecraft attacks aristocracy, hereditary privilege and Burke’s rhetoric. Critiques Burke's defense of women’s passivity and its link to social inequality. Uses Burke's own rhetorical terms, "sublime" and "beautiful," to argue against his position. Argues for republican virtue, middle-class ethos and Enlightenment ideals over tradition. Describes an ideal society with self-sufficient farms, contrasting it with Burke’s "theatrical" society. Reviews initially focused on Wollstonecraft’s gender, contrasting her “passion” with Burke’s “reason.”

A Vindication of the Rights of Woman: with Strictures on Political and Moral Subjects (1792): early feminist philosophy. Response to Talleyrand-Périgord’s 1791 report advocating domestic education for women. Responds to 18th-century theorists denying women rational education. Women need education equal to societal roles; vital as educators of children, companions to husbands. Rejects view of women as ornaments or property; asserts women's humanity and equal rights. Blames men for fostering emotional excess in women; condemns double standards. Hastily written due to events; planned second volume unfinished due to her death. Well-received in 1792; praised as original by biographer Emily W. Sunstein: "perhaps the most original book of [Wollstonecraft's] century". Influenced 19th-century women’s rights, notably the 1848 Seneca Falls Convention and Declaration of Sentiments.

Letters Written During a Short Residence in Sweden, Norway, and Denmark (1796): her travel narrative. 25 letters covering sociology of Scandinavia, its peoples and identity. Published by Joseph Johnson, Wollstonecraft's lifetime publisher. Last work released during her lifetime.

POSTHUMOUS PUBLICATIONS: “The Cave of Fancy” (1798); “Letter on the Present Character of the French Nation” (1798); “Fragment of Letters on the Management of Infants” (1798); “Lessons” (1798); “Hints” (1798)

Adam Smith (1723 – 1790)

Scottish economist and philosopher. Key figure in political economy and Scottish Enlightenment. Known as "Father of Economics" or "Father of Capitalism." Wrote The Theory of Moral Sentiments (1759) and The Wealth of Nations (1776). Explained wealth distribution through natural, social and economic factors, not divine will. Introduced the concept of absolute advantage in economics. Critiqued mercantilism, advocating classical free-market theory. Works and style were controversial, satirized by writers like Horace Walpole. Smith destroyed most manuscripts shortly before death. Planned two treatises: one on law/history, one on sciences/arts.

Essays on Philosophical Subjects (1795) includes history of astronomy, ancient physics/metaphysics, likely part of second treatise. Lectures on Jurisprudence: early lecture notes, draft of The Wealth of Nations. Other posthumous works: Lectures on Justice, Police, Revenue, and Arms (1763) (first published in 1896).

WORKS:

The Theory of Moral Sentiments (1759): Basis for The Wealth of Nations (1776), Essays on Philosophical Subjects (1795) and Lectures on Justice, Police, Revenue, and Arms (1763, pub. 1896).  Diverged from "moral sense" ideas of Shaftesbury, Hutcheson, Hume. Central concept: sympathy replaces moral sense. Sympathy = understanding/feeling others' passions via imaginative mirroring. People naturally care for others' well-being, finding pleasure in their happiness. Defined as "fellow-feeling with any passion whatsoever" (p. 5). Sympathy arises when: We see others' fortune/misfortune firsthand. Fortune/misfortune vividly depicted.

An Inquiry into the Nature and Causes of the Wealth of Nations (1776): generally referred to by its shortened title The Wealth of Nations. Foundational work in classical economics. First comprehensive system of political economy. Explores wealth-building in nations during Industrial Revolution. Key topics: division of labour, productivity, free markets.

Edward Gibbon (1737 – 1794)

English historian, essayist, and politician. His major work: The History of the Decline and Fall of the Roman Empire (6 volumes, 1776-1789). Gibbon described himself as "a puny child, neglected by my Mother, starved by my nurse". First historical narrative: History of Switzerland (unfinished, unpublished). Second work: Memoires Litteraires de la Grande Bretagne (two volumes on English literature and society). Known as a son of the Enlightenment, his view on the Middle Ages: "the triumph of barbarism and religion." Praised for his style, epigrams and irony.

Winston Churchill admired Gibbon's Decline and Fall for its captivating story and style: “I devoured Gibbon. I rode triumphantly through it from end to end and enjoyed it all”.

The History of the Decline and Fall of the Roman Empire (1776-1789): sometimes shortened to Decline and Fall of the Roman Empire is a six-volume work by historian Edward Gibbon.

Covers: Roman Empire peak (98-1590), rise of Christianity, fall of Western Roman Empire, Genghis Khan, Tamerlane, Byzantium's fall and Ancient Rome's ruins. Volume I published in 1776, Volumes II & III in 1781, Volumes IV-VI in 1788-1789. Original volumes printed in quarto sections.

Initially focused on Rome's decline, later expanded to the entire Roman Empire. Gibbon’s autobiography, Memoirs of My Life and Writings, reflects on the work's importance to his life. Gibbon compared the publication of each volume to the birth of a child.

 Influenced by Montesquieu’s Considerations on the Causes of the Greatness of the Romans and their Decline (1734), Voltaire’s Essay on Universal History (1756) and Bossuet’s Discourse on Universal History (1681).
