Percy Bysshe Shelley (1792 to 1892)

 Harold Bloom describes him as "a superb craftsman, a lyric poet without rival, and surely one of the most advance d sceptical intellects ever to write a poem." He went into permanent self-exile in Italy in 1818. Elizabeth Hitchener, a 28-year-old unmarried schoolteacher of advanced views he considered to be "sister of my soul" and "my second self". Already married to hariet, he eloped with Mary Godwin in 1814 to Europe. He was one of the "Pisan circle" whose members were  Byron, Thomas Medwin, Edward Williams and Edward Trelawny. He died in a boating accident, his body found 10 days later with a copy of Keats's Lamia in a jacket pocket. His epitaph is Latin inscription Cor Cordium (Heart of Hearts), and a few lines of "Ariel's Song"

 Shelley was bullied at  Eton College (which came to be known as "Shelley-baits"). For his eccesntric experiments with gunpowder and acid he was seen as  "Mad Shelley".

Zastrozzi: A Romance (1810) gothic novel published anonymously as "by P.B.S." Shelley's first published prose work. Epigraph taken from Paradise Lost (1667),  Book II. Pietro Zastrozzi, an outlaw, and his two servants, Bernardo and Ugo, disguised in masks, abduct Verezzi from the inn near Munich where he lives and take him to a cavern hideout. He is chained; but escapes, given refuge by an elderly lady Claudine. Later, saves Matilda from jumping off of a bridge who gives her refuge in her castle. She seduces her; fails as he loves Julia. Zastrozzi spreads a false rumour that Julia has died; later Verezzi belives it and relents to marry her. Later the truth being revealed, distressed at his betrayal he kills himself.  Matilda kills Julia in retaliation.  Zastrozzi and Matilda are arrested for murder. She repents. Zastrozzi reveals that Verezzi's father had deserted his mother, Olivia, who died young, destitute, and in poverty. He had killed his father and vowed revenge to  "his progeny for ever", his son Verezzi. His objective was to achieve the eternal damnation of Verezzi's soul. An atheist himself, he embraces execution "with a wild convulsive laugh of exulting revenge".  Patrick Bridgwater, in Kafka, Gothic and Fairytale (2003), argued that the novella anticipated Franz Kafka's work in the twentieth century.

St. Irvyne; or, The Rosicrucian: A Romance (1810) gothic novel published as "by a Gentleman of the University of Oxford". Epigraph for chapter three is from Paradise Lost (1667) by John Milton, Book II. Wolfstein is a wanderer in the Swiss Alps who seeks cover from the storm. A social outcast he means to kill himself. He is saved from a group of monks which is attacked by bandits and Wolfstein is taken by them. There he meets Megalena whose father has been killed by the bandits. With her, he escapes them. He befriends one of the bandits Ginotti and falls for Megalena who later in jealousy asks him to kill Olympia as she tried to seduce him (though he fails to kill her ). Ginotti  is a member of the Rosicrucian, or Rose Cross, Order. He is an alchemist who seeks the secret of immortality. He tells Wolf. if he renounces his faith and join his cult, he will reveal the secret. Eloise de St. Irvyne is the sister of Wolfstein who lives in Geneva, Switzerland. Ginotti tells Wolf. to meet him there is her Abbey in France where he will reveal the secret. Later, he doesn not consent to renouncing his faith and both are struck by lightning for tampering with forces unknown.

The story was a success and two chapbooks were published:  Wolfstein; or, The Mysterious Bandit;  Wolfstein, The Murderer; or, The Secrets of a Robber's Cave: A Terrific Romance.

The Necessity of Atheism (with T. J. Hogg) (1811) pamphlet, Shelley made a number of claims in Necessity, including that one's beliefs are involuntary, and, therefore, that atheists do not choose to be so and should not be persecuted. Poetical Essay on the Existing State of Things (1811)  poem. The poem opens with a description of carnage and ruin on the battlefield: "Destruction marks thee! o'er the blood-stain'd heath,/Is faintly borne the stifled wail of death." The French Revolution and the Napoleonic Wars had resulted in turmoil in Great Britain. Shelley attacks British imperialism and colonialism in India: “The fainting Indian, on his native plains,/Writhes to superior power’s unnumbered pains.” "Though hot with gore from India's wasted plains,/Some Chief, in triumph, guides the tightened reins."  Security can only be found in laws. In conclusion, Shelley asserts that "peace, love, and accord, once shall rule again" and "error's night turned to virtue's day."

 

An Address, to the Irish People; Proposals for an Association of Philanthropists; and Declaration of Rights (1812) preaches non-violence.

"The Devil's Walk: A Ballad" (1812) consisted of seven irregular ballad stanzas of 49 lines. It was a satirical attack and criticism of the British government. Satan is depicted meeting with key members of the British government. It is a continuation of "The Devil's Thoughts" of 1799 by Samuel Taylor Coleridge and continued by Robert Southey. The devil, Beelzebub, is seen as “an agriculturist” in Sunday clothes depicting (and conversing) with people of power.

Queen Mab; A Philosophical Poem; With Notes (1813) in nine cantos with seventeen notes revised as The Daemon of the World (1816). It is his first major poem. Applies his theory of revolution based on  William Godwin's idea of "necessity and his owb idea of ever-changing nature to arrive at the conclusion that perfect society not obtained by violent revolution but incrementally as people modified. Ended up becoming a "bible" for Chartism. Some libelous verses omitted from Mary Shelley’s Poetical Works in 1839.

In the form of a fairytale. Queen Mab descends and sees Lanthe sleeping. Takes her soul on a ride to see Utopian version of earth. Queen Mab interprets, analyses, and explains Ianthe's dreams. She shows her visions of the past, present, and the future. The past and present are characterised by oppression, injustice, misery, and suffering caused by monarchies, commerce, and religion. In the future, however, the condition of man will be improved and a utopia will emerge. Her soul is given back and  Ianthe then awakens with a "gentle start". The theme of the work is the perfectibility of man by moral means. Ahasuerus the "Wandering Jew" appears in Queen Mab as a phantom, but as a hermit healer in Shelley's last major work, the verse drama Hellas.

Alastor, or The Spirit of Solitude and Other Poems(1816) Thomas Love Peacock suggested the title Alastor as "evil genius". The epigraph to the poem is from St. Augustine's Confessions, III, I: "I was not yet in love, and I loved to be in love, I sought what I might love, in love with loving." It recounts the life of a Poet who zealously pursues the most obscure part of nature in search of "strange truths in undiscovered lands", journeying to the Caucasus Mountains ("the ethereal cliffs of Caucasus"), Persia, "Arabie", Cashmire, and "the wild Carmanian waste". He rejects an "Arab maiden" in his search for an idealised embodiment of a woman as he dreams of a "veiled maid" who becomes symbol of supernatural. He wants to unite nature with this supernatural he tasted in the dream. The night's blackness swallows the vision and severs his dreamy link to the supernatural. Once touched by the maddening hand of the supernatural, the Poet restlessly searches for a reconciliation with his lost vision. In frustration, thoughts of death as the possible next step. Poet notices a small boat ("little shallop") floating down a nearby river. Rows and goes deeper into the nature. Like the water's surface supports the boat, the supernatural world "cradles" the mutability both of nature and of man. His imagination helps him sense the spirit's supernatural presence. The boat flows onward to an "immeasurable void" and the Poet finds himself ready to sink into the supernatural world and break through the threshold into death.  He reaches the "obscurest chasm," his last sight is of the moon. He has attained transcendence to the supernatural world. The journey to the very source of nature led, finally, to an immanence within nature's very structure and to a world free of decay and change.

Mont Blanc: Lines Written in the Vale of Chamouni (1817) influenced by their tour;  Mary Shelley's History of a Six Weeks' Tour through a Part of France, Switzerland, Germany and Holland was her response. Shelley compares the power of the mountain against the power of the human imagination. Although he emphasised the ability of the human imagination to uncover truth through a study of nature, he questions the notion of religious certainty. The poet concludes that only a privileged few can see nature as it really is, and are able to express its benevolence and malevolence through the device of poetry. Shelley's response to William Wordsworth's Tintern Abbey and as a "defiant reaction" against the "religious certainties" of Samuel Taylor Coleridge's "Hymn before Sun-rise, in the Vale of Chamouni",[13] which "credits God for the sublime wonders of the landscape".  By listening to the mountain, one can learn that nature can be both benevolent and malevolent

The Revolt of Islam (1818) in twelve cantos dedicated to  Mary Shelley, a revised version of  Laon and Cythna; or, The Revolution of the Golden City: A Vision of the Nineteenth Century (1817). It is written in Spenserian stanzas:  eight lines in iambic pentameter followed by a single Alexandrine line in iambic hexameter. The plot centres on two characters named Laon and Cythna, inhabitants of Argolis under Ottoman rule who initiate a revolution against its despotic ruler. The work is a symbolic parable on liberation and revolutionary idealism following the disillusionment of the French Revolution. In the Preface, he explains: “ It is an experiment on the temper of the public mind, as to how far a thirst for a happier condition of moral and political society survives, among the enlightened and refined, the tempests which have shaken the age in which we live.” The poet sees a fight of eagle and snake on a mountain and an elderly woman resuces the snake and the poet. Laon and Cythna live in daydreams of delight. But soon attacked by Ottomon army and Cynthia is taken while Laon imprisone to be freed later by a hermit and then taken to an island where he lives with an old man. Later the old man tells him of the revolt in  "Golden City" and the fall of Ottomon tyrant. He joins. Later the revolut is subdued and then Cynthia and Laon are burnt alive on stake. Finally, Laon and Cythna undergo a miraculous transformation. In the final scenes, their spiritual odyssey of transmogrification is recounted.

Prometheus Unbound, A Lyrical Drama in Four Acts, With Other Poems (1820)

Prometheus: closet drama. Recounts the torments of Greek mythological figure Prometheus, who defies the gods and gives fire to humanity, for which he is subjected to eternal punishment and suffering at the hands of Zeus.  It is inspired by the classical Prometheia, a trilogy of plays attributed to Aeschylus. However, unlike Aeschylus' version, there is no reconciliation between Prometheus and Jupiter (Zeus). Instead, Jupiter is abandoned by his supportive elements and falls from power, which allows Prometheus to be released. In the preface, he declares how principles of moral conducts are mere seeds strewn on a highway and trampled by travellers unless the mind is ready to love, hope, endure etc. earth is triumphant and chants ‘Man, oh, not men! A chain of linked thought, / Of love and might to be divided not” when it sees overthrow of Jupiter and Prometheus’s win. Demogorgon concludes with moralising abstraction: “...to hope, till Hope creates / From its own wreck the thing it contemplates;”

6 Great Odes Written 1819

1. "Ode to Psyche" it is important because it is Keats's first attempt at an altered sonnet form that would include longer more lines and would end with a message or truth. The original myth is that Aphrodite punishes Psyche, an admirable girl, by asking Cupid to make her fall for him. He instead falls for her and goes to meet her in dark so he could hide identity. She later seeks him everywhere and has to do tasks. Finally Zeus asks Aphrodite to transform her into a goddess so she could be with her love. But it has nothing to do with the myth (Bloom). The poem begins with a narrator witnessing two individuals embracing in a forest: “ But who wast thou, O happy, happy dove? / His Psyche true!” He finds Psyche to be better than other worshipped goddesses and resolves to be her priest; “I see, and sing, by my own eyes inspired.” Now he will open his mind to “let the warm love in”. Leigh Hunt declared that "When Mr Keats errs in his poetry, it is from the ill management of the good things,--exuberance of ideas. Once or twice, he does so in a taste positively bad, like Marino or Cowley, as in a line in his 'Ode to Psyche'... but it is once or twice only, in his present volume."

2. "Ode on a Grecian Urn". "Thou still unravished bride of quietness!" Has 2 scenes: lover’s pursuit of beloved and the villagers gathering for sacrifice.

3. "Ode on Indolence" (epigraph taken from Matthew 6:28) contemplates lazy morning. 3 figures Ambition, Love and Poesy are present and are questioned about life and art. The poet wishes to be with the three figures, but he is unable to join them. The poem transitions into the narrator providing reasons why he would not need the three figures and does so with ambition and love, but he cannot find a reason to dismiss poesy. All of them are rejected.

4. "Ode on Melancholy" the shortest of the odes with three stanzas. Opening: “No, no, go not to Lethe, neither twist”. Negative capability appears subtly in it.

5. "Ode to a Nightingale" the longest with 8 stanzas, a personal poem which describes Keats' journey into the state of negative capability. The nightingale described experiences a type of death but does not actually die. Instead, the songbird is capable of living through its song, which is a fate that humans cannot expect. The poem ends with an acceptance that pleasure cannot last and that death is an inevitable part of life. He sees himself dead—as a "sod" over which the nightingale sings. Opening: “My heart aches, and a drowsy numbness pains / My sense, as though of hemlock I had drunk,”. Rudyard Kipling referred to lines 69 and 70, alongside three lines from Samuel Taylor Coleridge's Kubla Khan, when he claimed of poetry: "In all the millions permitted there are no more than five—five little lines—of which one can say, 'These are the magic. These are the vision. The rest is only Poetry.'"

6. "To Autumn" autumn is both a force of growth and maturation, and deals with the theme of approaching death.Begins: “Season of mists and mellow fruitfulness, / Close bosom-friend of the maturing sun;”

"La Belle Dame sans Merci" (written 1819) "The Beautiful Lady without Mercy.” A ballad, title of a 15th-century poem by Alain Chartier called La Belle Dame sans Mercy. The poem is about a fairy who condemns a knight to an unpleasant fate after she seduces him with her eyes and singing. The fairy inspired several artists to paint images that became early examples of 19th-century femme fatale iconography. Opens: “O what can ail thee, knight-at-arms, / Alone and palely loitering?” The poem is mentioned in the story entitled "The Adventure of the Three Gables" from the 1927 book The Case-Book of Sherlock Holmes by Sir Arthur Conan Doyle. In Agatha Christie's 1936 mystery novel Murder in Mesopotamia, the plot is centered upon an unusual woman named Louise Leidner who is described multiple times as "a kind of Belle Dame sin Merci". Vladimir Nabokov's books The Real Life of Sebastian Knight (1941), Lolita (1955) and Pale Fire (1962) allude to the poem. Pale Kings and Princes, a 1987 Spenser novel by Robert B. Parker, takes its title from the poem. David Foster Wallace's 2011 novel The Pale King alludes to the poem in its title.

The relationship between Lycius and Lamia, however, is destroyed when the sage Apollonius reveals Lamia's true identity at their wedding feast, whereupon she seemingly disappears and Lycius dies of grief. Hyperion, a Fragment an abandoned epic poem based on the Titanomachia, and tells of the despair of the Titans after their fall to the Olympians. It stops abruptly in the middle of the third book. He gave it up as having "too many Miltonic inversions." The poem opens with Saturn bemoaning the loss of his power, which is being overtaken by Jupiter. Thea leads him to a place where the other Titans sit, similarly miserable, and they discuss whether they should fight back against their conquest by the new gods. Oceanus declares that he is willing to surrender his power to Neptune as he’s more beautiful; Clymene describes first hearing the music of Apollo, which she found beautiful to the point of pain. Finally, Enceladus makes a speech encouraging the Titans to fight. Hyperion is the only Titan who is still powerful. The scene changes to Apollo (the new sun god, also god of music, civilisation and culture) weeping on the beach. Here Mnemosyne (goddess of memory) encounters him and he explains to her the cause of his tears: he is aware of his divine potential, but as yet unable to fulfill it. By looking into Mnemosyne's eyes he receives knowledge which transforms him fully into a god. The poem as usually printed breaks off at this point, in mid-line, with the word "celestial". Keats's friend Richard Woodhouse, transcribing this poem, completed this line as "Celestial Glory dawn'd: he was a god!" Opening by Hyperion: “Saturn is fallen, am I too to fall? / Am I to leave this haven of my rest,” ***

The Fall of Hyperion: A Dream (1856) abandoned before Keats's death. Opens with narrative about the possibility of the poem being a dream or a vision. The poem is divided into three scenes before its final fragmentation. After enjoying the sensory delight, he is compelled to partake of a "cool vessel of transparent juice" that causes him to fall into a deep sleep. He opens his eyes and finds himself before a temple, with the gates to the East (the same direction as the gates of Eden) shut. Moneta, a mysterious figure challenges him to climb upon stairs, which he experiences a painful death that is reminiscent of Apollo's pain when "dying into life" in Hyperion. Once the poet narrator makes it up the steps, he is questioned thoroughly by Moneta on the nature of poetry, on visions, and what one must do with their life, which reflects the second part of Sleep and Poetry, where the narrator has to condemn the false poets in each. Once the poet has passed the test, Moneta allows the poet to witness a vision of the Titans and of Hyperion. This scene ends with the image of Hyperion rising, which leads to the beginning of the previous fragment, Hyperion.

In it also: “Darkness” theme of an apocalyptic end of the world as 1816 was known as the Year Without a Summer since  Mount Tambora  has erupted and there was sulfur in the sky. It is seen as "last man" poem (last man living).

"Oriental Tales" or "Heroic Poems"

The Giaour: A Fragment of a Turkish Tale (1813) an offensive Turkish word for infidel "kafir". It’s first in the series of Byron's Oriental romances. Byron's only fragmentary narrative poem. The story with three narrators giving their individual points of view about the series of events. The main story tells of a member of Hassan's harem, Leila, who loves the giaour and is killed by being drowned in the sea by Hassan. In revenge, the giaour kills Hassan and then enters a monastery due to his remorse. It reflects the gloom, remorse, and lust of two illicit love affairs, one with his half-sister Augusta Leigh and the other with Lady Frances Webster. Leila is seen as a personification of Greece, for the sake of which there was a war between the Ottoman Empire and Russia. Poe’s "Tamerlane" emulates both the manner and style of The Giaour.

The Bride of Abydos (1813) (Turkish tale) in 2 cantos, tale of Selim and his half-sister Zuleika’s love (vs. his for Augusta Leigh). Opens with Pasha, his father, refusing Selim a key to the royal harem as he declares his wish to marry his half-sister. She is forbidden to marry Selim. He, in turn, decries Giaffir's judgment as well and vows vengeance. Later, Selim is dressed as a dashing pirate and declares that Zuleika is not his sister. Reveals to Zuleika Giaffir had killed Abdallah, Selim's father and Giaffir's brother. Knew his identity from one of his father's loyal servants, Haroun. He became a pirate so that he could gather a posse for revenge, and asserts his lust for Giaffir's blood. He is later seen dying on the beach, the fatal blow administered by Giaffir himself. The second canto thus ends with Zuleika dying of sorrow for Selim, while Giaffir is forced to live out the rest of his life in solitude.

The Corsair (1814) extremely popular, selling ten thousand copies on its first day of sale. It was dedicated to Irish poet Thomas Moore. Relates the story of the corsair or privateer Conrad in his youth he was rejected by society because of his acts and his later war against humanity (excepting women). Byronic hero emerges, "that man of loneliness and mystery" who perceives himself a "villain", an anti-hero. Conrad plans to attack the Pacha Seyd and seize his possessions. Attack is successful but he tries to save harem which becomes his doom as pasha’s forces gain ground and later imprison him. Gulnare vows to help him for his generosity for women. Gulnare initiates the escape plan by trying to trick Seyd into freeing Conrad. Fails. She asks Conrad to kill him and procures a knife but he refuses. Gulnare therefore kills the pacha herself. She and Conrad escape, successfully reaching his island. Conrad learns that his wife, Medora, believing Conrad had died in Seyd's counterattack, has died of grief. In the final scene, Conrad departs from the island alone, without Gulnare: "He left a Corsair's name to other times,/ Linked with one virtue and a thousand crimes."

Lara, A Tale (1814) in 2 cantos, rhymed tragic narrative poem published anonymously in conjunction with Samuel Rogers' Jacqueline. Byron explains that even though Lara is Spanish name, it doesn’t belong to any country. Count Lara returns home after spending years abroad traveling the orient accompanied solely by an oriental page, Kaled, who is silently devoted to him. Living his youth as dissolute, he returns altered, —reserved, haughty, and apparently palled with pleasure and fame. In the night household is awakened by a shriek coming from his room and he is seen prostrating and Kaled revives him. Later, count Otho invites him to dinner where he meets Sir Ezzelin, Otho’s relative who claims to know about Lara’s criminal activities in foreign lands and thus Otho tells them to meet the next day before dignitaries to resolve it. The next day Sir Ezzelin doesn’t come and Otho fights him and Lara wins. Lara has made an army and decides to go to border and to other country. They are intercepted and killed. Kaled is later revealed to be a woman. And in PS it is revealed an unknown horseman threw Sir Ezzelin’s body in the river that night.

The Siege of Corinth (1816) tragic narrative. Published with Parisina. It was inspired by the Ottoman massacre of the Venetian garrison holding the Acrocorinth in 1715. Byron recounts the final, desperate resistance of the Venetians on the day the Ottoman army stormed Acrocorinth: revealing the closing scenes of the conflict through the eyes of Alp (a Venetian renegade fighting for the Ottomans) and Francesca (the beautiful maiden daughter of the governor of the Venetian garrison: Minotti). They both are narrators. Minotti doesn’t let Alp (originally called Lanciotto) marry Francesa, his daughter. He resolves to fight under the Turkish flag and repudiates both his nationality and his religion, as well as his old name 'Lanciotto', only to be challenged by Francesca herself the night before the final assault to repent his apostasy, to forgive his accusers, and to save the Venetian garrison from certain slaughter. His moral dilemma to choose the side forms the climax.

Parisina (1816) based on a story related by Edward Gibbon in his Miscellaneous Works (1796) about Niccolò III d'Este, one of the dukes of Ferrara in the 15th century finds his second wife Parisina Malatesta has incestual rel. with his bastard son Ugo and both are put to death. In Byron’s version Azo (Niccolo) marries Parisina who was engaged to Hugo (Ugo). She mutters his name in sleep and he sentences him (not her) to be executed and she is to witness the same. She utters a shriek which indicates her approaching madness and Azo repents his decision. ***

Manfred: A dramatic poem (1817) a closet drama, or "metaphysical drama", as he called it. The epigraph taken from Hamlet: “There are more things in heaven and earth, Horatio, than are dreamt of in your philosophy." Manfred is a Faustian noble living in the Bernese Alps who feels some mysterious guilt, which has to do with the death of his most beloved, Astarte, he uses his mastery of language and spell-casting to summon seven spirits, from whom he seeks forgetfulness. Yet they cannot grant Manfred's plea. At the end, Manfred dies, defying religious temptations of redemption from sin. Manfred directs his final words to the Abbot, remarking, "Old man! 'tis not so difficult to die". "The unconquerable individual to the end, Manfred gives his soul to neither heaven nor hell, only to death."

Beppo: A Venetian Story (1817) in ottava rima,  tells the story of a Venetian lady, Laura, whose husband, Giuseppe (or "Beppo" for short), has been lost at sea for the past three years. According to Venetian customs she takes on a Cavalier Servente, simply called "the Count". When the two of them attend the Venetian Carnival, she is closely observed by a Turk who turns out to be her missing husband. Beppo explains that he has been captured and enslaved, and was freed by a band of pirates that he subsequently joined. Having accumulated enough money he left piracy and returned to reclaim his wife and be re-baptized. Laura rejoins Beppo and befriends the Count.  Robert Southey appears as "Botherby".

Mazeppa (1819)

Don Juan (Cantos I-II in 1819, III-V in 1821, VI-XIV 1823, XV-XVI in 1824, Canto XVII unfinished ) 16 cantos in ottava rima, eight iambic pentameters, with the couplet rhyme scheme of AB AB AB CC and the final rhyming couplet introduces anticlimax by transitioning from lofty style of writing to a vulgar. Spanish folk legend of Don Juan, not as a womaniser as historically portrayed, but as a victim easily seduced by women. Ironic dedication to Southey.  

Don Juan lives in Seville; affair with mother’s friend, 23-yeary-old Donna Julia and her husband catches him, send to Cadiz accompanied by Pedrillo, a tutor, and servants. Shipwrecked in Aegean sea; pirates catch him. He falls for Haidee, daughter of the pirate. Later, he’s sold to slavery in Constantinople. "The Isles of Greece", a digression section of canto III starts here. Also insult his literary rivals, the Lake Poets, specifically William Wordsworth , Robert Southey, and Samuel Taylor Coleridge: “Thou shalt believe in Milton, Dryden, Pope; / Thou shalt not set up Wordsworth, Coleridge, Southey…”

Sultana (Gulbayez) sees him and buys him, disguised as girl and he enters harem and tries to seduce him: “"The prisoned eagle will not pair, nor I / Serve a sultana's sensual phantasy." Juan says: "in the East, they are extremely strict, / And Wedlock and a Padlock mean the same". Barely escapes the jealous husband. Enlists as Russian army soldier and proves himself in the Russian siege upon the Turks to conquer the fort of Izmail. He saves a Muslim girl (Leila)  from two Christian Cossacks who try to rape her and Catherine the Great, adds him to royal court. There he grows ill because of climate and sent to England. His adventures with British aristocracy begin. He is sent with the entourage. Kills a cockney robber is self-defense. The he describes his visit to the house of Lady Adeline Amundeville and her husband, Lord Henry Amundeville. He possibly has an affair with her. He is soon attracted to Aurora Raby, a catholic. Later, he dreams of  "Black Friar" pacing the hallway at night his face covered with hood. He is scared beyond imagination. Next night too, but he realizes it wasn’t dream. He pulls the hood and it’s revealed to be Duchess of Fitz-Fulke. The last canto ends in a fragment of literary mentions.

Cain (1821) closet drama which dramatizes the story of Cain and Abel from Cain's point of view. Cain tells his father he has nothing to thank God for because he is fated to die and refuses to participate in thanksgiving. Complicated further by the fact that he does not know what death is. Lucifer provides him that knowledge by taking him on a space tour where he shows him vision of universal death. Cain murders Abel. The play concludes with Cain's banishment.

The Prophecy of Dante (1821) tale in verse dedicated it to the Countess Guiccioli.

Irish Avatar (1821)  pamphlet on the so-called "Irish Question". On January 1, 1801, under the Acts of Union, the kingdoms of Great Britain and Ireland were merged into the United Kingdom;  Irish parliament was abolished. Influenced by  Lines on the Entry of the Austrians into Naples (1821)  Thomas Moore. After the royal visit by King George IV to Ireland, criticised the attitudes displayed by the Irish people towards the Crown (of welcoming the oppressor). He was dismayed by the positive reception George IV received during his visit. He voiced muted support towards Irish nationalism. He describes King George IV having been received by a 'legion of crooks, and an army of slaves.'

The Vision of Judgment (1822) in first edition "Quevedo Redivivus"  name used. A satirical poem in ottava rima in response to the Poet Laureate Robert Southey's A Vision of Judgement (1821) which saw soul of king George triumphantly entering Heaven to receive his due. He was provoked by the High Tory point of view from which the poem was written, and he took personally Southey's preface which had attacked those "Men of diseased hearts and depraved imaginations" who had set up a "Satanic school" of poetry, "characterized by a Satanic spirit of pride and audacious impiety". In his preface to this poem, he attacks "The gross flattery, the dull impudence, the renegado intolerance, and impious cant, of the poem", and mischievously referred to Southey as "the author of Wat Tyler". "Southey's reputation has never recovered from Byron's ridicule" (Geoffrey Carnall). It relates the dispute in Heaven over the fate of George III's soul. Due to Napoleonic war and carnage, Recording Angel is busy. When George III comes, witnesses are brought. Lucifer claims him. Southey is brought. Starts reading his Vision and angels and devils flee. St. Peter knocks him down. Falls back to earth and George III slips into heaven. Hunt was fined 100 pounds for libel against George IV. Goethe called it "Heavenly! Unsurpassable!" Swinburne wrote “But this poem which we have by us stands alone, not in Byron's work only, but in the work of the world.”
