Horace was the Augustan Hero whose golden mean dictates style and versification more concerned with order, elegance and gentlemanly. Poetry was concerned with general human condition and nature of humanity.

The town had defeated the court. Therefore literature as the only fallback was to entertain and EDUCATE since the courtly ideals were gone for good. Thus Addison and Steele come up as great educators of the time.

Richard Steele (1672-1729) and Thomas Addison (1672- 1719)

Steele invents, Addison refines it. Samuel Johnson says they “adjusted” daily intercourse via their politeness and propriety.

The Tatler (1709-1711) an essay periodical by Steele issued 3 times a week (Tuesday, Thursday, Sat.) (who is more erratic and impulsive than Addison) with Addison as his assistant—later Steele formed the club of eccentrics: the Tatler’s Trumpet Club. (Steele). Addison contributes 42/271 papers; 36 in colab.

Aim: “expose false arts of life”; says his aim is to instruct the politic persons who are zealous yet intellectually weak as to “what to think” as well as entertainment for the “fair sex.” Steele writes under pseudonym Isaac Bickerstaff.

The Spectator (March 1, 1711 to 1714): daily periodical in 7 volumes upto 1712 and thrice/week 1712 to 14 by Adison who was senior partner the first issue introduces Mr Spectator (figure) as belonging to squirarchy as the “spectator of mankind, than one of the species.”  Addison contributes 274/555 vs Steele 240/555 papers. Very popular: around 50 collected editions printed by 1800. Addison’s prose style was lucid and poised which left marks on the age, e.g. on Jane Austin. Johnson considers his prose to be “model of middle style.” He said whoever wants his discourse to be “familiar but not coarse,” “elegant but not ostentatious” must read his by day and night.

Another aim of Spectator was to bridge gap between country and town life opened by restoration. Thus, the character of Sir Roger de Coverley, old fashioned country gentleman, later seen as belonging to squirarchy, is introduced as a lovable Tory squire to amuse Whig audience. Says he supped with Lord Rocheter and Etherege and now likes the way of life of Sir Roger. Characters from other classes are also depicted.

The main aim of the essays was educational: “to enliven morality with wit and to temper wit with morality.” In the 10th Spectator Addison says his aim is to take people out of desperate vice and folly and to finally claim “I have brought philosophy out of closets and libraries, schools and colleges, to dwell in clubs and assemblies, at tea-tables and coffee-houses.”

Daniel Defoe (1660-1731) Father of English Novel:

Born Daniel Foe, added “De” to sound aristocratic. Came from Presbyterian dissenters family. Dissenting figure with several projects. He used at least 198 pen names. Became a merchant. He  joined the ill-fated Monmouth Rebellion but gained a pardon. Got debts as clnflict with France (of England). He died with little wealth and evidently embroiled in lawsuits with the royal treasury.

 545 titles (works) have been attributed to Defoe.

Essay on Projects (1697) his first work, a series of proposals for social and economic improvement and portrait of his time as a "Projecting Age". Somr projects include: Refinement of English language; Academy for women; roads; banks.

The Trueborn Englishman (1701), satirical poem, supports King William III’s throne against charges of xenophobia as he was Dutch-born, calling English to be of mixed stock: “a race uncertain and uneven,/ Derived from all the nations under heaven.” English race came from the Ancient Britons to Anglo-Saxons, Normans and beyond: “we are really all Foreigners our selves.”

Legion's Memorial (1701) he presented to Robert Harley, then Speaker of the House of Commons, demanded the release of the Kentish petitioners, who had asked Parliament to support the king in an imminent war against France. He was sentenced a fine, imprisonment and  public humiliation in a pillory. According to legend, the publication of his poem Hymn to the Pillory caused his audience at the pillory to throw flowers instead of the customary harmful and noxious objects and to drink to his health.  John Robert Moore later said that "no man in England but Defoe ever stood in the pillory and later rose to eminence among his fellow men". In exchange for release, agreed to be spy for Tories.

The Shortest Way with the Dissenters; Or, Proposals for the Establishment of the Church (1702) 29 pahe pamphlet, satirizes Anglican Tory, ironically suggests persecution. In it he ruthlessly satirised both the high church Tories and those Dissenters who hypocritically practised so-called "occasional conformity", such as his Stoke Newington neighbour Sir Thomas Abney. Imprisoned for it in 1703. prompted to write the pamphlet by the increased hostility towards Dissenters in the wake of the accession of Queen Anne to the throne. It is a satire which attacks handling of the issue by the Tory ministry. It opens with the fable of the Cock and the Horses. Later directly addresses the Dissenters ("You have butchered one King! deposed another King! and made a Mock King of a third!"). Ends in “Now let us crucify the Thieves.”

 The Storm (1704), which includes a collection of witness accounts of the tempest; regarded as one of the world's first examples of modern journalism it details the Great Storm of 1703, which raged through the night of 26/27 November. 

A Review of the Affairs of France (1704-13) his periodical (supported the Harley Ministry, Torries) chronicling the events of the War of the Spanish Succession (1702–1714), 3 times/week. When Harley was ousted from the ministry in 1708, Defoe continued writing the Review to support Godolphin. This was the main mouthpiece of the English Government promoting the Act of Union 1707.

 A True Relation of the Apparition of One Mrs. Veal the Next Day after her Death to One Mrs. Bargrave at Canterbury The 8th of September, 1705 (1706) a pamphlet, deals with the interaction between the spiritual realm and the physical realm and was most likely written in support of Charles Drelincourt's The Christian Defence against the Fears of Death (1651). It describes Mrs. Bargrave's encounter with her old friend Mrs. Veal after she had died.

The History of the Union of Great Britain (1709) gives details of the events leading up to the Acts of Union 1707, dating as far back as 6 December 1604, when King James I was presented with a proposal for unification.

Appeal to Honour and Justice (1715) comments on the tendency to attribute tracts of uncertain authorship to him.

The Family Instructor (1715), a conduct manual on religious duty

Minutes of the Negotiations of Monsr. Mesnager (1717), in which he impersonates Nicolas Mesnager, the French plenipotentiary who negotiated the Treaty of Utrecht (1713)

A Continuation of the Letters Writ by a Turkish Spy (1718), a satire of European politics and religion, ostensibly written by a Muslim in Paris.

Religious Courtship (1722),

Tour thro' the whole Island of Great Britain (1724-27) in 3 volumes, made use of his Scottish experience. Defoe's description of Glasgow (Glaschu) as a "Dear Green Place" has often been misquoted as a Gaelic translation for the town's name. His most popular and financially successful work.

Everybody's Business is Nobody's Business (1725) written under name under the nom-de-plume of Andrew Moreton.

The Political History of the Devil (1726) Defoe really did think of the Devil as a participant in world history. He spends some time discussing John Milton's Paradise Lost and explaining why he considers it inaccurate. His view is that of an 18th-century Presbyterian – he blames the Devil for the Crusades and sees him as close to Europe's Catholic powers. This expresses Defoe's anti-Catholicism. The book was banned by the Roman Catholic Church. The book is listed as one belonging to Mr. Tulliver and read by his daughter Maggie in George Eliot's The Mill on the Floss.

The Complete English Tradesman (1726)  tradesman in England in comparison to tradesmen internationally, arguing that the British system of trade is far superior. Defoe also implied that trade was the backbone of the British economy: "estate's a pond, but trade's a spring." Overall, Defoe demonstrated a high respect for tradesmen, being one himself. Trade, Defoe argues, is a much better catalyst for social and economic change than war. He wrote in the work that increased consumption, by laws of supply and demand, increases production and in turn raises wages for the poor therefore lifting part of British society further out of poverty.

Fiction

The Consolidator, or Memoirs of Sundry Transactions from the World in the Moon: Translated from the Lunar Language (1705) satirical novel that mixes fantasy, political satire, and social satire. The narrator travels to the Moon through the means of the titular "consolidator" – a chariot with two feathered winged creatures. The chariot has also been described as one of the earliest spaceships (or airships) in known fiction.

Robinson Crusoe (1719) gave birth to a genre, the Robinsonade, of prudential heroism. Not introspective but create the same order of man in nature. Prudence rather than heroism is key to his actions. It’s claimed to be second only to the Bible in its number of translations. The first edition credited the work's protagonist Robinson Crusoe (born Robinson Kreutznaer) as its author, leading many readers to believe he was a real person and that the book was a non-fiction travelogue. He is cast away and spends 28 years on a remote tropical desert island near the coasts of Venezuela and Trinidad, encountering cannibals, captives, and mutineers before being rescued. The story has been thought to be based on the life of Alexander Selkirk, a Scottish castaway who lived for four years on a Pacific island called "Más a Tierra" (now part of Chile) which was renamed Robinson Crusoe Island in 1966. Other possible sources for the narrative include Ibn Tufail's Hayy ibn Yaqdhan, a twelfth-century philosophical novel also set on a desert island, and translated from Arabic into Latin and English a number of times in the half-century preceding Defoe's novel. Some allege it is a contender for the first English novel, but Dale Spender's research proves otherwise in her 1986 book Mothers of the Novel: 100 Good Women Writers Before Jane Austen.

Crusoe sets sail from Kingston upon Hull, England, on a sea voyage in August 1651, against the wishes of his parents, who wanted him to pursue a career in law. Destroyed; goes for second adventure, enslaved by a Moor. Two years later, he escapes in a boat with a boy named Xury. Crusoe procures a plantation in Brazil. Years later, Crusoe joins an expedition to purchase slaves from Africa but the ship gets blown off course in a storm about forty miles out to sea and runs aground on the sandbar of an island off the Venezuelan coast in 1659. Crusoe is the sole human survivor. Crusoe discovers cannibals.  He plans to kill them for committing an abomination, but later realizes he has no right to do so, as the cannibals do not knowingly commit a crime. During the cannibals' next visit to the island, when a prisoner escapes, Crusoe helps him, naming his new companion "Friday" after the day of the week he appeared. Crusoe teaches Friday the English language and converts him to Christianity. After more cannibals arrive to partake in a feast, Crusoe and Friday kill most of them and save two prisoners (including Friday’s father). A plan is devised wherein the Spaniard would return to the mainland with Friday's father and bring back the others, build a ship, and sail to a Spanish port. However, an English ship appears aboard which the crew had a mutiny; Crusoe and the ship's captain strike a deal in which Crusoe helps the captain and the loyal sailors retake the ship. Later, the mutineers take up Crusoe's offer to remain on the island rather than being returned to England as prisoners to be hanged.  Crusoe leaves the island on 19 December 1686 and arrives in England on 11 June 1687.  He learns that his family believed him dead; as a result, he was left nothing in his father's will. In conclusion, he transports his wealth overland to England from Portugal to avoid travelling by sea.  Friday accompanies him and, en route, they endure one last adventure together as they fight off famished wolves while crossing the Pyrenees.

The Farther Adventures of Robinson Crusoe (1719) sequel, origibally titled The Farther Adventures of Robinson Crusoe; Being the Second and Last Part of His Life, And of the Strange Surprising Accounts of his Travels Round three Parts of the Globe. Crusoe's marriage in England and his wife’s death; desire to see "his island." Sets sail in 1694. They discovered that the English mutineers left on the island by Crusoe a decade earlier had been making trouble. Crusoe takes various steps to consolidate leadership on the island and assure the civility of the inhabitants, including leaving a quantity of needed supplies, setting up a sort of rule of law under an honour system and ensuring cohabitating couples are married. On the way to the mainland once again from Crusoe's island, the ship is attacked by the cannibals. Friday dies from three arrow shots during an attempt to negotiate, but the crew eventually wins the encounter without further serious casualty. They landed on Madagascar where one crew man takes a girl to tree; The natives hanged this person, so the crew massacred 32 persons and burned the houses of the native town. Crusoe opposed all these, therefore he was marooned, and settled at the Bay of Bengal for a long time. Then his travels to China, Russian (Siberia) and finally home. He arrived at London on 10 January 1705, having been gone from England ten years and nine months.

Although intended to be the last Crusoe tale, the novel is followed by a non-fiction book involving Crusoe by Defoe entitled Serious Reflections During the Life and Surprising Adventures of Robinson Crusoe: With his Vision of the Angelick World (1720) it is not a work of narrative fiction. Rather, it consists of a series of essays written in the voice of the character Robinson Crusoe. These essays touch on topics including solitude, religion, liberty, and epistemology of mind, showing the influence of Locke and Montaigne

Captain Singleton (1720) full title: The Life, Adventures and Piracies of the Famous Captain Singleton. It’s believed to have been partly inspired by the exploits of the late 17th century English pirate Henry Every. the Englishman, Singleton, stolen from a well-to-do family as a child and raised by Gypsies, eventually making his way to sea. Later, he is captured by Turkish pirates and subsequently rescued by Portuguese sailors who carry him to Lisbon. After a two-year stay there, he sails for Brazil and later the East Indies. The first half of the book concerns Singleton's crossing of Africa, the second half concerning his life as a pirate in the Indian Ocean and Arabian Sea. Defoe's description of piracy focuses for the most part on matters of economics and logistics, and Singleton's pirate behaves more like a merchant adventurer, perhaps Defoe's comment on the mercantilism of his day.

Memoirs of a Cavalier (1720) a  historical fiction with full title: Memoirs of a Cavalier; or A Military Journal of the Wars in Germany, and the Wars in England. From the Years 1632 to 1648. Written threescore years ago, by an English gentleman, who served first in the army of Gustavus Adolphus, the Glorious King of Sweden, till his death, and after that in the Royal Army of King Charles the First, from the beginning of the Rebellion to the end of the War. It’s set during the Thirty Years' War and the English Civil War. Nominal author is 'Colonel Andrew Newport', a Shropshire-born soldier taken to be  Andrew Newport (1622-1699) but disclaimed by others. Winston Churchill modeled his six-volume histories The World Crisis and The Second World War on Memoirs of a Cavalier.

A Journal of the Plague Year (1722) title: A Journal of the Plague Year: Being Observations or Memorials, Of the most Remarkable Occurrences, As well Publick as Private, which happened in London During the last Great Visitation In 1665. both as novel and as nonfiction; account of Great Plague of London in 1665 (Bubonic Plague), which is undersigned by the initials "H. F.", suggesting the author's uncle Henry Foe as its primary source. It is a historical account of the events based on extensive research and written as if by an eyewitness, even though Defoe was only about five years old when it occurred. The book is told somewhat chronologically, though without sections or chapter headings, and with frequent digressions and repetitions. In the book, Defoe goes to great pains to achieve an effect of verisimilitude, identifying specific neighbourhoods, streets, and even houses in which events took place. The book is often compared to the actual, contemporary accounts of the plague in the diary of Samuel Pepys. Defoe's account, which appears to include much research, is far more systematic and detailed than Pepys's first-person account. Michael D. O'Brien's 1999 novel Plague Journal, where the narrator and main character chooses the title to describe the theme of the book (jokingly referring to himself as a modern-day Defoe) and Norman Spinrad's 1995 Journals of the Plague Years, a satirical novel about a sexually transmitted viral disease that cannot be defeated by vaccines,[10] referencing how AIDS was in its earliest days known as "the gay plague".

Fiction or not? Edward Wedlake Brayley claims it is historical and non-fictious as he compares it with Loimologia by Dr. Nathaniel Hodges (1672), the diary of Samuel Pepys, and Thomas Vincent's God's Terrible Voice in the City by Plague and Fire (1667). As a romance – "one of the peculiar class of compositions which hovers between romance and history" as it was described by Sir Walter Scott. 

Colonel Jack (1722) picaresque novel can be considered as a crime fiction, full title: The History and Remarkable Life of the truly Honourable Col. Jacque, commonly call'd Col. Jack, who was Born a Gentleman, put 'Prentice to a Pick−Pocket, was Six and Twenty Years a Thief, and then Kidnapp'd to Virginia, Came back a Merchant; was Five times married to Four Whores; went into the Wars, behav'd bravely, got Preferment, was made Colonel of a Regiment, came over, and fled with the Chevalier, is still abroad compleating a Life of Wonders, and resolves to dye a General.

It follows an orphaned boy, Jack, from a life of poverty and crime to prosperity in the colonies, military and marital imbroglios, and religious conversion, driven by a problematic notion of becoming a "gentleman." An illegitimate son of a man, he is given to a nurse and his father tells her to inform him when he is old that he is gentleman. She has her son also named jack so she names her son  "Captain Jack", him  "Colonel Jack" and the other boy she calls "Major Jack". She later dies and three young boys, thrown into the outside world, turn to crime. They are tricked into boarddgin a ship to Virginia. There they are sold into servitude; impresses master and becomes a plantation owner himself. On return, his ship is captured by the French, and Jack is landed at Bordeaux, where he is exchanged for a French merchant held by the English. Returns, calling himself Colonel Jacque. Marries. His wife proves to be a spendthrift and adulteress, and the marriage ends in divorce. Again,  marriage with a calculating woman, who is again an adulteress. He chooses to resettle in Virginia, his new wife, Moggy, having died in the meantime. There Jack encounters his divorced wife, reduced to being a house-keeper on his plantation, with whom he is reconciled and remarries.  Jack's ship is captured by the Spanish, and he finds himself taken to Havana. The novel ends with Jack speaking of his intentions to travel to Cadiz, then from there to London, to be rejoined by his wife from Virginia.

Moll Flanders (1722) autobiography of a prostitute. Points towards Becky Sharp and Pamela.

The original printing did not have an author, as it was an apparent autobiography. The attribution of Moll Flanders to Defoe was made by bookseller Francis Noble in 1770, after Defoe's death in 1731. Moll's mother is a convict in Newgate Prison in London who is given a reprieve by "pleading her belly," a reference to the custom of postponing the executions of pregnant criminals. She’s  transported to British America, and Moll Flanders (not her birth name but pseudonym). There she’s servant to a good family; she is loved by both sons, the elder of whom convinces her to "act like they were married" in bed. Unwilling to marry her, he persuades her to marry his younger brother. After five years of marriage, she then is widowed, and then she gets into passing herself off as a fortuned widow to attract a man. First one, a "gentleman-tradesman" spendthrift husband goes bankrupt and flees to mainland Europe, leaving her. They had one child together, but "it was buried." Second to a aman who takes her to  Virginia Colony. She learns that her mother-in-law is actually her biological mother, which makes her husband her half-brother ans dissolves marriage. Then goes to live in Bath to seek a new husband. Meets a man in Bath whose wife is elsewhere confined due to insanity. Moll becoming something of a "kept woman" in Hammersmith, London. He, after a severe illness he repents, breaks off the arrangement, and commits to his wife. Moll, now 42, resorts to another beau, a bank clerk, who while still married to an adulterous wife (a "whore"). While waiting for the banker to divorce, she gets another guy:  marries a supposedly rich man who claims to own property in Ireland. They each quickly realise that they were both conned and manipulated by the acquaintance. So he discharges her. Moll marries the banker,  but feels for him as she is cheating him. They live in happiness for five years before he becomes bankrupt and dies of despair, the fate of their two children left unstated. Truly desperate now, Moll begins a career of artful thievery, which, by employing her wits, beauty, charm, and femininity, as well as hard-heartedness and wickedness, brings her the financial security she has always sought. She becomes well known among those "in the trade," and is given the name Moll Flanders. Moll is finally caught by two maids whilst trying to steal from a house. In Newgate she is led to her repentance. At the same time, she reunites with her soulmate, her "Lancashire husband", who is also jailed for his robberies. Set to be hanged, they somehow manage to be transported to the Colonies to avoid hanging, where they live happily together. Once in the colonies, Moll learns her mother has left her a plantation and that her own son (by her brother) is alive, as is her husband/brother. With the help of a Quaker, the two found a farm with 50 servants in Maryland. Meets her son from her brother who gives her her mother's inheritance and comes clean with her husband about her brother/husband who is dead now.

His intention was to reduce all literature to journalis; no imaginative understanding; journalist’s eye for details.

 Roxana: The Fortunate Mistress (1724) complete title: The Fortunate Mistress: Or, A History of the Life and Vast Variety of Fortunes of Mademoiselle de Beleau, Afterwards Called the Countess de Wintselsheim, in Germany, Being the Person known by the Name of the Lady Roxana, in the Time of King Charles II.

Born in France, grew to adolescence in England. At 15, marries; eight years of marriage, penniless with five children. The cruel sister-in-law will raise the five children, with the help of her kinder husband. Amy, Roxana's maid, despite no salary doesn’t leave. Mr. ——, her landlord, becomes benevolent and gives her everything. Amy, because she loves her mistress, offers to lie with the landlord in Roxana's place because of his generosity which Rox. rejects now. He convinces her but to be with him; has no child. Roxana sends Amy to him to beget a child. Amy does bear a child, which Roxana takes as her own to save the maid embarrassment. Two years later, Roxana has a daughter, who dies within six weeks. A year later, a son. Go to Paris  where he, who’s a jeweller, is murdered  although his case of jewels safe. She becomes mistress of a German prince who came to pay his respects to her following the jeweler's murder.  Later, Roxana and the Prince travel to Italy where he has business to attend to, and there they live together for two years. During this time she is gifted a Turkish slave who teaches her the Turkish language and Turkish customs, and a Turkish dress which will become central to her later character development. Then the Prince's wife (the Princess) become ill and dies and he now leaves mistress out of repentance. Returns to England. Has her wealth transported via a Dutch merchat but requests to see the jewel case first. The Jew recognized the jewels as being the ones which had been allegedly stolen from an English jeweller many years prior. He schemes to take it; but she evades the Jew with merchant’s help. Has a child with merchant but doesn’t want to marry. She becomes a great hostess in England where she becomes famous for her parties and the Turkish dress she wears and the Turkish dance the slave taught her. This exotic display earns her the name of Roxana (prior to this moment, Roxana is never named, we only know she is called Roxana through this incident, but that her true name is Susan, according to a comment she makes later about her daughter.) Then she even became mistress to the King. Her reputation as a mistress and a whore tires her, and she wishes to lead a simpler life. Roxana moves to the outskirts of London and takes board in a Quaker woman's house, with whom she quickly becomes friends. One day she comes across the Dutch merchant who had helped her return to England, and marriage is envisaged. She moves to Holland with the Dutch merchant where she becomes a countess to her great pleasure. However, her new life is threatened by the reappearance of her oldest daughter, Susan (which Roxana admits to be named after her, unveiling possibly her true name). Susan's motives to have her mother recognize her as her daughter are unclear. Nevertheless, Roxana feels threatened, and Amy proposes to murder her. The novel ends on ambiguity as to whether Amy actually kills Susan. Roxana only laments the crime that has tainted her life, strongly suggesting Susan was murdered for Roxana to retain her status and reputation. The text ends on an "unfinished" note, with Roxana living in wealth with her husband in Holland, but assuring the reader that events eventually bring her low and she repents for her actions and experiences a downturn in fortune.

Jonathan Swift (Irish) (1667-1745)  "Dean Swift”, Irish

 Dean of St Patrick's Cathedral, Dublin. Pseudonyms include: Lemuel Gulliver, Isaac Bickerstaff, M. B. Drapier. Esther Johnson, then eight years old, was his Stella. "Swiftian" satire:  deadpan, ironic writing style, particularly in A Modest Proposal. Secretary to William Temple (who die in 1699). He went then to find place in Chruch of England. He had Whig friends like Addison but later joined Torries in 1710 frustrated by being sidelined. He was against dissenters, Catholics and Papists. He was made the editor of editor of The Examiner (Tory newspaper) in 1714. After 1714,  a virtual exile, to live "like a rat in a hole" went to Ireland.  "Vanessa", he called Hester Vanhomrigh who followed him to Dublin as she fell for him.

“The Battle of Books” prolegomena to his A Tale of a Tub relates the ancient vs. Modern controversy. Sir William Temple published an answer to Bernard le Bovier de Fontenelle (French who favoured moderns as superior to ancients, a debate called "Quarrel of the Ancients and Moderns”) entitled Of Ancient and Modern Learning in 1690,  introduced two metaphors: modern man was just a dwarf standing upon the "shoulders of giants"; they possessed a clear view of nature, and modern man only reflected/refined their vision. In turn, Richard Bently and William Wotton responded and ridicule the book. Then Temple’s friends called  "Christ Church Wits," came defending him and this book is such.  It depicts a literal battle between books in the King's Library (housed in St James's Palace at the time of the writing), as ideas and authors struggle for supremacy. Portrays battle of books but makes victory ambiguous. He portrays the manuscript as having been damaged in places, thus leaving the end of the battle up to the reader. It’s parody alike Hudibras battle. The battle is interrupted by the interpolated allegory of the spider and the bee. As bee accidently crashes into spider’s nest and destroys it they argue. The bee is like the ancients and like authors: it gathers its materials from nature and sings its drone song in the fields. The spider is like the moderns and like critics: it kills the weak and then spins its web (books of criticism) from the taint of its own body digesting the viscera.

"The Mechanical Operation of the Spirit," illustrates the other theme: an inversion of the figurative and literal as a part of madness.

Tale is a prose parody divided into sections of "digression" and a "tale" of three brothers, each representing one of the main branches of western Christianity. The story of three brothers, Peter, Martin, Jack, representing sects of Christianity:  Peter (named for Saint Peter) stands in for the Roman Catholic Church; Jack (named for John Calvin) represents the various dissenting Protestant churches such as Baptists, Presbyterians, Quakers, Congregationalists and Anabaptists. The third brother, middle born and middle standing, is Martin (named for Martin Luther), whom Swift uses to represent the 'via media' of the Church of England that he supported. The brothers have inherited three wonderfully satisfactory coats (representing religious practice) by their father (representing God), and they have his will (representing the Bible) to guide them. They are not to alter the coats but that’s all they ever do. Serves as an apology for the Anglican church's refusal to alter its practice in accordance with Puritan demands and its continued resistance to ally with the Roman church. They brothers come across “flame-coloured satin” (doctrine of purgatory) lining and try to rummage their will to find a loophole. Then a scholar says I have found a codice to the will which is a part of it and I will annex it (apocrypha) to it and it justifies satin. Chapter X is the commonly anthologised "Digression Concerning the Original, the Use and Improvement of Madness in a Commonwealth": madness is vapours from lower body rising to brain. It is behind great inventions by single individuals as well as religious factions, parties and philisophies. After this chapter, title labels are intentionally mistaken: the labels for the sections are incorrect. Sections then called "Tale" are Digressions, and those called "Digression" are also Digressions. Swift writes the Tale through the pose of a Modern or New Man. Title refers to dissenter pulpit which was called tub: the ship (state) is under attack by the Leviathan (of Thomas Hobbes) and sailors throw away the tub to distract and save ship. There is the "persona theory," which holds that the Tale is not one parody, but rather a series of parodies, arising out of chamber performance in the Temple household. Some hold that the Tale is the narrator. The Tale represents intellectual dilemmas of the Age of reason as he talks about superiority of reason over fancy and imagination but at the same time tells us to go beyond surface meaning and not dwell in abstractions.

William Wotton wrote that it made a game of "God and Religion, Truth and Moral Honesty, Learning and Industry" to show "at the bottom [the author's] contemptible Opinion of every Thing which is called Christianity." It disbarred its author from proper preferment" in the Church of England.

An Argument Against Abolishing Christianity (1708) a satirical essay regarded as a prime example of political satire. He attacks contemporary assaults by its various opponents, including freethinkers, deists, Antitrinitarians, atheists, Socinians, and other so-called "Dissenters."

The Whig opposition attempted to rally support against the Treaty of peace with France with their slogan "No Peace Without Spain" (he was supporter of Torries). The incoming Tory government conducted secret (and illegal) negotiations with France, resulting in the Treaty of Utrecht (1713) ending the War of the Spanish Succession. Bolingbroke helped Swift with the book's composition. Discusses government's case for a unilateral peace with France; attack the previous Whig government and justify the appointment of a Tory ministry in 1710. Swift's anti-war and anti-Whig arguments captured parliamentary and popular opinion. The government subsequently signed the Peace of Utrecht.

The Essay on Criticism (1711, anonymously) in 3 parts, a response to an ongoing debate on the question of whether poetry should be natural, or written according to predetermined artificial rules inherited from the classical past. The first Part 1 On Taste: "Imitation of the ancients" is the ultimate standard for taste. The opening: “'Tis hard to say, if greater Want of Skill / Appear in Writing or in Judging ill;” contends that wrong criticism, than bad writing, is more dangerous. Recounts faults of poets, e.g., settling for easy and clichéd rhymes: e.g. whenever there’s word breeze, it’s rhymed with trees; same for creep/sleep. Pope refers to ancient writers such as Virgil, Homer, Aristotle, Horace and Longinus.  Art nature relation is expanded upon. So, he says, “First follow Nature, and your judgement frame / By her just standard, which is still the same,”. Nature (not WWW’s Nature) is “source, and end, and test of Art.” rules are discovered “not devis’d” and are therefore nature but “Nature methodiz’d”. So to copy them is to copy nature (Homer, Virgil, Aristotle). But then “great wits” may offend (these rules) only to rise above. Part 2 recounts ills in man that hinder judgement: Pride, little learning. Excessive enthusiasm, judging by parts and not whole, looking at one particular aspect only. He concludes that the chief object of censure is obscenity which otherwise if mixed with dullness is a crime like Charles II Part 3 Gives rules for good critical behaviour which are truth and candour, good sense, sincerity, diffidence and good breeding. He warns against being Appius who reddens at every word (John Dennis’s Appius and Virginia is referred). A good critic is an ideal man. Then gives a thumbnail history of criticism from Aristotle to Walsh. Ends with eulogy to a dead friend. Some quotations "To err is human; to forgive, divine", "A little learning is a dang'rous thing", "Fools rush in where angels fear to tread". In heroic couplet  and in the Horatian mode of satire it’s a verse essay primarily concerned with how writers and critics behave in the new literary commerce of Pope's contemporary age. Takes inspiration from Horace's Ars Poetica and Lucretius' De rerum natura. A poet isn’t born but made through industry. An Essay on Criticism was famously and fiercely attacked by John Dennis, who is mentioned mockingly in the work. Consequently, Dennis also appears in Pope's later satire, The Dunciad.

The Works of Mr Alexander Pope (1717) in it famous poem Eloisa to Abelard, a verse epistle in heroic couplet. "Eloisa to Abelard" is an Ovidian heroic epistle of which Pope had earlier published an example translated from the Latin in 1714, "Sappho to Phaon".[2] His own original exercise in this genre was inspired by the 12th-century story of Héloïse d'Argenteuil's illicit love for, and secret marriage to, her teacher Peter Abelard, a famous Parisian philosopher some twenty years her senior. After their affair and marriage, her family took brutal vengeance on Abelard and castrated him, following which he entered a monastery and compelled Héloïse to become a nun. Both then led comparatively successful monastic careers. Years later, Abelard completed the Historia Calamitatum (History of misfortunes), cast as a letter of consolation to a friend. When it fell into Heloise's hands, her passion for him was reawakened and there was an exchange of four letters between them written in an ornate Latin style. In an effort to make sense of their personal tragedy, these explored the nature of human and divine love. However, their incompatible male and female perspectives made the dialogue painful for both. She looks forward to the release of death when "one kind grave" will reunite them. Other two poems in the collection:  "Elegy to the Memory of an Unfortunate Lady" and the original version of the "Ode on St Cecilia's Day". “Elegy” is about a women whose ghost has appeared and the poets gets to knw she has committed suicide because of love and her uncle. There follows a description of her foreign burial in a "humble grave" unattended by friends and relatives, which Pope sums up in the striking couplet: “A heap of dust alone remains of thee;/ 'Tis all thou art, and all the proud shall be!” The concluding lines contain the speaker's application of this lesson of mortality to himself: someday he too will die and the last thought of the lady will be torn from him as he passes away. The lady in question is Elizabeth Gage (Weston). Best lines: “Is it, in heav'n, a crime to love too well?/To bear too tender, or too firm a heart,/ To act a Lover's or a Roman's part?”

Opening lines of Essay "Awake, my St John...". It is an effort to rationalize or rather "vindicate the ways of God to man". Because man cannot know God's purposes, he cannot complain about his position in the great chain of being (ll.33–34) and must accept that "Whatever is, is right" (l.292), a theme that was satirized by Voltaire in Candide (1759). Voltaire called it "the most beautiful, the most useful, the most sublime didactic poem ever written in any language". In 1756, Rousseau wrote to Voltaire admiring the poem and saying that it "softens my ills and brings me patience". Kant was fond of the poem and would recite long passages from it to his students. Later, however, Voltaire renounced his admiration for Pope's and Leibniz's optimism and even wrote a novel, Candide, as a satire on their philosophy of ethics. Rousseau also critiqued the work, questioning "Pope's uncritical assumption that there must be an unbroken chain of being all the way from inanimate matter up to God". Pope reveals in his introductory statement, "The Design" that he insisted the four epistles to be first book and this to be second.

The Rape of the Lock (1712 published anonymously in Lintot's Miscellaneous Poems and Translations 1712; "Written by Mr. Pope" in 1714 in 5 cantos accompanied by six engravings; final version 1717 with the addition of Clarissa's speech on good humour.)  the storm in a teacup, example of high burlesque (from Italian word ‘burlesco’ meaning mockery, ridicule). ‘Rape’ refers to earlier definition of the word derived from the Latin rapere which means ‘snatch, carry off’. Based on an event recounted to Alexander Pope by his friend John Caryll. Arabella Fermor and her suitor, Lord Petre, were each a member of aristocratic recusant Catholic families. Lord Peter’s cutting off Arabella Fermor’s (Belinda) whose suitor he is, hair—heroic-comical poem; Epic machinery introduced in 1714. He introduced an entire system of "sylphs", or guardian spirits of virgins, a parodised version of the gods and goddesses of conventional epic. Pope derived his sylphs from the Rosicrucian doctrine of Spirits taken from 17th-century French Rosicrucian novel Comte de Gabalis (which he mentions in dedictory letter to Fermor). Pope, writing pseudonymously as Esdras Barnivelt, also published A Key to the Lock in 1714 as a humorous warning against taking the poem too seriously.  The abduction of Helen of Troy becomes here the theft of a lock of hair;  the Description of Achilles' shield becomes an excursus on one of Belinda's petticoats. He adds parody to imitation by following the framework of actual speeches in Homer's Iliad. Attaches dedicatory letter to Mrs. Arabella Fermor in second edition. He begins by saying there has been a  "dire offence"  on a "gentle belle" (line 8), causing her to reject him. As Belinda sleeps, her guardian Sylph Ariel forewarns her that "some dread event impends". The Baron, one of Belinda's suitors, greatly admires her locks and conspires to steal one. Ariel summons many sylphs to her and instructs them to guard Belinda. So protected, Belinda arrives at Hampton Court and is invited to play a game of ombre. The conspiring Baron acquires a pair of scissors and tries to snip off one of her locks, but he is prevented by the watchful Sylphs. This happens three times, but in the end the Baron succeeds (also cutting a Sylph in two although Pope reassures us, parodying a passage in Paradise Lost, that "airy substance soon unites again". A gnome named Umbriel now journeys to the Cave of Spleen and from the Queen receives a bag of "sighs, sobs, and passions, and the war of tongues" (canto 4 line 84) and a vial filled "with fainting fears, soft sorrows, melting griefs, and flowing tears" (line 85–86) and brings them to Belinda. Many people, moved by Belinda's grief, demand the lock back, but the Baron is unrepentant and refuses. Clarissa admonishes them to keep their good humour, but they will not listen and instead a battle ensues with glares, songs and wits as weapons. Belinda fights with the Baron and throws snuff up his nose to subdue him. When she demands that he restore the lock, however, it is nowhere to be found. It has been made a constellation and is destined to outlast the contestants.

The Dunciad (1728 anonymous; Dunciad Variorum in 1729, sequel; New Dunciad in 1742 was 4th book; The Dunciad in Four Books 1743 final form with a new character, Bays, replacing Theobald as the "hero") mock-epic narrative. Hero: Lewis Theobald, who published Shakespeare restored, or, A specimen of the many errors, as well committed, as unamended, by Mr. Pope: in his late edition of this poet. Designed not only to correct the said edition, but to restore the true reading of Shakespeare in all the editions ever yet published (1726) a sound criticism of Pope’s 1725 Shakespeare edition. Intended as conclusion of Miscelanies volume by Pope and Swift in 1728, it was published separately and the volume was rather supplied with prose satire Peri Bathous. In 1743 edition, Colly Cibber replaces Theobald. Goddess Dullness insists to make Bays (Cibber) the king...King of Dunces. When planning the Dunciad he based the character Dullness on Queen Caroline, as the fat, lazy and dull wife. The King of the Dunces as the son of Dullness was based on George II. Pope makes his views on the first two Georgian kings very clear in the Dunciad when he writes "Still Dunce the second reigns like Dunce the first". Daniel Defoe is mentioned almost as frequently as anyone in the poem, and the booksellers picked out for abuse both specialised in partisan Whig publications.

Inspired by MacFlecknoe. Pope attacks, over and over again, those who write for pay (not the professional author, but the mercenary author). The plot of the poem is simple. Dulness, the goddess, appears at a Lord Mayor's Day in 1724 and notes that her king, Elkannah Settle, has died. She chooses Lewis Theobald as his successor. In honour of his coronation, she holds heroic games. He is then transported to the Temple of Dulness, where he has visions of the future. Book I covers the night after the Lord Mayor's Day, Book II the morning to dusk, and Book III the darkest night. Moreover, the poem begins at the end of the Lord Mayor's procession, goes in Book II to the Strand, then to Fleet Street (where booksellers were), down by Bridewell Prison to the River Fleet, then to Ludgate at the end of Book II; in Book III, Dulness goes through Ludgate to the City of London to her temple. Opens with invocation: "Books and the Man I sing, the first who brings/ The Smithfield Muses to the Ear of Kings". Dullness mentions Thomas Heywood, Daniel Defoe (for writing political journalism), Ambrose Philips, Nahum Tate, and Sir Richard Blackmore as her darlings. Theobald is debating whether to return to being a lawyer (for that had been Theobald's first trade) or to become a political hack. He decides to give up poetry and become an entirely hired pen for Nathaniel Mist and his Mist's Journal. Then takes poetry books never written and fires them to make sacrifice upon which Dullness appears in a fog and drops a sheet of Thule (a poem by Ambrose Philips that was supposed to be an epic, but which only appeared as a single sheet) on the fire, extinguishing it with the poem's perpetually wet ink. The book ends with a hail of praise, calling Theobald now the new King Log (from Aesop's fable). Book II centres on the highly scatological "heroic games" to celebrate coronation. The first game is for booksellers. Dulness therefore decides upon a race between booksellers Lintot and Curl. She creates a phantom Poet. The phantom poet is named More, a reference to James Moore Smythe, who had plagiarised both Arbuthnot (Historico-physical Account of the South-Sea Bubble) and Pope (Memoirs of a Parish Clark), and whose only original play had been the failed The Rival Modes. The next contest Dulness proposes is for the phantom poetess, Eliza (Eliza Haywood). She is compared to their Hera. The next contest is for authors, and it is the game of "tickling": getting money from patrons by flattery. Another contest, primarily for critics, comes next. In this, Dulness offers up the prize of a "catcall" and a drum that can drown out the braying of asses to the one who can make the most senseless noise and impress the king of monkeys. The critics are then invited to all bray at the same time. In this, Richard Blackmore wins easily ("Everlasting Blackmore"). Book three is set in the Temple of Dulness in the city. Theobald sleeps with his head on the goddess's lap, with royal blue fogs surrounding him. Settle gives Theobald full knowledge of Dulness. In the fourth boo, Colly Cibber is attacked. Cibber introduced jokes onstage at the expense of a poorly received farce, Three Hours After Marriage, written by Pope with John Arbuthnot and John Gay. Cibber as "Anti-Christ of Wit". Book 4 is the most expansive and ends in showing how chaos reigns as Dullness wins. Ends in “Thy hand, great Anarch! Lets the curtain fall, / And universal Darkness buries all.” (his epitaph?)

  6. The Beggar’s Opera (1728):  Influenced by Swift’s remark of having a Newgate pastoral but Gay decided on a satirical opera. Ballad operas were satiric musical plays that used some of the conventions of opera, but without recitative. It was premiered at the Lincoln's Inn Fields Theatre on 29 January 1728; ran for 62 consecutive performances, the second-longest run in theatre history up to that time (after 146 performances of Robert Cambert's Pomone in Paris in 1671). Called "the most popular play of the eighteenth century". The piece satirised Italian opera, which had become popular in London. Bertolt Brecht  adapted the work into Die Dreigroschenoper (The Threepenny Opera) in 1928, sticking closely to the original plot and characters but with a new libretto, and mostly new music by Kurt Weill. For his songs, Gay used Scottish folk melodies mostly taken from the poet Allan Ramsay's hugely popular collection The Gentle Shepherd (1725) plus two French tunes. The narrator informs that through the play audience will know that “ Whether… the fine gentlemen imitate the gentlemen of the road, or the gentlemen of the road the fine gentlemen.”

Peachum, a fence and thief-catcher, justifies his actions. His wife  protests regarding one of them: Bob Booty (the nickname of Robert Walpole). Polly, their daughter, has secretly married Macheath, the famous highwayman. They conclude that the match may be more profitable to the Peachums if the husband can be killed for his money. They leave to carry out this errand. However, Polly has hidden Macheath. Macheath goes to a tavern where he is surrounded by women of dubious virtue: among them Jenny Diver, Suky Tawdry who contracted with Peachum to capture him. He is now a prisoner in Newgate prisonrun by Peachum's associate, the corrupt jailer Lockit. His daughter, Lucy Lockit, has the opportunity to scold Macheath for having agreed to marry her and then broken this promise. She tells him that to see him tortured would give her pleasure. Macheath pacifies her, but Polly arrives and claims him as her husband.  Macheath tells Lucy that Polly is crazy. Lucy helps Macheath to escape by stealing her father's keys.  Polly visits Lucy to try to reach an agreement, but Lucy tries to poison her. Macheath now finds that four more pregnant women each claim him as their husband. He declares that he is ready to be hanged. The narrator (the Beggar), notes that although in a properly moral ending Macheath and the other villains would be hanged, the audience demands a happy ending, and so Macheath is reprieved, and all are invited to a dance of celebration, to celebrate his wedding to Polly. Beggar's last remark: "That the lower People have their Vices in a Degree as well as the Rich, and are punished for them."

So she goes disguised as a man. The play ends with Macheath being executed on the orders of the Indian King Pohetohee, and Polly marrying his son Cawwawkee, after her period of mourning.
