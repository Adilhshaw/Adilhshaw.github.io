Chapter 20

Scottish Literature from Allan Ramsay to Walter Scott

Decline of Scots Literary Language:

  1. Scottish writers increasingly wrote in English but spoke and “felt” in Scots.
  2. Scots began to disappear as a literary language, becoming only regional dialects.
  3. Influences: The Reformation, the Union of the Crowns in 1603, and the prestige of English Elizabethan writers.
  4. The Union of Parliaments in 1707 marked a further step in assimilating Scottish culture into English, ending Scotland’s political independence and making it part of Great Britain.

Response to Loss of Political Independence:

  1. Frustrated by the loss of a separate political identity, Scots turned to their literary past for cultural identity.
  2. Antiquarian interest in older Scottish literature grew in the 18th century.

Scots Dialect Verse:

Revival of Scots verse, but it was primarily dialectal and often used for humorous or sentimental themes. Such verse was seen as nostalgic, patronizing, and sometimes exhibitionist.

James Watson’s Collection:

  1. In 1706 (before the Union of Parliaments), James Watson, an Edinburgh printer, published A Choice Collection of Comic and Serious Scots Poems both Ancient and Modern.
  2. Watson’s anthology included three volumes (1706, 1709 and 1711).
  3. His work mixed various types of Scottish poems: revelry, courtly English exercises, macaronics, mock elegies, serious 16th-century Scots poetry, epigrams, epitaphs, patriotic pieces, and more.
  4. The collection represented a wide range of Scottish poetic traditions, though it lacked ballads, most songs, and works by Sir David Lyndsay.

Representation of Scottish Literary Traditions in Watson’s Collection:

  1. The makar tradition was represented by poets like Montgomerie.
  2. Scottish Chaucerians were unavailable until later (reintroduced by Allan Ramsay).
  3. Courtly tradition in English was represented by Drummond and Aytoun.
  4. Popular tradition included “Christ’s Kirk on the Green” and “Habbie Simson.”
  5. Watson aimed to use the best available texts, though many were only broadsides or flawed versions, reflecting poor 17th-century printing practices.

Interest in Vernacular and Primitive Poetry:

  1. Interest in Scots literary traditions soon merged with ideas about vernacular poetry, “primitive” art, and the “natural man.”
  2. This ideological blend created challenges for later poets like Robert Burns but also encouraged the creation of vernacular poetry.

Rewriting and Imitating Traditional Songs:

  1. 18th-century Scotland saw attempts to modernize and rewrite traditional Scottish songs.
  2. Collections of Scottish songs with new, English words were popular.
  3. Notable examples: Lady Grizel Baillie’s “Werena my heart licht, I wad dee” and Lady Wardlaw’s imitation ballad “Hardyknute.”
  4. William Hamilton of Gilbertfield produced a modernized version of Blind Harry’s Wallace, inspiring Robert Burns. And continued the “Habbie Simson” tradition with his poem “Last Dying Words of Bonnie Heck.” Also exchanged vernacular verse epistles with Allan Ramsay, beginning a tradition later embraced by Burns.

Allan Ramsay (1686 – 1758)

Scottish poet (or makar), playwright, publisher, librarian and impresario of early Enlightenment Edinburgh. Came to Edinburgh from Lanarkshire. From wigmaker’s apprentice to a literary figure. Began writing poetry as a member of the Easy Club and in 1715 became Club Laureate. Ramsay published verses and turned bookseller in 1718, selling poetry collections like Wealth and the Woody. In 1720, he collected and published his poems, establishing a circulating library in 1726.

In the society of the members he assumed the name of Isaac Bickerstaff, and later of Gawin Douglas. In 1716 he had published a rough transcript of "Christ's Kirk on the Green" from the Bannatyne Manuscript, with some additions of his own. In 1718 he republished the piece with more supplementary verses. In the following year he printed a collection of Scots Songs. He published Wealth and the Woody in 1720, a satire on the South Sea Company, describing its fortunes as shifting sands. Ramsay edited The Tea-Table Miscellany (1723–37) and The Ever Green (1724) and is considered as a pastoral writer and editor who revived interest in vernacular literature. The Tea-Table Miscellany is "A Collection of Choice Songs Scots and English", containing some of Ramsay's own, some by his friends, several well-known ballads and songs, and some Caroline verse. Its title was suggested by the programme of The Spectator: as Addison had sought for his speculations the hour set apart "for tea and bread and butter", so Ramsay laid claim to that place for his songs "e'en while the tea's fill'd reeking round" In The Ever Green: being a Collection of Scots Poems wrote by the Ingenious before 1600, Ramsay sought to revive interest in early Scottish literature, primarily using pieces from the Bannatyne Manuscript (anthology of earlier Scottish poetry compiled by George Bannatyne in 1568), though he made adaptations. They included his version of "Christ's Kirk" and a remarkable pastiche by the editor entitled "The Vision". His dramatic pastoral  The Gentle Shepherd (1725), showed an appreciation of country life and anticipates the attitude of Romanticism with a neo-classical tradition. It was dedicated to Susanna Montgomery, Lady Eglinton, to whom Ramsay gifted the original manuscript. In the volume of poems published in 1721 Ramsay had shown his bent to this genre, especially in "Patie and Roger", which supplies two of the dramatis personae to his greater work. In 1726 he published anonymously Poems in English and Latin, on the Archers and Royal Company of Archers, by several Hands for the Royal Company of Archers.

Influence of Ramsay’s Tea-Table Miscellany: Inspired many song collections throughout the century.

Patriotic Scottish Interest: Interest in Scottish poetry, tied with patriotism, predated the publication of Thomas Percy’s Reliques of Ancient English Poetry (1765).

Notable Collections:

  1. Orpheus Caledonius (1726) - London publication of Scots songs with music.
  2. Scots Musical Museum (1787) - Key collection with contributions from Burns.
  3. David Herd’s Ancient and Modern Scots Songs (1769, 1776) - Important for preserving original Scottish songs without editing, helping to transmit Scottish poetic tradition.

Cultural Shift Towards English: Despite efforts to preserve Scots, Edinburgh intellectuals favoured English, especially during Scotland’s “second Golden Age.”

Discouragement of Scots Dialect:

  1. James Beattie’s book Scotticisms, arranged in Alphabetical Order, designed to correct Improprieties of Speech and Writing (1788) - Guide to correct “improper” Scots expressions.
  2. Henry Mackenzie’s periodical The Mirror (1780) - Criticized Scots for writing in English they didn’t speak and speaking a dialect they didn’t write.

Robert Fergusson (1750 – 1774)

Scottish poet, short life, highly influential, inspired Robert Burns (Burns called him “by far my elder brother in muse”). Wrote in Scottish English and Scots language, praised for masterful Scots works. Contributed Scots poems in 1772 and 1773 to Ruddiman’s Weekly Magazine and Edinburg Amusement. Earliest extant poem: satirical elegy on David Gregory (math professor). First published Scots poem: "The Daft Days" (2 Jan 1772). Submitted works in both languages after this date. Mid-1773: Published "Auld Reekie", his masterpiece, a vivid portrayal of Edinburgh, planned as part of a longer poem. Hard self-critic, destroyed some of his manuscripts.

Member of the Cape Club, met at a tavern in Craig's Close. Late 1773 wrote "Poem to the Memory of John Cunningham," expressed fear of madness after Cunningham's death in an asylum. In his mock elegies, he carried on the “Habbie Simson” tradition and passed it on to Burns.

Influenced Burns:

  • "Leith Races" inspired Burns' "Holy Fair."
  • "On seeing a Butterfly in the Street" parallels Burn’s "To a Mouse."
  • "The Farmer's Ingle" compared to Burn’s "The Cotter's Saturday Night."

Biographies included in works by David Irving (Lives of the Scottish Poets), Robert Chambers (Lives of Illustrious and Distinguished Scotsmen), and Grosart (“Famous Scots Series")

His Scots poems include: “The Daft Days”; “The King's Birthday in Edinburgh”; “Caller Oysters”; “Hallow Fair”; “Leith Races”; “The Rising of the Session”; “The Sitting of the Session”.

Robert Burns (1759 – 1796)

known familiarly as Rabbie Burns, Scottish poet and lyricist, national poet of Scotland. Son of a tenant farmer on the Carrick border, Ayrshire. Not an “illiterate plowman”; had sporadic but substantial education. In 2009 voted greatest Scot by Scottish public (STV poll). Famous works: "Auld Lang Syne" (sung at Hogmanay); "Scots Wha Hae" (unofficial national anthem). First poem: "O, Once I Lov'd A Bonnie Lass". 1775: Studied at Kirkoswald, met Peggy Thompson; wrote "Now Westlin' Winds" and "I Dream’d I Lay" for her. First child, Elizabeth "Bess" Burns, with Elizabeth Paton (family servant).

Love affair with Mary Campbell; dedicated "The Highland Lassie O", "Highland Mary", "To Mary in Heaven" to her. Planned emigration to Jamaica with Mary (suggested in his song "Will ye go to the Indies, my Mary").

Started Commonplace Book in 1783 for poems and reflections (three Commonplace Books span 1783–1785, 1787–1790, and 1789–1794).  Motivation: poetry began after falling in love; described it as the “spontaneous language of the heart.” Early poems in English tipped with Scots; later shifted focus to pure Scots vernacular. Key Works: The Death and Dying Words of Poor Maillie (1785): humorous “mock testament” put in the mouth of a dying sheep. By 1784-85, poetry became his main medium for personal expression—emotions, social critique, satire and irony.

1786: Published Poems, Chiefly in the Scottish Dialect (Kilmarnock volume published by John Wilson) containing "The Twa Dogs", “Scotch Drink”, “The Holy Fair”, "To a Mouse", “To a Louse”, "Halloween", "To a Mountain Daisy", "Address to the Deil", "The Cotter's Saturday Night", "Epitaph for James Smith” and more (written at Mossgiel farm). Some weaker Scots works (“Hallowe’en”, “The Vision”) and six gloomy English poems (“Despondency, an Ode”, “Man Was Made to Mourn, a Dirge”). Four songs: “It was upon a Lammas Night” (to Corn Rigs tune), Masonic farewell, and others. Omissions in Kilmarnock Edition (avoided controversial satires to appeal to genteel Edinburgh audiences): “Holy Willie’s Prayer” (attack on Calvinist hypocrisy); “The Ordination” (satire on church politics); “Death and Doctor Hornbook” (humorous critique of rural medicine); “The Twa Herds” (burlesque of Calvinist ministers’ quarrel); “Address to the Unco Guid” (attack on puritan hypocrisy).

Development of Style: Adopted stanza forms with long history in Scottish poetry (e.g., Fergusson, Ramsay). Drew on folk traditions, oral rhythms, and older Scottish poetry collections. Focused on satire, description, and emotional control in his verse.

Contributed 100+ songs to The Melodies of Scotland, George Thomson's A Select Collection of Original Scottish Airs for the Voice, and James Johnson's Scots Musical Museum. Greatest British songwriter; blended preservation with creativity. Notable Songs:

  • “A Red, Red Rose” (combines tenderness and male swagger, written for Jessie Lewars the girl who nursed him in his final illness).
  • “Oh wert thou in the cauld blast” (poignant male protectiveness).
  • “The Rantin’ Dog, the Daddie o’t” (celebrating joy in parenthood).

Preserved/revised Scottish folk songs; notable collection: The Merry Muses of Caledonia (bawdy lyrics). Wrote "A Man's a Man for A' That" at Dumfries, inspired by Thomas Paine's The Rights of Man. Advocated reform and democracy; wrote poems like "Parcel of Rogues to the Nation" and "The Rights of Women".

Famous poems set to traditional tunes: "Auld Lang Syne" (tune "Can Ye Labour Lea"), "A Red, Red Rose" (tune "Major Graham"), "The Battle of Sherramuir" (tune "Cameronian Rant"). J.D. Salinger’s The Catcher in the Rye takes its title from "Comin' Through the Rye".

In poetry Burns tradition degenerated into the Whistle-Binkie tradition. Whistle-Binkie, a Collection of Songs for the Social Circle appeared in 1832.

Burns Night (25 January): Celebrated globally with Burns suppers, more widely observed than St. Andrew's Day.

“The Twa Dogs”:  Inspired by Fergusson’s works, rooted in Scottish tradition; traditional Scottish mode. Characters: Dialogue between Caesar (aristocratic dog) and Luath (humble dog).Class disparity: Caesar pities Luath’s life, while Luath highlights the joys of rustic life. Satirical critique of the rich: Caesar ironically exposes their hypocrisy and excess (e.g., gambling, masquerading, bullfighting reduced to “fecht wi’ nowt”). Criticism of Parliament: MPs depicted as frivolous, corrupt, and self-serving. Tone: Satirical but controlled; avoids sentimentalizing dogs or humans. Key Moment: Ends with dogs rejoicing they are not humans, highlighting human flaws.

“Epistle to Davie”: Traditional complex Scottish stanza. Opening: Vivid January scene described with poetic skill. Shift: Midway, shifts to sentimental, exhibitionist tone, aiming to appeal to genteel audiences.

“The Holy Fair”: Uses Fergusson’s Leith Races as a model; old Scottish stanza form. Setting: Outdoor “tent preaching” during annual communion services. Characters: Fun, Superstition, Hypocrisy personified as women. Ironical portrayal of human weaknesses amidst religious events. Calvinist sermons contrasted with human indulgences (e.g., drinking, flirting). Tone: Humorous and bawdy, not morally indignant. Key Lines: “An’ monie jobs that day begin / May end in Houghmagandie” (fornication).

“Address to the Deil”: written in Mossgiel in 1785 and published in the Kilmarnock volume in 1786. It begins by quoting from Milton's Paradise Lost as a contrast with the first two lines of the poem itself: “O thou! Whatever title suit thee,/Auld Hornie, Satan, Nick or Clootie”. These lines are also a parody of a couplet in Alexander Pope's satire The Dunciad. The poem was written in a Habbie stanza with the stanza six lines long and the rhyme scheme AAABAB. Burns used a similar stanza in Death and Doctor Hornbook.

Draws from folklore rather than Calvinist theology. Tone: Playful and familiar, reducing Devil’s stature to that of a mischievous joker. Implicitly critiques theological dogmas by reinterpreting them through ordinary experience. Calls Devil “auld Cloots” and “auld Nickie-ben” (playful nicknames). Suggests even the Devil might repent: “O wad ye tak a thought an’ men’!”

“To A Louse, On Seeing One on a Lady's Bonnet at Church” (1786) eight-stanza satirical poem in his favourite meter, standard Habbie. Addresses a louse crawling on a lady’s bonnet in church. Contrast between vulgarity (louse) and social pretension (lady). Tone: Humorous and ironic, shifting to friendly warning. Famous moral: “O wad some Pow’r the giftie gie us, / To see oursels as others see us!”

“To a Mouse, on Turning Her Up in Her Nest With the Plough, November, 1785: Reflects on the plight of a mouse displaced by a plow. Themes: Human destruction of nature; Fragility of life and unpredictability of the future. Famous reflection: “The best-laid schemes o’ mice an’ men / Gang aft agley.” John Steinbeck's Of Mice and Men (1937) and Sidney Sheldon's The Best Laid Plans (1997) both take their titles from a line in the penultimate stanza.

“Halloween”: written in 1785. First published in 1786, the poem is included in the Kilmarnock Edition. one of Burns' longer poems, with twenty-eight stanzas.

“To a Mountain Daisy”: Mourns the destruction of a flower while plowing. Crushed daisy symbolizes fragility, likened to a betrayed maiden. Themes: Symbol of nature’s fragility and humanity’s disregard for beauty; Reflection on inevitable decay and destruction. Burns’ Letter: He described it as reflecting his “native querulous feelings.” The final stanza is in some ways reminiscent of Andrew Marvell's poem To His Coy Mistress: “But at my back I always hear/Time's wingèd chariot drawing near”

“The Cotter’s Saturday Night”: first published in Poems, Chiefly in the Scottish Dialect in 1786. Romanticized depiction of Scottish peasant life. Burns wrote The Cotter's Saturday Night at Mossgiel farm (1785–86), using Spenserian stanzas inspired by Robert Fergusson's “The Farmer's Ingle”. It includes an epigraph from Thomas Gray’s Elegy and is dedicated to his patron, Robert Aiken. Gordon Williams's novel From Scenes Like These takes its title from stanza 19. Criticism: Undermined by sentimental moralizing and rhetorical exclamations; written to impress Edinburgh gentry.

“Holy Willie’s Prayer”: Satire of Calvinist predestination and hypocrisy. Tone: Liturgical, exposing moral horror and egotism disguised as humility. Highlights: Speaker blames lust on God, equating personal desires with divine will. Ends with ironic plea for both divine grace and earthly wealth to glorify God. Brilliant critique of Calvinism’s contradictions.

“Tam o’ Shanter” (1790): Folk legend about a farmer encountering witches at Alloway Kirk. Octosyllabic couplets. A 224-line narrative poem mixing Scots and English. It follows Tam, a farmer in Ayr, who drunkenly witnesses witches and the Devil dancing in a haunted church. He shouts at a witch in a short dress, provoking a chase. Tam flees on his horse Meg, reaching the River Doon just in time, though Meg loses her tail to the pursuing witches.

“The Jolly Beggars”: Cantata of social outcasts singing of defiance and independence. Tone: High-spirited, anarchistic, and unromanticized. Captures human drive for freedom despite poverty and vice.

Carolina Oliphant, Lady Nairne (1766 – 1845)

also known as Carolina Baroness Nairn in the peerage of Scotland and Baroness Keith in that of the United Kingdom. Scottish songwriter. Continued Jacobite songwriting tradition of the 18th century. Inspired by the failure of the 1745 Jacobite rebellion, fueling nostalgic “sentimental Jacobitism.”  Focused on mournful Scottish pride despite fading Jacobite influence. Many of her songs, such as, "Will ye no' come back again?", "Charlie is my Darling" , "The Rowan Tree" and "Wi' a Hundred Pipers' remain popular today. Nairne concealed her achievements as a songwriter throughout her life; they only became public on the posthumous publication of “Lays from Strathearn” (1846).

James Hogg (1770-1835)

Scottish poet, novelist and essayist who wrote in both Scots and English. Known as the “Ettrick Shepherd”; Border shepherd and farmer with limited formal education. Wrote Jacobite songs and rustic Scots songs influenced by Burns. Adapted folk material into stories and poetry Claimed to be “king of the faery school”; wrote fairy-themed works like “Kilmeny”. Literary persona of Ettrick Shepherd idealized by John Wilson (who wrote under the name of Christopher North) in Noctes Ambrosianae which appeared in Blackwood’s Magazine. (Blackwood’s was founded in 1817 as a Tory rival to Francis Jeffrey’s Edinburg Review founded in 1802)

In 1801 Hogg was recruited to collect ballads for Walter Scott's collection Minstrelsy of the Scottish Border. Hogg is a direct ancestor of Nobel Prize-winning Canadian writer Alice Munro.

POETRY:

Scottish Pastorals* (1801): containing five poems and two songs, was the first book published by Hogg: “Geordie Fa's Dirge”; “Dusty, or, Watie an' Geordie's Review of Politics”; “An Eclogue; Willie an' Keatie, A Pastoral”; “A Dialogue in a Country Church-Yard”; “The Death of Sir Niel Stuart, and Donald M'Vane, Esq. An Auld Tale Made New Again”; Song I "'Twas up yon wild an' lonely glen"; Song II "O Shepherd, the weather is misty and changing"

The Mountain Bard* (1807): containing 21 poems, was James Hogg's first substantial poetical publication.

The Forest Minstrel* (1810):  is an anthology of 83 songs, assembled by Hogg, divided into four sections: 'Pathetic Songs', 'Love Songs', 'Humorous Songs', and 'National Songs'. Hogg himself is the author of 56 items. There are also 15 by Thomas Mounsey Cunningham, 5 by John Grieve, 3 by William Laidlaw, 3 by James Gray, and one perhaps by John Ballantyne.

“The Queen's Wake” (1813): spans over 5,000 lines, including an Introduction, three Nights, and a Conclusion with authorial notes. It depicts a bardic contest organized by Mary, Queen of Scots in 1561. Introduction: Mary invites Scotland's bards for a competition. Night the First: Rizzio sings of tragic love in "Malcolm of Lorn." Gardyn tells of seduction, murder, and madness in "Young Kennedy." The bard of Leven narrates witchcraft in "The Witch of Fife." Night the Second: Farquhar describes defiance against nature in "Glen-Avin." The Ettrick bard recounts a daring rescue in "Old David." The bard of Lomond sings haunting tales like "Macgregor." The Clyde bard tells of heroism in "Earl Walter." Drummond describes a mystical journey in "Kilmeny."

Night the Third: The Borders bard sings a romantic tragedy in "Mary Scott." The Lammermoor bard envisions a united Scotland in "King Edward's Dream." The Nithsdale bard celebrates triumph in "Drumlanrig." The Hebrides bard recounts betrayal in "The Abbot M'Kinnon."

Conclusion: After debate, Queen Mary's harp is awarded to Gardyn. The Ettrick bard receives a simpler harp, symbolically inherited by Hogg.

“The Pilgrims of the Sun” (1815): The poem, under 2,000 lines, in four cantos, follows Mary Lee's journey to an ideal world and her return. Part 1 (ballad stanzas): Mary Lee of Carelha' is taken by the spirit Cela to a "blest land of love and truth" near the Sun. Part 2 (blank verse): Reaching the Sun, they approach God’s pavilion surrounded by adoring hosts. Cela explains a passing comet as an obsolete world cut adrift by God. Part 3 (heroic couplets): They visit worlds of lovers and warriors before Cela returns Mary to Earth, where her widowed mother and friends mourn her death. Part 4 (heroic couplets): Mary is revived and marries Hugo of Norroway, a bard who resembles Cela. After their deaths, their spiritual presence lingers, but Mary does not appear to the narrator.

“Mador of the Moor” (1816): is a narrative poem of over 2,000 lines, mostly in Spenserian stanzas, with an Introduction, five cantos, and a Conclusion. Set in medieval Scotland, it tells of a maiden seduced by a minstrel, her search for him, and their eventual marriage. Introduction: The poet addresses the River Tay. Canto 1: The King of Scots hears "The Harper's Song," then returns from hunting to find his party mysteriously slain. Canto 2: Ila Moore, betrothed to Albert of the Glen, is wooed by minstrel Mador, who flees after a fight with Albert. Canto 3: Albert expels Ila and her parents before she gives birth to Mador's son. Canto 4: Ila, joined by a remorseful palmer, sets out to find Mador. Canto 5: At Stirling, Mador is revealed as the king. Ila and Mador marry, and their son is christened. Conclusion: The poet bids his harp return to the Border region.

“Queen Hynde” (1824): an epic poem in six cantos (nearly 9000 lines). Set in western Scotland in the sixth century, it tells the story of the defeat of an invading Norwegian army by forces loyal to Queen Hynde, advised by Columba, and of the winning of her hand by the legitimate claimant of the throne Eiden. It is mostly in octosyllabic couplets.

Winter Evening Tales* (1820): a collection of four novellas, a number of short stories (some of them semi-fictional) and sketches, and three poems, published in two volumes in 1820. Eleven of the items are reprinted, with varying degrees of revision, from Hogg's periodical The Spy (1810‒11).

A Queer Book* (1832): a collection of 26 poems, mostly short narratives.

NON-FICTION:

The Shepherd's Guide (1807) (treatise on sheep)

The Spy (1810–11) (weekly periodical)

The Shepherd's Calendar (1829) (collected essays)

Familiar Anecdotes of Sir Walter Scott (1834) (memoir)

A Series of Lay Sermons (1834) (moral and religious discourses)

PROSE FICTION:

The Brownie of Bodsbeck (1817) (novel): Hogg's first novel, set in the Scottish Borders in 1685, portrays the persecuted Covenanters sympathetically and presents a harsh view of the Royalists led by Claverhouse. Walter Laidlaw, a man with no strong religious views, helps a group of Covenanters hiding near his farm. Unbeknownst to him, his daughter Katharine also aids them, using local superstition to cast their leader as a Brownie. She uncovers another sympathizer in the servant Nanny Elshinder. After Clavers arrests Walter, he witnesses the commander’s cruelty but escapes death at trial by discrediting the witnesses. Upon returning home, he meets John Brown of Caldwell, the Covenanter leader, who turns out to be Nanny’s long-lost husband.

The Surpassing Adventures of Allan Gordon (1818) (novella)

The Three Perils of Man; or, War, Women, and Witchcraft. A Border Romance (1822) (novel): set in the Scottish Borders during the reign of Robert II, King of Scots (1371–90), this novel tells of Sir Philip Musgrave, an Englishman who captures Roxburgh Castle and must hold it for a time to satisfy his mistress, Lady Jane Howard. Princess Margaret, Robert's daughter, challenges James, Earl of Douglas, to recapture it. Sir Walter Scott of Rankleburn aids Douglas by disrupting the English supply lines. Both Jane and Margaret disguise themselves as men to spy on their lovers. After Margaret arranges for Jane’s capture and execution, she is guided by spirit instructions to seek revenge. Meanwhile, a delegation from Sir Walter Scott seeks answers from the wizard Michael Scott at his castle of Aikwood, where supernatural events unfold. Eventually, Sir Walter captures Roxburgh, and after more disguises, Margaret marries Douglas. The story ends with a final battle between Michael Scott and a diabolical rival.

The Three Perils of Woman (1823) (novel): is a three-volume work with one novel and two novellas. The three perils are love, leasing (an old Scots term for lying) and jealousy. The novel follows Gatty Bell, who falls in love with M'Ion but misunderstands his feelings, leading to a catatonic state. After regaining her memory, they are happily reunited. A subplot features Richard Rickleton’s comic courtship and marriage to Katie, which ends in forgiveness after infidelity. The novella Leasing is set during the Jacobite rising and follows Sally Niven, who lies to protect her love, Peter Gow, leading to their separation. In Jealousy, Sally mistakenly believes her husband has another lover, leading to a tragic confrontation between him and Peter, resulting in both men’s deaths.

The Private Memoirs and Confessions of a Justified Sinner: published anonymously in 1824. Follows Robert Wringhim, a Calvinist who believes he is predestined for salvation and justified in murdering those he deems damned. Under the influence of the mysterious Gil-Martin, Robert’s delusions spiral into violence and madness, culminating in the murder of his brother George. The novel is told from two perspectives: the editor’s account and Robert’s own confessions. It explores themes of faith, identity, and moral corruption, blending gothic, psychological, and metaphysical elements. The story ends with Robert's death and the discovery of his memoir. The novel influenced works like Stevenson's Dr Jekyll and Mr Hyde and The Master of Ballantrae, exploring duality in good and evil. It inspired Gilchrist (1994), The Lecturer's Tale, and The Testament of Gideon Mack (2006). Critics call it a "forgotten classic," and modern adaptations include The Bad Sister and the short film Voices.

The Brownie of the Black Haggs (1828) (short story/tale)

Altrive Tales* (1832): is a collection of autobiographical writings, a novella, and short stories. It includes an updated version of his memoir, a continuation of personal reminiscences, and a picaresque novella, The Adventures of Captain John Lochy. Other works in the volume are The Pongos: A Letter from Southern Africa, about an orangutan abduction, and Marion’s Jock, a tale in Scots of a gluttonous cowherd. The volume also features a dedication to Lady Anne Scott and a revised version of Hogg's earlier memoir.

Tales of the Wars of Montrose* (1835) (short stories): a set of six fictional narratives. Each of them centres on the fortunes of an individual during the civil conflict of the 1640s in Scotland: 'Some Remarkable Passages in the Life of An Edinburgh Baillie. Written by himself'; 'The Adventures of Colonel Peter Aston'; 'Julia M,Kenzie'; 'A few remarkable Adventures of Sir Simon Brodie'; 'Wat Pringle o' the Yair'; 'Mary Montgomery'.

Tales and Sketches of the Ettrick Shepherd (1837)

Walter Scott (1771–1832)

Scottish novelist, poet, historian. Famous Works: Ivanhoe (1819), Rob Roy (1817), Waverley (1814), Old Mortality (1816), The Heart of Mid-Lothian (1818), The Bride of Lammermoor (1819), Marmion (1808), The Lady of the Lake (1810).

Limp due to childhood polio. Enthusiasm for German literature (1790s) prompted literary career. Translated Der wilde Jäger and Lenore (1796) as The Chase and William and Helen. "German-mad" during this period (1827 reflection).

Influenced by Gothic romance. Collaborated with 'Monk' Lewis on Tales of Wonder (1801). Inspired by diaries of Samuel Pepys, Lord Byron. Began journal during Byron's era, published posthumously (1890) as The Journal of Sir Walter Scott. Raised Presbyterian, Church of Scotland.

Reputation declined in late 19th/20th century as serious writers shifted to realism. Seen as suitable for children; trend accelerated in 20th century. E.M. Forster criticized writing style, characters, and plots in Aspects of the Novel (1927).

 Jane Austen gained critical esteem while Scott's reputation waned. Charlotte Brontë referenced Marmion in Jane Eyre (1847). Emily Brontë's Wuthering Heights influenced by Scott’s novels. Admired by Edgar Allan Poe, who praised The Bride of Lammermoor. Features as a character in Sara Sheridan's The Fair Botanists (2021).

Waverley or, ’Tis Sixty Years Since (1814): Historical novel. Published anonymously in 1814; Scott's first prose fiction. Later novels attributed to "the author of Waverley". Russell analyzed "Scott is the author of Waverley" in On Denoting to study meaning and logical structure. Scott admitted authorship publicly in 1827. Regarded as one of the first Western historical novels (Georg Lukács called Waverley the first major modern historical novel). Inspired by works like The History of the Rebellion in the Year 1745 (John Home), Letters from a Gentleman in the North of Scotland (Edmund Burt), and A Collection of Gaelic Proverbs (Donald Macintosh). PLOT: Set during the 1745 Jacobite uprising aiming to restore the Stuart dynasty through Charles Edward Stuart ("Bonnie Prince Charlie"). Edward Waverley, a young English soldier from an aristocratic Tory/Jacobite family, lives with his uncle Sir Everard Waverley, a Tory Jacobite sympathizer; his father is a Whig working for the Hanoverian government. Edward receives a commission in the Hanoverian army and is posted to Dundee. Visits Baron Bradwardine in the Lowlands and meets his daughter Rose. Travels to the Highland lair of Clan Mac-Ivor, befriending chieftain Fergus and sister Flora, active Jacobites.  Encouraged by Flora, Edward joins the Jacobite cause and meets Bonnie Prince Charlie at Holyrood Palace. Participates in the Battle of Prestonpans (1745), rescuing Hanoverian Colonel Talbot, a friend of his uncle. Jacobites victorious at Prestonpans but ultimately fail in 1746. Edward accused of desertion and treason but is pardoned through Talbot's intervention. Marries Rose Bradwardine and restores her father’s estate, damaged by soldiers. Fergus Mac-Ivor is executed; Flora becomes a nun in France. Waverley sells his late father’s estate to fund the restoration of Bradwardine’s property. The Talbots purchase an estate near Waverley Honour. (Last Ch. 25) Ends with comments and a dedication to Henry Mackenzie.

Old Mortality (1816): part of Tales of My Landlord (1816) with The Black Dwarf, is set in south-west Scotland during the Covenanters’ rebellion, focusing on events in 1679, including the Battle of Drumclog (victory) and Bothwell Bridge (defeat), and in 1689 at Killiecrankie. Originally titled The Tale of Old Mortality, it features an introduction by fictional editor Jedediah Cleishbotham and is narrated by Peter Pattieson, who recounts meeting Robert Paterson (‘Old Mortality’), an 18th-century Scotsman re-engraving Covenanter martyrs’ tombs. Lady Margaret Bellenden, a Royalist, holds a wapenshaw to rally tenants, most of whom support the Covenanters. At a popinjay contest, Henry Morton, son of a Covenanter, wins and meets Edith Bellenden, his Royalist love interest. Morton defends John Balfour of Burley (archbishop assassin) from Cavalier dragoons, shelters him, and flees, becoming an outlaw and joining the Covenanters. After their victory at Drumclog, factionalism weakens them, leading to defeat at Bothwell Bridge. Morton, caught between his cause and love for Edith, is captured by extreme Covenanters but rescued by Claverhouse, guided by Cuddie Headrigg, his loyal servant who reluctantly joined the rebellion due to his fanatical mother, Mause. Witnessing trials and torture of rebels, Morton goes into exile, returning in 1689 after James VII’s overthrow to a changed Scotland, where he reconciles with Edith. The novel inspired Têtes rondes et Cavaliers (1833) by Ancelot and Saintine.

Rob Roy (1817): historical novel Set around 1715, the year of the second Jacobite Rising. Rob Roy’s portrayal is fictionalized, differing from the historical figure. The story is narrated by Frank Osbaldistone, son of an English merchant. Frank is sent to live with his uncle in Northumberland after a disagreement with his father. He falls in love with Diana Vernon, whose father is a Jacobite. Frank’s cousin Rashleigh is unscrupulous and causes business trouble. Frank pursues Rashleigh to Scotland after he steals important documents. The story is set against the backdrop of the Jacobite Rising, with significant bloodshed. Frank encounters Rob Roy MacGregor, a powerful figure linked to Diana’s uncle. The action shifts to Loch Lomond, where a British army detachment is ambushed. Sir Hildebrand’s sons are killed, and Rashleigh meets a violent end. Frank inherits Sir Hildebrand's property and marries Diana.

The Heart of Mid-Lothian (1818): Sir Walter Scott's seventh Waverley Novel, published 25 July 1818 as Tales of My Landlord, 2nd series, under pseudonym Jedediah Cleishbotham. Set between 1736-1737, begins with Edinburgh’s Porteous Riots. Captain Porteous orders soldiers to fire on rioters, killing several, and is later lynched at Old Tolbooth prison. Jeanie Deans, a devout Presbyterian, journeys to London to secure a pardon for her sister Effie, sentenced to death for alleged infanticide. Inspired by Helen Walker, who made a similar journey to save her sister. Effie’s lover, George Staunton ("Geordie Robertson"), leads the mob against Porteous and tries to free Effie, who refuses to avoid seeming guilty. Effie, tried without evidence of the baby, is sentenced to death. Jeanie, unable to lie in court, decides to walk to London for a pardon. Waylaid by Madge Wildfire and Meg Murdockson, who stole Effie’s baby, Jeanie escapes Meg's murder attempt. With help from the Duke of Argyle, Jeanie secures an audience with Queen Caroline, who convinces the King to pardon Effie. Effie is freed, Jeanie marries Reuben Butler, who gains promotion and land. Effie reveals her child was sold by Meg and that Staunton is the criminal Robertson. Staunton is killed by their violent son, who flees to America. Effie converts to Catholicism and becomes a nun in France. Jeanie and Butler live happily on the Duke’s estate.

The Bride of Lammermoor (1819):  historical novel. Set in Lammermuir Hills, Scotland, before or after 1707 Act of Union. Tragic love between Lucy Ashton and Edgar Ravenswood, based on an actual incident. Published anonymously with A Legend of Montrose as third Tales of My Landlord. Basis for Donizetti's opera Lucia di Lammermoor. Edgar's father loses title for supporting deposed King James VII; Lucy's father, Sir William Ashton, buys Ravenswood estate. Edgar hates Sir William for the usurpation but falls in love with Lucy, renouncing revenge. Lady Ashton, Edgar's enemy, manipulates to break the engagement and forces Lucy into an arranged marriage with Francis, Laird of Bucklaw. Lady Ashton intercepts letters, spreads rumors of Edgar’s unfaithfulness, and recruits Ailsie Gourlay, a "witch," to deceive Lucy. Lucy clings to her love for Edgar, but Lady Ashton suppresses her letters and manipulates religious persuasion through Reverend Bide-the-bent. Lady Ashton forces Lucy to marry Bucklaw; Edgar returns but repudiates Lucy upon seeing her betrothal papers.  Lucy, unable to speak, marries Bucklaw, later stabbing him in the bridal chamber. Lucy descends into madness and dies; Bucklaw survives but refuses to explain the incident. Edgar returns at Lucy's funeral, challenged to a duel by Lucy's brother, who blames him. Edgar falls into quicksand on way to duel and dies.

Ivanhoe: A Romance (1819): set in 1194 during the aftermath of the Third Crusade and tells the story of Sir Wilfred of Ivanhoe, a Saxon knight loyal to King Richard the Lionheart, who has been disinherited by his father, Cedric of Rotherwood. Cedric plans to marry his ward, Lady Rowena, to Athelstane, a Saxon claimant to the throne. Ivanhoe, returning from the Crusades, finds himself caught in the tense power dynamics between Saxons and Normans.

The narrative begins with Ivanhoe’s family hosting a group of Normans, including the Templar Brian de Bois-Guilbert, who seeks to capture the Jewish moneylender Isaac of York. The mysterious palmer (later revealed as Ivanhoe) helps Isaac escape and, in return, Isaac offers him armor and a warhorse. Ivanhoe participates in a grand tournament at Ashby-de-la-Zouch, where he defeats Bois-Guilbert in jousting, and as the masked "Desdichado" (the disinherited), earns Lady Rowena’s favor. During the tournament’s melee, Ivanhoe is severely wounded and taken to York, where Isaac’s daughter, Rebecca, tends to him.

On the journey, Ivanhoe, Isaac, and Rebecca are captured by the Norman knights de Bracy and Front-de-Boeuf and taken to Torquilstone Castle. Cedric, Rowena, and Athelstane, traveling nearby, are also captured. Ivanhoe is rescued by the Black Knight, revealed to be King Richard, and together with Robin of Locksley and his band of outlaws, they besiege the castle. During the siege, Front-de-Boeuf is killed, and Bois-Guilbert escapes with Rebecca, taking her to a Templar preceptory, where she is tried for witchcraft.

In the meantime, Cedric and Ivanhoe reconcile after Cedric learns of Ivanhoe’s loyalty to King Richard. Athelstane, presumed dead, reappears alive, and Cedric finally agrees to Ivanhoe's marriage to Rowena. Rebecca, now facing trial by combat, requests Ivanhoe as her champion. Although Ivanhoe is exhausted from travel and injury, he arrives just in time to fight Bois-Guilbert, who is torn by his love for Rebecca and his knightly honor. Bois-Guilbert dies from emotional conflict during the duel. In the end, Ivanhoe marries Rowena, and they live happily together, while Rebecca and Isaac prepare to leave England for Granada, after facing persecution. Five years later, Ivanhoe’s military service ends with the death of King Richard.

Sir Walter Scott named Ivanhoe after Ivinghoe, inspired by a rhyme cited in his 1830 Magnum edition: Tring, Wing, and Ivanhoe... In Ivanhoe, "Locksley" becomes Robin Hood's title, taken from a 1600 manuscript, solidifying "Robin of Locksley" as his identity. In 1850, William Makepeace Thackeray wrote Rebecca and Rowena, a spoof sequel to Ivanhoe. Later adaptations include Edward Eager’s Knight's Castle (1956), where children are transported into the Ivanhoe story, Simon Hawke’s The Ivanhoe Gambit (1984), the first novel in his TimeWars series, Pierre Efratas's sequel Le Destin d'Ivanhoe (2003), and Christopher Vogler’s Ravenskull (2006).

The Monastery (1820); The Abbot (1820); Kenilworth (1821); The Pirate (1822); The Fortunes of Nigel (1822); Peveril of the Peak (1822); Quentin Durward (1823); St. Ronan's Well or Saint Ronan's Well (1824); Redgauntlet (1824)

The Betrothed (1825): set in the Welsh Marches during Henry II’s reign. Eveline, daughter of Sir Raymond Berenger, is saved by Damian Lacy from a Welsh siege and betrothed to Sir Hugo de Lacy, who leaves for the Crusades. Rebels led by Ranald Lacy kidnap her, and Damian defends her, but misunderstandings make the king believe they are traitors. Gwenwyn, Prince of Powys, plans to marry Eveline, but her betrothal to Sir Hugo offends the Welsh. After Sir Raymond is killed, Damian rescues Eveline, and she is sent to Gloucester. There, she sees a ghost that foretells her future. Sir Hugo returns, but Eveline is kidnapped, and Damian is wounded. The king, hearing rumors, accuses them of treason. Sir Hugo returns disguised as a palmer, and Ranald, posing as him, is killed by a Welsh bard. Sir Hugo reveals himself, gives Eveline to Damian, and joins the Crusade in Ireland.

The Doom of Devorgoil (1830): written in 1817–1818, reworked in 1829–1830, and published alongside Auchindrane in 1830, was a critical failure despite Scott's intent to oblige Mr. Terry, Manager of the Adelphi Theatre. Described as a melodrama, it follows Oswald of Devorgoil, a ruined Scottish baron, his wife Eleanor, daughter Flora, and niece Katleen. Flora is courted by Leonard, a ranger, and Gullcrammer, a divinity student, while Katleen and her lover Lancelot Blackthorn prank Gullcrammer disguised as spirits. A prophecy predicts that the ghost of Lord Erick will return 50 years after his death to doom the family. The ghost reveals a treasure chamber but blocks access with a portcullis. As the castle faces flooding, Katleen uses a key from the ghost to help Leonard unlock the chamber, saving the castle. The family regains its fortune, and harmony is restored.

Marmion: A Tale of Flodden Field (1808): Historical romance in verse. Set in 16th-century Scotland and England, concluding at the Battle of Flodden (1513). The Brontë sisters admired Marmion.  In Jane Eyre, St. John Rivers gives the poem to Jane. In The Tenant of Wildfell Hall, Gilbert Markham gives a copy to Helen Graham. John Wilson Croker was inspired by Marmion for his 1809 work The Battles of Talavera. The quote "Oh, what a tangled web we weave, / When first we practise to deceive!" is from Canto 6, stanza 17 of Marmion. This line is often mistakenly attributed to Shakespeare. PLOT Lord Marmion, a favorite of Henry VIII, desires Clara de Clare, a rich woman. Marmion and mistress Constance De Beverley forge a letter implicating Sir Ralph De Wilton in treason. De Wilton goes into exile after losing a duel to defend his honor against Marmion. Clara enters a convent to escape Marmion’s advances. Constance hopes to reconcile with Marmion but is abandoned and walled up alive for breaking vows. Constance reveals De Wilton's innocence, exonerating him. De Wilton, disguised as a pilgrim, gets the documents proving his innocence and regains his knighthood. De Wilton plans revenge but is overshadowed by the Battle of Flodden. Marmion dies at Flodden, while De Wilton shows heroism, regains honor, and marries Clara.

The Lady of the Lake (1810):  Narrative poem set in Trossachs, Scotland; six cantos, each covers a single day. Three main plots: Contest for Ellen Douglas’s love among Roderick Dhu, James Fitz-James, and Malcolm Graeme; Feud and reconciliation between King James V and James Douglas; and War between Lowland Scots (King James V) and Highland clans (Roderick Dhu). Inspired the Highland Revival in the 19th century. Third canto features a burnt cross summoning Clan Alpine against King James.

The Lord of the Isles (1815): is a narrative poem in six cantos with extensive notes. Set in Scotland between 1307 and 1314, it tells the story of Robert the Bruce's return from exile and his struggle to secure Scottish independence, culminating in the Battle of Bannockburn. A romantic subplot follows Ronald, Lord of the Isles, who initially loves Bruce's sister Isabel, but she takes the veil. Ronald then transfers his affections to Edith of Lorn, whom he marries at the end.

John Gibson Lockhart (1794 – 1854)

Scott’s son-in-law, biographer, literary journalist. Career symbolic of Scottish man of letters in the period. With John Wilson, made Blackwood’s Magazine famous (vigour, liveliness). Lockhart wrote virulent articles on "The Cockney School of Poetry" of Leigh Hunt, Keats and their contemporaries, although he did show appreciation of Coleridge and Wordsworth, and he praised Percy Bysshe Shelley, calling him "a man of genius". Motivated by Tory politics, blended German Romanticism, English neoclassicism. Wrote Peter’s Letters to his Kinsfolk (1819): vivid sketches of Edinburgh life. Moved to London (1824) to edit Quarterly Review (Tory magazine). Best known as the author of the seminal, and much-admired, seven-volume biography of his father-in-law Sir Walter Scott: Memoirs of the Life of Sir Walter Scott, Bart. Four novels: 

Valerius (1821): historical novel in three volumes. Seen as an attempt to imitate the style of his father-in-law's Waverley Novels. It is set in Ancient Rome, a historical period that Scott himself avoided writing about. Valerius, the hero, converts to Christianity and has to flee to Britain to escape the persecution of the Emperor Trajan.

 Adam Blair (1822): In this a Church of Scotland minister is accused of adultery. Lockhart's biographer Andrew Lang praised the novel, comparing its theme to the later 1850 novel The Scarlet Letter by Nathaniel Hawthorne, as did Henry James. Its full title is Some Passages in the Life of Mr Adam Blair.

Reginald Dalton (1823): comedy novel.  It takes place around Oxford University which Lockhart had himself attended. It helped to launch the genre of "Oxford novels" which focus on the development of a young student. Reginald Dalton, a naïve young vicar's son, arrives at Oxford to study but soon falls in with a bad crowd and runs into debt. His father can scarcely afford to support him and he becomes a servitor, leading to snobbish mockery and exclusion from those who had once been his friends. He ends up fighting a duel.

Matthew Wald (1824): It was the fourth and last novel written by Lockhart. The novel is partly set in a lunatic asylum. It was influenced by the writings of William Godwin and resembles Justified Sinner by James Hogg, published the same year.

John Galt (1779 – 1839)

John Galt, a Scottish novelist and social commentator, is considered the first political novelist in English for addressing Industrial Revolution issues. Depicted Scottish life, especially in Ayrshire. Themes: humor, Scottish character, and middle-class manners. Used Scots vernacular, picturesque idioms. Balanced sentimentality and dry realism (e.g., execution scene in The Provost). Exhibited conflicting attitudes towards Scottish culture. He published the first full biography of Lord Byron and the first biography of painter Benjamin West, The Life and Studies of Benjamin West (1816, expanded 1820). Galt also penned fiction and educational texts under the pseudonym Reverend T. Clark. In 2006, Guelph, Canada, proclaimed the first Monday in August as "John Galt Day."

Kailyard School:

Late 19th-century sentimental depictions of Scottish life. Notable works:

  • J.M. Barrie: Auld Licht Idylls (1888), A Window in Thrums (1889).
  • S.R. Crockett: The Stickit Minister (1893).
