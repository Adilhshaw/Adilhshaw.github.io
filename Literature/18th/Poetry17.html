CHAPTER 17: POETRY FROM THOMSON TO CRABBE

Augustan poets: believed proper study of mankind was man though they were not indifferent to the beauties of Nature. Poets of this period were intrigued by the blend of burlesque and seriousness created by using elevated, periphrastic language to describe rustic or everyday subjects. Octosyllables remained a popular verse from throughout this period.

James Thomson (1700-48)

Scottish poet and playwright (though emphatically an English rather than a Scottish poet). Known for his poems The Seasons and The Castle of Indolence, and for the lyrics of “Rule, Britannia!”. Burned most of his early poems on New Year’s Day each year. Became a member of the Grotesque Club, a literary group at Edinburgh. His first play was Sophonisba (1730) best known for its mention in Samuel Johnson's Lives of the English Poets, where Johnson records that one 'feeble' line of the poem – "O, Sophonisba, Sophonisba, O!" was parodied by the wags of the theatre as, "O, Jemmy Thomson, Jemmy Thomson, O!" major work, Liberty (1734). His poem “Poem Sacred to the Memory of Sir Isaac Newton” expressed his view of an ordered universe by the original designer God: “A universe complete”

Another major work, Liberty (1734), a lengthy monologue by the "Goddess of Liberty", describing her travels through the ancient world. In 1740, he collaborated with Mallet on the masque Alfred. Thomson's words for "Rule, Britannia!", written as part of that masque became one of the best-known British patriotic songs. His final work is  The Castle of Indolence, which was published just before his untimely death on 27 August 1748. Johnson writes about Thomson's death: "by taking cold on the water between London and Kew, he caught a disorder, which, with some careless exasperation, ended in a fever that put end to his life".

A dispute over the publishing rights to one of his works, The Seasons, gave rise to two important legal decisions (Millar v. Taylor; Donaldson v. Beckett) in the history of copyright.

MAJOR POEMS:

The Seasons: series of four poems: Winter in 1726, Summer in 1727, Spring in 1728 and Autumn only in the complete edition of 1730. Mentioned by Emily Dickinson in poem 131, "Besides the Autumn poets sing". Oscar Wilde included this poem, only half-sarcastically, in a list of ‘books not to read at all’. In his Preface to Winter: expressed a view of his subject, “I know of no subject more elevating…than the works of Nature”. His Hymn that concluded The Seasons sees Nature as the result of the benevolent contrivance of God: “These, as they change, Almighty Father, these/Are but the varied God!”. His description of fishing in Spring is similar to that of Gay’s description in Rural Sports. He extended Milton's narrative use of blank verse to use it for description and to give a meditative feeling. As Dr Johnson puts it, “His blank verse is no more than the blank verse of Milton…than the rhymes of Prior are the rhymes of Cowley”. Johnson in his “Life of Thomson” combined admiration with the remark that, “the great defect of Seasons is want of method”. Swift also objected to the want of action in the poems: “I am not over fond of them, because they are all descriptions, and nothing is doing”.

The Castle of Indolence (1748): descriptive poem in two cantos written in Spenserian stanzas with half-hearted attempt at a Spensarian vocabulary. In “Advertisement” prefixed to the poem he wrote: “This poem being writ in the manner of Spenser…were necessary to make the imitation more perfect”. Washington Irving quotes four lines from Canto I, VI from the poem in his tale "The Legend of Sleepy Hollow", using them to open the story and set the scene: “A pleasing land of drowsy-hed it was,/Of dreams that wave before the half-shut eye;/And of gay castles in the clouds that pass,/Forever flushing round a summer-sky”. Lines from Canto I, XXX are used as the epigraph to Chapter XIII of Ann Radcliffe's The Mysteries of Udolpho. Thomas De Quincey cites from Thomson in Confessions of an English Opium-Eater, laced into his description of sitting fireside upon a wintry evening: “And at the doors and windows seem to call,/As heav'n and earth they would together mell;/Yet the least entrance find they none at all;/Whence sweeter grows our rest secure in massy hall”

John Dyer (1699 – 1757) 

painter and Welsh poet who became a priest in the Church of England. Was most recognised for Grongar Hill, one of six early poems featured in a 1726 miscellany. Longer works published later include the less successful genre poems, The Ruins of Rome (1740) and The Fleece (1757). Dyer's dislike for Westminster was chronicled in a 1714 entry in his Journal of Escapes: “Ran from school and my father, on a box of the ear being given me strolled for three or four days – found at Windsor.” His first attempt in writing Miltonic octosyllabic couplets was addressed to his mentor, "An Epistle to a Famous Painter". Dyer's poem "To Mr Savage, son of the late Earl Rivers" encourages Savage to bear misfortune, to which Savage responds with "An Epistle to My John Dyer in Answer to his from the Country." Dyer’s celebration of country life in "To a Friend in Town" is complemented by Aaron Hill’s poem "The Choice," forming a Horatian dialogue. Another set of poems centers around Dyer’s portrait of Martha Fowke, known as Clio. Dyer’s "A Country Walk" and "The Inquiry" express admiration for her, and in "To Clio from Rome," he references his art with "the wreathed urn among the vines." Martha Fowke responds, praising both his poetry and art, while Savage also honors Dyer’s portrayal of Clio in his poem. In his epistle "To a Famous Painter" (addressed to his teacher Jonathan Richardson), Dyer modestly admits that for now, he can only "paint" in verse. Also wrote anecdotal pastoral "My Ox Duke".

Grongar Hill:  First version (1726): Published in Miscellaneous Poems and Translations by Several Hands, edited by Richard Savage. Written in irregular pindarics but burdened by conventional poetic diction (e.g., "mossy Cells," "grassy Bed"). The language was heavily formulaic, with phrases like "rugged Cliffs" and "gay Carpet of yon level Lawn" being unoriginal. Second version (later in 1726): Dyer rewrote Grongar Hill in four-stressed octosyllabic couplets describing a landscape in his native Carmarthenshire and roughly modelled on those of Milton's L'Allegro and contrasting strongly with the version of pastoral in Alexander Pope's Windsor Forest and was recognised as a precursor of Romanticism. Marvell's shorter poem, Upon the Hill and Grove at Billborrow, also used this octosyllabic meter. Dyer's poem belongs to the tradition of prospect poems, from Denham’s Cooper’s Hill to the end of the 18th century. The poem is shorter than others in this tradition (158 lines). It mentions Grongar Hill and the River Towy but avoids specific historical references. Instead, the ruins take on Gothic qualities: "Raven’s bleak Abode," "Fox securely feeds," and "Adder breeds." Unlike later "parson poets" who used landscapes for moralizing, Dyer’s moral is private. For William Wordsworth, in his sonnet addressed to the poet, Dyer is rescued from the fashionable preference formerly given unworthier models by his ability to conjure up “a living landscape” that will ultimately be remembered "Long as the thrush shall pipe on Grongar Hill."

The Ruins of Rome was a descriptive poem in 545 lines of Miltonic blank-verse. Though it was first published anonymously in 1740, its reference to the earlier "Grongar" in its opening lines establishes Dyer's authorship: “Enough of Grongar, and the shady dales/Of winding Towy, Merlin's fabled haunt”. The poem has been described as "largely responsible for the eighteenth-century revival of a unique subgenre of landscape poetry dealing with the ruins of the ancient world.”

The ambitious four-book The Fleece (1757), was also written in blank-verse. It was a georgic in the line of Virgilian imitations written during the 18th century that included John Philips' Cyder, Christopher Smart's The Hop-Garden (1752) and James Grainger's The Sugar Cane (1764). Dyer's poem deals with the tending of sheep, the shearing and preparation of the wool, weaving, and trade in wollen manufactures. In the following century William Wordsworth was to address a sonnet to Dyer as the: “Bard of the Fleece, whose skilful genius made/That work a living landscape fair and bright”.

John Philips (1676–1709)

English poet. He was one of the few to write in blank verse in an age when the heroic couplet was the standard form. His Splendid Shilling (1701, 1705) is a parody of Milton. Cyder (1708), a utilitarian poem describing the cultivation of apples and the pressing of cider, is modeled after Vergil's.

Matthew Green (1696–1737)

British poet born of Nonconformist parents. His Grotto, a poem on Queen Caroline's grotto at Richmond was printed in 1732. His chief poem, The Spleen, in 1737 with a preface by his friend Richard Glover. The Spleen was not originally intended for publication, was an epistle to Cuthbert Jackson, advocating cheerfulness, exercise and a quiet content as remedies. It is a witty poem on melancholy and its cure written in octosyllables with colloquial liveliness. Thomas Gray said of it: "There is a profusion of wit everywhere…for even his wood-notes often break out into strains of real poetry and music". Green is thought of as a "one-poem" poet, to the point that he became known as "Spleen-Green." Green is perhaps best remembered (if at all) for witty aphorisms, such as:

"By happy alchemy of mind / They turn to pleasure all they find." "Laugh and be well." "Fling but a stone, the giant dies." (The Spleen) "Though pleased to see the dolphins play / I mind my compass and my way." (The Spleen)

Edward Young (1683 – 1765)

English poet, best remembered for Night-Thoughts, a series of philosophical writings in blank verse. First publication was an Epistle to ... Lord Lansdoune (1713). This was followed by a Poem on the Last Day (1713), dedicated to Queen Anne; The Force of Religion: or Vanquished Love (1714), a poem on the execution of Lady Jane Grey and her husband, dedicated to the Countess of Salisbury; and an epistle to Joseph Addison, On the late Queen's Death and His Majesty's Accession to the Throne (1714). In 1719 his play, Busiris, King of Egypt (dedicated to the Duke of Newcastle) was produced and in 1721 his The Revenge. In 1759, at the age of 76, he published a piece of critical prose under the title of Conjectures on Original Composition.

The Complaint: or, Night-Thoughts on Life, Death, & Immortality, better known simply as Night-Thoughts (1742 -1745), is published in nine parts (or "nights") . It was illustrated with notable engravings by William Blake. Poem in blank verse, 9 "nights" reflecting on death, loss, and human frailties. Famous line: "procrastination is the thief of time" (Night I). Each night is a separate poem: "Life, Death, and Immortality" (dedicated to Arthur Onslow), "Time, Death, Friendship" (to Spencer Compton), "Narcissa" (to Margaret Bentinck), "The Christian Triumph" (to Philip Yorke), "The Relapse" (to George Lee), "The Infidel Reclaim'd" (in two parts, to Henry Pelham), "Virtue's Apology" (no dedication), and "The Consolation" (to Thomas Pelham-Holles). In his 1791 book, Life of Samuel Johnson, James Boswell called Night-Thoughts "the grandest and richest poetry that human genius has ever produced".

Robert Blair (1699 – 1746)

Scottish poet. Known for his poem The Grave, which in a later printing was illustrated by William Blake. Published only three poems. One was a commemoration of his father-in-law and another was a translation. The Grave (1743), a poem written in blank verse quite un-Miltonic on the subject of death and the graveyard. The poem, 767 lines long, is an exemplar of what became known as the school of graveyard poetry.

John Wesley (1703–1791)

Anglican cleric, theologian and evangelist. Founder of Methodism, a major Protestant Christian movement. Emphasized personal faith, holiness and practical Christianity. Advocated for "scriptural holiness" and social reform. The Journal of John Wesley (published posthumously) is extensive personal diaries documenting his missionary work, travels and reflections on faith. The transition from the Age of Reason to the Age of Sensibility involved growing dissatisfaction with rational deism and the Church of England's emotional restraint, leading to John and Charles Wesley's introduction of a more emotional approach through the Methodist Movement. This period also saw a cultural shift toward the picturesque and Gothic styles in art and literature, alongside significant societal changes like the enclosure movement and the Industrial Revolution, all contributing to an increased value placed on emotional expression in both religion and the arts.

Mark Akenside (1721 –1770)

English poet and physician. Slightly lame all his life.  His “Hymn to Science” (1739) shows his enthusiasm for the world of knowledge opened up by Newton and Locke. In 1740, he printed his Ode on the Winter Solstice in a small volume of poems. In 1744, published his didactic poem, The Pleasures of the Imagination, which was well received and later described as 'of great beauty in its richness of description and language'. In 1744, he published his Epistle to Curio, attacking William Pulteney (afterwards Earl of Bath) for having abandoned his liberal principles to become a supporter of the government, and in the next year he produced a small volume of Odes on Several Subjects (1745), in the preface to which he lays claim to correctness and a careful study of the best models. His lifelong friendship was with Jeremiah Dyson. In 1746 he wrote his much-praised "Hymn to the Naiads", and he also became a contributor to Dodsley's Museum, or Literary and Historical Register. Edmund Gosse described him as "a sort of frozen Keats".

The Pleasures of the Imagination (1744) First book: Defines imagination, discusses pleasures from beauty. Second book: Differentiates imagination from philosophy. Third book: Explores pleasure in studying man, sources of ridicule, mind's role in creating imagination, and imagination's impact on morals. Influences: Joseph Addison's The Spectator, Lord Shaftesbury. William Warburton offended by Akenside's note on ridicule in the third book. Warburton attacked Pleasures of the Imagination (anonymous) in his preface to Remarks on Several Occasional Reflections (1744). Dyson, possibly with Akenside's help, replied in An Epistle to the Rev. Mr Warburton.

William Shenstone (1714 – 1763) 

English poet and one of the earliest practitioners of landscape gardening through the development of his estate, The Leasowes. Joseph Heely gave an account of this estate of his in Letters on the Beauties of Hagley, Envil and the Leasowes (1777). Poems on various occasions, written for the entertainment of the author (1737) contains the first draft of The Schoolmistress. In 1741 he published The Judgment of Hercules. In 1742 he published anonymously a revised draft of The Schoolmistress, a Poem in imitation of Spenser. Robert Burns, in the preface to Poems, chiefly in the Scottish Dialect (1786), called him ... “that celebrated poet whose divine elegies do honour to our language, our nation and our species”. Best example of technical skill: mastery of anapaestic trimeter in his Pastoral Ballad in Four Parts (written 1743, printed 1755 in Dodsley's Collection of Poems). In 1741 letter: Shenstone first recorded the word "floccinaucinihilipilification" (longest word in English in OED first edition). Other poems include: “To the Winds”; “To a Friend”; “The Landskip”. Some of his most attractive verses are “inscriptions” written for a particular object or place (“Written at an Inn at Henley”). Shenstone’s Unconnected Thoughts on Gardening (1764) presents his view of natural beauty and the picturesque (books on gardening appeared throughout the century most important among them Walpole’s Essay on Gardening 1771)

Joseph Warton (1722 – 1800) 

English clergyman, academic and literary critic. In his early days Joseph wrote poetry, of which the most notable piece is The Enthusiast (1744), an early precursor of Romanticism. Like his brother, Warton was a friend of Samuel Johnson, and was part of the literary coterie centered around publisher Robert Dodsley. Works:  The Enthusiast, or The Lover of Nature (1744): In this poem it is not Nature to advantage dressed that Warton admires, but Nature unimproved by man. In the poem there is a picture of the poet as contemplative lover of the night that derives from Milton’s “Il Penseroso”; Odes on Various Subjects (1746); Essay on the Genius and Writings of Pope (volume 1: 1756; volume 2: 1782): This work emphasizes sublime and pathetic as key poetic qualities. Classifies English poets into four groups. First class: Spenser, Shakespeare, Milton (sublime poets). Second class: Dryden, Prior, Addison, etc. (moral and ethical poets). Third class: Butler, Swift, Donne (witty but not sublime). Fourth class: mere versifiers. Pope lacks depth of the sublime. Praised as the “great Poet of Reason”. Positioned at the top of the second class for elegance and insight.

Thomas Warton (1728 – 1790) 

English literary historian, critic, and poet. He was appointed Poet Laureate in 1785, following the death of William Whitehead. He is sometimes called Thomas Warton the younger to distinguish him from his father, who had the same name. His most famous poem is The Pleasures of Melancholy, a representative work of the Graveyard Poets, written at the age of seventeen, echoes Milton’s “Il Penseroso”.  Warton, along with his brother, was among the first to argue that Sir Thopas, by Geoffrey Chaucer, was a parody. He was a general supporter of the poetry of Thomas Gray—a fact that Johnson satirized in his parody "Hermit hoar, in solemn cell." In 1749, Warton wrote and published anonymously The Triumph of Isis, a poem praising Oxford, countering William Mason's Isis, an Elegy (1748) which criticized the university. His first major academic work was Observations on the Faerie Queene of Spenser, published in 1754, saw Spenser’s imagination as unfettered by judgment. Concluded: "if the critic is not satisfied, yet the reader is transported." Advocated for critics to read contemporary works of the author's time. Argued Spenser should not be judged by modern compositional standards. Supported critical relativism, echoed by Bishop Hurd's Letters on Chivalry and Romance (1762). Hurd argued The Faerie Queene should be judged by Gothic art standards.

Among his minor works were an edition of Theocritus, a selection of Latin and Greek inscriptions, the humorous Oxford Companion to the Guide and Guide to the Companion (1762); lives of Sir Thomas Pope and Ralph Bathurst; The Oxford Sausage (1764) – an anthology of verse and Oxford wit; and an Inquiry into the Authenticity of the Poems attributed to Thomas Rowley (1782). Warton also wrote Newmarket, a Satire, followed by a collection of verses. His complete poetical works were later included in an anthology published in 1853. He is best known for the three-volume The History of English Poetry from the Twelfth to the Close of the Sixteenth Century (1774–81), which covered the poetry of the 11th through the 16th centuries. In 1782, he wrote The History and Antiquities of Kiddington, an early example of English local history. Also wrote: “Verses on Sir Joshua Reynolds’s Painted Window at New College, Oxford”.

William Collins (1721 – 1759) 

English poet. Second in influence only to Thomas Gray. His odes marked a shift from Augustan to Romantic ideals. His 1747 collection's failure, combined with alcoholism, led to mental instability. By 1754, he was confined to McDonald's Madhouse in Chelsea.

His Persian Eclogues (1742): renewed the pastoral genre by setting them in Oriental locations like Baghdad, the Caucasus, and Circassia, framed as translations. Revised as Oriental Eclogues in 1757, they were republished frequently and translated into German in 1767 and 1770. The work influenced later exotic eclogues, such as Thomas Chatterton's African-themed ones (1770) and Scott of Amwell's Oriental Eclogues (1782), set in Arabia, Bengal, and Tang dynasty China. Scott's introductory "Advertisement" justifies his poems as both a homage to and variation upon the work of Collins.

His Verses humbly address'd to Sir Thomas Hanmer on his edition of Shakespeare's works (1743) was republished in a revised edition in 1744, in which "A Song from Shakespeare's Cymbeline" was included.

His Odes on Several Descriptive and Allegorical Subjects (1746): originally planned to appear in a joint volume with Joseph Warton’s Odes on Various Subjects (1746). This collection of odes include: “Ode to Pity” (celebrates Euripides); “Ode to Fear”; “Ode to Simplicity”; “Ode on the Poetical Character” (most complex of his odes in imagery and thought); “Ode on the Death of Mr. Thomson”; “The Passions: An Ode for Music”; “Ode to Evening” (most technically successful of all his poems after “How sleep the brave”, exemplifies the unrhymed lyric form in English). Collins favoured personification, using it exclamatorily ("Ah Fear! Ah frantic Fear!/I see, I see thee near") and with quiet dignity like in “Ode” written in the beginning of 1746.

His “Ode on the Popular Superstitions of the Highlands of Scotland” is incomplete and posthumously published, a genuine version of which was discovered in 1788. It is one of the first attempts in English literature to exploit the romantic aspects odf Scottish scenery and legend. His proposed “Ode on the Music of the Grecian Theatre” was never fully completed, with only an 18-line fragment surviving.

Thomas Gray (1716 – 1771) 

English poet, letter-writer. Widely known for his “Elegy Written in a Country Churchyard” (1751).Was a self-critical writer, published only 13 poems in his lifetime (his collected works published during his lifetime amount to fewer than 1,000 lines). He once wrote that he feared his collected works would be "mistaken for the works of a flea." Offered the position of Poet Laureate in 1757 after the death of Colley Cibber, though he declined. At Eton, Gray made three close friends: Horace Walpole, Thomas Ashton, and Richard West. They formed the "Quadruple Alliance," known for their style, humor, and appreciation of beauty. Gray's nickname in the group was Orozmades, after a Zoroastrian deity mentioned in Lee’s The Rival Queens. Gray began writing seriously in 1742, inspired by the death of Richard West, which led to his poem “Sonnet on the Death of Richard West”. He became associated with the "Graveyard poets" of the late 18th century, including Oliver Goldsmith, William Cowper, and Christopher Smart. Poems:

“Ode on the Spring” (written in 1742)

“On the Death of Richard West” (written in 1742) Wordsworth, in Lyrical Ballads prefaces (1800, 1802), criticizes Gray's Sonnet on the Death of Richard West as an example of flawed poetry, accusing Gray of widening prose-poetry divide and being overly elaborate in poetic diction.

“Ode on the Death of a Favourite Cat, Drowned in a Tub of Goldfishes” (written in 1747)

“Ode to a Distant Prospect of Eton College” (written in 1747 and published anonymously)

“Elegy Written in a Country Churchyard” (written between 1745 and 1750) completed in 1750 and first published in 175. Inspired by Gray's thoughts following the death of the poet Richard West in 1742.  Originally titled Stanzas Wrote in a Country Church-Yard. Two versions of the poem, Stanzas and Elegy, approach death differently; the first contains a stoic response to death, but the final version contains an epitaph which serves to repress the narrator's fear of dying. Opening lines: “The curfew tolls the knell of parting day,/ The lowing herd wind slowly o'er the lea” The end of the poem is connected to Locke's An Essay Concerning Human Understanding in that the beginning of the poem deals with the senses and the ending describes how we are limited in our ability to understand the world. Poet turns to address himself in the 24th stanza and to move the poem round until it reveals his own epitaph.

“The Progress of Poesy: A Pindaric Ode” (written between 1751 and 1754)

“The Bard: A Pindaric Ode” (written between 1755 and 1757 in the style of old Celtic poetry)  set at the time of Edward I's conquest of Wales. PLOT: As the victorious army of Edward I marches along the slopes of the Snowdonian mountains near to the river Conwy they encounter a Welsh bard, who curses the king. The bard invokes the shades of Cadwallo, Urien and Mordred, three of Edward's victims, who weave the fate of Edward's Plantagenet line, dwelling on the various miseries and misfortunes of his descendants. The bard goes on to predict the return of Welsh rule over Britain in the form of the house of Tudor, and the flowering of British poetry in the verse of Spenser, Shakespeare and Milton. Dr. Johnson:  "I do not see that The Bard promotes any truth, moral or political", and found much of the imagery ridiculous.

“The Fatal Sisters: An Ode” (written in 1761 illustrating the heroic Norse style) “The Bard” and this poem ironically called by Dr. Johnson “the Wonderful Wonder of Wonders. Adapted from a Latin version of a Norse poem. Written in simple four-line stanzas with alternating rhyme and trochaic beat (seven syllables to a line)

James Macpherson (1736 –1796)

was a Scottish writer, poet, literary collector, and politician. Known for the Ossian cycle of epic poems, which he claimed to have discovered and translated from Gaelic. His work The Highlander (1758) is a six-canto epic poem.

1760: Macpherson publishes Fragments of Ancient Poetry, claiming it was collected in Scottish Highlands and translated from Gaelic. Later, he claims to find more manuscripts, including an epic on hero Fingal (“white stranger”), attributed to Ossian. The full title of the work was Fingal, an Ancient Epic Poem in Six Books, together with Several Other Poems composed by Ossian, the Son of Fingal, translated from the Gaelic Language (1761). Another related poem, Temora, followed in 1763, and a collected edition, The Works of Ossian, in 1765. Poems translated in poetic prose, short/simple sentences; epic mood without single narrative but recurring characters: Ossian (old, blind narrator), father Fingal (based on Irish Fionn mac Cumhaill), son Oscar, and Oscar’s lover Malvina (name invented by Macpherson). Themes of battles, tragic love; minimal context on religion, culture, or buildings. Goethe incorporated his translation of a part of the work into his novel The Sorrows of Young Werther. The authenticity of these translations challenged by Irish historians and also by Samuel Jonhson in A Journey to the Western Islands of Scotland (1775).

Macpherson went on to write several historical works, the most important of which was Original Papers, containing the Secret History of Great Britain from the Restoration to the Accession of the House of Hanover, to which are prefixed Extracts from the Life of James II, as written by himself (1775).

Thomas Chatterton (1752 – 1770)

was an English poet. Raised in poverty, suicide in a London garret at age 17, made him the martyr poet. Was an influence on Romantic artists of the period such as Shelley, Keats, Wordsworth and Coleridge. He was able to pass off his work as that of an imaginary 15th-century monk from Bristol called Thomas Rowley though he was denounced by Horace Walpole. The first of his literary mysteries was the dialogue of "Elinoure and Juga," pretending it was the work of a 15th-century poet. Chatterton adopted for himself the pseudonym Thomas Rowley for poetry and history. Thomas Warton, in his History of English Poetry (1778) included Rowley among 15th-century poets, but apparently did not believe in the antiquity of the poems. Chatterton's genius and his death are commemorated by Percy Bysshe Shelley in Adonais (1821); by William Wordsworth in "Resolution and Independence" (describes Chatterton as “the marvellous Boy/The Sleepless Soul that perished in his pride”); by Samuel Taylor Coleridge in "Monody on the Death of Chatterton"; by Dante Gabriel Rossetti in "Five English Poets", and in John Keats' sonnet "To Chatterton".  Keats also inscribed Endymion "to the memory of Thomas Chatterton". Two of Alfred de Vigny's works, Stello and the drama Chatterton, give fictionalized accounts of the poet.

Samuel Johnson (1709-84)

Often called Dr Johnson. The Oxford Dictionary of National Biography: "arguably the most distinguished man of letters in English history." Johnson's A Dictionary of the English Language (1755) acclaimed as a major scholarly achievement.

Dr. Johnson: devout Anglican, committed Tory. Boswell’s Life of Samuel Johnson provides detailed account of Johnson's behavior, influencing posthumous Tourette syndrome diagnosis. Walter Jackson Bate called Life "the most famous single work of biographical art."

Infant Johnson did not cry, sparking health concerns; his aunt considered him "a poor creature." Johnson improved; wet-nursed by Joan Marklew. Contracted scrofula ("King's Evil"), treated by royal touch from Queen Anne (1712); ritual failed, leading to scarring. Queen Anne gave Johnson an amulet, worn for life.

Devastated by father's death; stood bareheaded in rain by his father's old book stall to atone. Boswell’s Life led to statue being erected at the site.

Lived with friend Harry Porter during Porter’s terminal illness; Porter died on 3 Sept 1734. Courted Elizabeth Porter (46), widow of Harry Porter; she had three children. Jervis, her son, severed ties over their marriage.

13 Dec 1784, Johnson met Miss Morris and Francesco Sastres; his final words: "Iam Moriturus" ("I who am about to die"). Johnson fell into a coma and died at 7:00 p.m. Boswell described the moment as one of "Stupor." William Gerard Hamilton: "He has made a chasm…nothing can fill up." Johnson buried 20 December 1784 at Westminster Abbey; inscription reads: Samuel Johnson, LL.D. Obiit XIII die Decembris, Anno Domini M.DCC.LXXXIV. Ætatis suœ LXXV.

Suspicious of Milton's poetic language, especially blank verse, which he believed led to bad imitations. Opposed poetic language of Thomas Gray; criticized obscure allusions in works like Lycidas; preferred poetry that was clear and easily understood. Disagreed with Plutarch's use of biography for praise and morality; struggled against societal reluctance to accept tarnishing biographical details (Rambler 60). Believed autobiography and diaries were significant; in Idler 84, claimed autobiography least likely to distort life. Works on Shakespeare: Preface to Shakespeare, rejected classical unities, argued drama should reflect life.

Accounts of his life: A Biographical Sketch of Dr. Samuel Johnson (Thomas Tyers, 1784), The Journal of a Tour to the Hebrides (Boswell, 1785), Anecdotes of the Late Samuel Johnson (Hester Thrale), Life of Samuel Johnson (John Hawkins), An Essay on the Life and Genius of Samuel Johnson (Arthur Murphy, 1792).

Matthew Arnold, in Six Chief Lives from Johnson's Lives of the Poets, regarded Milton, Dryden, Pope, Addison, Swift, and Gray as "points" to return to for literary navigation. F. R. Leavis agreed with Arnold and praised Johnson's criticism as "powerful and distinguished," highlighting it as genuine, first-hand criticism.

Birmingham Journal (1732–33): Birmingham Journal: First newspaper in Birmingham, England. Only surviving copy: Number 28, dated May 21, 1733 (likely first published Nov 14, 1732). Published weekly on Thursdays by Thomas Warren from his house over the Swan Tavern on High Street. Samuel Johnson contributed essays while lodging with Warren in 1733. Plan for a Dictionary of the English Language (1747)

The Rambler (1750–52): 208 articles, published Tuesdays and Saturdays. Neoclassical style, aimed at rising middle class seeking integration into aristocratic circles. Essays focused on moral issues, didactic but explorative; emphasized "profit and delight." Cheaply available for middle-class readers. Inspired by The Spectator (1711–1712, Addison and Steele) but more serious, sermon-like, and less dramatic. Revived in 1901 by Herbert Vivian; ended in 1902, lacked public favor.

The Adventurer (1753–54): London 18th-century bi-weekly newspaper undertaken after the successful conclusion of The Rambler. Contributors included John Hawkesworth and Samuel Johnson.

The Idler (1758–60): Series of 103 essays (1758–1760), mainly by Samuel Johnson, published in Universal Chronicle. Chronicle likely created to feature The Idler, ceased when the series ended. Other contributors: Thomas Warton, Bennet Langton, Joshua Reynolds. James Boswell noted Johnson wrote some essays hastily, including one completed in 30 minutes at Oxford. When collected in book form, the essay The Vulture (anti-war satire) was omitted and replaced with one on debtors’ imprisonment. Essays were originally unsigned ("Idler"); titles added in book form. Johnson specified 12 essays were not his; authorship of five remains unclear. Key Essays:

  • No. 1 (Idler's Character, 15 April 1758): Introduces the Idler as "censorious," promises satire, and praises satisfaction with simplicity.
  • No. 2 (Invitation to Correspondents, 22 April 1758): Requests essays, stresses secrecy for contributors.
  • No. 3 (Idler's Reason for Writing, 29 April 1758): Offers "intellectual food" for idle minds, critiques criticism's harm.
  • No. 101 (Omar's Plan of Life, 22 March 1760): Ambitions unfulfilled due to life's distractions.
  • No. 102 (Authors Inattentive to Themselves, 29 March 1760): Advocates for authors’ memoirs; explores fame’s fleeting nature.
  • No. 103 (Horrour of the Last, 5 April 1760): Reflects on endings; ties to Holy Week and life's transience.

A Journey to the Western Islands of Scotland (1775): Travel narrative by Samuel Johnson about an 83-day journey through Scotland and the Hebrides in 1773.  Johnson (63) traveled with friend James Boswell (32), who also published A Journal of a Tour to the Hebrides (1785). Often published together for contrasting perspectives: Johnson focused on Scotland; Boswell on Johnson. Boswell: Johnson saw Scotland's four universities, three main cities, and Highland/insular life for "philosophical contemplation."

Taxation no Tyranny (1775): an influential essay which addressed the issue of Parliamentary sovereignty in the United Kingdom in response to the Declaration and Resolves of the First Continental Congress. Famous quote: "In sovereignty, there are no gradations." Influenced John Wesley’s A Calm Address to Our American Colonies.

Poetry:

Messiah (1728), a translation into Latin of Alexander Pope's Messiah (1712); Johnson translated it into Latin in Dec. 1728. First published poem by Johnson; 119 lines of Latin verse, completed in 2 days. Submitted to Pope for appraisal; included in Miscellany of Poems (1731) by John Husbands. Pope's Messiah based on Virgil's Fourth Eclogue, merging Isaiah's prophecy with Virgilian language. Johnson's Latin translation relies directly on Virgil, incorporating more of the Eclogue's language. Brought to Pope by Charles Arbuthnot, who claimed Pope praised it, saying he couldn't distinguish it from the "original." Walter Jackson Bate called the translation a "major effort."

London (1738): his first major published work. A 263-line poem imitating Juvenal's Third Satire, with the character Thales deciding to leave London for Wales. Johnson imitated Juvenal because of his admiration for the Roman poet and to follow the 18th-century trend of Augustan poets, led by Alexander Pope. Published anonymously in May 1738, it went into a second edition the same year. Quickly praised, notably by Pope, marking the second time Pope praised Johnson’s poetry (the first being for Messiah). A part of 18th-century neoclassical poetry, based on Juvenal's Third Satire, where Umbricius leaves Rome for Cumae to escape vices and dangers, mirrored by Thales’s escape to Cambria (Wales). Epigraph from Juvenal: "Quis ineptae [iniquae] / Tam patiens urbis, tam ferreus ut teneat se?" ("Who is so patient of the foolish [wicked] city, so iron-willed, as to contain himself?"). Describes London's problems, emphasizing crime, corruption, and the squalor of the poor, personified as Malice, Rapine, and Accident, who conspire to destroy the city. The poem begins with Thales bidding the town farewell: "Though grief and fondness in my breast rebel, When injur'd Thales bids the town farewell..." Main emphasis of the poem: "Slow rises worth, by poverty depressed" (line 177).

Prologue at the Opening of the Theatre in Drury Lane (1747)

The Vanity of Human Wishes (1749): The Vanity of Human Wishes: The Tenth Satire of Juvenal Imitated written in late 1748, published in 1749. First published work with Johnson's name on the title page. Imitates Juvenal’s Satire X, but differs by sympathizing with his subjects. Focuses on human futility, the quest for greatness, and concludes that Christian values are crucial for proper living. Second imitation of Juvenal by Johnson; first was London (1738). Emphasizes philosophy over politics, unlike London. The poem wasn’t financially successful, but later critics, including Walter Scott and T.S. Eliot, considered it Johnson’s greatest poem. Howard D. Weinbrot called it "one of the great poems in the English language."  368 lines, written in closed heroic couplets. Opening lines announce the universal theme that "the antidote to vain human wishes is non-vain spiritual wishes." Depicts Charles as a "Soul of Fire," with a military career destined to end in disaster, a "cautionary example." Famous passage reduces the king's career to a moral lesson: "He left the Name, at which the World grew pale, To point a Moral, or adorn a Tale." Draws from Johnson’s own experience as a writer. Original manuscript (lines 159–160) included "Garret" and "Jayl"; later revised after Johnson's disillusionment with patronage: "Toil, Envy, Want, the Patron, and the Jail." Weinbrot and Robert Folkenflik say The Vanity of Human Wishes is superior to London. Samuel Beckett, an admirer of Johnson, worked on a play entitled Human Wishes, inspired by the poem.

Irene, a Tragedy (1749): written between 1726 and 1749, considered his greatest failure. First performed 6 February 1749, directed by David Garrick, his friend and former pupil. Never revived during Johnson’s lifetime; no full-scale productions until 1999, making it one of the most unsuccessful plays by a major author. Johnson’s main source: Richard Knolles’s Generall Historie of the Turkes (1603). Also used George Sandys’s Relation of a Journey... (1615), Herbelot’s Bibliothèque Orientale (1697), and Humphrey Prideaux’s Life of Mahomet (1697). Plot: Sultan Mahomet conquers Constantinople, captures Greek Christian Irene, and offers her life and power if she converts to Islam. Johnson emphasizes Irene's temptation and the corruption of worldly power. Irene’s conversion leads to a fall-out with her virtuous friend Aspasia and a power struggle at court. Mahomet suspects Irene and her advisor Bassa of plotting against him. Irene is killed by Abdalla’s captains, but reveals that Bassa's true accomplice was the Greek soldier Demetrius, who escapes with his lover, Aspasia. Mahomet distraught upon learning Irene's innocence. The play is in blank verse but criticized by Walter Jackson Bate for being overly rigid with meter, sacrificing style and rhythm.

Life of Mr Richard Savage (1744): Life of Savage (full title: An Account of the Life of Mr Richard Savage, Son of the Earl Rivers) was Johnson's first major biography. Published anonymously in 1744, almost 200 pages long detailing the life of Richard Savage, a London poet and Johnson’s friend, who died in 1743. Includes Savage's claim of being the illegitimate son of a noble family who abandoned him at birth. Later included in The Lives of the Poets (1779). Described as "one of the best short biographies in English." Johnson dedicated a lot of time to it, claiming to write "forty-eight octavo pages at a sitting."

Lives of the Poets (1779–81): biographies and critical appraisals of 52 poets, most from the 18th century. Poets arranged by date of death, with special prefaces written for most, except for some prior works: Richard Savage’s 1744 life largely unchanged; Article on the Earl of Roscommon, from The Gentleman's Magazine (1748), revised to fit the series; “Dissertation on Pope’s Epitaphs” (1756) added to Pope’s life; William Collins’ character from The Poetical Calendar (1763) included; Sir Herbert Croft wrote the life of Edward Young at Johnson’s request, as Croft knew him personally.

Included lengthy quotes from other authors, such as Edmund Smith’s “Prefatory Discourse” on John Philips. Excluded recently deceased poets, like Charles Churchill (due to Johnson’s disapproval) and Oliver Goldsmith (likely due to copyright issues). Women poets were comprehensively omitted, which was criticized. Some details considered unreliable, with criticisms of Johnson’s prejudices: Criticism of Milton’s Lycidas, Gray’s Odes, and Swift’s works; Hostile characterization of Metaphysical poets, particularly Abraham Cowley. 1821–1824: Cary published essays in The London Magazine. Essays collected posthumously in 1846 as Lives of English Poets, from Johnson to Kirke White. Work intended as a continuation of Johnson's Lives of the Poets. Matthew Arnold's Six Chief Lives (1878) highlights Milton, Dryden, Pope, Addison, Swift, and Gray as key figures in Johnson's work.

The poets included are: Abraham Cowley, Sir John Denham, John Milton, Samuel Butler (Hudibras), John Wilmot, Earl of Rochester, Wentworth Dillon, Earl of Roscommon, Thomas Otway (Mnemonic:A Clever Student Just Makes Smart Jokes With Every Thought Offered.)…John Dyer, William Shenstone, Edward Young, David Mallet, Mark Akenside, Thomas Gray, George Lord Lyttelton. (Mnemonic:Just Dance With Every Dog Making Their Great Leap)

A Dictionary of the English Language (1755):  A Dictionary of the English Language by Samuel Johnson, published 15 April 1755. Written at 17 Gough Square, London (1746–1755). Plan of a Dictionary (1747) outlined methodology, influenced by legal precedents.  Patron: Philip Stanhope, Earl of Chesterfield, though Johnson criticized his delayed support. First edition: 42,773 words, printed in two large folio volumes.  Featured 114,000 literary quotations (e.g., Shakespeare, Milton, Dryden). Notable definitions: "Excise" (a hateful tax), "Lexicographer" (a harmless drudge), "Oats" (grain for horses in England, food for Scots).  Alphabetical order followed 18th-century conventions (e.g., e.g., "jargon" precedes "idle," "vagabond" before "ultimate”). Two forms: 1755 folio edition (two volumes) and 1756 abridged edition (two octavo volumes). Johnson called the book "Vasta mole superbus" ("Proud in its great bulk"). Referenced in Vanity Fair by Thackeray, where Becky Sharp throws a copy out the window.

The History of Rasselas, Prince of Abissinia (1759): originally titled The Prince of Abissinia: A Tale, is a philosophical apologue by Johnson, focusing on bliss and ignorance. Written in one week at age 50 to fund his mother’s funeral. The story follows Rasselas, the fourth son of the King of Abyssinia (modern-day Ethiopia), who is trapped in the "Happy Valley" until the throne is his. He escapes with his sister Nekayah, attendant Pekuah, and poet-friend Imlac, seeking happiness in Cairo and Suez. The narrative is episodic, illustrating the futility of their search for happiness. Johnson’s writing is described as slow and musical, with balanced sentences. He wrote while mourning his mother. Rasselas and Voltaire’s Candide (both published in 1759) are similar in theme, involving young men traveling with teachers to explore human suffering and the pursuit of happiness, but Johnson’s focus is on the possibility of attaining happiness, not a critique of Leibniz’s philosophy. Johnson, a devout Christian, does not attack religious responses to life’s questions, unlike Voltaire’s satirical approach in Candide. Johnson was a strong opponent of slavery, and Rasselas became a name adopted by emancipated slaves. Rasselas inspired at least two continuations: Dinarbas (1790) by Cornelia Knight; The Second Part of the History of Rasselas (1835) by Elizabeth Pope Whately (wife of Richard Whately). Rasselas is mentioned in later notable literature:

  • Mansfield Park (1814) by Jane Austen – Fanny Price compares it to Mansfield Park.
  • Jane Eyre (1847) by Charlotte Brontë – Helen Burns reads it.
  • Cranford (1851) by Elizabeth Gaskell – Captain Brown denigrates it, offending Miss Jenkyns.
  • The House of the Seven Gables (1851) by Nathaniel Hawthorne – Hepzibah Pyncheon reads it.
  • The Mill on the Floss (1860) by George Eliot – Maggie reads it.
  • Little Women (1868) by Louisa May Alcott – Jo drops the book while talking to Mr. Laurence.
  • Middlemarch (1871) by George Eliot – Lydgate enjoys it as a child, along with Gulliver’s Travels and the Bible.

Explorer Henry Stanley read Rasselas when young, after being released from a Victorian workhouse. The Mountains of Rasselas by Thomas Pakenham (1991) references Johnson’s work. Rasselas was translated into Amharic in 1946/47 by Sirak Heruy, son of Ethiopian intellectual Heruy Welde Sellase. C.S. Lewis references Rasselas in a footnote in The Abolition of Man (1943).  Rasselas is mentioned in Ursula Dubosarsky’s novels Zizzy Zing and Abyssinia. Rasselas appears in The Book of Sequels by Henry Beard, Christopher Cerf, Sarah Durkee, and Sean Kelly. The description of the Happy Valley is similar to Samuel Taylor Coleridge’s Kubla Khan (1797), though published in 1816. A quote from Rasselas, “Rasselas was too happy and went out to seek unhappiness,” appears in Morning Mist, a short story by Japanese author Tatsuo Nagai (translated by Edward Seidensticker). Emily Dickinson referred to a bird as "Mr. Rasselas" in a letter to Mary Bowles (1859).

Charles Churchill (1732 -1764)

was an English poet and minor verse satirist. Churchill's The Rosciad (March 1761) satirically critiques London actors, with Shakespeare and Jonson naming Garrick the top actor. His Apology to Critical Reviewers argues that only authors attack each other, repeating his stage critique and praising John Dryden. In Night, an Epistle to Robert Lloyd (1761), Churchill defended himself, arguing faults were better than hypocrisy. The Prophecy of Famine: A Scots Pastoral (1763) was based on a paper for The North Briton. Epistle to William Hogarth (1763) responded to Hogarth's caricature of Wilkes, attacking Hogarth's vanity and envy. Hogarth retaliated with an engraving from The Painter and his Pug, depicting Churchill as a bear in torn clerical bands, hugging a pot of porter and a club labeled with "lies" and "NB," while Hogarth's pug, Trump, urinates on Churchill's Epistle. The Duellist (1763) satirizes Wilkes's opponents in the House of Lords, especially Bishop Warburton. He attacked Dr Johnson among others in The Ghost as "Pomposo, insolent and loud, Vain idol of a scribbling crowd".

Other poems are: The Conference (1763); The Author (1763), highly praised by Churchill's contemporaries; Gotham (1764), a poem on the duties of a king, didactic rather than satiric in tone; The Candidate (1764), a satire on John Montagu; The Farewell (1764); The Times (1764); Independence, and an unfinished Journey. A partial collection of Churchill's poems appeared in 1763.

Christopher Smart (1722 – 1771)

English poet, contributor to The Midwife and The Student. Friend of Samuel Johnson and Henry Fielding. Pseudonym "Mrs. Mary Midnight"; confined to a mental asylum by father-in-law John Newbery for supposed religious "mania". Released but burdened by debt; died in debtors' prison. Major works: A Song to David and Jubilate Agno (written during asylum confinement). Known for Seaton Prize poems and mock epic The Hilliad. Explored themes: religion, nature, and English nationalism. Feud over "Night Piece" with William Kenrick, who attacked it in Kapelion, or Poetical Ordinary. Responded via The Midwife and planned An Old Woman’s Dunciad, but Kenrick preemptively used the title. Engaged in a "paper war" (1752) after Poems on Several Occasions (including The Hop-Garden). John Hill attacked Smart's poetry; Smart replied with The Hilliad, a notable critique during the literary feud involving Henry Fielding and Grub Street writers. Reputation as a "dedicated gardener"; The Hop-Garden showcased this, even during asylum confinement.

Major Works:

A Song to David (1763): poem focuses on King David’s life, shifts to Christ and Christianity. Republished in 1765 in A Translation of the Psalms of David, Attempted in the Spirit of Christianity, with Psalms and Hymns and Spiritual Songs. Four stanzas set in Malcolm Archer’s "Praise Above All, for Praise Prevails" for St. Helena Church’s 300th anniversary in 2012. Bridges human and divinely inspired Biblical poetry. David as a poetic creativity symbol in Jubilate Agno, becomes a religious poet model here. Christ becomes central; poem ends with a "final rush for glory." Features "seven pillar" imagery linked to Freemasonry and Solomon’s Temple. Pillars interpreted as:

  • Alpha – mason's compass, "God as Architect."
  • Gamma – mason's square, "vault of heaven."
  • Eta – Jacob's ladder, seven pillars.
  • Theta – all-seeing eye or point in circle.
  • Iota – pillar and temple.
  • Sigma – blazing star or hexalpha.
  • Omega – lyre, David as poet.

Influenced by Newton’s Chronology of Ancient Kingdoms Amended, Bunyan’s Solomon’s Temple Spiritualiz’d, and the Geneva Bible. William Mason thought Smart was "mad" after reading the poem. Browning praised it in Parleyings (1887), ranking Smart with Milton and Keats. Browning described Smart’s poetry as revealing divine truth: "Pierced the screen / Twixt thing and word, lit language straight from soul."  Browning’s remarks inspired later recognition and appreciation of the work.

The Hilliad (1753): mock epic poem by Smart targeting John Hill. Title puns on Pope's The Dunciad and references the Iliad, showing Smart's debt to Pope’s form and style. Innit "Book the First": Hillario (Hill) seduced by a Sibyl, leaves apothecary work, becomes writer, descends into "arch-dunce." Origin: literary dispute ("paper war") between Hill and Henry Fielding; Hill attacked Smart's Poems on Several Occasions. Smart retaliated with The Hilliad, calling it "balance due" for Hill's treatment of him. Work prompted various responses but marked Smart’s last major role in the conflict; attacks on Smart ceased afterward. Poem: 259 lines with "Notes Variorum" more than doubling its length. Likely contributors to notes: Smart, Arthur Murphy, and Henry Fielding. Preface: Smart’s letter and a reply (likely by Murphy) linking The Hilliad to works by Pope and Fielding critiquing corrupt writers. "Prolegomena" includes extracts attacking Smart, notably from Impertinent and Gentleman's Magazine 

Features "An Accurate and Impartial STATE of the ACCOUNT Between Mr. Smart and Dr. Hill": six essays from each, listing Hill’s "praises" versus Smart’s "abuses." Opening lines mock Hill’s many titles: "O thou, whatever name delight thine ear,/ Pimp! Poet! Puffer! 'Pothecary! Play'r!

Sibyl transforms Hillario into "th' INSPECTOR," echoing Pope’s Dulness and Smart’s "Mrs Midnight" Hillario’s fate: "The UNIVERSAL BUTT of all mankind" (Line 237). Concludes with Fame proclaiming Hill’s eternal duncehood while praising great art, music, and literature.

The Hop-Garden first published in Poems on Several Occasions (1752). Rooted in Virgilian georgic and Augustan literature; one of Smart's first long poems. Literally about a hop garden, teaching proper farming methods in the Virgilian tradition. Split into two books (733 lines total), with 429 lines in Book 1 and 304 in Book 2, written in Miltonic blank verse. Expected to use Augustan rhyming couplets, but Smart uses Miltonic language, emphasizing this in the opening lines: “Under what sign to pluck the crop, and how…I teach in verse Miltonian”. Expresses anti-Hanoverian views, critiquing Walpole and the later Carteret/Compton administration. Personal work, with many biographical references, including the death of his friend Theophilus Wheeler. In memory of Wheeler, Smart dedicates a portion of Book 2: "THEOPHILUS, thou dear departed soul..." The Hop-Garden is examined in Chapter 4 of Chris Mounsey's biography of Smart.

Hymns and Spiritual Songs for the Fasts and Festivals of the Church of England (1765): Includes a translation of Psalms of David and a new version of A Song to David. Written while in a mental asylum, during the same period as Jubilate Agno. Hymns modeled after Robert Nelson’s tradition of hymn writing. Relied on the Bible and the Book of Common Prayer for hymn content. Contains 35 hymns.

Hymns for the Amusement of Children (1771) Smart’s final work, completed while imprisoned for debt. His last exploration of religion, completed shortly before his death. First hymns dedicated to children, teaching Christian virtues. Dedicated to Prince Frederick, Bishop of Osnabrug. Structure: 3 Theological Virtues (Faith, Hope, Charity), 4 Cardinal Virtues (Prudence, Justice, Temperance, Fortitude), plus Mercy. 6 hymns on Christian duties, 10 on the Gospels, followed by miscellaneous Christian virtues. Total of 39 hymns. Hymn XXIX ("Long-Suffering of God") noted as particularly poignant. Final poem: Hymn XXXIX, "The Conclusion of the Matter."

Jubilate Agno ("Rejoice in the Lamb") written between 1759-1763 during his confinement for insanity at St. Luke’s Hospital, Bethnal Green, London. First published in 1939 as Rejoice in the Lamb: A Song from Bedlam. Divided into four fragments (A, B, C, D), with over 1,200 lines. Sections starting with "Let" associate Biblical human figures with animals, symbolizing religious themes like baptism. Sections starting with "For" consist of aphoristic verses. Fragment A: Pairs Patriarchs with animals (e.g., Noah, Abraham, Balaam, Daniel), using symbolic consistency (e.g., Balaam with the ass, Abraham with a ram). Fragment B: Mixes Old and New Testament figures with local animals or puns, such as Salmon with Salome for John the Baptist. The fish image is expanded, symbolizing the Apostles as "fishers of men." Pairing stops at B295, after which only "For" verses remain. Fragment C: Resumes pairing of Biblical figures from the books of Ezra and Nehemiah with plants/herbs. Fragment D: Personal friends and acquaintances of Smart are paired with stones, gems, minerals, and some herbs. References to scientific works of John Locke and Isaac Newton throughout the poem. Jeoffry, Smart’s cat, is a central figure in the poem, praised for serving God and displaying virtues. The 74-line section about Jeoffry is the most famous and widely anthologized part of the poem. Jeoffry is considered "the most famous cat in English literature." Jeoffry is transformed into a manifestation of the Ars Poetica tradition in the poem.

A Defence of Freemasonry (1765): also known as A Defence of Freemasonry as practised in the regular lodges. Refuted Laurence Dermott's Ahiman Rezon and its claims about Freemasonry. Response to Dermott's queries about regular Masons. No direct attribution on title page, but established as his work since publication. Includes a poem directly attributed to him.

Other Poems: “Epistle to Mrs. Tyler”; “On A Lady Throwing Snow-Balls At Her Lover”; “For I Will Consider My Cat Jeoffry”; “On My Wife's Birth-Day”; “The Sweets Of Evening”; “Where's The Poker?”; “The Pig; The Long-Nosed Fair”; The "Seatonian Prize" poems

William Cowper (1731-1800)

English poet, Anglican hymnwriter, forerunner of Romantic poetry. Coleridge called him "the best modern poet"; Wordsworth admired "Yardley-Oak." After being institutionalized for insanity, found refuge in evangelical Christianity. Suffered doubt about salvation; in 1773, believed he was doomed to eternal damnation but later recovered. Known for religious hymns, many written with John Newton, who wrote Amazing Grace. Olney Hymns (1779) includes "Praise for the Fountain Opened"(beginning "There is a fountain fill'd with blood") and "Light Shining out of Darkness “(coined the phrase: "God moves in a mysterious way/ His wonders to perform”) Wrote anti-slavery poems; The Negro's Complaint (1788) became famous, quoted by Martin Luther King Jr. in civil rights speeches. His mother's death at an early age troubled him deeply, leading to the poem "On the Receipt of My Mother's Picture," (written 1790 and published in 1798) written 50+ years later. Wrote The Progress of Error, suggested by Mary Unwin to keep his mind occupied, then wrote seven other satires. These poems were collected and published in Poems by William Cowper, of the Inner Temple, Esq. (1782).

Poems:

The Snail (1770); The Winter Nosegay (1777)

Olney Hymns (1778–1779; in collaboration with John Newton): Written for Newton's rural parish, reflecting Evangelical movement ideologies. 348 hymns in total; 6 regularly sung today, including "Amazing Grace", "Glorious Things of Thee Are Spoken", and "How Sweet the Name of Jesus Sounds". Newton's preface: hymns should be for public worship and "plain people". Cowper wrote 66 hymns (1772-1773), Newton wrote the rest. "Oh for a closer walk with God" written by Cowper in response to Mary Unwin's illness. "Amazing Grace" originally titled Faith's Review and Expectation; appears as Hymn 41 in Book I. Olney Hymns divided into three books: Book I: Select Texts of Scripture (Bible as authority). Book II: Occasional Subjects (focus on Evangelical spiritual life). Book III: Progress and Changes of Spiritual Life (stages of spiritual awakening).

The Diverting History of John Gilpin Shewing how he went Farther than he intended, and came safe Home again (1782): Comic ballad about John Gilpin, a draper, who rides a runaway horse. Inspired by a story from Lady Anna Austen during Cowper's depression. The poem cheered Cowper and he turned it into verse. Published anonymously in Public Advertiser (1782), then with The Task (1785).

Table Talk (1782): Form: Rhymed couplets. This work consists of Cowper’s reflections on various topics, from religion to the role of the poet in society. It’s mostly a dialogue in couplets. The poem is often criticized for its dullness. However, it contains sharp critiques of Pope’s mechanical poetry. Notable Passages: A critique of Pope’s style: “Then Pope, as harmony itself exact, / In verse well disciplined, complete, compact, / Gave Virtue and Morality a grace, / That, quite eclipsing Pleasure’s painted face, / Levied a tax of wonder and applause, / E’en on the fools that trampled on their laws.”

Epitaph on a Hare (1782); Poems by William Cowper, of the Inner Temple, Esq. (1782; Cowper's First Independent Volume); The Rose (1783)

The Task: A Poem, in Six Books (1785): considered his supreme achievement. Form: Blank verse. Six books: "The Sofa," "The Timepiece," "The Garden," "The Winter Evening," "The Winter Morning Walk," "The Winter Walk at Noon." Begins with a mock-Miltonic passage on the origins of the sofa. Develops into a meditation on nature, the retired life, religious faith, with critiques of slavery, blood sports, fashionable frivolity, lukewarm clergy, and French despotism. Preface: A lady, fond of blank verse, asked Cowper to write a poem on the sofa. Cowper, with much leisure, combined this with other subjects, leading to the creation of a serious volume instead of a simple trifle. Extensively quoted in Jane Austen’s novels, influential on her works. The conversational diction of the Lake Poets, including Coleridge and Wordsworth, stems from The Task. Coleridge praised the poem’s "divine chit-chat," "chastity of diction," and "harmony of blank verse." Charles Lamb in a 1796 letter mentioned Coleridge’s appreciation for Cowper and wrote of The Task as an old favorite. Wordsworth, influenced by the poem, borrowed a copy as a schoolboy, and The Task's influence is seen in Tintern Abbey and The Prelude.  Late 19th-century novelist George Gissing read three books of The Task in April 1892, calling it "rather a favourite of mine, oddly."  Notable Passages: The countryside, particularly the river Ouse, is described with great affection and detail, reflecting Cowper’s love for nature.

Cowper’s humanist approach is evident in his reflections on compassion and shared humanity, such as in the famous lines from Book III: “I was born of woman, and drew milk, / As sweet as charity, from human breasts. / How then should I and any man that lives / Be strangers to each other?”

The Morning Dream (1788); Homer's Iliad and Odyssey (1791; translations from the Greek); The Retired Cat (1791); To Mary (1793); The Poplar-Field (1785)

The Castaway (written 1799): published posthumously 1803. Form: Rhymed verse with a strong, relentless rhythm. This poem describes the fate of a sailor lost at sea, who struggles to survive for an hour before ultimately drowning. The poem is often interpreted as an expression of Cowper’s own feelings of desolation and abandonment, but also reflects the poet’s broader concerns with human suffering and divine indifference. Notable Passages: The sailor’s lonely, doomed struggle: “No voice divine the storm allayed, / No light propitious shone; / When, snatched from all effectual aid, / We perished, each alone.” The final turn to Cowper’s own sense of personal desolation: “But I beneath a rougher sea, / And whelmed in deeper gulphs than he.”

George Crabbe (1754-1832)

English poet, surgeon, clergyman. Known for realistic narrative style, focus on middle/working-class life. He is considered a belated Augustan poet who bridged the gap into the Romantic era. Early Work: Crabbe’s early work was praised by prominent figures like Edmund Burke and Dr. Samuel Johnson. Later, Lord Byron described him as “Nature’s sternest painter, yet the best.” First major work: Inebriety (1775), satirical poem in Pope's couplet form; self-published and later disowned. He preferred the heroic couplet, a verse form inherited from Alexander Pope and Samuel Johnson. Moralist and Psychologist: Crabbe believed that stories of human behavior would engage readers’ curiosity and provide insight into human nature.

His poetry often explores the ironies of character and fate. His natural descriptions, particularly of the Suffolk coast, are well-known and often colored by the moral tone of the characters being described (e.g., Peter Grimes in The Borough). Although his poetry influenced the development of the English novel (particularly in its detailed character studies), Crabbe was not a novelist. He destroyed the few prose novels he attempted. Was a coleopterist; discovered first Suffolk record of Calosoma sycophanta L. Wrote Natural History of the Vale of Belvoir in Bibliotheca Topographia Britannica (1790); extensive beetle list, over 70 species.

Key Works:

Inebriety (1775); The Candidate (1780); The Library (1781)

The Village (1783): His first major poem, depicting the poverty and harsh realities of life in a Suffolk coastal village. It contrasts the grim truth of village life with the sentimentalized portrayal in Oliver Goldsmith’s The Deserted Village. Achieved popularity, praised by Samuel Johnson ("original, vigorous, elegant") and James Boswell.

The Newspaper (1785)

The Parish Register (1807): A series of sketches describing village life, presenting a cross-section of the community and individual stories. Crabbe rejected the idealized view of the peasantry, instead offering a more realistic portrayal.

The Borough (1810): A loose collection of anecdotes, character sketches, and stories. Written in heroic couplets. Series of 24 letters on borough life and inhabitants' stories. Famous Letter XXII (Peter Grimes) inspired Britten’s opera. Letter XXI: story of Abel Keene, a schoolmaster-turned-clerk, who hangs himself. Begun in 1804, predating The Parish Register (1807). Bridges pastoral themes of The Village and individual life stories of Tales.

Tales in Verse* (1812): A series of unrelated verse tales with no overarching narrative or thematic link.

Tales of the Hall* (1819): A more structured work, using the device of two half-brothers recounting their experiences. This structure allowed for a more dynamic and layered exploration of character and moral themes.

Posthumous Tales (1834); New Poems by George Crabbe (1960); The Voluntary Insane (1995)
