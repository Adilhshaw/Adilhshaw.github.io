Henry Fielding (1707-54) 

As a magistrate, founded the Bow Street Runners, London's first professional police force. He went to Portugal in 1754 to seek a cure of his diseases but died in Lisbon.

Fielding was "unshakably a Whig" . He,  in his first writings used two forms of "rhetorical poses" that were popular during the eighteenth century. These are: "the non-ironic pseudonym such as Addison and Steele used in the Spectator, and the ironic mask or Persona, such as Swift used in A Modest Proposal."

Wrote political satire and satires of current arts and letters. After Licencing Act 1737, triggered by annonymous The Golden Rump, Fielding retired from the theatre and resumed his legal career. In The Craftsman, Fielding voiced an opposition attack on bribery and corruption in British politics.

The Covent-Garden Journal, published under the pseudonym "Sir Alexander Drawcansir, Knt., Censor of Great Britain" started 1752 gave rise to Paper War, 1752-53. Attacks authors on London's Grub Street (hack writers) in the first issue (Jan. 1752). Hill wrote as "The Inspector", Smart as Mrs Mary Midnight. Began as a dispute between Fielding and John Hill, other authors, such as Christopher Smart, Bonnell Thornton, William Kenrick, Arthur Murphy and Tobias Smollett, were soon dedicating their works to aid various sides of the conflict. Mock heroic poems began starting with Smart's The Hilliad.

Rape upon Rape; or, The Justice Caught in his own Trap aka The Coffee-House Politician (1730) One of the 'she tragedies’. Though the play was influenced by the rape case of Colonel Francis Charteris, it used "rape" as an allegory to describe all abuses of freedom, as well as the corruption of power, though it was meant in a comedic, farcical manner. It is known that another act was added to the play, titled The Battle of the Poets, on 30 November 1730, but its author is unknown (?). Adapted into musical titled  Lock Up Your Daughters (1959).

The play is begun with a prologue that describes the origins of the heroic Muse who uses satire to deal with those who are villains. However, she is not limited to satire, and the heroic Muse is said to provoke individuals towards the destruction of evil in general.

After this set up, Fielding introduces a traditional comedic love plot in which Hilaret contemplates running off and marrying Constant. Her father, Politic, stops her from running off with Constant to argue about the role of private and public concerns in regards to love. Their discussion serves as a model for the actions surrounding Justice Squeezum and Justice Worthy, particularly Justice Squeezum's corruption when it comes to the judicial system, and the play promotes the need for virtue within public and private settings.

As the plot progresses, Hilaret is set upon by Ramble, which provokes her to cry out "rape". During this time, Constant, in an attempt to help a woman, is also accused of rape. They are taken before Justice Squeezum, who reveals his abuses of the judicial system. Hilaret comically forms a plot to catch the corrupt Justice Squeezum; he is able to overcome it because Fielding promotes dealing with corruption only through legal means, which Hilaret fails to do when she resorts to relying on falsified evidence. Although Justice Squeezum and his corrupt nature almost overcomes the other characters, the manner in which this happens is done in a farcical way to provoke laughter.

The Author's Farce and the Pleasures of the Town (1730) Written in response to the Theatre Royal's rejection of his earlier plays, it was his first stage success. Written in 3 acts: The first and second acts deal with the attempts of the central character, Harry Luckless, Harriot, the daughter of his landlady Mrs. Moneywood, and his efforts to make money by writing plays. When his friend, Witmore, pays his rent behind his back, Luckless steals the money from Mrs. Moneywood. In the second act, he finishes a puppet theatre play titled The Pleasures of the Town, about the Goddess Nonsense's choice of a husband from allegorical representatives of theatre and other literary genres. It begins when the Goddess of Nonsense chooses a mate from a series of suitors along the River Styx. All dunces, the suitors include Dr. Orator, Sir Farcical Comic, Mrs. Novel, Bookseller, Poet, Monsieur Pantomime, Don Tragedio and Signior Opera. She finally chooses foreign castrato, Signor Opera.  Mrs. Novel then claims that she loved Signior Opera, and died giving birth to his child. After its rejection by one theatre, Luckless's play is staged at another. The third act becomes a play within a play, in which the characters in the puppet play are portrayed by humans. The Author's Farce ends with a merging of the play's and the puppet show's realities. The play within the play is interrupted by Constable and Murdertext, who arrive to arrest Luckless "for abusing Nonsense", but Mrs. Novel persuades Murdertext to let the play finish. Someone from the land of Bantam then arrives to tell Luckless that he is the prince of Bantam. News follows that the King of Bantam has died, and that Luckless is to be made the new king. The play concludes with the revelation that Luckless's landlady is in reality the Queen of "Old Brentford" and that her daughter, Harriot, is now royalty. An epilogue in which four poets discuss how the play should end is brought to a conclusion by a cat, in the form of a woman.

The structure and plot of The Author's Farce are similar to those of George Farquhar's Love and a Bottle (1698), in that both plays describe the relationship between an author and his landlady.

Tom Thumb (1730) written as Scriblerus Secondus, is an addition to The Author's Farce. In another edition, the new Act, titled The Battle of the Poets, or, The Contention for the Laurel, was added, not by Fielding. In its preface says that our Grubstreet authors are enamoured by heroic accounts of Homer or Virgil, Livy or Plutarch while they are not good for society. We have our own heroes like “Tom Tram, Hickathrift &c” which they should have used. The play is a low tragedy that describes Tom Thumb, small in size ans status,  arriving at King Arthur's court showing off giants that he defeated. As a reward, Arthur grants Tom the hand of princess Huncamunca, which upsets both his wife, Dollalolla, and a member of the court, Grizzle. The two plot together to ruin the marriage, which begins the tragedy. Part way through the play, two doctors begin to discuss the death of Tom Thumb and resort to using fanciful medical terminology and quoting ancient medical works with which they are not familiar. However, it becomes apparent that it was not Tom who died, but a monkey.[10] The tragedy becomes farcical when Tom is devoured by a cow. This is not the end of Tom, because his ghost later suffers a second death at the hands of Grizzle. Then, one by one, the other characters farcically kill each other, leaving only the king at the end to kill himself. Jonathan Swift, when witnessing the death of Tom's ghost, was reported in the memoirs of Mrs. Pilkington to have laughed out loud; such a feat was rare for Swift and this was only the second time such an occurrence was said to have happened.

It was revised as Tragedy of Tragedies.

The Tragedy of Tragedies; or, The Life and Death of Tom Thumb the Great (1731) the printed version under  pseudonym H. Scriblerus Secundus, this play focuses on abuse of language. It is his most enduring play.  Also replaced some of the humour in favour of biting satire. Performed with  companion piece The Letter Writers. Fielding believed modern tragedies to be superior to the Classic Greek tragedies because the Classics could only invoke pity and fear in the audience, while modern tragedies were able to leave the audience laughing at the ridiculous situations onstage. Fielding referred to these modern works as "laughing tragedies" and claimed that the only difference between his work and the modern tragedies was that his work was intentional in its laughter. Fielding's play was later adapted into The Opera of Operas; or Tom Thumb the Great by playwrights Eliza Haywood and William Hatchett. The novelist Frances Burney played Huncamunca in private productions of 1777, there was a private production done by the family of Jane Austen at Steventon in 1788. The play is same as the eraly one albeit few changes: He completely removed a scene in which two doctors discuss Tom Thumb's death, and in doing so unified the type of satire that he was working on. Tom is not killed by Grizzle, but instead defeats him. However, Tom is killed by a giant, murderous cow offstage, the news of which prompts a killing spree, leaving seven dead bodies littered on stage and the King alone, left to boast that he is the last to fall, right before stabbing himself. The ghost of Tom in Tom Thumb is replaced by the ghost of Gaffar Thumb, Tom's father. It is his only printed play that originally includes a frontispiece.

The Expedition of Humphry Clinker (1771) epistolary picaresque novel. His best and funniest work. The most mature and the only true comic novel.

In the form of letters written by six characters: Matthew Bramble, a Welsh Squire; his sister Tabitha; their niece Lydia and nephew Jeremy Melford; Tabitha's maid Winifred Jenkins; and Lydia's suitor Wilson. Much of the comedy arises from differences in the descriptions of the same events and places seen by the participants. Smollett relies heavily on a scatological humour and references to the body.

Matthew Bramble, his family and servants are traveling through England and Scotland to cure his gout. They leave Brambleton Hall, in Monmouthshire and family passe through many cities, making extended or significant stops at Gloucester, Bath, London, Harrogate, Scarborough and Edinburgh. He goes to natural spas and corresponds primarily with his physician, Dr. Lewis. His letters and others’ about him reveal Bramble is misanthropic and something of a hypochondriac. But he is generally reasonable and extremely charitable with growing disillusionment at the changing moral and social landscape.

Tabitha Bramble, his sister, is a foolish and cantankerous spinster who is out there to search for a husband. Her correspondence with Mrs. Gwyllim, the house-keeper at Brambleton Hall, reveals her selfishness and lack of generosity towards servants. She has social pretensions and frequent misunderstandings, misuse of common idioms, and atrocious spelling.

Winifred or Win Jenkins, her servant, also corresponds with the servants at Brambleton Hall. She’s the only correspondent not related to Matthew Bramble thus provides commentary on the family. Serving as a comic foil to Tabitha Bramble, she shares many of her misspellings and malapropisms but demonstrates considerably more common sense. At London, she becomes infatuated with Humphry Clinker and Methodism both.

Jeremy Melford, Bramble's nephew, is a young man looking for amusement. He corresponds with Sir Watkin Phillips of Jesus College, Oxford. He also reflects upon issues of city life, class, and the growing public sphere albeit with a more optimistic attitude. He is revealed through Bramble's letters to be "hot-headed" and prone to rash anger. He delves in socializing at the coffeehouse. His study of the places and people of his journey includes the members of his family, whom he comically sketches for the reader.

Lydia Melford, Bramble's niece, is trying to recover from an unfortunate romantic entanglement with a stage actor named Wilson who is revealed to be a gentleman named George Dennison. She writes letters to Miss Letitia Willis at Gloucester which reveal her struggles between familial duty and her affection for Wilson. She describes her secret communications with him, as well as her surprise encounter with the disguised Wilson in Bath. Lydia also reflects upon the wonders of city life, with astonishment and excitement as she has been in boarding school and now debuts in society.

Humphry Clinker, is an ostler, a stableman at an inn, who does not make his first appearance until about a quarter of the way through the story. He is taken on by Matthew Bramble and family, while they are traveling toward London. Humphry Clinker is a primarily foolish character whose good-natured earnestness earns him the esteem of Matthew Bramble. He is largely described through the letters of Matthew Bramble and Jeremy Melford and, despite his frequent misunderstandings, is presented as a talented worker and gifted orator, attracting a devoted following of parishioners during a brief oratorical stint in London. Humphry suffers false imprisonment due to accusations of being a highway robber, though he retains the confident support of Matthew Bramble and his family. He is rescued and returned to his sweetheart, the maid Winifred Jenkins. The Life and Opinions of Tristram Shandy, Gentleman ( nine volumes; 2 in 1759; vols. 3 and 4, 1761; vols. 5 and 6, 1762; vols. 7 and 8, 1765; vol. 9, 1767) Seen as precursor of stream of consciousness. Its style is marked by digression, double entendre, and graphic devices. Tristram's narration of his life story but he cannot explain anything simply, that he must make explanatory diversions to add context and colour to his tale, to the extent that Tristram's own birth is not even reached until Volume III. Other characters include his father Walter, his mother, his Uncle Toby, Toby's servant Trim, and a supporting cast of popular minor characters, including the chambermaid Susannah, Doctor Slop and the parson Yorick, who later became Sterne's favourite nom de plume and a very successful publicity stunt. Yorick is also the protagonist of Sterne's second work of fiction, A Sentimental Journey Through France and Italy. There is little of his own story in it except the story of a trip through France and accounts of the four comical mishaps which shaped the course of his life from an early age. Firstly, while he was still only an homunculus, Tristram's implantation within his mother's uterus was disturbed. At the very moment of procreation, his mother asked his father if he had remembered to wind the clock. The distraction and annoyance led to the disruption of the proper balance of humours necessary to conceive a well-favoured child. Secondly, one of his father's pet theories was that a large and attractive nose was important to a man making his way in life. In a difficult birth, Tristram's nose was crushed by Dr. Slop's forceps. Thirdly, another of his father's theories was that a person's name exerted enormous influence over that person's nature and fortunes, with the worst possible name being Tristram. In view of the previous accidents, Tristram's father decreed that the boy would receive an especially auspicious name, Trismegistus. Susannah mangled the name in conveying it to the curate, and the child was christened Tristram. According to his father's theory, his name, being a conflation of "Trismegistus" (after the esoteric mystic Hermes Trismegistus) and "Tristan" (whose connotation bore the influence through folk etymology of Latin tristis, "sorrowful"), doomed him to a life of woe and cursed him with the inability to comprehend the causes of his misfortune. Finally, as a toddler, Tristram suffered an accidental circumcision when Susannah let a window sash fall as he urinated out of the window because his chamberpot was missing. People in the novel have eccentricities which he calls “hobby horse”. Walter Shandy’s is theory if names and their influence on life as well as importance of long noses. Uncle Toby’s is about fortifications and mock seiges.

Dedication to Lord William Pitt.

Volume 1

The first volume starts with a humorous recount of Tristram Shandy’s conception and birth, focusing on his parents, Walter and Elizabeth Shandy. Walter is a scholarly but impractical man with many opinions on child-rearing, while Elizabeth is a passive and often bewildered presence. The narrative dives into a series of digressions, such as Walter's detailed discussions on trivial matters like the importance of a child’s name and the "hobby-horses" (obsessions) of various characters. Uncle Toby, a central figure in the novel and Walter's brother, is introduced. Toby’s obsession with military reenactments, which stems from his experience in the army and his war injury, begins to shape the tone of the story.

Volume 2

shifts to Tristram’s early childhood, offering comic insights into the struggles of the Shandy household. Tristram’s birth is retold from several perspectives, emphasizing the mishaps and misunderstandings that complicate it. One of the most significant events is a bungled baptism, during which Tristram is accidentally christened with a name he dislikes. The volume also explores the relationship between Toby and his servant, Corporal Trim, who shares Toby’s love of military strategy and adds further humor to the story with his own quirks and mannerisms. Through their interactions, Sterne expands on themes of friendship, loyalty, and eccentricity.

Volume 3

covers Tristram’s early experiences, but it also broadens to describe Uncle Toby’s war injury in more detail. Walter Shandy, concerned about his son's education, is continually frustrated by various setbacks, which Sterne relays with satirical humor. There is a particularly memorable scene in which Tristram, as a child, suffers an injury to his nose—a result of the clumsy doctor Dr. Slop, who is often at odds with Walter. Tristram’s nose becomes a symbol of the father’s misplaced anxieties about his son’s future. Meanwhile, Uncle Toby’s war injury becomes a frequent topic, and his interactions with Dr. Slop and the parson, Mr. Yorick, reflect the novel’s digressive style.

Volume 4

Here, Sterne focuses on Tristram’s botched circumcision, which occurs accidentally due to a window sash falling on him. This unfortunate event, told in a humorous manner, reflects the novel’s emphasis on the ridiculousness of misfortune. The volume also delves into Uncle Toby's romantic interest in Widow Wadman, who appears as a character to lighten Toby’s mood and create tension in his life. Through these incidents, Sterne parodies traditional storytelling by interrupting the main narrative with elaborate asides, such as Walter's rants on scientific theories and philosophical digressions on human nature.

Volume 5

includes more satirical commentary on various subjects, notably time and memory, which Sterne explores through Tristram’s attempts to tell his story in a chronological fashion. This volume reflects Sterne’s philosophical musings on the impossibility of narrating life in a linear way. Uncle Toby’s courtship of Widow Wadman is further developed, providing comic relief and shedding light on Toby’s naivety. Sterne uses the character’s innocent misunderstandings to reveal Toby's sensitivity and humanity, contrasting it with Walter’s intellectualized detachment.

Volume 6

Tristram, now grown, decides to travel across Europe, adding an episodic element to the novel. He shares details of his journey, blending travelogue with introspection. This section reflects Tristram's desire to escape the disappointments of his youth and explore new perspectives. Along the way, he meets various characters, who add to the novel’s diversity of eccentric personalities. Sterne uses these travel experiences to comment on the absurdities of social conventions, poking fun at cultural differences and the often futile nature of travel as a means of self-discovery.

Volume 7

Tristram’s European adventures continue, but they are secondary to his commentary on mortality and human suffering. Sterne offers poignant reflections on the fleeting nature of life, which contrast with the earlier, lighthearted volumes. In a memorable scene, Tristram suffers a severe illness in France, which leads him to confront the fragility of human existence. The volume maintains the digressive structure, filled with philosophical asides and humorous observations that underscore Sterne’s belief in the importance of enjoying life despite its many uncertainties.

Volume 8

Returning to England, Tristram reflects on the Shandy family estate and the peculiarities of his upbringing. He comments on Uncle Toby’s continued relationship with Widow Wadman, which has evolved into a delicate courtship. This volume is marked by a return to the eccentricities of the Shandy household, with Walter’s latest schemes and plans occupying much of the narrative. Sterne also elaborates on Toby’s obsession with military reenactments, offering a poignant reflection on how people use hobbies to cope with past traumas and disappointments.

Volume 9

The final volume brings together the major themes of the novel, including time, memory, and identity. Uncle Toby’s relationship with Widow Wadman comes to a head, with a comical twist as she inquires about the specifics of his war injury, hinting at her ulterior motives. Sterne closes the novel with a return to Tristram’s reflections on the act of writing itself, emphasizing the limitations of language and narrative. The novel ends abruptly, fitting the style of Sterne’s storytelling, leaving readers with a sense of life’s unpredictability and the elusive nature of meaning.

The Traveller; or, a Prospect of Society (1764) philosophical poem in  heroic verse of an Augustan style was the work which first made Goldsmith's name. in the dedication he tells us “I have endeavoured to shew, that there may be equal happiness in states, that are differently governed from our own; that every state has a particular principle of happiness, and that this principle in each may be carried to a mischievous excess.” He begins the poem by extolling the happiness of his brother Henry's simple family life. Then, from a vantage-point in the Alps, he surveys the condition of the world. Every nation, he says, considers itself the happiest, but this is only because each nation judges by its own standards. In fact, happiness is probably equally spread, though in different forms which tend to be mutually exclusive. Goldsmith turns to consider various countries individually: Italy is naturally fertile and was formerly successful in commerce, but has since been overtaken by other countries; The Swiss have poverty, but also equality but have no nobility of soul; France is a nation motivated by honour, and is therefore too prone to vanity; Holland’s industry has brought prosperity but  liberty itself is barter'd here; Britain's free constitution has led to a lack of social cohesion, the rich defending their own liberties by oppressing the poor. “An Essay on the Theatre; Or, A Comparison between Laughing And Sentimental Comedy” (1773) noted the extent to which the comedy in the England of his day had departed from its traditional purpose, the excitation of laughter by exhibiting the follies of the lower part of humankind. He questioned whether an exhibition of its follies would not be preferable to a detail of its calamities. In sentimental comedy, Goldsmith continued, the virtues of private life were exhibited, rather than the vices exposed; and the distresses rather than the faults of humankind generated interest in the piece. Characters in these plays were almost always good; if they had faults, the spectator was expected not only to pardon but to applaud them, in consideration of the goodness of their hearts. Thus, according to Goldsmith, folly was commended instead of being ridiculed. Goldsmith concluded by labeling sentimental comedy a “species of bastard tragedy,” “a kind of mulish production,” a designation that ironically brings to mind Guarini’s comparison of tragicomedy in its uniqueness (a product of comedy and tragedy but different from either) to the mule (the offspring of the horse and the ass but itself neither one nor the other).

The Wanderer; or, Female Difficulties (1814) historical novel with Gothic overtones set during the 1790s. It opens with a group of people fleeing the Terror. Among them is the protagonist, who refuses to identify herself. Even  her nationality and race are in doubt. She arrives [in England] as a nameless Everywoman. She also charts the downward spiral of Juliet from gentility to working woman; she begins as a musician and slips into the less-reputable positions of milliner and seamstress. Elinor Joddrel is the antagonist of the story. She controls her own destiny, largely because she is an umarried heiress, and articulates "feminist views on the economic and sexual oppression of women". During the 1790s, novelists often portrayed feminist characters, sometimes as heroines, such as in Mary Hays’s Memoirs of Emma Courtney (1796), but more frequently as "grotesque satires" as in Elizabeth Hamilton’s Memoirs of Modern Philosophers (1800).  Elinor falls hopelessly in love with Harleigh, the brother of her fiancé and a suitor of Juliet's. After Harleigh rejects her, Elinor “abandons decorum altogether”;[8] she dresses up as a man and frightens Juliet with her threats of suicide.  It is Harleigh who discovers Juliet's true identity—she is the daughter of a "clandestine" marriage of the Earl of Granville. She was raised in France and forced to marry a revolutionary to save her guardian from the guillotine. Juliet fled the marriage, but her husband pursued her, believing that she would inherit the Granville fortune.   Harleigh abandons Juliet after discovering that she is married. She is eventually rescued by a friend. In the end, Juliet's husband is deported and executed as a spy; her guardian comes to England, thereby granting her respectability and her inheritance. Harleigh returns and proposes. Finally, “Elinor is brought to repudiate, if not her feminist principles, at least her suicidal intentions, and order is restored to the novel”.

Castle Rackrent (1800) the first historical novel, the first regional novel in English, the first Anglo-Irish novel, the first Big House novel, and the first saga novel. It is widely regarded as the first Irish novel to use the device of a narrator who is both unreliable and an observer of, rather than a player in, the actions he chronicles. It also has a glossary (which was a last-minute addition). Unlike many of her other novels, which were heavily "edited" by her father, Richard Lovell Edgeworth, before their publication, the published version is close to her original intention. Shortly before its publication, an introduction, glossary and footnotes, written in the voice of an English narrator, were added to the original text to blunt the negative impact the Edgeworths feared the book might have on English enthusiasm for the Act of Union 1800. Her main sources were her own family history called The Black Book of Edgeworthstown. The novel is set prior to the Constitution of 1782. It is narrated by an Irish catholic worker on the estate, named Thady Quirk, and portrayed the rise of the catholic-Irish middle class. It tells the story of four generations of Rackrent heirs through their steward, Thady Quirk. The heirs are: the dissipated spendthrift Sir Patrick O'Shaughlin, the litigious Sir Murtagh Rackrent, the cruel husband and gambling absentee Sir Kit Rackrent, and the generous but improvident Sir Condy Rackrent. Their sequential mismanagement of the estate is resolved through the machinations—and to the benefit—of the narrator's astute son, Jason Quirk. William Butler Yeats pronounced Castle Rackrent "one of the most inspired chronicles written in English". Walter Scot credited her novel for inspiring him to write his Waverley series of novels.

Practical Education (1798) combines the ideas of Locke and Rousseau with scientific inquiry. Edgeworth asserts that "learning should be a positive experience and that the discipline of education is more important during the formative years than the acquisition of knowledge". The system attempted to "adapt both the curriculum and methods of teaching to the needs of the child; the endeavour to explain moral habits and the learning process through associationism; and most important, the effort to entrust the child with the responsibility for his own mental culture". The ultimate goal of Edgeworth's system was to create an independent thinker who understands the consequences of his or her actions.

Darcy proposes to Elizabeth but is rejected as she tells him of how he broke her sister’s heart and also tortured Wickham. He later tells her of Wickham as the son of his late father's steward. He squandered all the money and wanted to elope with Georgiana, her sister. And he separated Jane and Bingley because of her indifference and her family’s behaviour. Elizabeth accompanies the Gardiners on a tour of Derbyshire. They visit Pemberley, Darcy's estate. Lydia has run off with Wickham. Wickham agrees to marry Lydia. Lydia and Wickham visit the Bennet family at Longbourn, where Lydia tells Elizabeth that Mr Darcy was at her wedding. Then Darcy and Bingley return to Netherfield and Jane gets engaged. Lady Catherine comes and tells Elizabeth not to propose to Darcy as she wants him for her daughter Anne. She kicks her out. Darcy, heartened by his aunt's indignant relaying of Elizabeth's response, again proposes to her and is accepted.

Mansfield Park (1814) Austen's longest novel

Ten-year-old Fanny Price is taken in by her wealthy relatives, the Bertrams, at Mansfield Park from her family in Portsmouth. Their children Tom, Edmund, Maria and Julia all mistreat her (except Edmund). Also, Mrs Norris, wife of the clergyman at the Mansfield parsonage who has a daughter Maria whom she gets married to Rushworth, also pesters her. Sir Thomas leaves to deal with problems on his sugar plantation in Antigua, along with Tom. Henry Crawford and his sister Mary arrive in the parsonage and Edmund is attracted to Mary. Henry flirts with both Maria and Julia. After Tom returns to Mansfield Park ahead of his father, he encourages the young people to begin rehearsals for an amateur performance of Elizabeth Inchbald's play Lovers' Vows. Edmund tries to stop them but is forced to take a role. Thomas comes and is reprimanded. Sir Thomas sees many improvements in Fanny and Mary Crawford initiates a closer relationship with her. Henry proposes to Fanny but she rejects as he knows his behaviour which astonishes Thomas who sends her to visit her parents in Portsmouth. Henry visits, but although she still refuses him, she begins to appreciate his good features. Later, Fanny learns that Henry and Maria have had an affair which is reported in the newspapers. Mr Rushworth sues Maria for divorce and the Bertram family is devastated. Tom meanwhile falls gravely ill as a result of a fall from his horse. Edmund takes Fanny back to Mansfield Park, where she is a healing influence. Sir Thomas realises that Fanny was right to reject Henry's proposal and now regards her as a daughter.  Edmund discovers that Mary's regret is only that Henry's adultery was discovered. He breaks off his engagement and marries Fanny.

Emma (1815) comedy of manners. Austen wrote, "I am going to take a heroine whom no one but myself will much like." Austen described "Emma" as her "favorite child."

Emma Woodhouse after taking credit for the marriage of her friend Miss Taylor with Mr. Weston decides she’s good at match making and takes upon herself, despite her friend Me Knightly’s (his brother is married to her sister Isabella) warning not to, to match Harriet with Mr Elton, a social climber who actually sees Emma noticing him thinking she’s into him. Thus she tells Harriet to reject a proposal from a good farmer Robert Martin. When she tells Elton of her, he is disgusted. He proposes her; Emma rejects him, Mr. Elton goes to Bath gets a rich wife. Harriet is heartbroken, and Emma feels ashamed about misleading her. Frank Churchill, Mr. Weston's son arrives for a two-week visit. Jane Fairfex also arrives to visit aunt Miss Bates.Emma sees Harriet as falling for Frank as he saved her when she swooned after seeing gipsies. Knightly tells her she’s wrong and Jane and Frank have a thing. Later these two get engaged secretly but Jane breaks engagement as Frank keeps it secret until his aunt dies. But then they patch up and get married. Emma had been wrong about it. Harriet now develops feelings for Knightly but Emma too has feelings. He proposes to her and she accepts. Harriet goes on accepting  Robert Martin's second proposal and they are the first couple to get married followed by Emma and Knightly and later Jane and Frank.

Persuasion (1817) last novel completed. The most complex of all novels. Very mature.

Anne Elliot, who reflects on her past engagement to Captain Frederick Wentworth, which she was persuaded to end due to family pressures. Eight years later, they meet again, as her family rent their estate, Kellynch Hall, to his sister and husband (Admiral Croft and his wife Sophia) as they are in financial trouble on account of Sir Walter's lavish spending. So they take residence in Bath. Her sister Mary is now married to Charles Musgrove of Uppercross Hall, the heir to a respected local squire. Anne visits Mary and her family, where she is well-loved. Frederick also comes over. Anne still loves Wentworth. When Anne saves Louisa as she jumped thinking Wentworth will catch her but doesn’t, he is impressed. Anne attracts the attention of her cousin Wiliam Elliot who will inherit their wealth. They think that the family fortunes will be restored. But she finds his character opaque and difficult to judge. Anne visits Mrs Smith, an old school friend who informs how calculating William Eliott is. Here Wentworth grows jealous of William. He got Smith’s wealth after his death and never came to see them. She informs her that he right now is trying to prevent Mrs Clay from marrying Sir Walter (it's rumoured that he wants to marry her) as that will disinherit him from their property.  Wentworth writes her a note declaring his feelings for her as he overhears Anne discussing how women keep their feelings strong despite hopelessness to Harville. They confess feelings. William leaves Bath; Mrs Clay soon follows him and becomes his mistress, making it more likely that he will inherit Kellynch Hall as the danger of her marrying Sir Walter has passed. They marry and Wentworth helps Mrs Smith recover her property from William.

The novel explores themes of regret, second chances, and the passage of time. Anne's character development is central to the narrative, as she learns to assert her agency and embrace her desires. The emotional depth of her relationship with Wentworth ultimately highlights the enduring nature of true love and the significance of personal conviction.
