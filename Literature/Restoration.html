The Two theatres of London (?)

Restoration Comedy (1660 to 1710s) is mainly relevant to London and has no counterpart in country. In fact, the restoration drama shows an apathy to country life. It is metropolitan comedy. It was a bridge between Elizabethan and 19th century theatre.

In ridiculing middle class and pitching for the upper-class ethos, it was a class drama. According to Lamb, it’s no relation with real life and is therefore just dream land. But it does have a relation with the class that declined towards the end of the century. Best comedies are Etherege’s Man of the Mode and Congreve’s The Way of the World.

Commedy of manners: theirs is this type of comedy which is influenced by BJ’s comedy of humours. Fletcher is a bug influence. French influence is great as Charles II and followers has been in France. Moliere is a great influence. Comedia dell’arte of Italy had also influence.

Comedy of Manners

1. Sir George Etherege (1634/35 to 1691) Easy Etherege, Gentle George. Studied law

first major Restoration playwright and the creator of the English comedy of manners, one of the "big five" in Restoration comedy.  Edmund Gosse wrote his biography in Seventeenth Century Studies (1883).

The Comical Revenge or Love in a Tub (1664) partly in rhymed heroic verse, like the stilted tragedies of the Howards and Thomas Killigrew, but it contains comic scenes that are notably bright and fresh.  The sparring between Sir Frederick and the Widow introduced a style of wit hitherto unknown upon the English stage. It is one of the earliest Restoration Comedies. Etherege's employment of two separate language styles. The style employed in the verbal sparring between Sir Frederick and the Widow would set the standard for the language of the Restoration comedy. The subtitle refers to the comical subplot which deals with the locking up of Dufoy, servant of Sir Frederick, in a tub by the chambermaids Betty and Lettice.

After Mulberry Garden, Courtall meets her. Then Oliver comes and rejects her kiss painting her as the victim. William Wycherley (1641 to 1716) 

Voltaire was a great admirer of Wycherley's plays and once said of them, “It seems that the Englishmen take too much liberty and the Frenchwomen don't take enough.” adaptation Duw Due ti his foul language, he was  bowdlerised: The Plain Dealer by Isaac Bickerstaffe and a version of The Country Girl by David Garrick. Married secretly the Countess of Drogheda in 1679. He was imprisoned in Fleet Prison as a debtor until he was released by King James II, who paid off Wycherley's execution creditor and provided Wycherley a pension of £200 a year.

The title character is Captain Manly, a sailor who doubts the motives of everyone he meets except for his sweetheart, Olivia, and his friend, Vernish. When Olivia jilts him and marries Vernish, Captain Manly attempts to gain revenge by sending a pageboy (who, unknown to him, is a girl in disguise and is in love with him) to seduce Olivia. When the truth of the page's identity is discovered, Manly marries her instead.

Love in a Wood; Or, St James's Park (1671) dedicated to the Duchess of Cleveland, mistress of Charles II.

The Country Wife (performed1672 or 1673; published in 1675) a rake's trick of pretending impotence to safely have clandestine affairs with married women, and the arrival in London of an inexperienced young "country wife", with her discovery of the joys of town life, especially the fascinating London men. Known for colloquial prose dialogue in place of Molière's verse, a complicated, fast-paced plot tangle, and many sex jokes.

3 plots: They may be schematised as Horner's impotence trick, the married life of Pinchwife and Margery, and the courtship of Harcourt and Alithea. Impotence trick is based on the classic Roman comedy Eunuchus by Terence. Harry Horner, the guy who feigns impotence. Lady Fidget, her sister-in-law Mrs Dainty Fidget, and her tag-along friend Mrs Squeamish make appearances. Margery, wife of Pinchwife whom he seduces comes do his undoing. A final threat of exposure comes in the last scene, through the well-meaning frankness of the young country wife Margery Pinchwife. Margery is indignant at the accusations of impotence directed at "poor dear Mr. Horner", which she knows from personal experience to be untrue, and is intent on saying so at the traditional end-of-the-play public gathering of the entire cast. The last plot of Alithea and Harcourt: By means of persistence and true love, the witty Harcourt, Horner's friend, wins the hand of Pinchwife's sister Alithea, who is, when the play opens, engaged to the shallow fop Sparkish. Found in a compromised position (wrongly) with Horner, she’s left by Sparkish and then falls for Harcourt.

 "The china scene", a sustained double entendre dialogue mostly heard from off stage, where Horner is purportedly discussing his china collection with two of his lady friends.  The husband of Lady Fidget and the grandmother of Mrs. Squeamish are listening front stage and nodding in approval, failing to pick up the double meaning obvious to the audience. 

The Plain Dealer (1676) based on Molière's Le Misanthrope, was highly praised by John Dryden and John Dennis, though it was also condemned for its obscenity by many. The title character is Captain Manly, a sailor who doubts the motives of everyone he meets except for his sweetheart, Olivia, and his friend, Vernish. When Olivia jilts him and marries Vernish, he attempts to gain revenge by sending a pageboy (who, unknown to him, is a girl named Fidelia in disguise and is in love with him) to seduce Olivia. When the truth of the page's identity is discovered, Manly marries her instead.

William Congreve (1670 to 1729) Whig Kit-Kat Club member

He met Jonathan Swift, and at Trinity College Dublin. Congreve used the pseudonym Cleophil, under which he published Incognita: or, Love and Duty reconcil'd in 1692. Dryden supported him throughout his life, often composing complimentary introductions for his publications. Wrote only 5 plays.

The Old Bachelor (1693) is not in true Restoration mode; his first play, recognized as a success, and ran for a two-week period when it opened. Congreve's mentor John Dryden gave the production rave reviews and proclaimed it to be a brilliant first piece. 

The 'Old Bachelor' is Heartwell, 'a surly old bachelor, pretending to slight women', who falls in love with Silvia, not knowing her to be the forsaken mistress of Vainlove, and is lured into marrying her, only discovering her true character afterwards, from the gibes of his acquaintances. The parson who has been brought in to marry them, however, is in fact Vainlove's friend Bellmour, who has assumed the disguise for the purpose of an intrigue with Laetitia, the young wife of an uxorious old banker, Fondlewife; and Heartwell is relieved to discover that the marriage was a pretence. The comedy includes the amusing characters of Sir Joseph Wittol, a foolish knight, who allows himself to be really married to Silvia, under the impression that she is the wealthy Araminta; and his companion, the cowardly bully, Captain Bluffe, who under the same delusion is married to Silvia's maid. The success of this comedy was in part due to the acting of performers Thomas Betterton and Anne Bracegirdle.

The Double Dealer (1693) Mellefont, nephew and prospective heir of Lord Touchwood, about to marry Cynthia, daughter of Sir Paul Plyant. Lady Touchwood, a violent and dissolute woman, is in love with Mellefont, but as he rejects her advances, determines to prevent the match and ruin him in Lord Touchwood's esteem. In this design she finds a confederate in Maskwell, the Double Dealer, who has been her lover, pretends to be Mellefont's friend, and aspires to cheat him of Cynthia and get her for himself. To this end he leads Plyant to suspect an intrigue between Mellefont and Lady Plyant, and Touchwood an intrigue between Mellefont and Lady Touchwood; and contrives that Touchwood shall find Mellefont in the latter's chamber. Mellefont is disinherited and Cynthia is to be made over to Maskwell. The latter's plot, however, here goes wrong. Lord Touchwood informs Lady Touchwood of Maskwell's intention to marry Cynthia. This awakens her jealousy. She finds Maskwell and rebukes him, and is overheard by Lord Touchwood, who now perceives Maskwell's treachery, and defeats his final attempt to carry off Cynthia.

Love for Love (1695) Jonsonian comical. Most satirical of his plays as evident from Prologue. (Since "Plain Dealer's scenes of manly rage", none dares after that to lash this "crying age". I take it up that bold essay) (See Prologue). It was more famous that The wat of the World. Valentine has fallen under the displeasure of his father by his extravagance and is besieged by creditors. His father, Sir Sampson Legend, offers him £4000 (only enough to pay his debts) if he will sign a bond engaging to make over his right to his inheritance to his younger brother Ben. Valentine, to escape from his embarrassment, signs the bond. He is in love with Angelica, who possesses a fortune of her own, but so far she has not yielded to his suit. Sir Sampson has arranged a match between Ben, who is at sea, and Miss Prue, an awkward country girl, the daughter of Foresight, a superstitious old fool who claims to be an astrologer. Valentine, realizing the ruin entailed by the signature of the bond, tries to move his father by submission, and fails; then pretends to be mad and unable to sign the final deed of conveyance to his brother. Finally, Angelica intervenes. She induces Sir Sampson to propose marriage to her, pretends to accept, and gets possession of Valentine's bond. When Valentine, in despair at finding that Angelica is about to marry his father, declares himself ready to sign the conveyance, she reveals the plot, tears up the bond, and declares her love for Valentine. In a famous scene Mr Tattle, teacher of Prue, teacher her the importance of saying one thing and doing another. Famous coinage: "O fie, Miss, you must not kiss and tell."

The Mourning Bride (1697) (A Restoration tragedy, horror play with Ford and Webster influence) The play centres on Zara, a queen held captive by Manuel, King of Granada, and a web of love and deception which results in the mistaken murder of Manuel who is in disguise, and Zara's also mistaken suicide in response. Quote: “Heav'n has no rage, like love to hatred turn'd, / Nor hell a fury, like a woman scorn'd.”

The Relapse, or, Virtue in Danger (1696) (ironic sequel of Cibber's Love's Last Shift, or, The Fool in Fashion in which the rake, Loveless, husband of Amanda, is reformed as she acts as her whore and fulfils his dream one night but here he relapses): Colley Cibber played the luxuriant fop Lord Foppington in both Love's Last Shift and The Relapse. In 18th century, the play, owing to elements of adultery, was replaced by Sheridan's moralised version A Trip to Scarborough (1777). Both Amanda and Loveless exposed to fresh temptations. He falls for widow Berinthia; she can’t resist Worthy, the "fine gentleman of the play" who in actuality seduced Amanda on stage (played by George Powell) but she rejects him. Sir Novelty Fashion (of Cibber) here is "Lord Foppington" (only these three from Cibber, q.v. Love’s Last Shift). The trickster subplot in which young Tom tricks his elder brother Lord Foppington out of his intended bride’s large dowry. He is known as "the greatest of all Restoration fops". Love's Last Shift is one of the first illustrations of a massive shift in audience taste, away from the analytic bent and sexual frankness.

The Provoked Wife (1697): A later unfinished play by Vanbrugh was completed by Colley Cibber and staged under the title The Provoked Husband in 1728. A problem play. The hitherto virtuous Lady Brute is provoked to infidelity by her sour husband Sir John Brute. Married in haste – she for money, he for sex – the Brutes are shackled by wedlock but looking for diversions. He goes off for a drunken night on the town and ends up before a magistrate, disguised in his wife's frock. Meanwhile, Lady Brute and her niece Belinda dress as Shepherd Market doxies for a secret tryst with their suitors Heartfree and Constant and are spied on by the envious Lady Fanciful, who wants Heartfree for herself. Belinda, despite interference from Lady Fanciful, wins her man and marries for love. But it ends sadly for the boozy Brute who attempts to rape his wife, discovers two gallants lurking in his wardrobe and finally ends up accepting certain situations rather than becoming a human pincushion (i.e., fighting a duel to satisfy his honor).

Alexander Pope famously refers to the playwright in "The First Epistle of the Second Book of Horace, Imitated," where he comments (line 288), "What pert low Dialogue has Farqu'ar writ!"

Love and a Bottle (1698) first play; succeeded  "for its sprightly Dialogue and busy Scenes," the play deals with Roebuck, "An Irish Gentleman of a wild roving Temper" who is "newly come to London." Roebuck tells his friend Lovewell that he has left Ireland due to getting a woman pregnant with twins (a boy and a girl) and to Roebuck's father trying to force Roebuck to marry the woman; however, Roebuck remarks, "Heav'n was pleas'd to lessen my Affliction, by taking away the She-brat."

The Constant Couple (1699),  a sentimental comedy with mockery passages. A series of comic misunderstandings are triggered when three rivals vie for the hand of the wealthy heiress Lady Lurewell.

Heroic Drama: a widespread phenomenon of 1660s-70s

“The Restoration was an unheroic age.” Restoration tragedy was influenced by: Corneille. It was Upper-class drama. Added the dimension of admiration to Aristotelean pity and fear. The hero was tragic hero without flaws, self-doubt with pompous and dignified expression full of passion and valour. 

Buckhingam ridicules this in The Rehearsal (1671) the artificiality of heroism since it was an unheroic age.

Poetry: After Metaphysicals

New attitude to poetry in Charles II reign.

French influence due to returning royalists: emphasis on urbanity, decorum, elegance, sweetness of numbers and fitness. Control of forms of neoclassical like BJ. There was wit as defined by Addison in The Spectator. He separates wit and judgement. The neoclassical critical discourses of Rapin’s Reflexions sur la poetique (1672) and Boileau’s  L’Art Poetique(1674) with their focus on good sense, nature, reason influenced ENglish critical scene. 

Addison defines wit in his Spectator. Locke’s difference between Wit and Judgement (wit being an infusing force while judgement a power that differentiates where lies similitude), accepted by Dryden and Davenant. In Essay of Dramatic Poesie, Eugenius sees Sir John Suckling is yardstick of courtly wit (Dryden favours his “conversations of a gentleman” and poetic diction), Edmund Waller as sweet, Denham as majestic, Cowley as copious, full of spirit and elevated.  John Denham and Waller are thought to be the “reformers of our numbers.” 

Dryden’s Preface to Annus Mirabilis: Elegance and wit become good poetry which is equivalent to imagination. Searches memory for the best fitting object to dress thought.

Poets

Edmund Waller (1606-87): 

Best known poems— best known for using heroic couplet which later Pope improved. Royalist in sympathy and was accused in 1643 of organising a plot to seize London for Charles I. He allegedly escaped the death penalty by paying a large bribe. He lived in comfortable exile in France and Switzerland until allowed home in 1651 by Oliver Cromwell, a distant relative. He was a close friend of Thomas Hobbes and John Evelyn. When asked about the conundrum of turning support to King, he Waller is reported to have replied thus to Charles II "Poets, Sir, succeeded better at fiction than in truth".

The Sophy (1641) is a revenge tragedy in blank verse. Edmund Waller remarks of him, "In 1642 he broke outlike the Irish Rebellion, threescore thousand strong, when nobody was aware, or in the least suspected it", in the play of the Sophy. account of the life of Abbas the Great of Persia A father suspecting and plotting against a dear and noble son; a son deprived of sight by the command of a father, and meditating in his rage and revenge the murder of his own favourite daughter, because she is beloved by his father; and the deaths of both son and father by poison, administered through means of a courtier who has betrayed both. Such are the main hinges on which the plot of the piece turns.

Cooper’s Hill (Descriptive poem) (Dryden – “an exact pattern of good writing”)

Started a trend of natural description of which Pope’s Windsor Forest; John Dyer’s Grongar Hill and later WWW’s Tintern Abbey (or Rugby Chapel by Arnold?) are examples.

Johnson praises its smoothness and sweetness as well as concentration and economy.

George Wither (1588 to 1667)

1588 1667 a prolific English poet, pamphleteer, satirist and writer of hymns. Abuses Stript and Whipt twenty satires directed against Revenge, Ambition, Lust. These satires—aimed at exposing "th'abuses of these wicked Times" Contains poem called "The Scourge", in which the Lord Chancellor was attacked, and a series of epigrams A Satyre: Dedicated to His Most Excellent Majestie.

Wither's Motto: Nec habeo, nec careo, nec curo (Latin for "I have not, I want not, I care not") 1621. Ben Jonson turned satire back on Wither, portrayed as the Chronomastix of the masque Time Vindicated. In Dunciad "Withers, Ward, and Gildon rest" "Safe, where no Critics damn, no duns molest".
