Spenserian Stanza

9 line stanza, 8 : iambic pentameter and 1 : Alexandrian or iambic hexameter. (The foundation of most alexandrines consists of two hemistichs (half-lines) of six syllables each, separated by a caesura. The line's name derives from its use in the Medieval French Roman d'Alexandre of 1170. Though English alexandrines have occasionally provided the sole metrical line for a poem, for example in lyric poems by Henry Howard, Earl of Surrey and Sir Philip Sidney and in two notable long poems, Michael Drayton's Poly-Olbion and Robert Browning's Fifine at the Fair, they have more often featured alongside other lines.  Around the mid-16th century stricter alexandrines were popular as the first line of poulter's measure couplets, fourteeners (strict iambic heptameters) providing the second line.  John Dryden and his contemporaries and followers likewise occasionally employed them as the second (rarely the first) line of heroic couplets, or even more distinctively as the third line of a triplet. In his Essay on Criticism, Alexander Pope denounced (and parodied) the excessive and unskillful use of this practice).

Spenser varied existing epic stanza forms, the rhyme royal used by Chaucer, with the rhyme pattern ABABBCC, and the ottava rima, which originated in Italy, with the rhyme pattern ABABABCC.

As a statement of Spenser’s views on poetry, “October” is perhaps the most significant “moral” eclogue. When the disillusioned young poet Cuddie (“the perfect pattern of a poet”) complains that his oaten reeds are “rent and wore” without having brought him any reward, the idealistic Piers tries to convince him that glory is better than gain. He encourages Cuddie to leave rustic life, to lift himself “out of the lowly dust,” but Cuddie complains that the great worthies that “matter made for Poets on to play” are long dead. 

“November,” one of the most highly structured eclogues, is a pastoral elegy for Dido, the daughter of one “greate shephearde” and the beloved of another “greate shepheard Lobbin.” “December,” too, is a conventional love plaint, al-though it adds the dimension of Colin’s approaching death.  Spring spent, summer burnt up.

Rhyme scheme (usual): abab bcbc cdcd ee.

In emulation of Virgil's first work, the Eclogues, Spenser wrote this series of pastorals at the commencement of his career. However, Spenser's models were rather the Renaissance eclogues of Mantuanus. It is also remarkable for the extensive commentary included with the work in its first publication, ascribed to an "E.K." E.K. is an intelligent, very subtle, and often deeply ironic commentator, who is sometimes assumed to be an alias of Spenser himself. The term sarcasm is first recorded in English in Spenser's poem. 

The poem introduces Colin Clout, a folk character originated by John Skelton, and depicts his life as a shepherd through the twelve months of the year. The twelve eclogues of The Shepheardes Calender, dealing with such themes as the abuses of the church, Colin's shattered love for Rosalind, praise for Queen Elizabeth, and encomia to the rustic Shepherd's life, are titled for the months of the year. Each eclogue is preceded by a woodcut and followed by a motto describing the speaker. The opening line of each eclogue expresses characteristics of the month, and the poem as a whole charts common accuracy of the seasons, the toil and celebrations of the village year. The precision of the description of birds, flowers, and harvests is balanced by an underlying theme of the hardships and rituals that each season entails. Each pastoral in the poem can be classified into one of three categories, identified as moral, plaintive, or re-creative.

The plaintive and re-creative poems are each devoted to presenting Colin Clout in his double character of lover and poet, whereas the moral poems are mixed with mocking bitterness, which moves Colin from a dramatic persona to a more homely style. While the January pastoral tells of the unhappy love of Colin for Rosalind, the springtime of April calls for a song in praise of Elizabeth. In May, the shepherds, who are rival pastors of the Reformation, end their sermons with an animal fable. In summer, they discourse on Puritan theology. October brings them to contemplate the trials and disappointments of a poet, and the series ends with a parable comparing life to the four seasons of the year.

As the reader passes through each month and gets closer to the end of the year, the wording becomes less beautifully lyrical and more straightforward; closing together the poem the way the month of December closes up the year. Spenser uses rhyme differently in each month. A year after working together, the two of them, joined by Sir Philip Sidney, Edward Dryer, and Fulke Greville, created the literary group called "Areopagus". The group they formed supported Leicester's views on religion and politics (Bear). One thing that separates the poem from others of its time is Spenser's use of allegory and his dependence on the idea of antiquity.

Criticism of the Epic

The poem is dedicated to Elizabeth I who is represented in the poem as the Faerie Queene Gloriana, as well as the character Belphoebe. It is also known that James VI of Scotland read the poem and was very insulted by Duessa – a very negative depiction of his mother, Mary, Queen of Scots.[15] The Faerie Queene was then banned in Scotland. Though it praises her in some ways, The Faerie Queene questions Elizabeth's ability to rule so effectively because of her gender, and also inscribes the "shortcomings" of her rule.[16] There is a character named Britomart who represents married chastity. This character is told that her destiny is to be an "immortal womb" – to have children.

The House of Busirane episode in Book III in The Faerie Queene is partially based on an early modern English folktale called "Mr. Fox's Mottos". In the tale, a young woman named Lady Mary has been enticed by Mr. Fox, who resembles Bluebeard in his manner of killing his wives. She defeats Mr. Fox and talks about his deeds. Notably, Spenser quotes the story as Britomart makes her way through the House, with warning mottos above each doorway "Be bold, be bold, but not too bold".

Samuel Daniel (1562–1619)

seen as successor of Spenser; part of Wilton Circle, a group that included Edmund Spenser, Michael Drayton, Sir John Davies, and Abraham Fraunce. Also tutor to William Herbert.  C. S. Lewis called Daniel "the most interesting man of letters" whom the sixteenth century produced in England. Biographer Thomas Fuller in Histories of the Worthies of England (1662) states that he "was born not far from Taunton" in Somerset. He wrote a dedicatory poem that was included in John Florio's translation of Michel de Montaigne's Essays in 1603.

The Worthy Tract of Paulus Jovius (Daniel's first published work) a translation of an Italian treatise on impresa or emblems by historian Paolo Giovio.

Daniel's literary career was effectively launched in late 1591 with the unauthorized inclusion of some of his Delia sonnets in the posthumous first edition of Sir Philip Sidney's Astrophel and Stella. Mary, Phili[‘s sister objected it and the edition was retracted.

Michael Drayton (1563-1631)

English poet active from Elizabethan to Charles I's reign. Known for historical poetry. First English author to write Horace-style odes; popularized the term "ode" in England. Master of short, staccato Anacreontics measure. Retained Elizabethan style and attitude under the Stuarts.

In 1590, he produced his first book, The Harmony of the Church, a volume of spiritual poems, dedicated to Lady Devereux. It is notable for a version of the Song of Solomon

In 1593, appeared Idea: The Shepherd's Garland, a collection of nine pastorals, in which he celebrated his personal love-sorrows under the pseudonym of Rowland. Drayton then produced two further 'Ideas': a cycle of 51 sonnets entitled Ideas Mirrour. His early patriotic odes: “To the Virginian voyage” and “To the Cambro-Britons and their harp, his Ballad of Agnicourt”. 

In 1593 there appeared the first of Drayton's historical poems, The Legend of Piers Gaveston, and the next year saw the publication of Matilda, an epic poem in rhyme royal. It was about this time, too, that he brought out Endimion and Phoebe, a volume which he never republished.

In 1596, Drayton published his long and important poem Mortimeriados,.This account in ottava rima of “lamentable civil wars of Edward the Second and the Brons” is dull and episodic. He later enlarged and modified this poem, and republished it in 1603 under the title of The Barons' Wars.

In 1596 also appeared another historical poem, The Legend of Robert, Duke of Normandy

In 1597 appeared England's Heroical Epistles, a series of historical studies, in imitation of those of Ovid. These last poems, written in the heroic couplet, contain some of the finest passages in Drayton's writings.

In 1604 wrote a satire, The Owl and his scriptural narrative of Moses in a Map of his Miracles, a sort of epic in heroics printed the same year.

In 1605, under the title of Poems Lyric and Pastoral; these consisted of odes, eclogues and a fantastic satire called The Man in the Moon. Some of the odes are extremely spirited.

In 1613, the first part of vast and geographical poem was published under the title of Poly-Olbion, eighteen books being produced, to which the learned John Selden supplied notes. In 12 syllable couplets. topographical poem describing England and Wales. Divided into thirty songs, written in alexandrine couplets, consisting in total of almost 15,000 lines of verse. Each song describes between one and three counties, describing their topography, traditions and histories. Copies were illustrated with maps of each county, drawn by William Hole. The first book was accompanied by historical and philological summaries written by John Selden.

The Battle of Agincourt, an historical poem in ottava rima, and The Miseries of Queen Margaret, written in the same verse and manner; Nimphidia, the Court of Faery, an epic of fairyland; The Quest of Cinthia and The Shepherd's Sirena, two lyrical pastorals; and finally The Moon Calf, a sort of satire. Sir John Davies (1569-1626)

English poet, lawyer, and politician. Known for epigrams, sonnets, and varied poetry forms. Wrote Orchestra, or a Poem of Dancing (1596) in rhyme royal, exploring the natural order and human activity; described as brilliant and graceful by Bullen. The poem presents dance as the cosmic order’s principle. It dramatizes a debate between Antinous, one of Penelope’s suitors during Ulysses’ absence, and Penelope, whom Antinous persuades to dance by likening it to universal harmony. One of the most successful philosophical poems in English.

Favoured by Queen Elizabeth; wrote Hymns to Astraea (1599) with acrostics spelling "Elisabetha Regina”. His Epigrams (1599) included in a list of works ordered confiscated and burned by the state.

Published Nosce Teipsum (1599, on self-knowledge and soul's immortality) in decasyllabic quatrains; gained James I’s favour, leading to promotion in Ireland. A. H. Bullen described it as being "singularly readable for such a subject: highly accomplished verse, no Elizabethan quaintness, both subtle and terse".

Poems frequently included in Elizabethan anthologies; last complete edition published in 1876 and now out of print.

Neglected after the mid-17th century until rediscovered by T. S. Eliot. His poem "I know my soul hath power to know all things" set to music by Hubert Parry in Songs of Farewell.

 

Elizabethan Satire: Modeled on Horace (witty, moderate satire) and Juvenal (violent, direct satire), Elizabethan satire had varied tones and themes:

  •         Thomas Lodge’s A Fig for Momus (1595): Mild, Horatian satires expressed with precision and wit.
  •         John Donne’s satires (1590s): Violent, vivid, and deliberately rough iambic couplets. They criticize societal vices broadly and harshly.
  •         Joseph Hall’s Virgidemiarum (1597): A middle ground between Lodge’s mildness and Donne’s intensity. Rhymed iambic couplets become the standard for satire.
  •         John Marston’s Scourge of Villainy (1597): Harsh, dramatic, and exaggerated satire with mock-heroic tones.
  •         The Metamorphosis of Ajax (1596) by John Harington: A satirical work on sanitation, unique among Elizabethan satires.

Epigrams: A form of miniature satire, popularized by Davies and Harington under the influence of Martial. Their success spurred epigram collections in the early 17th century. Ben Jonson excelled in this form, particularly with epitaphs and verses of compliment, like “On My First Son” and “On Salomon Pavey.”

Elizabethan Lyric Poetry: The Elizabethan era is famed as the golden age of lyrical poetry, blending melody, structure, and emotion. Notable examples:

  •         Thomas Lodge’s “Love in my bosom like a bee” (Rosalind, 1590).
  •         George Peele’s “His Golden Locks Time Hath to Silver Turned” (Polyhymnia, 1590).
  •         Thomas Nashe’s “Adieu, Farewell Earth’s Bliss” (Summer’s Last Will and Testament, 1600): Features the haunting stanza: “Beauty is but a flower / Which wrinkles will devour: / Brightness falls from the air, / Queens have died young and fair, / Dust hath closed Helen’s eye”
  •         John Dowland’s “Weep You No More, Sad Fountains” (Third and Last Book of Songs or Airs, 1603).

Thomas Campion (1567–1619)

Lyric Poet and Composer. Five anonymous poems included in Songs of Divers Noblemen and Gentlemen (1591), appended to Newman's edition of Sir Philip Sidney's Astrophel and Stella. Published Poemata, a collection of Latin panegyrics, elegies, and epigrams (1595), earning considerable reputation.

Book of Airs (1601): Co-edited with Philip Rosseter, who contributed some music and possibly lyrics. Campion described airs as “short and well seasoned,” akin to epigrams in poetry. Notable lyrics:

  •           “Follow thy fair sun, unhappy shadow.”
  •           “Will you now so timely depart.”
  •         Campion’s meters often exhibit flexibility and musicality, as seen in “When thou must home to shades of underground.”

Observations in the Art of English Poesie (1602): Advocated for rhymeless, quantitative verse over rhyme, despite his own mastery of rhymed poetry. Samuel Daniel countered this in A Defence of Rhyme (1603), marking the end of the Renaissance rhyme controversy. Campion’s theories were influenced by his sense of musical rhythm, akin to Gerard Manley Hopkins’ later concept of “sprung rhythm.”

Campion’s only fully successful rhymeless poem, provided as an example in his Observations, showcases his mastery of vowel music and rhythmic flow: “Rose-cheeked Laura, come/ Sing thou smoothly with thy beauty’s / Silent music…”

Wrote and published a masque for Lord Hayes' marriage (1607). Published Songs of Mourning: Bewailing the Untimely Death of Prince Henry (1613). Likely published Two Bookes of Ayres (1613), with both words and music composed by himself.

Published A New Way of Making Fowre Parts in Counterpoint By a Most Familiar and Infallible Rule (1615), a technical treatise on counterpoint, later included with annotations by Christopher Sympson in Playford's Brief Introduction to the Skill of Musick. Two editions published by 1660.

Published Third and Fourth Booke of Ayres after 1617. Wrote the lyrics for airs performed at Brougham Castle during King James I's visit (1618); music composed by George Mason and John Earsden.

Reissued Epigrammatum Libri II. Umbra Elegiarum liber unus (1619), revising the 1595 collection with omissions, additions (another book of epigrams), and corrections.

Giles Fletcher (also known as Giles Fletcher, The Younger; 1586 – 1623)

English cleric and poet. Brother of the poet Phineas Fletcher, and cousin of the dramatist John Fletcher. Chiefly known for his long allegorical poem Christ's Victory and Triumph (1610). Full title Christ's Victorie and Triumph, in Heaven, in Earth, over and after Death, and consists of four cantos. The first canto, Christ's Victory in Heaven, represents a dispute in heaven between justice and mercy, using the facts of Christ's life on earth; the second, Christ's Victory on Earth, deals with an allegorical account of Christ's Temptation; the third, Christ's Triumph over Death, covers the Passion; and the fourth, Christ's Triumph after Death, covering the Resurrection and Ascension, ends with an affectionate eulogy of his brother Phineas as Thyrsilis. The meter is an eight-line stanza in the style of Spenser; the first five lines have the rhyme scheme ABABB, and the stanza concludes with a rhyming triplet. Milton borrowed liberally from Christ's Victory and Triumph in Paradise Regained.

Incorporates Spenserian diction (elamping, ydraded, embranded). Allegory (e.g., descriptions of Mercy and Repentance) and pictorial elements. Combines sensual and allegorical elements, with echoes of Spenser’s Bower of Bliss. Memorable phrases, e.g., “Swelter in quiet waves of immortality”, influenced Milton. Notable passages reminiscent of Milton’s “Nativity Ode”.

Phineas Fletcher (1582 – 1650)

English poet. One of the contributors to Sorrow's Joy (1603). His pastoral drama, Sicelides, or Piscatory was written (1614) for performance before James I. His work rivals in size the canons of Spenser and Milton. The collected works of Phineas Fletcher include three volumes of religious prose, an epic, an epyllion, a drama, several medium-length verse narratives, pastoral eclogues etc. In scope, variety, and quality, his writings are second to none of that age.

In 1627 he published The Locusts or Apollyonists (English version of the original Latin version Locustae, vel Pietas Jesuitica attacking the Jesuits). Anti-Catholic satire, critical of Jesuits, the Pope, purgatory, and the Gunpowder Plot (Book 5). Grosart saw in this work one of the sources of Milton's conception of Satan. Next year appeared an erotic poem, Brittain's Ida, with Edmund Spenser's name on the title page. It is certainly not by Spenser, and is printed by Grosart with the works of Phineas Fletcher.

In 1632 appeared two theological prose treatises, The Way to Blessedness and Joy in Tribulation, and in 1633 his magnum opus, The Purple Island. The book was dedicated to his friend Edward Benlowes, and included his Piscatorie Eclogues and other Poetical Miscellanies.

The Purple Island, or the Isle of Man, is a poem in twelve cantos describing allegorically the physiological structure of the human body and the mind of man. The intellectual qualities are personified, while the veins are rivers, the bones the mountains of the island, the whole analogy being worked out with great ingenuity. The Piscatory Eclogues are pastorals, the characters of which are represented as fisher boys on the banks of the Cam, and are interesting for the light they cast on the biography of the poet himself (Thyrsil) and his father (Thelgon).

Edmund Waller (1606 to 87)

Famous for closed couplets. “On a Girdle”, “Go, Lovely Rose”. Wrote topographical poems “On ST James’s Park” (like Denham’s Cooper’s Hill, Windsor Forest, Pope)

English poet and politician. One of the longest serving members of the English House of Commons. Was accused in 1643 of organising a plot to seize London for Charles I. After his sentence was commuted to banishment, he lived in comfortable exile in France and Switzerland until allowed home in 1651 by Oliver Cromwell, a distant relative. Best remembered now for his poem "Song (Go, lovely rose)".  Many of his works are addressed to members of the extended Percy family, such as the Countess of Carlisle, the Countess of Sunderland and the Earl of Northumberland. Hyde recorded Waller became a poet at the age of thirty, "when other Men give over writing Verses". When Charles II returned to the throne after The Restoration, Waller commemorated the occasion with his 1660 poem To the King, upon his Majesty's Happy Return.

His biographer Samuel Johnson wrote (1779) that it showed "a prostituted mind may retain the glitter of wit, but has lost the dignity of virtue". His extravagant praise for members of the court and Royal family was later parodied by Andrew Marvell in "Last Instructions to a Painter". Described by Francis Atterbury as "the Parent of English Verse", by the nineteenth century his work was out of favour. Edmund Gosse, author of his biography in the 1911 Encyclopædia Britannica, wrote: 'Waller's lyrics were at one time admired to excess, but with the exception of "Go, lovely Rose" and one or two others, they have greatly lost their charm'.

By 1995, the protagonist of The Information, a novel by Martin Amis, dismisses him as a 'seat-warmer, air sniffer and mediocrity'.  His longest and most ambitious work of this type portrayed the inconclusive 1665 Battle of Lowestoft; presenting it as an heroic victory and heaping praise on James, it was widely ridiculed. Two volumes of previously uncollected writings, "The Maid's Tragedy Altered" and "The Second Part of Mr Waller's Poems" were published after his death in 1690. They included Divine Poems, self-published by Waller in 1686.

Aglaura (1637) The acting company was paid with the production's lavish costumes (lace cuffs and ruffs made of cloth of silver and cloth of gold). Suckling wrote the play as a tragedy, but added an alternative happy ending, so creating an optional tragicomedy. A Suckling lyric from the play, "Why so pale and wan, fond lover," became a popular song of the era. He explains in it, Quit! This cannot take her. If she can’t on her own, she won’t. The King of Persia and his son, Prince Thersames, are both in love with Aglaura; she loves the Prince, but the King takes precedence. In the original tragic version, Aglaura secretly marries Thersames, but mistakenly stabs him to death, thinking he is the king. Most of the other characters, including Aglaura herself, die violent deaths. In the tragicomic revision, Aglaura merely wounds the prince, and the king repents and dispenses justice. (The actual difference between the versions amounts to only about 50 lines). The Queen, Orbella, is in love with the King's brother Ariaspes but is the mistress of Ziriff alias Zorannes, captain of the guard and Aglaura's brother.

Discontented Colonel (1639, printed 1646) is a satire on the Scots, disguised as Lithuanian rebels in the play.

The Goblins (1646) a musical comedy (Tempest influence), led to so-called "Second War of the Theatres" of the 1630s. Like the original Poetomachia or War of the Theatres of three decades earlier, the Second War of the Theatres involved Ben Jonson on one side and a set of rivals on the other. Suckling's ridicule of the recently deceased Jonson in The Goblins provoked Richard Brome to ridicule Suckling in his The Court Beggar. According to Samuel Pepys's diary (23 January 1666/67), he was persuaded to see "the dancing preparatory to to-morrow for 'The Goblins,' (a revival of it) a play of Suckling's, not acted these twenty-five years; which was pretty." Takes place in the mythical kingdom of Francelia where a band of robbers (the "goblins" of the title), led by their chieftain Tamoren, masquerade as devils and have the land in an uproar with their pranks. They mete out a kind of rough justice, much in the tradition of Robin Hood. Suckling's heroine Reginella is a version of Miranda, and the antics of the goblins depend on the precedent of Ariel.

 The Shadow of Night (1594) his first significant literary work, heavy references to classical Greek and Roman authors drawn from the Mythologiae of Niccolo Conti. Dedicated to fellow poet Matthew Roydon. The dedication contrasts superficial readers, who read verse to "curtail a tedious hour," with those few who "entertained learning in themselves, to the vital warmth of freezing science...." It talks about The School of Night, a cult who were interested in promoting new ideas. The work as a whole treats the theme of inspired melancholy, the concept that "melecholia" is not merely a negative state, but rather allows for deep and searching thought, self-examination, and study of the world. While humans often devote their daylight hours to trivial distractions, the night allows serious contemplation. Critics have linked the poem to a strain of abstruse and esoteric Renaissance thought on "Saturnian" melancholy:  "No pen can anything eternal write, / That is not steep'd in humor of the night". In the poem's second half, the portrait of the moon goddess Cynthia represents Queen Elizabeth I, and comprises the type of hagiographic personification that was common in the later Elizabethan era.  Swinburne described the poem as "full of loud and angry complaints of neglect and slight, endured at the hands of an unthankful and besotted generation."

“To His Coy Mistress”: Published posthumously in 1681, possibly written in the early 1650s while Marvell was tutoring Sir Thomas Fairfax's daughter. The poem is written in iambic tetrameter with rhyming couplets, following a logical progression: "if... but... therefore." The first stanza consists of ten couplets ("Had we but world enough, and time"), the second stanza contains six couplets ("But at my back I always hear"), and the third stanza has seven ("Now therefore while the youthful hue").

Annie Finch’s poem Coy Mistress and A.D. Hope’s His Coy Mistress to Mr. Marvell respond to Marvell’s poem in the character of the lady addressed. Finch suggests that poetry is a better use of time than lovemaking, while Hope rejects Marvell’s offer. The phrase "World enough and time" has been borrowed for various book titles, including Robert Penn Warren’s World Enough and Time (1950), a romantic novel about murder in early-19th-century Kentucky.

Ursula K. Le Guin's Hugo-nominated short story Vaster than Empires and More Slow draws its title from the poem.

T. S. Eliot's The Love Song of J. Alfred Prufrock (1915) includes the phrase "there will be time," often seen as an allusion to Marvell’s poem.

Virginia Woolf's Orlando: A Biography (1928) references the line "deserts of vast eternity."

Stephen King's Pet Sematary (1983) uses the line "A fine and private place, but none, I think, do there embrace."

The line "I would Love you ten years before the Flood, / And you should, if you please, refuse" appears as the preamble to part three of Greg Bear's Moving Mars (1993), which won a Nebula Award.

“The Mower's Song”: a pastoral poem published posthumously in 1681. It is the final poem in Marvell's series of four "Mower" poems, all written in the voice of Damon the Mower. In the poem, the mower sings about his lover, Juliana, comparing her cruelty to his own cruelty in cutting the grass.

“The Garden”: Published posthumously in Miscellaneous Poems (1681), with a Latin version, "Hortus." The first two stanzas reject worldly ambition for the solitude of the garden. Stanza 3 critiques lovers carving names into trees, favoring nature’s "lovely green." Stanza 4 alludes to Ovid’s myths, twisting them to suggest Gods pursue plants, not women. Stanza 5 eroticizes the garden, associating fruit with the Fall of Man. Stanzas 6-7 retreat into the mind, emphasizing the destructive power of thought. Stanza 8 evokes Eden before Eve, a state of unsexual bliss. Final stanza features a sundial, ironizing pastoralism’s escape from culture.

“Upon Appleton House”: written in 1651 for Thomas Fairfax, 3rd Lord Fairfax of Cameron, when Marvell was tutoring Fairfax's daughter, Mary. A country house poem, it describes Fairfax's Nunappleton estate while reflecting on the political and religious issues of the time. The poem is written in 97 stanzas, each of eight lines that are octosyllabic, in iambic tetrameters forming couplets. It has been analysed into six sections:

  1. Stanzas 1–10: architecture of the house.
  2. Stanzas 11–35: the story of Isabel Thwaites.
  3. Stanzas 36–46: the gardens and plants.
  4. Stanzas 47–60: the meadows.
  5. Stanzas 61–81: the wood.
  6. Stanzas 82–97: the river (closing with return to the house)

response to Gondibert (1651) by William Davenant and draws on Antoine Girard de Saint-Amant, whom Fairfax had translated.
