Thomas Hoccleve (1368/69 to1426)

Promoted Chaucer as "the father of English literature". Worked as clerk of the Office of the Privy Seal. He wrote begging letters (for his overdue pension to Lord Treasurer. His merit was discovered by John Burrow. He was paid annuity by the new king, Henry IV which wasn’t paid regularly. He says "Fader Chaucer fayn wolde han me taught, But I was dul and learned lite or naught". This later became known as the "humility topos", the posture would become a conventional form of authorial self-presentation in the Renaissance. He calls Chaucer “the first founder of our fair language”. Also calls him “universal father is science” in his Regiment. The Oxford English Dictionary cites Hoccleve as the first recorded user of many words, including annuity, causative, flexible, innate, interrupt, manual, miserable, notice, obtain, pitiless, slut and suspense.

The Letter to Cupid (1402) his first work is a translation of Christine de Pizan's L'Epistre au Dieu d'Amours.

La Male Regle (c. 1406) a mock-penitential poem that gives some glimpses of dissipation in his youth.

Regement of Princes (c. 1411) dedicated to the future Henry V. It is known for its frank autobiographical descriptions. Written shortly before his accession, it was adapted from Aegidius de Colonna's Latin work of the same name, from a supposed epistle of Aristotle known as Secretum Secretorum, and from a work of Jacques de Cessoles The Game and Playe of Chesse (Caxton’s translation). It comments on Henry V's lineage, to cement the House of Lancaster's claim to England's throne. Its proem, occupying about a third of the whole, contains reminiscences of London tavern life in a dialogue between the poet and an old man.

Series is important as the earliest autobiographical description of mental illness in English. Begins with Complaint (1420), he combines autobiographical poetry, poetic translations and prose moralizations of the translated texts, begins with a description of a period of "wylde infirmitee" as he has lost his "wit" and "memorie” which have been restored five years ago but he still goes through social alienation and the gossip about his insanity has not died. In the Dialogue with a Friend in which he talks about his worsening eyesight, which further hindered his work as a scribe. It is written after his recovery and he paints a pathetic picture of a poor poet, now 53, who plans to write a tale he owes to his good patron, Humphrey of Gloucester, and translating a portion of Henry Suso's popular Latin treatise on the art of dying. Then it moves to Jereslaus' Wife and of Jonathas (both from Gesta Romanorum). And later to, Learn to die, a theologically and psychologically astute verse translation of Henry Suso's Latin prose Ars Moriendi.

John Lydgate of Bury (1370 to  1451) a monk and poet.

He was a prolific writer of poems, allegories, fables and romances. Joseph Ritson (whofor the first time edited volume of Robinhood Ballads in 1795) called him 'A voluminous, prosaick and drivelling monk'. "Needs must" is a terms originating from him. He also translated the poems of Guillaume de Deguileville into English.

Isopes Fabules (between 1406 and 1408) when he was a student at Oxford University

Troy Book (printed 1513) in 5 books, his first full-scale work, he says he started writing the book at four o'clock on the afternoon of Monday, 31 October 1412; he completed it in 1420. His source is Guido delle Colonne's Historia destructionis Troiae. Prince of Wales (later Henry V) commissioned it to show English language to be as fit for a grand theme as the other major literary languages: “Ywriten as wel in oure langage / As in Latyn and in Frensche it is.” His aim might have been to outdo Chaucer's Trojan romance Troilus and Criseyde. The modernized version Is sometimes attributed to Thomas Heywood, called The Life and Death of Hector, appeared in 1614. He acted as Shakespeare's sources for Troilus and Cressida.

Siege of Thebes (1420 to 22) written without an identifiable patron, it is seen as a response to Geoffrey Chaucer's The Canterbury Tales. It uses pilgrimage-based framing device and is written as an additional tale in the cycle. Lydgate establishes himself as the narrator of the work, and recounts the siege of Thebes. He delivers a complex Lancastrian and pacifist message. Source: Statius' epic, the Thebaid and Seneca's Oedipus. However, he uses French Le Roman de Thébes as an expanded source of the original.

The Fall of Princes (1431-38) in nine books from Giovanni Boccaccio's work De Casibus Virorum Illustrium whose French translation by Laurent de Premierfait he read. In rhyme royal, the poem tells about lives and tragic deaths of many historical and legendary persons. A sixteenth-century poem The Mirror for Magistrates by various authors is a sequel to The Fall of Princes.

London Lickpenny, a well-known satirical work attributed to him but now discredited.

The Complaint of the Black Knight was falsely attributed in Scotland as a work of Geoffrey Chaucer. It was chosen by UNESCO (in 2010) to become part of its Memory of the World Register.

Dance macabre in which death addresses people from every class and age and talks about mortality.

The Churl and the Bird poem talks of how churl has imprisoned the bird and says "Who lesith his fredam, in soth, he lesith all." It tells us how prison and song are not in accordance.

The Assembly of Gods in ABABBCC rhyme pattern of the rhyme royal was originally attributed to John Lydgate but is disputed.

House of Tudor dynasty

From 1485 to 1603. Henry VII establishes it by marrying Elizabeth of York, daughter of King Edward IV, heiress of the Yorkist claim to the throne, thus symbolically uniting the former warring factions of Lancaster and York under the new dynasty (represented by the Tudor rose).

Caxton comes from Burgundy with his art of printing and this leads to a revival of interest in medieval romance for a period which was later checked by Humanism of thinkers like Roger Ascham. Stephen Hawes (1474 to 1523)

Praises Lydgate as his master and sees him superior to Chaucer.

The Court of Sapience (dating from 1470) sometimes attributed to him is in 2 parts. In the first part, a dispute ensues between Mercy, Peace, Righteousness and truth regarding the fate of a man. The second part deals with different medieval learnings. In the opening he confesses that he’s “moost naked in al artes” and his native tongue is corrupt.

Thomas Field, in his Conversation between a Lover and a Jay (1530), wrote "Yong Steven Hawse, whose soule God pardon, Treated of love so clerkly and well". Novelist Hilda Lamb made Hawes a character of her novel The Willing Heart published in 1958, where he is fictionally portrayed as an illegitimate son of King Richard III's. C.S. Lewis has treated Hawes dismissively: "faculty was what he lacked; there was more and better poetry in him than he could express".

The History of Graunde Amour and la Bel Pucel, conteining the knowledge of the Seven Sciences and the Course of Mans Life in this Worlde or The Passetyme of Pleasure (1509) printed by Wynkyn de Worde and dedicated to Henry VII in 1506. It’s a long allegorical poem in seven-lined stanzas of man's life in this world. It is divided into sections after the manner of Le Morte d'Arthur and borrows the machinery of romance. Its main motive is the education of the knight, Graunde Amour, based, according to William John Courthope (History of English Poetry, vol. I. 382), on the Marriage of Mercury and Philology, by Martianus Capella. Fame accompanied by her greyhounds, Grace and Governance, sends him on a quest of La Bel Puce. He reaches the Tower of Doctrine or Science and learns arts of grammar, logic, rhetoric and arithmetic. With a dispute with lady in the Tower of Music, he leaves and goes to his studies. Then to Tower of Geometry; then Tower of Doctrine, the Castle of Chivalry, and finally Castle of La Bel Pucel where he is met by Peace, Mercy, Justice, Reason and Memory. Amour marries Pucel but then later comes Age and with it evils of Avarice and Cunning. Then to his relief come Contrition and Conscience. Remembraunce has delivered an epitaph chiefly dealing with the Seven Deadly Sins, and Fame has enrolled Graunde Amours name with the knights of antiquity and we part with the hero as he addresses us from the grave about how death is the end of all. It had influence on the genius of Edmund Spenser. William Nevill’s Castell of Pleasure (1518) is in this vein but the printer (Robert Copland) introduces dialogue between author and printer in the beginning of the poem. Otherwise it’s dull.

The Conversyon of Swerers (1509) and A Joyfidi Medytenon to all Englonde, a coronation poem (1509); Example of Virtue.

Dr Alexander Barclay (1476 to 1552) Scot

A poet and clergyman, provost of Oriel College, Oxford.

The Ship of Fools (1509) in rhyme royal,  partly a translation from Sebastian Brant’s Das Narrenschiff or Ship of Fools (1494), a satirical book. Originally appears in Book VI of Plato's Republic, which is about a ship with a dysfunctional crew sailing towards Naragonia. It represent the problems of governance prevailing in a political system not based on expert knowledge. In Sebastian’s version, a ship—an entire fleet at first—sets off from Basel, bound for the Paradise of Fools. Brant conceives Saint Grobian, whom he imagines to be the patron saint of vulgar and coarse people. As a "complex political satire", it targets "ark of salvation" as the Catholic Church was styled. He shows Pope Paul II and the Holy Roman Emperor Frederick III wrestling at the top of a ship's mast, wearing only briefs and their respective Papal tiara and crown. It was the starting-point of a new satirical literature. As an allegory, the figures are no longer abstractions; they are concrete examples of the folly of the bibliophile who collects books but learns nothing from them, of the evil judge who takes bribes to favour the guilty, of the old fool whom time merely strengthens in his folly, of those who are eager to follow the fashions, of the priests who spend their time in church telling "gestes" of Robin Hood and so forth.

Life of St George (c. 1515) for Thomas Howard, 3rd Duke of Norfolk with a dedication to Nicholas West.

Mirrour of Good Manners (c. 1518), from the Italian of Dominic Mancini. This work was written at the command of Sir Giles Alington. It was printed by his printer Richard Pynson with whom he mostly collaborated.

Eclogues (1518) five. 3 translated from Pope Pius II’s Miserae Curialium and 2 from Italian Mantuanus (Mantuan for English). The Eclogues include laments for John Alcock, John Morton, and Roger Westminster, Prior of Ely. This brings pastoral as the satire against city, ecclesiastical life and court. He become the first English writer to use it as a satirical device which was to be used by Spenser in his The Shepherd's Calendar (1579).

John Skelton (1460-1529)

English poet and tutor to King Henry VIII of England. Pope saw him as “beastly Skelton”. Puttenham, in his Arte of English Poesie (1589) calls him "rude rayling rimer". His patron Countess of Richmond, Henry VII’s mother, for whom he wrote Of Mannes Lyfe the Peregrynacioun, a lost translation of Guillaume de Diguileville’s “Pèlerinage de la vie humaine”. He wrote an elegy “Of the death of the noble prince Kynge Edwarde the forth,” on the death of Henry Percy, fourth earl of Northumberland. He wrote for his pupil, Prince Henry a lost Speculum principis, and Erasmus, in 1500, dedicated an ode to the prince speaking of Skelton “the one light and glory of British letters.” Skelton frequently signed himself “regius orator” and poet-laureate (after  appointment by Henry VII).

Garlande of Laurell written in praise of himself as Fame and Pallas discuss his qualifications and Gower, Chaucer and Lydgate hail him. A group of ladies make him a garland to crown him. He sings lyrically for them especially Margery Wentworth. Skelton provides a lengthy list of his works, although only a few have survived. The poem was composed during his stay with the Duke of Norfolk at Sheriff Hutton Castle.

The Bowge of Court: is a satire in rhyme royal on the dangers and vices of court life, presented through an allegorical dream in which Skelton encounters deceitful figures aboard a ship (alike Ship of Fools).

Speke, Parrot the poet speaks through the bird in rhyme royal (and other meters as it was composed veriously at different times) and attacks people like Cardinal Wolsey.

Boke of the Thre Foles: this long poem draws inspiration from Alexander Barclay’s Narrenschiff, critiquing foolishness through allegory.  Written in the seven-lined Rhyme Royal, a Continental verse-form first used in English by Chaucer, but it is in an irregular metre of his own—known as “Skeltonics” 

The Boke of Phyllyp Sparowe: is a 1,400-line poem inspired by Catullus, with Jane Scroop lamenting the death of her pet bird and then focusing on her beauty. The poem humorously diverges into Jane's extensive knowledge of medieval romances and includes Skelton's opinions on Chaucer, Gower, and Lydgate. Written in “Skeltonical” verse, characterised by short (2 beat line), irregular lines and frequent rhymes.

Colyn Cloute: poem attacks the sins of the clergy, particularly focusing on greed and ignorance, while indirectly criticizing Cardinal Wolsey.

Why Come Ye nat to Courte? rails at Cardinal Wolsey's ostentation, at his almost royal authority, his overbearing manner to suitors high and low, and taunts him with his mean extraction. It was circulated in MS as it couldn’t be printed while Wolsey was alive.

The Tunnynge of Elynour Rummynge: offers a vivid depiction of drunken women at a popular alehouse,  kept by Elynour Rummynge at Leatherhead, using the same “Skeltonical” verse for a comic effect. It brought him  charge of coarseness. "Tudor Portraits" set to music by Ralph Vaughan Williams in or around 1935. The tudor portraits by Skelton are 5 in number; other four be: "My Pretty Bess", "Epitaph of John Jayberd of Diss", "Jane Scroop (her lament for Philip Sparrow)", and "Jolly Rutterkin."

"Skelton Laureate against the Scottes" celebrating the victory of Flodden. "Jemmy is ded And closed in led, That was theyr owne Kynge," says the poem.

3 Plays (of which 1 important)

Magnificence (ca. 1515) the first secular morality play in English. Skelton influenced English drama by shifting from medieval allegories to individual character studies in this morality play. Aimed at Cardinal Wolsey, shows the rise, fall and final repentance of a wordly prince who is seduced and, in the end, redeemed by allegorical figures representing different virtues and vices. Magnificence is weighed down by Vices, conquered by Adversity but finally redeemed by goodhope and Perseverance. The play’s moral, namely “how suddenly worldly wealth doth decay,” was a favourite with him.

The primary candidate for authorship has been Thomas Malory of Newbold Revel in Warwickshire. A.T. Martin proposed that the author was Thomas Malory of Papworth St Agnes on the Huntingdonshire-Cambridgeshire border. There are other 2 claims.

Le Morte d'Arthur (1485; originally in 8 books) le morte Darthur; "The Death of Arthur", written in prison, printed by Caxton, who mentions: "For this was written by a knight prisoner Thomas Malleorre, that God send him good recovery." He was in prison for attempted murder of Humphrey Stafford, 1st Duke of Buckingham, an accusation of at least two rapes, and that he had attacked and robbed Coombe Abbey. A reworking of tales about the legendary King Arthur, Guinevere, Lancelot, Merlin and the Knights of the Round Table, along with their respective folklore from French and English sources. Source: French prose (the so-called Old French Vulgate romances) and compiled them together with Middle English sources (the Alliterative Morte Arthure and the Stanzaic Morte Arthur) to create this text (Vulgate Cycle). The title intended was to be The hoole booke of kyng Arthur & of his noble knyghtes of the rounde table, and only its final section to be named Le Morte Darthur but Caxton added: "Thus endeth this noble & joyous book entytled le morte Darthur, Notwythstondyng it treateth of the byrth, lyf, and actes of the sayd kynge Arthur; of his noble knyghtes of the rounde table, theyr meruayllous enquestes and aduentures, thachyeuyng of the sangreal, & in thende the dolorous deth & departynge out of this worlde of them al." He divided Malory's eight books into 21 books and 506 chapters. Other editions were The Boke of Kyng Arthur Somtyme Kynge of Englande and His Noble Actes and Feates of Armes of Chyvalrye (1498) and The Boke of the Moost Noble and Worthy Prince Kyng Arthur Somtyme Kyng of Grete Brytayne Now Called Englande (1529) by Wynkyn de Worde who succeeded to Caxton's press. William Copland's The Story of the Most Noble and Worthy Kynge Arthur (1557), Thomas East's The Story of Kynge Arthur, and also of his Knyghtes of the Rounde Table (1585), and William Stansby's The Most Ancient and Famous History of the Renowned Prince Arthur King of Britaine (1634).
