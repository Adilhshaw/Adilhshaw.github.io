Drama From the Miracle Plays to Marlowe

Miracle plays: divorced from the liturgy but still religious in subject matter.

The feast of Corpus Christi, established in 1264 and confirmed in 1311, became a popular day for miracle plays, though some cities preferred Whitsun. The CHESTER CYCLE contains 25 plays written in 8-line stanza with rime couvee, derived from the Romances. In the YORK CYCLE 48 survive out of an original cycle of 44. The WAKEFIELD cycle contains 32 plays (sometimes known as the Towneley Plays after a family who once owned the manuscript). Famous among them: Prima Pastorum and Secunda Pastorum. The “Wakefield Master”, anonymous author of 5 Wakefield plays is the first English writer of realistic comedy.

Morality Plays: does not deal with biblical or pseudobiblical story rather with personified abstractions of virtues and vices who struggle for man’s soul. Common themes: “psychomachia” (the battle for the soul); “Dance of Death” (God’s messenger, Death summoning all high and low alike).

The Pride of Life is the earliest extant morality play. A king, Rex Vivus boasts of his power and dismisses advice about his end, indulging in Mirth. A bishop reproves him, but he is dismissed with abuse, and Mirth is sent to defy Death. The fragment ends here, but the prologue reveals that Death ultimately claims the king, whose soul is saved by Our Lady’s prayers.

The Castle of Perseverance (c. 1425) is the earliest complete extant morality play, featuring 34 characters. It depicts the struggle between Mankind’s Good and Bad Angels, and the Seven Deadly Sins, from birth to Judgment Day. Mankind is initially saved by the Good Angel but is later seduced by Covetyse and ultimately faces Death. It is one of three plays in the Macro MS, along with Wisdom and Mankind. Both plays are in stanzaic verse.

Everyman is the most famous surviving 15th-century morality play. Summoned by Death for a journey from which he cannot return, Everyman seeks companionship from various allegorical figures—Fellowship, Goods, and Kindred—but they all abandon him. Only Good Deeds, initially too weak, eventually accompanies him after he repents and seeks guidance from Knowledge and Confession. As he nears his end, only Good Deeds remains with him. The play ends with an angel announcing Everyman’s soul entering heaven and a Doctor delivering a moral lesson. Everyman may be related to the Dutch play Elckerlik and is written in naïve rhymed couplets.

Interludes: towards the end of 15th century developed a type of morality play which dealt in the same allegorical way with general moral problems but with more pronounced realistic and comic elements. These plays mark the transition from medieval religious drama to Tudor secular drama.

TUDOR PLAYWRIGHTS: (associated with the household of Cardinal Morton; Medwell was Cardinal’s chaplain; Rastell married Moore’s sister; Heywood married their daughter, More’s niece.)

1)    Henry Medwall (1462 – c.1501/2?)

Nature: his early morality play

Fulgens and Lucrece is a late 15th-century interlude. It is the earliest purely secular English play that survives. The play is based on a Latin novella De Vera Nobilitate (On True Nobility) by the Italian humanist Bonaccorso or Buonaccorso da Montemagno of Pistoia, written in 1438 that had been translated into English by John Tiptoft, 1st Earl of Worcester and published in 1481 by William Caxton. Medwall used Tiptoft’s translation as his source. The plot is set in ancient Rome, where Lucrece, daughter of Senator Fulgens, is courted by Publius Cornelius, a wealthy patrician, and Gaius Flaminius, a modest plebeian. They both plead their worthiness to Lucrece (and not to the senate, as in Medwall’s source). Despite Publius’ charms and noble lineage, Lucrece chooses the sincere Gaius for his genuine love. A comic subplot involves the characters A and B, who first discuss the play and later appear as servants to Publius and Gaius, competing for the affection of Joan, Lucrece’s handmaid. In his epilogue Medwell explains his purpose was to edify and to amuse.

Of the Finding of Truth, carried away by Ignorance and Hypocrisy: his interlude.

2)    John Rastell (c. 1475 – 1536)

The Nature of the Four Elements: published anonymously in the early 16th century, is a Humanist morality play featuring allegorical characters that teach about new science and geography. It includes typical low-life scenes with the Vices. John Redford’s Wit and Science, another Humanist morality play likely from the 1530s, follows Wit, a student, who must overcome Tediousness and journey to Mount Parnassus to marry Science. These plays focus on education rather than salvation, marking a shift from medieval to secular, educational entertainment.

His best known legal works are The Abbreviacion of Statutis (1519) and Expositiones terminorum legum Angliae (1527).

Rastell’s best-known work is The Pastyme of People, the Chronycles of dyvers Realmys and most specially of the Realme of England (1529), a chronicle dealing with English history from the earliest times to the reign of Richard III.

His interludes emphasize more on amusement than instruction:

Witty and Witless

The Play Called the Four PP (1530): Lying competition between a palmer, a pardoner, apothecary with the pedlar as a judge. The apothecary tells a bawdy tale about a cure, while the pardoner recounts visiting Hell to find the troublesome Margery Corson, whom Lucifer wants to banish. The palmer, denying the idea of shrewish women, wins the competition with his blatant lie that he has never seen “any one woman out of patience”

The Play of Love (1533)

The Play of the Weather (1533): Jupiter resolves celestial conflicts and descends to earth to gather views on ideal weather. Various English citizens petition for their preferred weather, with disputes arising between Millers, a Gentlewoman, and a Laundress. Merry Report summarizes these arguments for Jupiter, who concludes that no one’s needs outweigh others, so the weather will remain unchanged to please everyone partially.

A Merry Play between Johan Johan the husband, Tybe his wife, and Sir Johan the Priest: John John begins with John debating whether to punish his wife, Tyb, for her absence. When she returns, Tyb quickly takes control, dismissing his suspicions about her infidelity with the priest, Sir John, by claiming she was baking a pie with other women. She orders John to invite the priest and complete household chores. At Sir John's house, the priest deceives John by pretending to support him. Back home, Tyb and the priest conspire to humiliate John, sending him to fetch water with a broken bucket while they eat the pie and mock him. John eventually fights back, driving them out, but his anxiety leads him to follow them.

John Bale (1495-1563)

wrote the oldest known historical verse drama in English (on the subject of King John). His Three Laws of Nature, Moses and Christ, corrupted by the Sodomytes, Pharisees and Papystes most wicked (produced in 1538 and again in 1562) was a morality play. Bale's most important work is Summary of the Famous Writers of Great Britain, that is, of England, Wales and Scotland.

King Johan: 16th century protestant propagonist play about John of England, who reigned during the thirteenth century in England. King John is represented as the champion of English church rites, as opposed to the Catholic Pope. He is shown to be against a number of Catholic villains, but is eventually assassinated by Sedition and Dissimulation before being defended by Verity. Allegorical characters are mixed with the real persons. Ynglonde vidua (Widow England) represents the nation, and the jocular element is provided by Sedwyson (sedition), occupying the role of Vice in a pure morality play.

EARLY COMEDIES: (domesticating the themes from Plautus into English comedies)

Nicholas Udall (1504 – 1556)

He was tutored under the guidance of Thomas Cromwell, who mentions him in a letter to John Creke as “Maister Woodall” and he appears again in Cromwell’s accounts for 1535 as “Nicholas Woodall Master of Eton”. At one time headmaster of Eton and at the end of his life headmaster of Westminster. With John Leland, he wrote a number of songs to celebrate the coronation of Anne Boleyn on 31 May 1533, using his Latinized name “Udallus”. Udall wrote a propaganda tract in response to the Prayer Book Rebellion in 1549, An Answer to the Articles of the Commoners of Devonshire and Cornwall Declaring to the Same How they have been Seduced by Evil Persons. This tract has sometimes been wrongly attributed to Philip Nichols.

Respublica his interlude first performed in 1553.

Ralph Roister Doister: written in about 1552. Regarded as the first comedy to be written in the English language. The play is written in five acts. The plot of the play centres on a rich widow, Christian Custance, who is betrothed to Gawyn Goodluck, a merchant. Ralph Roister Doister is encouraged throughout by a con-man trickster figure (Matthew Merrygreeke) to woo Christian Custance, but his pompous attempts do not succeed. Ralph then tries with his friends and servants (at Merrygreek's behest) to break in and take Christian Custance by force, but they are defeated by her maids and run away. The merchant Gawyn arrives shortly after and the play concludes happily with reconciliation, a prayer and a song. Taking its theme from Miles Gloriosus of Plautus, thus bringing the “braggart soldier” archetype for the first time into English Drama.

Another early English comedy drama, Gammer Gurton’s Needle: written by “Mr. S.,” probably William Stevenson. Five act play about Gammer Gurton’s loss of her needle which is later accidently discovered sticking in the seat of Hodge’s breeches who is Gammer’s farm servant. Diccon the Bedlam is the central comic figure of the play.

George Gascoigne (c. 1535 – 1577)

English poet, soldier and unsuccessful courtier. He was the first poet to deify Queen Elizabeth I, in effect establishing her cult as a virgin goddess married to her kingdom and subjects. His most noted works include A Discourse of the Adventures of Master FJ (1573), an account of courtly intrigue; one of the earliest English prose comedies, The Supposes; the frequently anthologised short poem “Gascoignes wodmanship” (1573); “Certayne Notes of Instruction concerning the making of verse or ryme in English” (1575), the first essay on English versification, written at the request of Master Edouardo Donati, prefixed to his Posies.

The Supposes (performed in 1566, printed in 1573), an early translation of Ariosto’s Gli Suppositi and the first comedy written in English prose, which was used by Shakespeare as a source for The Taming of the Shrew. The plot follows a student and his clever servant using disguise and intrigue to outwit the older generation and win the girl, resulting in a chaotic mix of misunderstandings and deceptions.

EARLY TRAGEDIES: (humanist interest in the Latin and Greek classics)

No tragedies among miracles or morality plays until the classical influence. Seneca was the favourite classical writer of tragedies among English Humanists. Seneca, a Stoic Roman, wrote nine tragedies never to be acted based on the Greek myths. They were translated into English by Jasper Heywood. A group of plays produced in the 1560’s and 1570’s and still popular in Shakespeare’s day:

Richard Edwardes (also Edwards, 1525 – 1566)

English poet, playwright, and composer. Known for his comedies and interludes. He was also rumoured to be an illegitimate son of Henry VIII. He joined the Chapel Royal by 1557 and was appointed Master of the Children in 1561. In 1566, his Palamon and Arcite was performed before Elizabeth I at Oxford when the stage fell — three people died and five were injured as a result. Damon and Pythias (played in 1564), a comedy, is his only extant play which chronicles the Greek friendship story of Damon and Pythias. It was originally performed by the Children of the Chapell for Queen Elizabeth. Aristippus, a philosopher turned courtier, pretends to befriend the deceitful Carisophus. Damon and Pythias arrive in Syracuse, and Carisophus has Damon arrested as a spy to gain favor with King Dionysius. Pythias offers to be imprisoned while Damon settles affairs in Greece, but if Damon doesn’t return, Pythias will be executed. Damon returns just in time, and their true friendship moves Dionysius to spare Pythias and renounce his tyranny. The play ends with Carisophus being banished and a reflection on the value of genuine friendship.

John Pickering’s New Interlude of Vice Containing the History of Horestes (printed in 1567), tells the story of Orestes’ revenge on his mother and Aegisthus (“Egestus”) with a remarkable mixture of military violence (“make your battle lively,” says a direction to the players), allegorical action with the Vice acting as both clown and tempter, and realistic English characters. Pickering’s source for his version of the story is William Caxton's translation of the French romance Recuyell of the Historyes of Troye (translated in 1475). The play’s full title is A new interlude of Vice containing the history of Horestes with the cruel revengement of his father's death upon his one natural mother.

Apius and Virginia by “R.B.” (printed in 1575 and described on the title page as “a new tragical comedy”)

Thomas Preston (1537–1598)

English master of Trinity Hall, Cambridge, and possibly a dramatist. Cambises (ca. 1570), which is described as “a lamentable tragedy, mixed full of pleasant mirth”. The dramatis personae include allegorical figures (e.g. Cruelty, Small Ability) as well as historical personages (such as the title character, Cambyses II of Persia). The play is largely written in rhyming fourteener couplets, with some irregular heroic verse (as in the speeches of the comic character Ambidexter) The lumbering “fourteeners”, were mocked by Shakespeare both in the play given by Bottom and his companions in A Midsummer Night’s Dream and in Falstaff’s “for I must speak in passion, and I will do it in King Cambyses vein” in Henry IV, Part I. Preston also wrote a broadside ballad entitled A Lamentation from Rome how the Pope doth bewayle the Rebelles in England cannot prevayle. To the tune of “Rowe well, ye mariners”  This ballad is written “in the person of a fly who happens to be lodged in the pope’s nose when news comes about the Catholic uprising in the north of England” and describes the pope raging and hurling furniture, to the fly’s terror.  Another ballad, titled A Ballad from the Countrie, sent to showe how we should Fast this Lente is extant and dated 1589. Both the surviving ballads, as well as Cambyses, are subscribed at the end “Quod Thomas Preston”

Sir Philip Sidney, in his Defence of Poesie approved only the Senecan tragedy Gorboduc (also known as Ferrex and Porrex), by Thomas Sackville (wrote the last 2 acts) and Thomas Norton (wrote the first 3 acts), produced both at Inner Temple and before the Queen at Whitehall in 1561-62. First English play in blank verse. Five-act play depicts a divided kingdom and civil war, exploring the consequences of split authority. The playtext summarizes the plot in the “Argument”: King Gorboduc divides his kingdom between his sons, Ferrex and Porrex, despite warnings. The brothers fall into conflict, with Porrex killing Ferrex. In revenge, their mother Videna kills Porrex. The people, outraged, kill both parents. The nobles quash the rebellion but face a civil war over the throne, as succession remains uncertain. Fergus, Duke of Albany, tries to seize power, but the nobles oppose him as a foreign invader. The play ends in chaos, with calls for Parliament to decide the next king, lamenting that this should have been done while Gorboduc was alive. Imitations of Gorboduc, includes Jocasta, by Gascoigne and Francis Kinwelmersh, presented at Gray's Inn in 1566, and Gismond of Salern which was first written in rhymed verse and then rewritten in blank verse by Robert Wilmot.

Elizabethan stage: round, wooden, roofless building. 3 galleries of seats and a Pit (no seats). 40 feet wide, 27 feet projection into the pit. The first private theatre, Blackfriars opened in 1576 for the children of the Chapel Royal.}

 

UNIVERSITY WITS: Term first used by George Sainstbury. Attended Oxford university: John Lyly, George Peele, Thomas Lodge; Cambridge university: Robert Greene, Thomas Nashe, Christopher Marlowe; Thomas Kyd did not attend any university. They transformed didactic and shapeless chronicle into real plays.

John Lyly (1554-1606)

He was best known during his lifetime for his two books Euphues: The Anatomy of Wit (1578) a romantic intrigue told in letters. Plot borrowed from Boccaccio. “Euphues”, a Greek word meaning “well bred man”. Got the name from Roger Ascham’s The Schoolmaster; and its sequel Euphues and His England (1580).  Lyly’s distinctive and much imitated literary style, named after the title character of his two books, is known as euphuism. The proverb “All is fair in love and war” has been attributed to Lyly’s Euphues. Most of his plays performed by Children of St. Paul’s.

Comedies of Lyly:

Campaspe performed 1583/84 (printed 1584): according to F. S. Boas, “the first of the comedies with which John Lyly inaugurated the golden period of the Elizabethan theatre”. A prose comedy by John Lyly based on the story of the love triangle between Campaspe, a Theban captive, the artist Apelles, and Alexander the Great, who commissioned him to paint her portrait. For his narrative source, Lyly depended on the Natural History of Pliny the Elder for the tale of Alexander the Great and Campaspe. PLOT:  In Athens, Alexander falls in love with the Theban captive, Campaspe, and grants her freedom. He commissions the artist Apelles to paint her portrait, but Apelles also falls in love with her and delays completing the portrait to spend more time with her. Campaspe reciprocates Apelles’ feelings. When Apelles presents the finished portrait, his behaviour reveals his love for Campaspe. Alexander magnanimously resigns his interest in her, allowing her true love with Apelles to flourish, and turns his attention to conquering Persia. Alexander spends time in Athens with his close friend Hephestion, who disapproves of his infatuation with Campaspe. He also converses with philosophers, notably Diogenes, who, unimpressed with the conqueror, nonetheless earns Alexander’s admiration: “Hephestion, were I not Alexander I would wish to be Diogenes.” Philosophers like Plato, Aristotle, and others from various schools debate in Alexander’s presence. The play also features witty pages and humorous scenes, including characters like soldiers who, distracted by a courtesan, forget their military duties, declaring, “Down with arms, and up with legs!”.

Sapho and Phao performed 1584 (printed 1584): likely the first that the playwright devoted to the allegorical idealisation of Queen Elizabeth I. The play takes place in Syracuse and nearby areas. Venus, aiming to humble Queen Sapho, grants a ferryman named Phao great beauty. Sapho’s ladies flirt with Phao, but he dismisses them. When Sapho sees Phao, they both fall in love, but their differing social status creates an insurmountable obstacle. Venus accidentally falls for Phao, enlisting Vulcan and Cupid to break love spells. Cupid cures Sapho’s love but instead falls for her. He fails to make Phao love Venus, even inspiring his revulsion for her. The play ends with Phao leaving Sicily, and Cupid rebelling by staying with Sapho. Comic relief is provided by Sapho’s ladies-in-waiting, a “Sybilla” whom Phao consults.

Gallathea performed 1587/88 (printed 1592): The pastoral play begins in a Lincolnshire village, where Tyterus and Melebeus both disguise their daughters, Gallathea and Phillida, as boys to avoid Neptune’s demand for a virgin sacrifice. Both girls, unaware of each other’s true identity, hide in the woods, where they meet and fall in love. Meanwhile, Cupid, angered by Diana's nymphs’ rejection, causes romantic chaos among them. As the play progresses, Neptune discovers the deception, and Diana uncovers Cupid’s mischief. Diana forces Cupid to reverse his spells, restoring order among the nymphs. In a subplot, the three Miller's sons—Raffe, Robin, and Dicke—navigate humorous encounters with various tradesmen and eventually become minstrels. In the climax, the villagers attempt to sacrifice Hebe, but Neptune’s monster refuses her, leading to a comedic twist. Finally, Venus intervenes, resolving the conflict between Neptune and Diana. Gallathea and Phillida’s love is revealed, and Venus promises to turn one into a boy so they can be together, though it is left unclear which one. The play ends with an Epilogue urging the audience to yield to love, celebrating its power.

Endymion, the Man in the Moon performed 1588 (printed 1591): While the title and characters are references to the myth of Endymion, the plot sharply deviates from the classical story. The play begins with Endymion's obsessive love for Cynthia, which concerns his friend Eumenides. Meanwhile, Tellus, angered by Endymion’s shift in affection, enlists the sorceress Dipsas to curse him into a deep sleep. As Endymion slumbers, Eumenides searches for a cure, while Cynthia learns of the curse and seeks justice. Throughout the play, various romantic and comic subplots unfold, including Sir Tophas’s misguided love for Dipsas and the relationship between Eumenides and Semele. After many years, Eumenides returns with a prophecy: only Cynthia's kiss can awaken Endymion. She complies, and he awakens, though aged. Upon the revelation of the plot against him, Tellus repents, and Dipsas is forgiven. Cynthia restores Endymion’s youth and resolves the conflicts, leading to the marriage of Tellus and Corsites, Eumenides and Semele, and the repentance of Dipsas. The play concludes with a moral fable directed at Queen Elizabeth, emphasizing the power of warmth and kindness over force.

Midas performed 1589/90 (printed 1592): Lyly’s Midas represents the fabulously wealthy Philip II of Spain. Lyly’s play, based on Ovid's Metamorphoses, enhances the Midas legend with allegorical elements. Midas, king of Phrygia, is granted the wish that everything he touches turns to gold, following advice to finance his invasion of Lesbos. However, his golden touch brings misfortune, turning food, wine, and even his beard to gold. He cures himself by bathing in the river Pactolus. Later, Midas judges a musical contest between Apollo and Pan, favoring Pan. As punishment, Apollo gives him ass's ears. Midas hides this affliction, but the secret eventually spreads. His daughter Sophronia seeks guidance from Delphi, where Midas repents and is cured, abandoning his conquests. The comic subplot involves Midas’s barber, Motto, who acquires Midas's golden beard. However, it is stolen by mischievous pages who blackmail Motto for its return, accusing him of treason if he doesn't comply.

Mother Bombie performed c.1590 (printed 1594): the title character specifically denies that she is a witch, and calls herself a “cunning woman.” His only play that did not use mythological stories: “vulgar realistic play of rustic life”. Set in Rochester, Kent, the play revolves around two young couples and their four scheming fathers. Wealthy old men Memphio and Stellio plan to marry their children, Accius and Silena, unaware that both are fools. Each father tries to deceive the other. Meanwhile, true lovers Candius and Livia face opposition from their fathers, Sperantus and Prisius, who prefer more lucrative matches for their children. The fathers disguise their foolish children as their sensible counterparts—Accius as Candius and Silena as Livia—but the plan fails, exposing their folly. Candius and Livia, however, trick their fathers by disguising themselves as Accius and Silena, and marry. Sperantus and Prisius reconcile with the marriage, while Memphio and Stellio almost allow their foolish children to wed, believing any marriage is better than none. The plot takes a twist when the nurse Vicinia reveals that Accius and Silena are her children, secretly swapped with the real heirs, Maestius and Serena. Once Maestius and Serena learn they aren’t siblings, they can marry. The rich fathers accept their true children, agree to care for the false ones, and the play ends happily. Mother Bombie, the local cunning woman, acts as a chorus, predicting outcomes and guiding the resolution by convincing Vicinia to reveal the truth.

Love’s Metamorphosis performed c.1589/90 (printed 1601): an allegorical pastoral. Set in Arcadia, the play follows two main storylines. The first involves Ceres and her three nymphs, Nisa, Celia, and Niobe, who scorn three foresters, Ramis, Montanus, and Silvestris. In retaliation, Cupid transforms the nymphs into a rock, a rose, and a bird. The subplot centers on Erisichthon, a cruel peasant who kills a transformed nymph, Fidelia, by cutting down a sacred tree. Ceres punishes him with famine, leading him to sell his daughter Protea. Protea escapes, rescues her beloved Petulius, and returns home. Ceres negotiates with Cupid to release her nymphs in exchange for pardoning Erisichthon. The nymphs are restored, accept the foresters as husbands, and the play ends with a quadruple wedding. Unlike most of Lyly’s works, this play lacks the usual wit and humour, suggesting it may be a revised version of an original.

The Woman in the Moon probably performed 1590-95 (printed 1597): it is the only play Lyly wrote in blank verse. All except this play performed by Children of Paul’s. This pastoral play is set in Greek mythology, at the dawn of humanity, before the creation of the first woman. The goddess of Nature, with Concord and Discord, creates Pandora from a statue. She is gifted the best qualities of the seven planets, who then retaliate by afflicting her with their negative influences. Saturn makes her melancholic, Jupiter fills her with pride, Mars turns her aggressive, and so on. As the planets take turns, Pandora's behavior changes drastically, causing chaos among the shepherds who encounter her. Although she briefly finds love with the shepherd Stesias, the planetary influences make her untrustworthy and mad. In the end, Pandora, rejected by all, chooses to ascend with Luna. Stesias is condemned to follow her eternally in the moon, unable to express his anger or hurt her.

George Peele (1558-1596)

The Arraignment of Paris (1581): courtly mythological pastoral play played by the Children of the Chapel. Performed before Queen Elizabeth in the early 1580s, it tells the story of Paris’s judgment with lyrical grace, including his love for Oenone and subsequent faithlessness. A subplot features Colin’s unrequited love for Thestylis, with rustic characters adding realism. Paris defends his judgment before the gods, and Diana awards the golden apple to a nymph symbolizing Queen Elizabeth. The play uses various verse forms, including “fourteeners” and blank verse, and features notable lyrics, such as the song “Fair and fair, and twice so fair,” which later echoes in Oenone's lament.

The Troublesome Reign of King John (1589): the source and model that William Shakespeare employed for his own King John (c. 1596).

The Battle of Alcazar (1591): is a five-act non-fiction play that tells the story of the battle. Like Shakespeare's Henry V (1599), it is narrated by a Chorus who describes the action in terms far more heroic than it warrants: King Sebastian of Portugal is referred to as “an honourable and courageous prince”, but is in fact shown to be foolish in invading Morocco, having been duped by Mulai Mohammed. The play centers on the conflict over the Moroccan throne after the death of the King of Morocco. His son, Muly Mahmet, seizes power by murdering his relatives, but his uncle, Abdelmelec, returns from exile with an Ottoman-backed army to reclaim the throne. Muly Mahmet seeks help from Portugal, promising to make Morocco a tributary state. King Sebastien of Portugal agrees, setting the stage for a climactic battle between the Ottomans and the Portuguese. The battle at Alcazar results in the deaths of Abdelmelec, Muly Mahmet, and Sebastien. Ultimately, Muly Mahmet's youngest uncle, Muly Mahamet Seth, becomes the new King of Morocco. The battle thus claims the lives of three kings: (i) King Abdelemec (who died of grief), (ii) Muly Mahamet (the ousted tyrant), and (iii) King Sebastien of Portugal.

The Famous Chronicle of King Edward the First (1593): The play revolves around the power struggle between Edward I and Llywelyn ap Gruffudd, while also referencing the fall of John Balliol. The narrative, influenced by ballads and the legend of Robin Hood, presents Llywelyn's rebellion against Edward and explores the ambition to unite Britain by force. The episodic plot includes scenes of Welsh resistance, Scottish fealty, and English victories. Key moments involve Edward's return from the Crusade, Llywelyn’s battles, and the various political maneuvers surrounding marriage alliances and betrayals. The play concludes with dramatic shifts, including the sinking of Queen Elinor and the capture of John Balliol. The final scenes include darkly comedic elements and references to the potential influence between Peele’s and Marlowe’s works.

The Love of King David and Fair Bethsabe (1594): based on the biblical story of David, Bathsheba, and Absalom in 2 Samuel. The full title of the play, as it appears on the title page of the 1599 quarto, is The Love of King David and Fair Bethsabe, with the Tragedie of Absalon. Written in slow moving blank verse.

The Old Wives’ Tale (1595): has been identified as the first English work to satirize the romantic dramas popular at the time. Uses the device of a play within a play to add to the confusion. The plot centers around three young men who become lost in the woods, but are given shelter for the night by Clunch, a blacksmith, and his wife Madge (the eponymous 'old wife'). During their stay, one retires to bed with Clunch, while the other two are entertained by their hostess, who tells them a fairy-tale, which, to her surprise, comes to life: her characters appearing and telling it for her (the 'play-within-the play'). One strand of the plot involves two brothers who are on an adventure searching for their sister, Delia, who is being held captive by the magician Sacrapant (compare Milton’s Comus). The magician also captures the brothers. Eventually they are all rescued by a knight aided by a ghost who is motivated by gratitude for past acts of kindness by the knight. Songs and magical invocations are interwoven into the play, imbuing it with a magical atmosphere. The introductory scene is in colloquial prose; the main part of the play is partly in prose and partly in blank verse.

Among his occasional poems are: The Honour of the Garter, which has a prologue containing Peele’s judgments on his contemporaries, and Polyhymnia (1590), a blank verse description of the ceremonies attending the retirement of the queen’s champion, Sir Henry Lee. This is concluded by the lyric poem, A Farewell to Arms: was written for the retirement ceremony in 1590 of Queen Elizabeth I's champion knight in which he pledges undying loyalty to the queen, addressed as “Goddess”. It was quoted by Thackeray in the seventy-sixth chapter of The Newcomes and notably served as the title of Ernest Hemingway’s novel of the same name.

Robert Greene (1558-92)

A prolific and versatile bohemian better known for his autobiographical prose than his plays. Both Oxford and Cambridge degrees. Got into a feud of Pamphlets with Gabriel Harvey. Wrote prose works imitative of Euphues and Arcadia.

Some prose works:

Mamillia: A Mirror or Looking-glass for the Ladies of England (1583), dedicated to Lord Darcy of the North.

The Anatomy of Lovers' Flatteries (1584), dedicated to Mary Rogers, wife to Master Hugh Rogers of Everton

Morando; The Tritameron of Love (1587), dedicated to Philip Howard, 20th Earl of Arundel

Pandosto (1588) dedicated to George Clifford, 3rd Earl of Cumberland. It is a prose romance. A later edition of 1607 was re-titled Dorastus and Fawnia. Was an inspiration for that of Shakespeare's play The Winter's Tale. He may himself have based the work on The Clerk's Tale, one of The Canterbury Tales of Chaucer. In Greene's tale, Pandosto, King of Bohemia, accuses his wife Bellaria of adultery committed with his childhood friend, the King of Sicilia. His pursuit of this unfounded charge leads him to send his infant daughter out to sea to die and causes the death of his son and his wife. His daughter drifts to Sicilia and is saved and raised by a shepherd. Dorastus, the Prince of Sicilia, falls in love with Fawnia, unaware that she is a Princess, and they run away to marry. They land in Bohemia, where Pandosto unwittingly falls in love with his daughter Fawnia. At the end of the story, after Fawnia's identity is revealed, Pandosto commits suicide out of grief for the troubles he caused his family.

Menaphon (1587), dedicated to Lady Hales, wife to the late deceased Sir James Hales.

Greene's Mourning Garment (1590), dedicated to George Clifford, 3rd Earl of Cumberland

Greene's Never Too Late (1590), dedicated to Thomas Burnaby, esquire

A Groatsworth of Wit Bought with a Million of Repentance (1592): written as a moralistic tale, which, towards the end, is revealed to have been autobiographical. The pamphlet tells the story of Roberto and Lucanio Gorinius, sons of a wealthy usurer. Roberto, a scholar, receives only a groat from his father, while Lucanio inherits wealth. Roberto introduces Lucanio to the courtesan Lamilia, who charms him. After Roberto’s failed scheme to profit from Lucanio’s naivety, Lamilia exposes his plan and casts him out. Roberto becomes a playwright, but Lucanio loses his fortune to Lamilia and ends up a pimp. Despite his success, Roberto squanders his wealth and dies with only a groat. The narrator, likening himself to Roberto, advises readers to live honorably and warns three playwrights, including an “upstart Crow,” (Shakespeare) to reform. The text concludes with an allegory on thrift and a letter in which Greene apologizes to his wife. Critised Shakespeare as “upstart crow beautified with our feathers”. The three other playwrights whom Greene admonishes were Thomas Lodge (“young Juvenal”); Christopher Marlowe (“famous gracer of Tragedians”); George Peele.

PLAYS:(his plays being the first English examples of “romantic comedy”)

Friar Bacon and Friar Bungay (circa 1590) a comedy originally entitled The Honorable Historie of Frier Bacon and Frier Bongay. Dialogue mostly in blank verse also occasionally in prose. Prince Edward, heir to King Henry III, enlists the necromancer Friar Bacon and his friend Earl Lacy to help seduce Margaret, the Fair Maid of Fressingfield. However, Lacy falls in love with Margaret, causing a rift with Edward, who eventually reconciles and marries Elinor of Castile. Meanwhile, Margaret becomes the cause of a fatal duel between two squires, Serlesby and Lambert. Lacy tests Margaret's fidelity before finally marrying her. Friar Bacon's magical feats, including summoning a brazen head to protect England, end in failure due to his servant Miles's incompetence. When Bacon accidentally provokes a fatal duel between two students using his magic, he renounces sorcery and turns to repentance. The play concludes with Miles humorously riding to Hell on a devil’s back after securing a tapster’s job there. The "Friar Bacon" of the title is Roger Bacon, the thirteenth-century polymath later a magician. The second friar was Bacon’s late contemporary Thomas Bungay. John of Bordeaux, or The Second Part of Friar Bacon, is the anonymous sequel to it.

The History of Orlando Furioso (circa 1590) is based on Ariosto.

A Looking Glass for London and England (circa 1590)  a collaboration between Thomas Lodge and Robert Greene. Recounting the Biblical story of Jonah and the fall of Nineveh, is a noteworthy example of the survival of the Medieval morality play style of drama in English Renaissance theatre. King Rasni of Nineveh, after defeating Jeroboam, declares himself a god and plans to marry his sister, Remilia, despite objections. He dismisses the King of Crete, giving his crown to the flatterer Radagon. The prophet Oseas, observing from a throne, comments on the events. Meanwhile, an usurer exploits the poor Alcon and the reckless Thrasybulus, with no justice found in corrupt courts. Rasni's wedding to Remilia is disrupted by her death from lightning, but he continues his wickedness with Alvida, who poisons her husband for Rasni's praise. Jonah, fleeing his divine mission, is eventually cast from a whale and accepts his task to warn Nineveh. Alcon’s son, Radagon, now powerful, scorns his poor family, but is cursed and consumed by fire. Ignoring divine warnings, Rasni and his court continue their sins until Jonah's preaching brings repentance. Even Rasni and Alvida repent, and the play concludes with Jonah urging the audience to heed its moral lessons.

The Scottish History of James the Fourth (circa 1590) a serious comedy derived from a story by the Italian Giraldi Cinthio. The play begins with a peculiar machine presented to Oberon, King of Fairies, by Bohan, a Scot, to illustrate his belief that the world is unfit for the wise. The central plot follows King James' love for Ida, the Countess of Arran’s daughter, and the ensuing troubles, including an attempt on the queen's life. Despite Ida's rejection and Queen Dorothea's trials, including being driven out and wounded, the story concludes happily.

The Comical History of Alphonsus, King of Aragon (circa 1590)

Thomas Lodge (ca. 1557-1625)

Practised medicine. Less important as a contributor to the development of drama. Responded to Stephen Gosson’s School of Abuse (See Gosson here) with Defence of Poetry, Music and Stage Plays (1579 or 1580) circulated privately as it was banned. It was answered by Gosson in his Playes Confuted in Five Actions (1582); and Lodge retorted with his Alarum Against Usurers (1584) a "tract for the times". Honest Excuses was his suppressed reply to Gosson. In 1584 he produced the first tale written by him on his own account in prose and verse, The Delectable History of Forbonius and Prisceria.  Published a historical romance, The History of Robert, Second Duke of Normandy, surnamed Robert the Devil.

Wits Miserie: sermon on seven deadly sins.

Rosalynde: Euphues Golden Legacy, Found After His Death In His Cell At Silexedra, (1590) his euphuistic prose tale. Source for As You Like it.

Life and Death of William Longbeard (1593) his second historical romance.

The Complaynte of Elsired (1593) narrative poem.

A Fig for Momus (1595): for this called the earliest English satirist, and contains eclogues addressed to Samuel Daniel and others, an epistle addressed to Michael Drayton, and other pieces.

Scillaes Metamorphosis, Enterlaced with the Unfortunate Love of Glaucus, more briefly known as Glaucus and Scilla. To this tale Shakespeare was possibly indebted for the idea of Venus and Adonis.

A Margarite of America (published 1596), a romance.

Wit’s Mercy (1596) contemporary abuses in euphuistic style.

TWO PLAYS:

The Wounds of Civil War (produced perhaps as early as 1587, and published in 1594): Lodge's only extant solo drama. A dramatization of the ancient Roman conflict between Marius and Sulla “Lively set forth in the true tragedy of Marius and Scilla”.

 A Looking Glass for London and England (published 1594): co-written with Robert Greene, recounting the Biblical story of Jonah and the fall of Nineveh, the play is a noteworthy example of the survival of the Medieval morality play style of drama in the period of English Renaissance theatre. The play begins with King Rasni of Nineveh basking in his triumph over Jeroboam, arrogantly proclaiming himself a god. He dismisses dissent, notably from the King of Crete, over his plan to marry his sister Remilia, and rewards sycophants while punishing opponents. The prophet Oseas, acting as an observer, warns of divine consequences. The subplot follows corrupt legal systems, with characters like Thrasybulus and Alcon suffering under a ruthless usurer. Rasni's incestuous wedding is interrupted by divine wrath when lightning kills Remilia. Rasni instead takes Alvida, a murderous queen, as his lover. Meanwhile, Jonah reluctantly accepts his mission to warn Nineveh of its sins after being swallowed and released by a whale. As Rasni's court descends into chaos, marked by betrayal and divine punishments, Jonah's preaching leads to repentance. Even Rasni and Alvida seek forgiveness.

Thomas Nashe (1567-1601)

He was involved in the Martin Marprelate controversy. In 1589 wrote Preface to Greene's Menaphon. In 1591 wrote Preface to Sir Philip Sidney's Astrophel and Stella. He famously featured in Thomas Dekker's News from Hell (1606). Nashe is widely remembered for three short poems, all drawn from his play,  Summer's Last Will and Testament: “Adieu, farewell, earth’s bliss,” “Fair summer droops” and “Autumn hath all the summer’s fruitful treasure.” He is also credited with the erotic poem The Choise of Valentines and his name appears on the title page of Christopher Marlowe's Dido, Queen of Carthage, though there is uncertainty as to what Nashe's contribution was.

The Anatomy of Absurdity: Contayning a Breefe Confutation of the Slaunders Obloquiously Scattered Against Women, Prescribing a Method How to Reclaime Bad Wives to Honesty, and Reprove the Ungodly Parrat-Like Discourse of Idle Poetasters (1589) an early prose work that critiques the moral and intellectual failings of Elizabethan society. Written in Nashe's characteristically sharp and witty style, the treatise attacks ignorance, vanity and the misuse of literature, presenting a scathing commentary on the absurdities of his time. The work condemns shallow writers and scholars who pursue superficial fame rather than true knowledge, emphasizing the dangers of valuing wit and entertainment over moral and intellectual substance. Nashe also criticizes the public’s fascination with frivolous trends and their disregard for meaningful cultural and literary pursuits. Throughout the text, he calls for a return to virtue, education, and literature that enlightens and uplifts rather than merely entertains.

An Almond for a Parrot (1590) credited to "Cutbert Curry-knave".

Pierce Penniless his Supplication to the Divell (1592) is a tall tale, or a prose satire. Satirized Gabriel Harvey and his brothers. Among the most popular of the Elizabethan pamphlets. It is written from the point of view of Pierce, a man who has not met with good fortune, who now bitterly complains of the world’s wickedness, and addresses his complaints to the devil. Pierce Penniless, a scholar and poet, laments his misfortune and the prevalence of wickedness. Desperate, he decides to appeal to the Devil, hoping to free up the wealth hoarded by the wicked. He seeks the Devil across Westminster, the Exchange, and St. Paul’s, where he meets a professional perjurer who delivers his supplication to the Devil. The supplication, based on the Seven Deadly Sins, personifies each vice and introduces various sinners. Nashe defends plays, praising historical dramas like Henry VI, Part 1. The tale ends abruptly with a reference to the Earl of Southampton.

Summer's Last Will and Testament (play performed 1592, published 1600): his only extant solo-authored play; his other surviving dramatic work, Dido, Queen of Carthage, is a collaboration with Christopher Marlowe. Nashe developed the play out of the interlude form that was popular in the royal and noble courts of sixteenth-century England. Seen as a bridge between the 16th-century interlude and the 17th-century masque. It features personifications of the four seasons, Summer, Autumn, Winter, and “Ver.” Summer is the “king of the world”, but now old and declining, and ready to make his will. First, all the officers and members of the kingdom are summoned to yield their accounts. The presences of Bacchus, satyrs, nymphs, hunters, reapers, maids and clowns and Morris dancers (complete with hobby horse), give the play a strongly pastoral feeling. The term “summer” in the title has a double meaning: the play is introduced and presented by the figure of Will Summer, or Summers, the jester of King Henry VIII. The play also contains a poem that later acquired independent fame, “A Litany in Time of Plague”. This contains the famous couplet, “Brightness falls from the air, Queens have died young and fair.” 

Strange News (1592)

Christ's Tears over Jerusalem (1593 ) (a pamphlet dedicated to Lady Elizabeth Carey.)

Terrors of the Night; Or A Discourse of Apparitions (1594) book which sceptically considers dreams, nightmares, and apparitions, which Nashe considers born of superstition, melancholy or imagination. Correspondence seen between the rationalism expressed in Act 5 of Midsummer Night’s Dream and the ideas expressed in The Terrors of the Night; Theseus in the play describes “the poet’s eye, in a fine frenzy rolling” and Nashe describes the constant “wheeling and rolling on of our braines”

The Unfortunate Traveller: or, the Life of Jack Wilton (1594 ) (originally published as The Unfortunate Traueller: or, The Life of Jacke Wilton) is a picaresque novel set during the reign of Henry VIII of England. The first English historical novel. It is told in first person. Contains a dedicatory letter to the Earl of Southhampton. Nashe's protagonist Jack Wilton navigates 16th-century Europe, interacting with historical events and figures. He begins by swindling superiors at a military camp and witnesses a massacre in Münster, reflecting on religious hypocrisy. Traveling with Henry Howard, Earl of Surrey, Jack encounters Erasmus and Sir Thomas More in Rotterdam, and later, Cornelius Agrippa shows Surrey a vision of his beloved. In Italy, Jack and Surrey swap identities for safety, engaging in deceit and trickery. Surrey wins a tournament in Florence but must return to England. Jack then explores Rome, marveling at ruins and gardens but lamenting the plague. He witnesses a rape, resulting in the victim's suicide, and narrowly avoids execution with help from the Banished Earl, who criticizes European cultures. Despite warnings, Jack stays in Italy and becomes entangled with Zadok the Jew and Zachary, the Papal Physician, who plan to use him as an anatomical specimen. Juliana, the Pope's courtesan, helps him escape, and he reunites with Diamante, robbing Juliana. The final episode involves Cutwolf, who avenges his brother by forcing the villain Esdras to blaspheme before killing him. Cutwolf defends his actions, but is executed. Jack and his wife Diamante flee Italy, returning to the English camp in France.

“Have with You to Saffron-Walden, or, Gabriell Harveys Hunt Is Up” (1596 ) is the title of a pamphlet. The work is Nashe’s final shot in his four-year literary feud with Dr. Gabriel Harvey. It consists of title-page, epistle dedicatory to Richard Lichfield, an address to “all Christian Readers”, and a lengthy dialogue between five characters. The pamphlet contains the earliest known reference to the line “Fee-fi-fo-fum.”  It also contains the earliest known reference to “If the worst come to the worst”, origin of the clause “If worst comes to worst.”  Lichfield answered in a tract called "The Trimming of Thomas Nash," (1597).

Isle of Dogs (1597) his play collaborated with Ben Jonson. For this Ben Jonson was arrested and Nashe escaped imprisonment.

Nashe’s Lenten Stuffe (1599) a mock-heroic celebration of the red hering, the origin of red hering is discussed and how it became fish. The origin is seen in Hero’s metamorphosis after his tragic end with leander. Leander after drowning changes into a ling (fish) and Hero (she) to a herring.

The Choise of Valentines: his famous erotic poem. Begins with a sonnet to "Lord S". It describes the Valentine's Day visit of a young man named 'Tomalin' to the brothel where his lover, "Mistris Francis", has recently become employed. Tomalin poses as a customer. Having paid ten gold pieces for her favours, Tomalin makes his way towards his erotic goal.

Thomas Kyd (1558-94)

Founder of “romantic tragedy”. In service of Lord Strange, the patron of Lord Strange’s Men. Believed to be the author of Spanish Tragedy only after Thomas Heywood, in his Apologie for Actors (1612), attributed the play to Kyd. Supposed to be the author of a Hamlet play pre-dating Shakespeare’s, which is now known as the Ur-Hamlet.

Don Horatio (partially extant in The First Part of Hieronimo, c. 1586)

The Spanish Tragedy or Hieronimo is Mad Again (c. 1587): established a new genre in English theatre: the revenge play or revenge tragedy. Personifies Revenge as its own character. Primarily takes place primarily in Spain. Set against the backdrop of the unification of Spain and Portugal, which was carried out as Phillip II ascended to the throne in Portugal.  Ben Jonson mentions "Hieronimo" in the Induction to his Cynthia's Revels (1600), has a character disguise himself in "Hieronimo's old cloak, ruff, and hat" in The Alchemist (1610). T. S. Eliot quoted the title and the play in his poem The Waste Land. The play also appears in Orhan Pamuk's 2002 novel Snow. PLOT: Play centers on Andrea, a Spanish officer killed by Balthazar, the Viceroy of Portugal's son, during a battle. Andrea's ghost and the personification of Revenge serve as the play's chorus, observing the events and awaiting justice. The play unfolds as Lorenzo, the King’s nephew, and Horatio, Andrea's friend, dispute over capturing Balthazar. Although Horatio deserves the credit, Lorenzo manipulates the situation to his advantage. Meanwhile, Bel-imperia, who loved Andrea, falls for Horatio, partly out of revenge against Balthazar. However, the Spanish King arranges a marriage between Bel-imperia and Balthazar to ease tensions with Portugal. Lorenzo, suspecting Bel-imperia’s new affection, discovers her relationship with Horatio through her servant Pedringano and convinces Balthazar to help murder Horatio during a secret meeting with Bel-imperia. Horatio's father, Hieronimo, finds his son’s body, driving him and his wife mad with grief. Bel-imperia is imprisoned by Lorenzo but manages to inform Hieronimo of the culprits using a blood-written letter. Hieronimo, denied justice by Lorenzo, pretends to forgive him and arranges a play where he and Bel-imperia exact real revenge by killing Balthazar and Lorenzo on stage. Bel-imperia also kills herself, leaving Hieronimo to confess the murders before biting out his own tongue and killing the Duke of Castille, Lorenzo's father, and himself. Andrea and Revenge, having witnessed the unfolding events, express satisfaction and plan eternal punishments for the guilty in the underworld. In A Midsummer Night’s Dream Titania speaks: “What angel wakes me from my flowery bed?” which is a parody of Hieronimo’s: “What outcries pluck me from my naked bed”.

The Householder’s Philosophy (translation, 1588)

The Murder of John Brewen (pamphlet, 1592) a pamphlet concerning the murder of a goldsmith by his wife.

Arden of Faversham (attributed, 1592): the earliest surviving example of domestic tragedy. Play depicts the real-life murder of Thomas Arden by his wife Alice Arden and her lover Mosbie, and their subsequent discovery and punishment. 

Solyman and Perseda (attributed, c. 1593)

Cornelia or Pompey the Great, his Fair Cornelia's Tragedy  (1594): The play is about Cornelia Metella, the widow of Pompey. The play ends with Pompey's death and the reactions from his family. Julius Caesar does not appear in person but has a presence throughout. It is an English language adaptation of Robert Garnier's play Cornélie from 1573

Christopher Marlowe, also known as Kit Marlowe (1564 – 1593)

Born in Canterburry, son of a cobbler. Was a spy, atheist, homosexual. Died in a drunken brawl at Deptford which Anthony Burgess wrote about in his A Dead Man in Deptford. He perfected the dramatic blank verse. “Marlowe’s Mighty Line”, a phrase attributed to him by Ben Jonson in his prefactory poem to Shakespeare’s First Folio. Major theme in his plays: renaissance quest for power and beauty. Features of Marlowian hero: mostly played in his time by Edward Alleyn; anti-heroes with humble beginnings but later rise to great wealth and power; lust for power followed by tragedy yet winning sympathies; hubris; monomania (single minded pursuit of a goal).

Marlowe was the anonymous author of the Cambridge University play The Return from Parnassus (1598) who wrote, "Pity it is that wit so ill should dwell, / Wit lent from heaven, but vices sent from hell".

Henry VI by William Shakespeare is now credited as a collaboration with Marlowe in the New Oxford Shakespeare series, published in 2016.

The most famous tribute to Marlowe was paid by Shakespeare in As You Like It, where he not only quotes a line from Hero and Leander ("Dead Shepherd, now I find thy saw of might, 'Who ever lov'd that lov'd not at first sight?'") but also gives to the clown Touchstone the words "When a man's verses cannot be understood, nor a man's good wit seconded with the forward child, understanding, it strikes a man more dead than a great reckoning in a little room." This appears to be a reference to Marlowe's murder which involved a fight over the "reckoning," the bill, as well as to a line in Marlowe's Jew of Malta, "Infinite riches in a little room."

PLAYS:

Dido, Queen of Carthage (First published 1594; posthumously). (full title: The Tragedie of Dido Queene of Carthage) is a short play. The title page attributes the play to Marlowe and Thomas Nashe. It tells an intense dramatic tale of Dido and her fanatical love for Aeneas (induced by Cupid), Aeneas' betrayal of her and her eventual suicide on his departure for Italy. Books 1, 2, and 4 of Virgil's Aeneid as primary source. PLOT:  Jupiter is fondling Ganymede, who complains that Juno is mistreating him out of jealousy. Venus enters, upset that Jupiter is neglecting her son Aeneas, who is lost in a storm while trying to reach Italy after fleeing Troy. Jupiter reassures her and calms the storm. Venus then goes to Libya, disguises herself, and meets Aeneas, who has arrived there with a few followers. She helps him reunite with Illioneus, Sergestus, and Cloanthes, who have been welcomed by Dido, Queen of Carthage. Dido promises to help Aeneas and asks him to recount the fall of Troy, which he does. Meanwhile, Dido’s suitor, Iarbas, pressures her to marry him, but Venus disguises Cupid as Aeneas's son, Ascanius. Cupid touches Dido with his arrow, causing her to fall in love with Aeneas, rejecting Iarbas, much to his dismay. Dido’s sister Anna, in love with Iarbas, encourages Dido to pursue Aeneas. Dido and Aeneas meet in a cave where they declare their love and make love. Iarbas vows revenge. Venus and Juno argue over Aeneas's fate, but Juno denies wanting to harm him. Aeneas's followers urge him to leave Libya for Italy, and he prepares to depart. Dido sends Anna to inquire about his plans. Aeneas returns, denies intending to leave, and Dido forgives him, but removes his ships' tackle as a precaution. She also entrusts Ascanius (actually Cupid) to the Nurse, believing Aeneas won’t leave without him. Dido declares Aeneas king of Carthage, but Mercury appears with the real Ascanius and orders Aeneas to fulfill his destiny in Italy. Aeneas reluctantly agrees, and Iarbas, seizing the opportunity, helps him depart. Dido, devastated, builds a funeral pyre and throws herself into the flames after cursing Aeneas's descendants. Iarbas, horrified, kills himself, and Anna, seeing Iarbas dead, follows suit.

Tamburlaine the Great (written in 1587 or 1588): a play in two parts. loosely based on the life of the Central Asian emperor Timur. Central role played by Edward alleyn (actor of Admiral’s Men owned by Philip Henslowe). PLOT: In Persepolis, Emperor Mycetes sends troops against Tamburlaine, a Scythian bandit. Meanwhile, Mycetes' brother, Cosroe, plots to overthrow him. Tamburlaine woos Zenocrate, captures her, and convinces Mycetes' soldiers and Cosroe to join him. He then betrays Cosroe, defeats Mycetes, and seizes Persia. Tamburlaine defeats Turkish Emperor Bajazeth, imprisoning him in a cage. After Bajazeth and his wife Zabina commit suicide, Tamburlaine conquers Africa and targets Damascus. Zenocrate pleads for her father’s life, and Tamburlaine spares him. They marry, and she becomes Empress of Persia. In Part 2, Tamburlaine grooms his sons for conquest. His son Calyphas refuses to fight, and Tamburlaine kills him. Callapine, Bajazeth’s son, seeks revenge but is defeated. Tamburlaine takes Babylon, massacres its people, burns the Qur'an, and declares himself greater than God. Though dying, he wins one last battle and urges his sons to continue his conquests before he dies. Zenocrite also dies. Marlowe based his work on an abridged translation of Pedro Mexía's 1543 Silva de varia lección (A Miscellany of Several Lessons).

The Jew of Malta (full title: The Famous Tragedy of the Rich Jew of Malta): written in 1589 or 1590. Prologue spoken by Machiavel, a Senecan ghost inspired by Machiavelli, introducing the tragedy. Barabas called 1st Machiavellian villain. PLOT: Barabas, a wealthy Jew in Malta, is stripped of his wealth by Governor Ferneze to pay off the Turks. Seeking revenge, Barabas, with his slave Ithamore, tricks Lodowick (the Governor’s son) and Mathias into a fatal duel over his daughter Abigail. When Abigail becomes a nun out of horror, Barabas poisons her and the entire nunnery. He then strangles Friar Barnardine and frames Friar Jacomo for the murder. Ithamore falls in love with the prostitute Bellamira, who, with her friend Pilia-Borza, tries to blackmail Barabas. Barabas poisons all three. Captured, Barabas fakes his death and allies with the Turks to besiege Malta. After becoming governor, he betrays the Turks, planning to destroy their forces and boil the Turkish prince Calymath alive. However, Governor Ferneze double-crosses Barabas, who falls into his own trap and dies, cursing them as he burns.

The Tragical History of the Life and Death of Doctor Faustus, commonly referred to simply as Doctor Faustus (written in 1592 or 1593). Based on German stories about the title character Faust. Two different versions of the play. The play is in blank verse and prose in thirteen scenes (1604) or twenty scenes (1616). Blank verse is largely reserved for the main scenes; prose is used in the comic scenes. PLOT:  The Chorus reveals that Faustus, born lowly, quickly earned a theology doctorate but turned to necromancy. Bored with traditional knowledge, Faustus decides only magic is worth learning. He summons magicians Valdes and Cornelius, who encourage his pursuit of magic. Ignoring the warnings of a Good Angel, Faustus conjures Mephistophilis and signs a blood contract to serve Lucifer for 24 years. Faustus and Mephistophilis travel, playing tricks on the Pope and impressing the Holy Roman Emperor. Despite wavering and considering repentance, Faustus renews his pact with Lucifer. Near the end of his 24 years, he conjures Helen of Troy and admits to some scholars that he sold his soul. Despite their prayers, devils claim Faustus in the final scene.

The Troublesome Reign and Lamentable Death of Edward the Second, King of England, with the Tragical Fall of Proud Mortimer, known as Edward II early modern period play by Marlowe. focuses on the homoerotic relationship between King Edward II of England and Piers Gaveston, and Edward's murder on the orders of Roger Mortimer. Marlowe found most of his material in the third volume of Raphael Holinshed's Chronicles (1587). PLOT: The play begins with Edward II recalling his exiled favorite, Piers Gaveston, after the death of Edward I. Gaveston returns, delighting the king but angering the nobles, who demand his exile. Although Edward banishes Gaveston, he soon recalls him, leading to renewed conflict. Eventually, the nobles capture and execute Gaveston despite Edward's pleas. Edward then favors the Spencers, alienating Queen Isabella, who allies with Mortimer and seeks support in France. Betrayed and outmaneuvered, Edward is captured and imprisoned. Mortimer, now in power, has the Earl of Kent executed for attempting to rescue Edward. Edward II is brutally murdered by Lightborn at Berkeley Castle. Edward III, discovering Mortimer and Isabella’s plot, arrests Mortimer and orders his execution, imprisoning his mother. The play concludes with Edward III taking the throne.

The Massacre at Paris (1593): concerns the Saint Bartholomew's Day Massacre, which took place in Paris in 1572. There is a restoration drama by Nathaniel Lee (1689) by the same name. PLOT: The play opens with the wedding of Henry of Navarre to Margaret of Valois, but tensions rise as Queen Catherine de' Medici and the Duke of Guise plot against the Huguenots. The Queen of Navarre is poisoned, and her admiral is gravely wounded, sparking the St. Bartholomew's Day Massacre. Guise leads the massacre, targeting Huguenots loyal to Navarre. Navarre escapes, raises an army, and defeats the French forces led by Joyeux. King Henry III, weary of Guise, has him assassinated at Blois. The Duke’s brothers are also killed, but Dumaine seeks revenge. A friar fatally stabs Henry III, who names Navarre as his successor before dying. The play ends with Navarre, now King Henry IV, vowing vengeance against the Catholic League.

POETRY:

The Passionate Shepherd to His Love (c. 1587–1588)  a pastoral poem in iambic tetrameter in six stanzas, and each stanza is composed of two rhyming couplets; thus the first line of the poem reads: “Come live with me and be my love”. Walter Raleigh replied to Marlowe’s shepherd with the poem "The Nymph's Reply to the Shepherd" (1600)

Hero and Leander (c. 1593, unfinished; completed by George Chapman, 1598; printed 1598) retells the Greek myth of Hero and Leander. After Marlowe's untimely death, it was completed by George Chapman. The minor poet Henry Petowe published an alternative completion to the poem. The poem was first published five years after Marlowe's demise. In Bartholomew Fair, Ben Jonson lampoons the poem in the fair's puppet show. PLOT: Poem tells the Greek legend of Hero and Leander, lovers separated by the Hellespont. Hero, a chastely devoted priestess of Venus in Sestos, is seen by Leander from Abydos, who falls in love with her. Hero, despite her vow of chastity, reciprocates his feelings. Leander convinces her to light a lamp in her tower each night, guiding him across the sea. On his first swim, Neptune mistakes him for Ganymede and nearly drowns him, but returns him with a protective bracelet. Leander reaches Hero’s tower, where she eventually succumbs to his advances. The poem ends at dawn, leaving the conclusion unresolved.
