Benjamin Jonson (1573 – 1637)

Bricklayer; soldier; actor & playwright. Renaissance Humanist turned dramatist and poet. Jonson has been called "the first poet laureate" and the "father" of cavalier poets. Developed “comedy of humour”.He effectively invented the anti-masque in The Masque of Queens.

“I admire him, but I love Shakespeare”: Dryden in his Essay on Dramatic Poesy (1688).

Imprisoned in Marshalsea Prison and charged with "Leude and mutynous behaviour", while Nashe managed to escape to Great Yarmouth for writing the satirical play The Isle of Dogs

Killed a fellow actor in a duel (escaped execution). While in jail Jonson converted to Catholicism.

Quarrelled bitterly with Inigo Jones, his stage designer. Quarelled with Marston and Dekker in “War of the Theatres”. His Cynthia's Revels satirised both John Marston, who Jonson believed had accused him of lustfulness in Histriomastix, and Thomas Dekker. Jonson attacked the two poets again in Poetaster (1601). Dekker responded with Satiromastix, subtitled "the untrussing of the humorous poet". This "War of the Theatres" appears to have ended with reconciliation on all sides. Jonson collaborated with Dekker on a pageant welcoming James I to England in 1603. Marston dedicated The Malcontent to Jonson and the two collaborated with Chapman on Eastward Ho!, a 1605 play whose anti-Scottish sentiment briefly landed both Jonson and Chapman in jail.

Regarded as the second most important English dramatist, after William Shakespeare, during the reign of James I. G. E. Bentley notes in Shakespeare and Jonson: Their Reputations in the Seventeenth Century Compared, Jonson's reputation was in some respects equal to Shakespeare's in the 17th century. Jonson was a principal purveyor of masques to James I and his queen.

As an actor, he was the protagonist "Hieronimo" (Geronimo) in the play The Spanish Tragedy by Thomas Kyd 

Patronage of aristocrats such as Elizabeth Sidney (daughter of Sir Philip Sidney) and Lady Mary Wroth. This connection with the Sidney family provided the impetus for one of Jonson's most famous lyrics, the country house poem To Penshurst.

Was something of a literary dictator at the Mermaid Tavern. In the 1620s the Sons of Ben or the "Tribe of Ben", younger poets such as Robert Herrick, Richard Lovelace, and Sir John Suckling who took their bearing in verse from Jonson, rose to prominence.

Jonson's most influential commentary on Shakespeare is in his poem "To the Memory of My Beloved the Author, Mr. William Shakespeare and What He Hath Left Us," included in the preface of Shakespeare's First Folio which upholds the traditional view of Shakespeare as a natural genius, despite his "small Latine, and lesse Greeke”: Yet must I not give Nature all: Thy Art,/ My gentle Shakespeare, must enjoy a part.

Was buried in an upright position in Westminster Abbey with the epitaph “O Rare Ben Jonson”.

Early comedies (all full of vivacity and fun): Every Man in His Humour; Every Man out of His Humour; Cynthia's Revels; The Poetaster. Middle comedies (best work, satirical in tone, realistic in dialogue): Volpone; Epicoene, or the Silent Woman; The Alchemist; Bartholomew Fair. Later comedies (less powerfull): The Devil is an Ass; The Staple of News; The New Inn, or The Light Heart; The Magnetic Lady, or Humours Reconciled

PLAYS: (wrote plays based on Roman history)

A Tale of a Tub (c. 1596 revised performed 1633; published1640): The last of his plays to be staged during his lifetime.  Caroline era stage play comedy. The play was published with a motto from Catullus: Inficeto est inficetior rure. PLOT: The plot, which unfolds on St. Valentine's Day, concerns the inept attempts of a variety of suitors to win the hand of Audrey Turfe, the daughter of a Middlesex constable. To break Audrey's engagement to John Clay the tilemaker, Squire Tub, a romantic rival, has the man falsely accused of theft. As Constable Turfe pursues the innocent man, yet another suitor, Justice Preamble, plays a comparable ruse against Squire Tub. All told, Audrey is chased after by four separate suitors, and apparently she has no particular preference among them. (She hesitates to accept Squire Tub, however, because of the social gap between them: "He's too fine for me, and has a Lady / Tub to his mother.") Amid the disorder, Pol-Marten, Lady Tub's usher, marries Audrey before others realise it. Their marriage is celebrated with a wedding masque, also titled "A Tale of a Tub," which retells the story of the play.

The Isle of Dogs (performed 1597, with Thomas Nashe; lost): A satirical comedy.

The Case is Altered (c. 1597–98; published 1609): comedy written possibly with Henry Porter and Anthony Munday.

Every Man in His Humour (performed 1598; published 1601): The play was revived around 1670; Sackville provided a verse epilogue in which Jonson himself appeared as a ghost. Shakespeare acted in it. Modelled on Roman comedy. Prologue attacks contemporary drama and explains his own aim: he will not “serve the ill customs of the age”. The play first set in Italy then in London. Much of the dialogue is in prose with blank verse largely confined to expository passages. Two plots. In the main plot, Wdward Kno'well, a concerned father, spies on his city-gallant son using his servant Brainworm, whose antics continually subvert the plan. These characters are Anglicized versions of ancient Greek New Comedy types: the senex, the son, and the slave. In the subplot, merchant Kitely's intense jealousy leads him to suspect his wife, Dame of infidelity with wastrels brought by his brother-in-law, Wellbred. Surrounding these plots are various English "humorous" characters, including an irascible soldier named Bobadill (one of Jonson’s greatest characters), a country gull, pretentious poets, a surly water-bearer, and an avuncular judge. The play concludes with Justice Clement resolving all grievances, revealing them as rooted in humour, misperception, or deceit. The play adheres to Aristotelian unities.

Every Man out of His Humour (performed 1599; printed 1600): a satirical comedy play. Is a conceptual sequel to his 1598 comedy Every Man in His Humour. It contains an allusion to John Marston's Histriomastix in Act III, scene i. The clown character Clove speaks "fustian" in mimicry of Marston's style. The characters Fastidious Brisk and Carlo Buffone in Every Man Out—like Hedon and Anaides in Cynthia's Revels and Crispinus and Demeter in The Poetaster—are representations of Marston and Thomas Dekker. It includes several references to Shakespeare and his contemporaneous works: a mention of Justice Silence from Henry IV, Part 2—"this is a kinsman to Justice Silence" (V,ii) and two allusions to Julius Caesar. The play is episodic ad features various characters, each dominated by specific “humours” or obsessive quirks of disposition. Macilente, a venomous malcontent, flees to the countryside due to society’s injustices. Sogliardo, an essential clown, seeks guidance from cynical jester Carlo Buffone to become a gentleman. Sir Fastidious Brisk, a braggart courtier, interacts with other characters. Puntarvolo, a fantastic, vainglorious knight, wagers that he and his wife can travel to Constantinople and back without mishap. Jonson himself is introduced as Asper explaining and commeting on the action in conversation with his friends Cordatus and Mitis (who act as a sort of chorus). Jonson explained his theory of humour in the “induction” of this play (4 humours: choler, melancholy, phlegm and blood). And says that thouse who “affect a humour” are apes who ought to be scourged.

Cynthia's Revels or The Fountain of Self-Love (performed 1600; printed 1601): acted by the boys of the Queen’s Chapel. The play begins with three pages arguing over who gets to wear the black cloak for the prologue, leading to a comedic fight and interruptions. In the main story, the goddess Diana, also called Cynthia, organizes a "solemn revels" in Greece. The gods Cupid and Mercury appear and argue, with Mercury awakening Echo, who mourns for Narcissus. Drinking from Narcissus's spring causes the courtiers and ladies at Cynthia's revels to become vain and self-absorbed. Asotus, a foolish spendthrift, also drinks from the spring and, emboldened by vanity, challenges others to a competition of "court compliment." He wins the competition, which has four phases, against the courtiers. Two symbolic masques are performed, revealing that vices have been masquerading as virtues. Cynthia, representing Queen Elizabeth, orders the revelers to purify themselves by bathing in the spring at Mount Helicon. The character Actaeon may represent Robert Devereux, while Cynthia's lady-in-waiting, Arete, may represent Lucy, Countess of Bedford, Jonson's patroness. In Satiromastix, another play of the Poetomachia (War of the Theatres), Thomas Dekker accuses Jonson of having portrayed himself as Criticus, who is described as "a creature of almost perfect and divine temper" (Cynthia's Revels, II, iii). Epilogue: “By God, ‘tis good, and if you lik’ t, you may.”

The Poetaster (performed 1601; published 1602): comedy set in Augustan Rome. The term poetaster, meaning an inferior poet with pretensions to artistic value, had been coined by Erasmus in 1521. It was used by Jonson in 1600 and then popularised with this play a year later. The principal character in the play is Ovid. The character of Horace in Poetaster represents Jonson himself, while Crispinus, who vomits up a pretentious and bombastic vocabulary, is Marston, and Demetrius Fannius is Dekker. Ovid’s famous encounter with a bore recorded in Book 1 of his Sermones is turned into an encounter with Crispinus (Marton) in an amusing street scene.

Eastward Ho (performed and published 1605): a city comedy in collaboration with John Marston and George Chapman. Its anti-Scottish satire resulted in a notorious scandal in which King James was offended and the play's authors were imprisoned. The play's title alludes to Westward Ho! by Thomas Dekker and John Webster who also wrote Northward Ho! in response that year. The play follows William Touchstone, a London goldsmith, who reprimands his lazy apprentice, Francis "Frank" Quicksilver, while praising his industrious apprentice, Golding. Touchstone hopes Golding will marry his modest daughter, Mildred. Meanwhile, Touchstone's vain daughter, Gertrude, marries the fraudulent and bankrupt Sir Petronel Flash, hoping to elevate her social status. After Gertrude's costly wedding, Touchstone dismisses Quicksilver for his gluttony. Quicksilver and Petronel, seeking quick wealth, plan a voyage to Virginia, funded by Gertrude's dowry. They deceive various characters, including Gertrude and Security, a usurer whose wife, Winifred, is seduced by Petronel. During their voyage, a storm shipwrecks them. Quicksilver and Petronel, stranded and disoriented, plan to make counterfeit money. Back in London, Golding, now a Master Deputy Alderman, learns of their crimes. Petronel and Quicksilver are arrested and confess their wrongdoings. Quicksilver's repentance moves Touchstone, who forgives them and reinstates Quicksilver as his apprentice and Petronel as his son-in-law. The play concludes with Gertrude reconciling with Petronel, and everyone ends happily.

Volpone (c. 1605–06; printed 1607): (Italian for "sly fox") is a comedy play. Set in Renaissance Italy. Volpone’s opening words: “Good morning to the day: and next, my gold!/Open the shrine, that I may see my saint”. PLOT: Volpone (The Fox), a Venetian gentleman, pretends to be on his deathbed to trick the captatores (legacy hunters) Voltore (The Vulture), Corbaccio (The Raven), and Corvino (The Crow) into believing they will inherit his fortune. Each brings lavish gifts, hoping to be named his heir, with Volpone's servant, Mosca, encouraging their delusions. Mosca even persuades Corbaccio to disinherit his son, Bonario. Volpone, disguised as a mountebank, becomes infatuated with Corvino's wife, Celia. Corvino, thinking Volpone can be cured by lying next to a young woman, offers Celia, hoping to secure his inheritance. When Volpone tries to assault Celia, Bonario intervenes. However, in court, the truth is buried by Voltore's legal skills and false testimonies from Mosca and the others. Meanwhile, the English travelers Sir and Lady Politic Would-Be and Peregrine have their own comedic subplot, where Peregrine humiliates Sir Politic. Volpone, disguised, fakes his death and leaves his wealth to Mosca, angering the other would-be heirs. When Mosca refuses to give up his newfound wealth, Volpone reveals his deception, leading to the punishment of Voltore, Corbaccio, Corvino, Mosca, and Volpone himself.

Epicoene, or the Silent Woman (performed 1609; published 1616): 1st play in English to be critically analyzed. Dryden in An Essay of Dramatic Poesy offers a model analysis of this play. Samuel Pepys  places it among the first plays legally performed after Charles II's restoration. Excluding its two prologues, the play is written entirely in prose. The play is set in London, primarily in the home of Morose, a wealthy old man who despises noise. To disinherit his nephew, Dauphine, Morose plans to marry. Dauphine, with his barber Cutbeard, tricks Morose into marrying Epicœne, a supposedly silent woman. However, after the marriage, Epicœne reveals herself to be loud and nagging, much to Morose's dismay. Meanwhile, Truewit, a friend of Dauphine, tries to dissuade Morose from marriage, predicting his unhappiness. Despite this, the marriage proceeds, and Morose's house is overrun by noisy characters, including Dauphine, Truewit, Clerimont, Otter and his domineering wife, and the Ladies Collegiates. Desperate for a divorce, Morose consults lawyers (actually Dauphine's friends in disguise), but they find no grounds. Finally, Dauphine reveals that Epicœne is actually a boy, nullifying the marriage. Morose, humiliated, agrees to give Dauphine his inheritance, while the other characters are left discomfited by the revelation.

The Alchemist (performed 1610; printed 1612): S.T. Coleridge believed that it had one of the three most perfect plots in literature. PLOT: During a plague outbreak in London, Lovewit leaves his house in the care of his butler, Jeremy, who transforms into "Captain Face" and teams up with Subtle, a conman, and Dol Common, a prostitute, to swindle various victims. They trick Dapper, a lawyer's clerk, Drugger, a tobacconist, and Sir Epicure Mammon, a nobleman seeking the philosopher's stone. As the scams unfold, tensions arise between the conspirators, especially over a wealthy widow, Dame Pliant, whom both Subtle and Face covet. The schemes fall apart when Lovewit returns unexpectedly, forcing Jeremy to confess. To save himself, Jeremy helps Lovewit marry Dame Pliant and secure the stolen goods. Subtle and Dol flee empty-handed, while the victims are dismissed. In the end, Lovewit praises Jeremy's cunning, and Jeremy asks for the audience's forgiveness.

Bartholomew Fair (performed 1614; published1631): The play opens with a meta-theatrical prologue, where the stage-keeper criticizes the play for lacking romance and fantasy, and the book-keeper outlines Jonson's terms for the audience, including the rejection of political satire and expectations of old-fashioned drama. The story begins with Littlewit and his friends, Quarlous and Winwife, scheming to woo Dame Purecraft away from the hypocritical Puritan Zeal-of-the-Land Busy. Meanwhile, Cokes, a country simpleton, and his entourage, including Grace Wellborn and Justice Overdo, are at Littlewit's house to obtain a marriage license and plan a visit to the fair. At the fair, the characters face chaos: Overdo is mistaken for a criminal and put in the stocks, Quarlous and Winwife seek to win Grace, and various schemes unfold involving stolen marriage licenses, disguises, and mistaken identities. Busy is arrested for unauthorized preaching and put in the stocks, while Cokes is repeatedly robbed. The climax occurs at a puppet show, where the disguised characters and masked prostitutes add to the confusion. Busy, shocked by the play's cross-dressing puppets, converts to being a "beholder" of plays. Overdo reveals himself, and after a chaotic series of events, he forgives all wrongdoers. Winwife marries Grace, Quarlous marries Purecraft, and the characters are invited to Overdo’s house for a feast. Zeal-of-the-land Busy is Jonson's second foray, after The Alchemist's Tribulation and Ananais, into a field of satire common in Jacobean drama: satire against Puritans.

The Devil is an Ass (performed 1616; published 1631): based on the events of the famous Leicester Boy Witch Trial. The play begins in Hell, where the devil Satan doubts the capability of Pug, a lesser devil, to tempt sophisticated Londoners. Pug convinces Satan to let him try, and is sent to torment the foolish gentleman Fabian Fitzdottrel. Fitzdottrel, obsessed with encountering a devil and finding treasure, is easily duped by various con artists. These include Wittipol and Manly, who seek to seduce Fitzdottrel's wife, and Meercraft, who schemes to steal Fitzdottrel's wealth through fraudulent projects. Meercraft manipulates Fitzdottrel into giving away his fortune, pretending to be a "Spanish woman" to gain access to Mistress Fitzdottrel. Pug tries to tempt Mistress Fitzdottrel into infidelity, but his efforts are clumsy and lead to him being beaten. Wittipol, moved by Mistress Fitzdottrel's plea for help, abandons his lustful pursuit and works with Manly to shift control of Fitzdottrel's fortune to Mistress Fitzdottrel, thwarting Meercraft's plan. As Meercraft attempts to regain control through deceit, Pug ends up imprisoned. In a final twist, Fitzdottrel realizes Pug was a devil, exposes the schemes, and restores order. Meercraft and the other con artists are defeated, while the virtuous characters find resolution.

The Staple of News (completed 1626; printed 1631): early Caroline era play, a satire. Borrows from no less than five plays by Aristophanes. Jonson also re-used some material from his unproduced 1624 masque Neptune's Triumph for the Return of Albion in the play. The play opens with the Prologue and four gossips—Mirth, Tattle, Expectation, and Censure—who critique the play for lacking a devil or fool. The main story centers on the Pennyboy family: Pennyboy Senior, a miser, and Pennyboy Junior, a spendthrift, both vying for the wealthy Lady Pecunia. Their brother, Pennyboy Cantor, disguised as a street singer, observes their behavior after faking his own death. Pennyboy Junior visits the News Staple, managed by Cymbal, who is also a suitor of Pecunia. The rival Pennyboy Senior tries to win Pecunia by bribing her servants but is mocked by the jeerers, led by Cymbal. The rivalry escalates, with Junior spending extravagantly and Pecunia being tracked by the jeerers. Pennyboy Cantor reveals his true identity, denounces his son, and withdraws Pecunia. Junior's defeat of the villain Picklock and his repentance help restore his relationship with his father. Pennyboy Senior, initially driven mad by the jeerers, regains his sanity and peace is restored. Pecunia agrees to marry Pennyboy Junior.

The New Inn, or The Light Heart (licensed 1629; printed 1631): PLOT: At the "Light Heart" inn, hosted by Goodstock, Lady Frances Frampul invites guests, including the melancholic Lord Lovel. Lovel's vivid description of love causes Lady Frampul to fall for him. Meanwhile, Prudence, Lady Frampul's chambermaid, organizes a mock court of love and dresses up Franck, Goodstock's adopted son, as Laetitia. Lord Beaufort secretly marries Laetitia, only to be exposed for marrying a boy. Eventually, it's revealed that Franck is actually Lady Frampul's sister and their father, disguised as Goodstock, and their mother, disguised as Laetitia's nurse, are all part of the inn's story.

The Magnetic Lady, or Humours Reconciled (licensed 1632; printed 1641): a Caroline-era stage play, the final comedy of Jonson PLOT: wealthy Lady Loadstone and her niece Placentia Steel are central to a comedy filled with gulls and fools, including Parson Palate, Doctor Rut, Bias, Practice the lawyer, and Sir Diaphanous Silkworm, all vying for Placentia's attention. Lady Loadstone's brother, Sir Moth Interest, controls Placentia's finances. The household is dominated by out-of-control female servants, such as Mistress Polish, Mother Chair, and Mistress Keep, though Lady Loadstone relies on male advisor Master Compass. A dinner party leads to the revelation that Placentia is actually the daughter of Mistress Polish, and the true Placentia is her maid. In the end, Captain Ironside and Master Compass are revealed as the appropriate matches for Lady Loadstone and Placentia.

The Sad Shepherd or, A Tale of Robin Hood: the last, incomplete pastoral play by  Jonson, written around 1635 and printed posthumously in 1641. The play contains an adaptation from a fragment of Sappho: "The dear good angel of the spring, / The nightingale." Act I: Robin Hood hosts a feast in Sherwood Forest, entrusting his beloved Marian and his men with preparations. They encounter the Sad Shepherd Æglamour, mourning his drowned love, Earine. Marian returns from hunting, showing displeasure and leaving abruptly, causing confusion among the guests. Act II: The witch Maudlin disguises herself as Marian to create chaos, stealing venison and sowing discord. Her daughter Douce tries to woo the shepherdess Earine, while Robin Hood and his men start to suspect Maudlin’s trickery. Maudlin, angry at losing the venison, is eventually exposed by the sage shepherd and hunted by Robin Hood’s men. Act III: Puck reveals himself and narrates his role. Douce, disguised as Earine, causes more confusion. Æglamour, mistaking Douce for a ghost, laments further. Lorel attempts to assault Earine but is thwarted. Maudlin tries to retrieve her lost girdle but is thwarted by Puck. The huntsmen eventually track and confront Maudlin, but she escapes using Puck's tricks.

Mortimer His Fall (published 1641): an unfinished history play about the overthrow of Roger Mortimer, 1st Earl of March, who had become de facto ruler of England in 1327 with Isabella of France after deposing and murdering Isabella's husband Edward II of England.

TWO TRAGEDY PLAYS:

Sejanus His Fall (performed 1603; printed 1605): a tragedy about Lucius Aelius Sejanus, the favourite of the Roman emperor Tiberius. According to Jonson, an unnamed co-author "had good share" in the version of the play. Preface cited the actual editions of the Latin authors he had used. He had not observed “the strict laws of time”.

Catiline His Conspiracy (performed and printed 1611): a Jacobean tragedy complete with ghost and chorus. As its title indicates, the play recounts the story of Catiline, the Roman politician and conspirator of the 1st century B.C. Besides Catiline appear other historical figures such as Julius Caesar, Sempronia, Fulvia, Crassus and Cicero. Wrote Catiline in the historical context of the Gunpowder Plot and surrounding events.

POETRY: "On My First Sonne"; "To Celia" (Drink to me only with thine eyes); "To Penshurst"

George Chapman (c. 1559 – 12 May 1634) 

A classical scholar whose work shows the influence of Stoicism.  William Minto speculated that Chapman is the unnamed Rival Poet of Shakespeare's sonnets.  He is best remembered for his translations of Homer's Iliad and Odyssey, and the Homeric Batrachomyomachia. Made a famous statement: “Of all books extant in all kinds, Homer is the first and best”. Keats wrote the sonnet “On First Looking into Chapman’s Homer” for his friend Charles Cowden Clarke .  His earliest published works were the obscure philosophical poems The Shadow of Night (1594) and Ovid's Banquet of Sense (1595 a response to the erotic poems of the age, such as Philip Sidney's Astrophil and Stella and Shakespeare's Venus and Adonis). Other poems by Chapman include: De Guiana, Carmen Epicum (1596), on the exploits of Sir Walter Raleigh; a continuation of Christopher Marlowe's unfinished Hero and Leander (1598); and Euthymiae Raptus; or the Tears of Peace (1609). In Percy Bysshe Shelley's poem The Revolt of Islam, Shelley quotes a verse of Chapman's as homage within his dedication "to Mary__ __". The Irish playwright Oscar Wilde quoted the same verse in his part fiction, part literary criticism, "The Portrait of Mr. W.H."  Wrote 5 tragedies which show interest in Stoic philosophy: Bussy D'Ambois (1603); Caesar and Pompey (1604); The Conspiracy and Tragedy of Charles, Duke of Byron (1608); The Revenge of Bussy D'Ambois (1610); The Tragedy of Chabot, Admiral of France (1612)

PLAYS:

The Blind Beggar of Alexandria (1596):  a comedy. First of Chapman's plays to be produced on the stage. Derives from the commedia dell'arte tradition of Italy. The plot depends heavily on the comedic effects of disguise.  The pretend-beggar Irus is named after the bragging beggar who foolishly challenges Odysseus to a fight in the final book of The Odyssey. PLOT: Cleanthes, a swindler and pretended duke, has wooed the imperious Queen Aegiale, who rewards his temerity by banishing him. Cleanthes returns to Alexandria in the guise of the blind beggar and fortune-teller Irus. In that disguise and others – Leon the usurer, and the "mad-brain" aristocrat Count Hermes – Cleanthes manipulates people and events to turn in his favor (and for the sheer egotistical fun of it). He seduces Aegiale; he marries a pair of sisters in his different personas – and then tempts both of them to engage in adultery, though only with himself in other guises. He ends up king of Egypt, and disposes of his two sibling wives (now pregnant) as the mates of two captured kings.

An Humorous Day's Mirth (1597): a comedy. Chapman's play was the first Elizabethan humors comedy which gained its greatest prominence in the works of Ben Jonson

Charleymayne, or the Distracted Emperor (1600): an early modern play. It is the only Jacobean play to survive in manuscript form. In the manuscript the play is anonymous, the script shows signs of a collaborative effort, with Act One possibly by George Chapman, and the rest by a young John Fletcher (or the usual suspects, Greene, Kidd, Lodge). The play is set in Charlemagne's court, embroiled in political intrigue and personal conflicts. Act I reveals Charlemagne's marriage to Theodora and introduces her scheming siblings, Ganelon and Gabriella. Ganelon is promoted to Constable, while Orlando’s conquests in Spain are celebrated. In Act II, Orlando learns of Theodora’s death and the birth of her son, leading Charlemagne to mourn deeply until Richard discovers a mysterious ring, which causes Charlemagne to fall for Bishop Turpin and punish Ganelon. Act III shows Charlemagne’s mental decline as he listens to sycophants, further punishes Ganelon, and Gabriella suffers separation from Richard. In subsequent acts, La Busse petitions for his father's restored titles, and Richard is murdered by Ganelon, whose crimes and Gabriella’s false letter are exposed. Charlemagne ultimately orders Ganelon’s execution, rewards Orlando with Spain, and the play concludes with a dramatic resolution of its complex plot.

Sir Giles Goosecap (1601): comedy first published anonymously, and generally attributed to George Chapman.

Bussy D'Ambois (1603): Classified as either a tragedy or "contemporary history". It is the earliest in a series of plays that Chapman wrote about the French political scene in his era, including the sequel The Revenge of Bussy D'Ambois, the two-part The Conspiracy and Tragedy of Charles, Duke of Byron, and The Tragedy of Chabot, Admiral of France. The play is based on the life of the real Louis de Bussy d'Amboise, who was murdered in 1579. The play opens with Bussy, an impoverished aristocrat and skilled swordsman, lamenting society’s corruption. He reluctantly accepts a payment to serve Monsieur, the brother of King Henri III. Bussy’s wild temperament soon causes conflict, leading him to quarrel with courtiers and survive a triple duel. He begins an affair with Tamyra, the wife of the powerful Count Mountsurry, who tortures her to reveal the affair. Tamyra’s chaplain dies from the torture, and Mountsurry disguises himself as the chaplain to deliver a letter summoning Bussy. Bussy, deceived, is ambushed and killed.

Caesar and Pompey (1604): a classical tragedy one of the more problematic works of English Renaissance Drama. Deals with the conflict between Julius Caesar and Pompey the Great that ended the First Triumvirate of ancient Rome in the 1st century BC. In the play, Chapman depicts the antagonism between the two title characters, but also the pupil/master relationship between Pompey and Cato the Younger in Stoic philosophy. Chapman's sources were Thomas North's translation of the Parallel Lives of Plutarch, and the Pharsalia of Lucan, and, among more abstruse works, the Contra Celsum of Origen.

All Fools (1604): a comedy based on Terence's Heauton Timorumenos and Adelphoe, the plot revolves around two old Florentine knights: the manipulative Gostanzo and the honorable Marc Antonio. Each has two children: Gostanzo's son Valerio and daughter Bellomora, and Marc Antonio's sons Fortunio and Rynaldo. Valerio secretly marries the poor Gratiana but is unable to publicly acknowledge it due to his father’s opposition. Fortunio loves Bellomora but cannot court her. Gostanzo discovers Valerio with Gratiana and, manipulated by Rynaldo, mistakenly believes Gratiana is Fortunio's wife. He tells Marc Antonio, who, enraged, drives Fortunio away to war. Gostanzo’s plans eventually result in Fortunio and Gratiana living with him, allowing Valerio and Gratiana to be together and Fortunio to pursue Bellomora. Gostanzo’s further meddling nearly derails these arrangements, but ultimately Valerio and Gratiana’s marriage is publicly accepted, and Fortunio marries Bellomora. The subplot involves Cornelio’s irrational jealousy of his wife Gazetta, leading to legal and medical humor, which ends with Cornelio reconciling with his wife. The play concludes with Gostanzo acknowledging the wisdom of Marc Antonio’s simple honesty.

Monsieur D'Olive (1605): a comedy Chapman structured his main plot to express his interest in the Neoplatonist philosophy of Marsilio Ficino. The play centers on Vandome, a French gentleman who returns from a three-year journey to find his friends and family in disarray. His chivalrous affection for Marcellina, the wife of his friend Count Vaumont, led to Marcellina retreating into nocturnal seclusion, while her husband’s jealousy grew. Vandome also discovers that his sister has died and her grieving husband, the Earl of St. Anne, has embalmed her body. Vandome resolves these issues by orchestrating a romance between the Earl and Eurione, Marcellina’s sister, who secretly loves the Earl. Meanwhile, Vandome tricks Marcellina and Eurione into confronting the Count Vaumont with false accusations of infidelity, thereby ending Marcellina’s seclusion. The subplot features Monsieur D’Olive, a vain and pompous gallant, who is duped by courtiers Mugeron and Rodrigue into believing he is on an important embassy. His subsequent attempts to fulfill this role result in comical embarrassment.

The Widow's Tears (1605): considered the last of Chapman's comedies. Set on the island of Cyprus, The Widow's Tears follows two plots involving the brothers Lysander and Tharsalio. Tharsalio, a wild and cunning youth, seeks to marry the wealthy widow, Countess Eudora, despite her vow never to remarry. Competing with the pompous Rebus, Tharsalio's bold strategy involves warning Eudora about his own reputation as a womanizer, subtly arousing her desires, leading to their marriage by the third act. In the main plot of the final two acts, Tharsalio, irritated by Lysander's criticism, provokes him into doubting his wife Cynthia's vow of fidelity. Lysander fakes his death to test her, returning disguised as a soldier. In a graveyard, he finds Cynthia mourning, weakens her resolve, and convinces her to "marry" him in his disguise. Tharsalio, unaware of the soldier's true identity, spies on them and believes his cynical views on women are confirmed. Lysander's ruse eventually leads to his arrest and a legal mess, which the now-married Eudora helps resolve. In the end, Lysander accepts the situation, recognizing that Cynthia remained a "constant wife" despite being deceived.

The Gentleman Usher (1606): alternative title Vincentio and Margaret (the names of its hero and heroine). The play is set in an Italian duchy ruled by Duke Alphonso, who, along with his son Prince Vincentio, is in love with Margaret, the daughter of Earl Lasso. Alphonso's plan to marry Margaret is supported by his court favorite, Medice, a disliked and illiterate poseur. Vincentio, with the help of his friend Strozza, pursues Margaret and wins her affection, pledging a spiritual marriage. As tensions rise, Strozza is wounded, and Medice manipulates events to expose Vincentio’s romance. Alphonso orders Vincentio’s capture, but Medice defies orders and severely injures him. Margaret, desperate to avoid a forced marriage, disfigures herself but is later healed along with Vincentio. In the end, Medice is exposed as a fraud, beaten, and driven out, while Vincentio and Margaret are united. Strozza’s final speech reflects on the nature of virtue and authority, highlighting Chapman’s philosophical concerns.

The Conspiracy and Tragedy of Charles, Duke of Byron (1608): Follows the formidable soldier Byron, whose overweening pride becomes his downfall. Obsessed with comparing himself to ancient heroes like Hercules and Alexander the Great, Byron's vanity makes him vulnerable to manipulation by enemies of the King of France. Despite becoming aware of Byron’s treason, the King values his past service and tries to reform him, even sending him to England to observe a stable monarchy. Byron temporarily curbs his pride, but his ego leads him back to plotting. Ultimately, he is apprehended, tried, convicted, and executed. Byron’s rebellion is likened to Catiline's revolt against Rome and the Earl of Essex’s uprising against Elizabeth I. The notorious face-slapping scene in The Tragedy may have been inspired by rumors of Elizabeth striking Essex.

May Day (1609): a comedy. The play parodies the passages in Hamlet, including the famous soliloquy. Chapman based the plot of May Day, and its Venetian setting, upon a Commedia erudita by Alessandro Piccolomini called Alessandro (1544).  Exploits the plot device of gender disguise and cross-dressing that was so common in English Renaissance drama. Lorenzo, an older man, pursues Franceschina, composing poor poetry in her honor. Angelo, the servant of Aurelio, who is in love with Aemilia, mockingly agrees to deliver Lorenzo’s poems. Lorenzo wants Aemilia to marry the wealthy but repulsive Gasparo, though Aemilia prefers Aurelio. Lodovico, Lorenzo’s nephew, helps bring the shy Aemilia and Aurelio together. Quintiliano, a boastful soldier, swindles a scholar named Giovenelle. Meanwhile, Lucretia, a mysterious woman staying with Aurelio’s father, becomes a target for seducers. Lodovico and Angelo trick Lorenzo into disguising himself as a chimney sweep, leading to his humiliation. Eventually, it’s revealed that Lucretia is actually a man named Lucretio in disguise, and his betrothed, Theagine, is disguised as the page Lionell. The play concludes with Lorenzo consenting to the marriage of Aurelio and Aemilia, and all loving couples are happily united.

The Revenge of Bussy D'Ambois (1610): a Jacobean revenge tragedy. Primary source for The Revenge was Edward Grimeston's A General Inventory of the History of France (1607). The historical events depicted in The Revenge occurred in 1588, during the reign of Henri III. Follows Clermont D'Ambois, brother of the late Bussy, as he grapples with his Christian Stoic beliefs. Loyal to the Duc de Guise, Clermont becomes a target of suspicion by the King, influenced by the manipulative Baligny. After Guise is assassinated, Clermont commits suicide. Meanwhile, Clermont is pressured by Tamyra, Bussy's former lover, to avenge her husband Montsurry, who was responsible for Bussy's death. Montsurry evades confrontation until Bussy's ghost compels Clermont to ensure Montsurry's death in a final duel. The play's stoic tone results in most events being narrated rather than shown on stage, contributing to its limited stage history

The Tragedy of Chabot, Admiral of France (1612): later revised by James Shirley. Been argued that Chabot is a "topical allegory on the career of Robert Carr, 1st Earl of Somerset." Follows the honorable Admiral Chabot, a man of principle in a corrupt royal court. When he refuses to enforce an unjust law, a jealous minister convinces the King to investigate him, suspecting hidden wrongdoing. Although the investigation reveals nothing, the judges are coerced into delivering an adverse verdict. The King offers Chabot a pardon, assuming no harm would come from it. However, Chabot, innocent and proud, rejects the pardon, as he committed no crime. The emotional blow to his honor proves fatal, and he dies as if physically wounded.

Rollo Duke of Normandy (1612): also known as The Bloody Brother written in collaboration by John Fletcher, Philip Massinger, Ben Jonson and George Chapman. The title character is the historical Viking duke of Normandy, Rollo. Rollo, Duke of Normandy, is locked in a struggle for power with his brother Otto. Urged on by the sycophantic LaTorch, Rollo eventually murders Otto. After he commits a number of other bloodthirsty deeds, Rollo is lured into a private meeting with the beautiful Edith, daughter of one of his victims, who plots to murder him. As Edith hesitates, Hamond, captain of the guard and brother of another of Rollo's victims, breaks in and murders Rollo. Hamond is himself killed in the process. Aubrey, Rollo's cousin, succeeds to the dukedom; he has Edith cloistered and LaTorch whipped and hanged.

The Memorable Masque of the Middle Temple and Lincoln's Inn (1613): a Jacobean era masque performed  as one item in the elaborate festivities surrounding the marriage of Princess Elizabeth, daughter of King James I, to Frederick V, Elector Palatine in the Rhineland.

Chapman also translated the Homeric Hymns, the Georgics of Virgil, The Works of Hesiod (1618, dedicated to Francis Bacon), the Hero and Leander of Musaeus (1618) and the Fifth Satire of Juvenal (1624). In 1616 the complete Iliad and Odyssey appeared in The Whole Works of Homer, the first complete English translation

John Marston (1576 – 1634)

Writer of violent and coarse verse satires later wrote melodramatic tragedies and cynical comedies. In 1598, he published The Metamorphosis of Pigmalion's Image and Certaine Satyres, a book of poetry in imitation of, on the one hand, Ovid, and, on the other, the Satires of Juvenal.  He also published another book of satires, The Scourge of Villanie, in 1598. (Marston issued these satires under the pseudonym "W. Kinsayder.") The Archbishop of Canterbury John Whitgift and the Bishop of London Richard Bancroft banned the Scourge and had it publicly burned.

PLAYS:

Histriomastix or The Player Whipped (acted in 1599): The play was the opening installment in the War of the Theatres of 1599–1601. Ben Jonson is lampooned as the character Chrisoganus. The play is a moral allegory about human nature

Antonio and Mellida (1599–1600): followed by a sequel, Antonio's Revenge. The play is a romantic comedy, which charts the "comic crosses of true love" faced by Antonio, son of the good Duke Andrugio, and Mellida, daughter of the wicked Duke Piero. The play also employs a metatheatrical induction, in which the boy actors are seen, apparently in propria persona, discussing the roles they are about to play and the way in which their parts should be performed. The play begins with Antonio, son of Andrugio, disguised as an Amazon to reunite with his lover, Mellida, daughter of Piero, as their fathers are rivals. Mellida and her companions notice the disguised Antonio, but she doesn't recognize him until later. They plan to escape and marry, but Piero discovers Antonio's deception and confines Mellida, while Antonio flees. Andrugio, disguised as a nobleman, reunites with Antonio, and they decide to seek Mellida and make peace. Antonio and Mellida briefly reunite but separate again, planning to marry. Andrugio informs Piero of Antonio's staged death to bring peace. Moved, Piero reconciles with Andrugio. Antonio reveals himself, and he and Mellida marry. A subplot involves Mellida's cousin, who humorously rejects her suitors and remains single.

Jack Drum's Entertainment, or the Comedy of Pasquil and Katherine (1599/1600): first performed by the Children of Paul's. The title is derived from an Elizabethan and Jacobean colloquial expression for rough or ill-mannered treatment, especially of an unwanted guest. Been described as a "hodgepodge of undeveloped romantic, comic, and satiric motifs". The play tells the story of the love between Pasquil and Katherine and the trials that they face on the way to happiness. It is based loosely on the story of Argalus and Parthenia in Sidney's Arcadia, which also provided material for the subplot concerning the love of Camelia and Planet. It satirizes human folly and the madness of love, but harshly criticizes those who can't love, like the wicked usurer Mamon, and those who think themselves superior, like the self-satisfied critic Brabant Senior. (was meant as a satirical portrait of Jonson).

Antonio's Revenge (1600): a sequel to Marston's comic play Antonio and Mellida. it chronicles the conflict and violence between Piero Sforza, the Duke of Venice, and Antonio, who is determined to take revenge against Piero for the death of his father and the slander of his fiancée (Piero's daughter Mellida, to whom he is bethrothed at the end of Antonio and Mellida). Piero poisons his rival Andrugio and betroths his daughter Mellida to Andrugio’s son Antonio. Antonio, grieving over his father's death and a foreboding dream, discovers Feliche’s body in Mellida's window. Piero confesses the murder, claiming to have found Feliche and Mellida together. Piero then imprisons Mellida and plots to have Antonio falsely accused of Andrugio's murder, with Strotzo’s help. Antonio, guided by Andrugio’s ghost, murders Piero’s son Julio. Disguised as a jester, Antonio attends Mellida's trial, where Strotzo reveals his and Piero's scheme. Piero strangles Strotzo, and Piero plans to marry his daughter to Galeatzo and himself to Maria. Maria reports Mellida’s death from grief, leading Piero to delay his wedding. The conspirators, including Antonio and Pandulpho, release Balurdo to aid their plot. They attack Piero at his wedding feast, kill him, and reveal Julio’s body. Antonio declines the Dukedom of Venice and vows to live celibate in Mellida’s honor.

What You Will (1601): late Elizabethan comedy. The play focuses on the relationship between two rival poets: the bitter, misanthropic satirist Lampatho Doria (Jonson) and the generous, lighthearted epicurean Quadratus (Marston). The play itself hardly has a plot, being more a series of comic vignettes and debates between characters.

The Malcontent (1604): Dedicated to Jonson. A new induction, written by John Webster is a metatheatrical one. PLOT: Duke Pietro of Genoa is troubled by the critical guest Malevole, who reveals Pietro's wife Aurelia's affair with adopted heir Mendoza. Malevole is actually Altofronto, the deposed Duke of Genoa, seeking revenge. Altofronto, aided by Celso, plots to reclaim his position. He warns Pietro of Mendoza's plan to murder him and marry Aurelia. Malevole provides Mendoza with two poison boxes, but instead disguises Pietro as a hermit to testify to his own death. Mendoza exiles Aurelia and orders Malevole to marry Maria and kill the disguised Pietro. Malevole convinces Aurelia to repent, and Maria remains faithful. Mendoza’s attempt to poison Altofronto fails, and at a masquerade ball, Altofronto, Pietro, and Ferneze expose Mendoza’s schemes. Altofronto is restored as Duke, Mendoza is exiled, and Pietro reconciles with Aurelia.

Parasitaster, or The Fawn (1604): The play tells the story of Duke Hercules, who disguises himself as a flattering courtier, the Fawn, in order to infiltrate the court of Duke Gonzago and observe the behaviour of his son Tiberio, who has been sent to Gonzago as an ambassador. In the process, Hercules observes the lechery of Gonzago's courtiers, who are all embroiled in seedy sexual adventures, and the vanity and stupidity of Gonzago himself, who is a pompous old windbag. At the play's conclusion, Hercules holds a symbolic Court of Cupid, in which all the foolish courtiers are arrested for their crimes against love and good sense.

The Dutch Courtesan (1605): the most famous adaptation of the play is Aphra Behn's The Revenge; or, a Match in Newgate. A satire on Thomas Dekker's contemporary play The Honest Whore. principal source of main plot was a story drawn from the first book of Les bergeries de Julliette by Nicolas de Montreulx, published in 1585. PLOT:  Freevill is involved with Franceschina but plans to marry Beatrice. He introduces Franceschina to his friend Malheureux, who falls for her. Franceschina demands Malheureux kill Freevill and bring her a ring from Beatrice. The two friends fake a quarrel, and Freevill vanishes. Franceschina receives the ring, informs both fathers, and Malheureux is sentenced to death. At the last moment, Freevill reveals he staged everything to cure Malheureux of his passion. Franceschina is whipped and imprisoned.

The Wonder of Women, or The Tragedy of Sophonisba (1606): The play follows Carthaginian princess Sophonisba, who, despite being married to the noble Massinissa, is pursued by the lecherous Syphax. As Carthage faces Roman general Scipio’s attack, King Hasdrubal breaks faith with Massinissa to defend the city. Sophonisba, torn between loyalty to her father and her ideals, ultimately commits suicide to prevent Massinissa from choosing between her freedom and delivering her to Scipio. Her sacrifice upholds Massinissa’s honor, making her both a "Wonder of Women" and a "just shame of men." Marston clarifies in the preface that the play is a poetic rather than historical account.

The Entertainment of the Dowager-Countess of Darby (1607): written by John Marston for Henry Hastings, 5th Earl of Huntingdon and Elizabeth Hastings, Countess of Huntingdon.

The Insatiate Countess (1608?): a tragedy by Marston and William Barksted. The title character Isabella Countess of "Swevia" (Swabia), is based on Bianca Maria, the Countess of Challant who was executed for adultery on 20 October 1526. (Marston also based his Franceschina in The Dutch Courtesan on Bianca Maria.) 

Thomas Heywood (early 1570s – 1641)

Claiming to have had "an entire hand or at least a maine finger in two hundred and twenty plays", although only 23 has survived. Heywood's best-known plays are his domestic tragedies and comedies. His best-known long essay is An Apology for Actors, a moderately-toned and reasonable reply to Puritan attacks on the stage. Wrote on historical and patriotic themes. Glorified London citizenry and prentices (not aristocrats) in his plays. Charles Lamb called him “a sort of prose Shakespeare”.

A Woman Killed with Kindness (c. 1603), a domestic tragedy. The plot of Heywood's play derives from an Italian novel by Illicini, which was translated into English and published in The Palace of Pleasure by William Painter (1566). PLOT: Anne and Frankford celebrate their marriage, while Charles and Francis plan a hunt that ends in a deadly quarrel. Charles repents and is arrested. COMEDIES:

 How a Man May Choose a Good Wife from a Bad (1602); The Wise Woman of Hoxton (performed c. 1604; printed 1634): a city comedy . It was published under the title The Wise-Woman of Hogsdon in 1638. The play is set in Hoxton. It consists of thirteen scenes divided into five acts, the longest of which is the final dénouement. It employs both blank verse and prose. Many characters adopt disguises—particularly Luce 2, who is cross-dressed as a male (the page, "Jack"), who is subsequently cross-dressed again as a female for the pivotal masked wedding in the middle of the play. In the play the contrast between the cunning ignorance of the illiterate Wise Woman and the pedantic Latin-speaker Sir Boniface drives much of the comedy. The Wise Woman, residing in the city's disreputable outskirts, orchestrates the play’s resolution while navigating the disruptive actions of the dissolute Young Chartley and the crossed-dressed heroine, Luce 2. Luce 2, an aristocrat in disguise, seeks to marry Young Chartley but discovers him pursuing the citizen Luce 1, who lacks agency and is bound by her social status. The play unfolds as Luce 2’s pursuit of Chartley and her intervention lead to the resolution of conflicts. Young Chartley, who objectifies women in terms of wealth and possession, regrets his marriage to Luce 1 and desires Gratiana, another woman he sees as more desirable. His shift in affection fuels the play's comic and dramatic action.

The Captives (licensed 1624); A Pleasant Comedy, called A Maidenhead Well Lost (performed and published 1634): a dark comedy set in Italy. The plot centres on Julia, the daughter of the Duke of Milan, who finds herself pregnant before she is officially married to the Prince of Parma. A sub-plot of the play is the story of Lauretta, who finds herself banished from the court of Milan and ends up falling in love with the Prince of Florence. PLOT: Duke of Milan's secretary, Stroza falsely accuses Julia of infidelity, causing her to banish Lauretta and her mother. Julia's engagement to Parma ends when he disbelieves her pregnancy. Lauretta finds refuge with the Prince of Florence, who falls for her. Stroza leaves Julia's baby in the woods, but Parma saves it, suspecting Stroza’s deception. The Duke arranges Julia’s marriage to the Prince of Florence, while Parma sows doubt about Julia’s virginity. Stroza’s scheme has Lauretta impersonate Julia on their wedding night to prove her virginity. Lauretta later reveals the truth, leading to Stroza's exposure and a duel. Ultimately, Parma marries Julia, and the Prince of Florence marries Lauretta.

The Late Lancashire Witches (1634), a Caroline-era stage play written in collaboration with Richard Brome. The play is a topical melodrama on the subject of the witchcraft controversy that arose in Lancashire in 1633. The play is set in Lancashire, around Pendle Hill, featuring various locations including Master Generous' house and the Seely family home. The Prologue references convicted witches awaiting execution in Fleet Prison. In the first act, three gentlemen—Arthur, Shakestone, and Bantam—experience mysterious disruptions and encounter Whetstone, who is socially scorned. The conversation reveals the Seely family’s reversed roles, with Lawrence and Parnell planning to become masters, leaving the Seelys as lodgers. The witches, introduced in Act 2, sing a song about feeding their familiars with their blood and plan further mischief. Meanwhile, Generous discusses Whetstone's foolishness, and Robin, Generous' servant, encounters Mall Spencer, who showcases her magical abilities. The boy, who originally spread tales of witchcraft, finds himself abducted by witches disguised as greyhounds. As the witches' tricks continue, they disrupt a wedding feast prepared by the Seelys, turning food inedible and causing chaos. Generous discovers his wife’s witchcraft when she is transformed into a horse by Robin, leading to a confession of her pact with the devil. Mistress Generous’ penitence is questioned, and the witches' power begins to wane. The Seelys are taken into care, and Parnell’s scandalous behavior leads to a skimmington ride mocking her and her husband. Whetstone seeks revenge, using his aunt's powers to embarrass the gentlemen. In the final act, Doughty, now a witch-finder, and the boy recount their stories. The mill is attacked by witches, and Generous finds undeniable proof of Mistress Generous’ witchcraft. The witches are arrested, and the Seelys return to normal. The play concludes with an Epilogue reflecting on mercy and justice for the accused.

ROMANCES:

 The Four Prentices of London (performed c. 1592; published 1615), a romantic drama. It provided the primary target of the satire in Francis Beaumont's The Knight of the Burning Pestle (1607). Performed, under the title Jerusalem, by the Lord Strange's Men in 1592. The text of the play in the 1615 volume is preceded by an Epistle to the Prentices, signed by Heywood, and a Prologue that provides yet a fourth title, True and Strange, or The Four Prentices of London. PLOT: The old Earl of Boloigne has four sons—Godfrey, Guy, Charles, and Eustace—and a daughter, Bella Franca. Due to his loss of the earldom, they are apprentices: Godfrey a mercer, Guy a goldsmith, Charles a haberdasher, and Eustace a grocer. He departs for a pilgrimage to Jerusalem, instructing his sons to remain good apprentices. Enrolled in a crusade led by Robert, Duke of Normandy, the brothers are shipwrecked and separated. Godfrey ends up in Spain, helps reclaim his father's title, and becomes Earl of Boloigne. Guy lands in France, where the King’s daughter falls in love with him and follows him disguised as a page. Charles reaches Italy, defeats a band of thieves, and becomes their leader. Eustace washes ashore in Ireland. Bella Franca travels to Jerusalem, where the brothers and Tancred, the County Palatine, fall in love with her, leading to conflicts. In Jerusalem, they defeat Soldan the Babylonian and Sophy the Persian. Reunited, they do not recognize each other until their father arrives. King Robert’s death leads to Robert’s succession. Guy becomes King of Jerusalem, Charles King of Cyprus, and Eustace King of Sicily. Guy marries the King of France’s daughter, and Tancred weds Bella Franca.

The Royal King and Loyal Subject (performed c. 1615–18; printed 1637); The Fair Maid of the West Parts One and Two (both printed 1631), a romantic drama of English Renaissance a two-part play. Part 1 dedicated to John Othow and Part 2 to Thomas Hammon (both lawyers of Gray’s Inn). PLOT: Bess Bridges, a tavern worker known for her beauty and virtue, is loved by Master Spencer. After Spencer kills a man, he flees, leaving Bess to manage his tavern. Bess disguises herself as a man to confront a bully and later becomes a privateer after hearing of Spencer’s supposed death. Spencer survives and reunites with Bess in Morocco, where they marry. In Part 2, Bess and Spencer navigate court intrigue in Morocco, face pirates, and reunite in Italy, where they outwit a Duke and end happily together.

A Challenge for Beauty ( ?); The English Traveler (performed c. 1627; printed 1633): tragicomedy in five acts. Consisting of one tragic and one comic plot, it trafficks in the elements of both city comedy and domestic tragedy. Contains a dedicatory epistle to Sir Henry Appleton. Also features a biographically-significant epistle “To the Reader,” which indicates both Heywood's boast about his contributions to English drama ("an entire hand or at least a maine finger in two hundred and twenty plays"). PLOT: Young Geraldine, recently returned from his travels, is loved by his friend Dalavill, his father Old Geraldine, Wincott, and Wincott's Wife, who was his childhood friend. Despite their mutual feelings, Young Geraldine and Wincott's Wife are kept apart by her marriage. Dalavill reveals Geraldine's intentions to his father, leading to a series of misunderstandings. Geraldine later overhears Wincott's Wife with Dalavill, leading to despair. In the end, Wincott's Wife dies, Dalavill flees, and Geraldine resolves to stay in England. Meanwhile, the comedic plot follows Young Lionell, who wastes his father's money on a wild lifestyle. His servant Reignald helps him deceive Old Lionell, who eventually forgives his son, leading to their reconciliation.

Fortune by Land and Sea (printed 1655), a romantic melodrama written in collaboration with William Rowley.  Juxtaposition of the themes of primogeniture and piracy. The play follows two intertwined rural families: the Forrests and the Hardings. Old Forrest, a once-prosperous, moral man, has two sons and a daughter, while Old Harding, a ruthless man, has three sons. The Forrests' fortunes decline, while the Hardings prosper. Frank Forrest, Old Forrest’s elder son, defies his father and associates with Rainsford, who later kills him in a fight. Meanwhile, Old Harding marries a young wife, upsetting his younger sons William and John. Philip Harding, Old Harding's eldest son, marries Susan Forrest, but is disowned and forced to become a servant in his father’s house. Despite hardships, Mrs. Harding shows kindness to Philip and Susan. Young Forrest, the second son, avenges his brother by killing Rainsford and fleeing. Mrs. Harding helps him escape, and he later becomes a successful sea captain, defeating pirates and rescuing the Merchant, who is Mrs. Harding’s brother. Meanwhile, Old Harding dies before disinheriting Philip, leaving Philip to inherit the estate. The news of Young Forrest’s success and the Merchant’s rescue increases the Harding family's wealth. The play concludes with Young Forrest returning home and proposing to Mrs. Harding. A general reconciliation occurs, with Philip generously offering help to his impoverished brothers and others who fell on hard times.

CHRONICLE PLAYS:

 If You Know Not Me, You Know Nobody Parts One and Two: is a two-part play by depicting the life and reign of Elizabeth I of England, written very soon after the latter's death. The title deliberately echoes that of Samuel Rowley's 1605 play When You See Me You Know Me. The play chronicles the key events of Queen Mary I's reign and concludes with Queen Elizabeth's accession. Part 1 was popular, with multiple editions published between 1605 and 1639, likely performed by Queen Anne's Men. Part 2 focuses on the building of the Royal Exchange by Thomas Gresham and the antics of Hobson, a haberdasher. Hobson struggles with his unruly apprentices, particularly John Gresham, who flees to France after stealing 100 pounds. Hobson chases John to France, where John tricks him into believing a courtesan is a reputable businesswoman. Hobson eventually brings John back to England. The play then shifts to Queen Elizabeth's life, where Hobson boldly introduces himself to the Queen during a pageant but is surprised she doesn't remember him. The play ends with Elizabeth surviving an assassination plot by Doctor William Parry and the defeat of the Spanish Armada by Sir Francis Drake.

Edward IV Parts One and Two: a two-part Elizabethan history play centring on the personal life of King Edward IV of England. Published without an author's name attached but is often attributed to Heywood. The play centers on Jane Shore, the mistress of King Edward IV. It depicts Edward wooing Jane, who struggles with the morality of accepting his advances but uses her influence to grant pardons to the wrongfully punished. After Edward's death, Jane expresses regret for their relationship and is cast out by the new king, Richard III. Before dying, she reconciles with her husband, and they are buried together in a ditch, which is said to give Shoreditch its name. The historical events of Edward IV's reign, including figures like the "bastard Faulconbridge" and the "Tanner of Tamworth," provide the backdrop for the story.

MASQUES: Love's Mistress or The Queens Masque (printed 1636), the story of Cupid and Psyche as told by Apuleius;

POETRY: Troia Britannica, or Great Britain's Troy (1609), a poem in seventeen cantos "intermixed with many pleasant poetical tales" and "concluding with an universal chronicle from the creation until the present time"; The Hierarchy of the Blessed Angels (1635), a didactic poem in nine books; Pleasant Dialogue, and Dramas Selected Out of Lucian, etc. (1637); The Conspiracie of Cateline [sic] and Warre of Jugurth [sic], translations of Sallust (1608).

Thomas Dekker (c. 1572 – 1632)

Literary hack, prose pamphleteer and playwright. Wrote chaotic comedies and citizen comedies. Involved in about 40 plays for Philip Henslowe (owner of Admiral’s Men). Written by Dekker alone: Old Fortunatus (1599); The Shoemaker's Holiday (1599). Three Tragedies: Lust's Dominion (written with Day, Marston, and William Haughton, 1600);  The Virgin Martyr (with Massinger, 1620); The Witch of Edmonton (with Ford and Rowley, 1621). Dekker His Dreame (1620) is his long poem describing his despairing confinement

PLAYS:

The Pleasant Comedie of Old Fortunatus (1599): a play in a mixture of prose and verse based on the German legend of Fortunatus and his magic inexhaustible purse. Called "the only example of an interlude inspired by the fully developed genius of the Renaissance".  Charles Lamb: man who wrote it "had poetry enough for any thing." PLOT: Fortunatus, a beggar, meets the goddess Fortune, who offers him a choice between wisdom, strength, health, beauty, long life, and riches. He chooses riches and is given a magical purse. He visits his sons, Andelocia and Ampedo, in Cyprus, where Fortune, Vice, and Virtue plant two trees. Fortunatus then tricks the Soldan of Turkey out of a miraculous hat. After Fortunatus dies, his sons inherit the purse and hat. Andelocia is tricked by Agripyne, loses both items, and is turned into a beast by eating from Vice's tree. He regains his form by eating Virtue's fruit and later retrieves the talismans. Ampedo burns the hat, but both brothers are captured and die in the stocks. Fortune reclaims the purse, and the audience is asked to decide whether Virtue or Vice has won.

The Shoemaker's Holiday or the Gentle Craft (1599):  city comedy. Inspired by Thomas Deloney (here). The story features three subplots: an inter-class romance between a citizen of London and an aristocrat, the ascension of shoemaker Simon Eyre to Lord Mayor of London, and a romance between a gentleman and a shoemaker's wife, whose husband appears to have died in the wars with France. Source: Thomas Deloney’s prose story of Simon Eyre in The Gentle Craft. PLOT: Sir Hugh Lacy and Sir Roger Otley discuss the potential marriage between Rowland Lacy and Rose Otley, despite class differences. Lacy is sent to France but disguises himself as a shoemaker, "Hans," to stay near Rose. Meanwhile, Simon Eyre, a shoemaker, rises to prominence. Lacy and Rose secretly marry, and Eyre, now Lord Mayor, helps them gain the King's pardon. Ralph, another shoemaker, reunites with his wife, Jane, preventing her marriage to Hammon. The King pardons Lacy, remarries him to Rose, and celebrates with the shoemakers.

Lust's Dominion (1600): a revenge tragedy. In the Restoration era, Aphra Behn adapted the play into Abdelazar, or The Moor's Revenge (1676), her sole venture into Restoration tragedy. PLOT: Moor Eleazar, Prince of Fez, is a prisoner in the Spanish Court but honored for his military victories. He harbors a grudge against the Spaniards and plots to seize the crown. The Queen Mother, Eugenia, loves Eleazar, while King Ferdinando loves Eleazar's wife, Maria. Cardinal Mendoza loves the Queen. Eleazar manipulates Eugenia into killing her son Philip, then kills her. He betrays Maria to strike at the Cardinal and eventually kills King Ferdinando. Despite his schemes, Eleazar is ultimately defeated and goes to his death unrepentant.

Satiromastix, or The Untrussing of the Humorous Poet (1601): a response to Jonson's The Poetaster. Horace in Satiromastix represents Ben Jonson.

Blurt, Master Constable (1601–02): subtitle, "The Spaniards' Night Walk". PLOT: Camillo and Hippolito, Venetian noblemen, return from war with Camillo's prisoner, Fontinelle, whom he leaves with Violetta, Hippolito's sister. Camillo loves Violetta, but she and Fontinelle fall in love. Camillo and Hippolito try to lure Fontinelle away with the courtesan Imperia but fail, leading Camillo to imprison Fontinelle. Despite this, Fontinelle and Violetta secretly marry. Eventually, Fontinelle is tempted by Imperia, and Violetta uses a bed trick to consummate their marriage.

The London Prodigal (1603)

Patient Grissel (1603): co-written with Henry Chettle and William Haughton. It is mentioned in Henslowe's diary in the entry for December 1599. The plot is a variant of the medieval tale of Patient Griselda, as told in Chaucer's Canterbury Tales and Boccaccio's Decameron. The play contains Dekker's poem "Golden Slumbers”.

The Honest Whore (1604) (1st part co-written with Thomas Middleton): early Jacobean city comedy, PLOT part 1: The play opens in Milan with a funeral procession for Infelice, the Duke's daughter, who is not truly dead but drugged by the Duke to break her romance with Hippolito. Viola, unhappy with her overly patient husband, Candido, plots with her brother Fustigo to provoke him. Meanwhile, Infelice awakens and is sent to Bergamo, where Hippolito plans to meet her. Bellafront, a former prostitute, falls in love with Hippolito and vows to change her ways. Various characters, including Candido, are involved in schemes and misunderstandings, culminating at the Bethlem Monastery, where Hippolito and Infelice marry. The Duke eventually accepts the marriage, and Candido is freed, proving his sanity. Part 2: In this second part, Hippolito has conceived a violent lust for Bellafront, whose husband has (perhaps not surprisingly) returned to the life of a wastrel. Yet, despite her dire need, Bellafront refuses to surrender her new virtue. All ends well through the parallel machinations of Hippolito's wife and Bellafront's father.

Westward Ho (1604): a satire and city comedy by  Dekker and  Webster. Inspired Ben Jonson, George Chapman and John Marston to respond to it by writing Eastward Ho. Thomas Middleton borrowed a plot element from Westward Ho in his later city comedy The Roaring Girl. PLOT: Mistress Birdlime, a bawd, brings gifts from an Earl to Mistress Justiniano, but Justiniano, disguised, observes his wife and their circle, which includes three citizens and their wives pursued by gallants. Justiniano, disguised as a tutor, helps the wives plan a trip to Brentford, where they exploit the gallants but avoid infidelity. Justiniano reveals his true motives and identity, leading the husbands to discover their suspicions were unfounded. The play ends with the couples returning to London, maintaining conventional morality with no actual adultery or sex.

Northward Ho (1607): a satire and city comedy by Dekker and Webster . Was a response to Eastward Ho. The three plays form a trilogy of "directional plays". PLOT: In a town near London, Featherstone and Greenshield try to seduce Mistress Mayberry but fail, so they prank her husband Mayberry by falsely claiming to have seduced her. Mayberry confronts his wife, who clears her name, leading him to seek revenge. Meanwhile, Bellamont's son Philip gets involved in a con with Doll, a prostitute. Mayberry tricks Featherstone and Greenshield into revealing their secrets, exposing their infidelity. Featherstone is tricked into marrying Doll, who vows to reform. The play contrasts the virtuous Mrs. Mayberry, adulterous Kate Greenshield, and Doll the prostitute.

Sir Thomas Wyatt (1607) (co-written with Webster): a history play. It is not divided into Acts or Scenes but consists of a series of short episodes. PLOT: The play begins with King Edward VI's death. Although Mary should succeed him, Lady Jane Grey is forced by her father-in-law, Northumberland, to become Queen. Mary gains support, and Northumberland, Jane, and her husband, Guildford Dudley, are arrested. Mary becomes Queen and accepts a marriage proposal from Philip II of Spain. Thomas Wyatt protests and leads a rebellion, but it fails, and he is captured. In the end, Jane Grey, Guildford Dudley, and Thomas Wyatt are executed.

The Bloody Banquet (1608–09) (co-written with Middleton)

The Roaring Girl (1607–10) (co-written with Middleton): Title page contains an "Epistle to the Comic Play-Readers" signed by "Thomas Middleton". It is a fictionalized dramatization of the life of Mary Frith, known as "Moll Cutpurse", a woman who had gained a reputation as a virago in the early 17th century. PLOT: Sebastian loves Mary Fitz-Allard, but their fathers oppose the match due to dowry demands. To change his father's mind, Sebastian pretends to woo Moll Cutpurse, a notorious cross-dresser. Sir Alexander, Sebastian's father, disapproves and hires Trapdoor to spy on Moll. Moll, meanwhile, outsmarts Laxton, who tries to seduce her, and assists Sebastian in his ruse. Eventually, Sebastian reveals his true plan, and he marries Mary with his father's blessing. Moll remains unmarried, and Sir Alexander regrets misjudging her. The play explores gender roles through Moll's unconventional character, questioning societal norms about women.

Match Me in London (1611):  Jacobean era tragicomedy dedicated to 'The Noble Lover, (and deservedly beloved) of the Muses, Lodowick Carlell, Esquire, Gentleman of the Bowes, and Groome of the King, and Queene's Privy-Chamber'. PLOT: In an imaginary Spanish court, the lecherous King of Spain seduces Tormiella, a shoemaker's wife, despite her father Andrada's wish for her to marry Gazetto. The King's brother, Prince John, plots to usurp the throne but is executed when discovered. The King falsely accuses the Queen of treason and has her executed, intending to marry Tormiella. However, Tormiella's plot to kill the King is revealed, leading to her rejection. In the end, the Queen and Prince John are found alive, and everyone is forgiven.

The Virgin Martyr (1620) (co-written with Philip Massinger): a tragedy. The play's central event, the martyrdom of St. Dorothea of Caesarea, is mentioned by John Foxe in his Acts and Monuments, or Book of Martyrs. Categorized as a "saint's play" or tragedia sacra. PLOT: Set in Caesarea under Diocletian, the play tells the story of Dorothea, a Christian martyr. Diocletian's daughter, Artemia, is betrothed to Antoninus, who loves Dorothea. Antoninus’s refusal to marry Artemia causes scandal. Dorothea’s Christian faith puts her at odds with Theophilus, who is determined to convert or kill her. Despite brutal attempts, including a failed rape and beatings by her former servants, Dorothea remains steadfast. She is executed, and Antoninus dies of grief. Theophilus later converts to Christianity after a vision and tasting of Heavenly fruits, and dies blissfully after enduring torture.

The Witch of Edmonton (1621) (co-written with William Rowley and John Ford): inspired by the real-life story of Elizabeth Sawyer, who had been executed for witchcraft on 19 April 1621, and draws heavily on a pamphlet by Henry Goodcole, The wonderful discovery of Elizabeth Sawyer, Witch (1621). PLOT: The play centers on Elizabeth Sawyer, an old woman ostracized and accused of witchcraft. Seeking revenge, she sells her soul to the Devil, who appears as a black dog named Tom. With Tom's help, Sawyer causes a neighbor’s madness and death, but her actions achieve little since many others willingly turn to evil. The second major plot follows Frank Thorney, who is forced into a bigamous marriage with Susan while secretly married to Winnifride. Frank murders Susan with possible devilish influence, tries to frame others, and is ultimately executed. Winnifride is later welcomed into the family of Old Carter, Susan’s father. The play ends with a note of optimism as the innocent yokel Cuddy Banks banishes Tom "Out, and avaunt!". Despite Sawyer's execution, evil persists, as Tom plans to corrupt souls in London, and corruption remains evident in the village's authority.

The Noble Spanish Soldier (1622): The Spanish king repudiates Onaelia, the duke of Medina's niece, and marries Paulina, the duke of Florence's daughter. Balthazar, a noble soldier, tries to convince the king to take Onaelia back, but the king refuses, despite the threat of civil war. Count Malatesta proposes that the queen pretend to be pregnant to sway public opinion. The queen then plots to murder Onaelia and her son, but Balthazar warns Medina, who disguises himself as a French doctor to reveal the plots to the king. The king, now convinced of the conspiracy, proposes a marriage between Onaelia and Cockadillio. The queen and Malatesta plan to poison Onaelia, but the king accidentally drinks the poisoned cup. Malatesta is killed by the king's faction, who revolt upon learning of his treachery. The king, dying, passes the crown to Sebastian and orders the queen to return to Florence with a generous dowry. Onaelia and Balthazar are assigned to protect Sebastian.

The Sun's Darling (1624) (co-written with Ford): is a masque, or masque-like play.

Cyril Tourneur (1575-1626)

Allegorical poem called The Transformed Metamorphosis (1600) is Tourneur's earliest extant work. An elegy on the death of Prince Henry, son of James I of England, is the latest (1613). His prose pamphlet, Laugh and Lie Down (1605). Tourneur's primary dramatic work is The Revenger’s Tragedy (published anonymously in 1607; not sure if by Tourneur) and The Atheist's Tragedy, or The Honest Man's Revenge (ca.1608).

The Revenger's Tragedy (1607):  influenced by Seneca and Medieval theatre. It is written in 5 acts and opens with a monologue spoken by Vindice (echoes from Hamlet). Said to be very relevant to, or even about, Shakespeare's Hamlet. PLOT: In an Italian court, Vindice seeks revenge for his fiancée's poisoning by the Duke. He and his brother Hippolito plot against the royal family. The Duke's heir Lussurioso desires Vindice's sister Castiza. Junior, the Duchess's son, rapes Antonio's wife, who commits suicide. The Duchess has an affair with Spurio, the Duke's bastard son. Vindice disguises himself as "Piato" to test his mother and sister's virtue. Lussurioso is arrested for treason but released. Ambitioso and Supervacuo mistakenly order Junior's execution. Vindice poisons the Duke and forces him to watch the Duchess's infidelity before killing him. Lussurioso becomes Duke but is quickly murdered by Vindice and conspirators. A second group of murderers kills each other. Vindice confesses to Antonio, who sentences him to death. Vindice accepts his fate.

The Atheist's Tragedy, or the Honest Man's Revenge ( 1608): is a Jacobean-era tragedy. PLOT: D'Amville, an atheist nobleman, murders his brother Montferrers and plots against his nephew Charlemont. He marries Charlemont's fiancée Castabella to his son Rousard. Charlemont returns, duels Sebastian (D'Amville's younger son), and is imprisoned. Sebastian bails him out. D'Amville attempts to kill Charlemont and rape Castabella. Charlemont kills his would-be assassin. Sebastian dies in a duel with Baron Belforest. Rousard dies. D'Amville goes mad, accidentally kills himself, and confesses. Charlemont and Castabella marry. Subplot: Levidulchia (Castabella's stepmother) has an affair with Sebastian. Both lovers and her husband Belforest die. Levidulchia commits suicide. Comic subplot: Chaplain Languebeau Snuffe fails to seduce Soquette, Castabella's servant. Montferrers' ghost advises Charlemont to avoid revenge. The play emphasizes divine judgment over personal vengeance.

John Webster (c. 1578 – c. 1632)

Known for two macabre revenge tragedies: The White Devil (1612); The Duchess of Malfi (1612–13). Rupert Brooke says in his essay on Webster: “Maggots are what the inhabitants of this universe most suggest and resemble”. T.S. Eliot says in the poem “Whispers of Immortality”, that Webster always saw “the skull beneath the skin”. Bertolt Brecht revised and presented The Duchess of Malfi with W.H. Auden in 1946.

PLAYS:

Westward Ho (1603–4); Northward Ho (1605); Sir Thomas Wyatt (1607)

The White Devil (1612): (full original title: The White Divel; or, The Tragedy of Paulo Giordano Ursini, Duke of Brachiano. With The Life and Death of Vittoria Corombona the famous Venetian Curtizan) is a tragedy. Loosely based on the murder of Vittoria Accoramboni in Padua on 22 December 1585. PLOT: Count Lodovico, banished for crimes, plans his return with the help of friends. The Duke of Brachiano desires Vittoria Corombona, despite their marriages. Vittoria's brother, Flamineo, aids the affair to advance himself, upsetting their mother, Cornelia. Brachiano and Flamineo orchestrate the murders of Vittoria's husband, Camillo, and Brachiano's wife, Isabella. Vittoria is falsely convicted for her husband's murder and imprisoned. Lodovico, secretly in love with Isabella, returns to avenge her death. Francisco, Isabella's brother, also seeks revenge, fueling Brachiano's jealousy with a fake love letter. Brachiano and Vittoria flee Rome but are excommunicated. They settle in Padua, where disguised avengers poison Brachiano. Flamineo, sensing his downfall, tests Vittoria and her maid Zanche by faking his death. They survive, only to be killed by Lodovico and Gasparo. The play ends with Giovanni, the new Duke, discovering his uncle's involvement and ordering Lodovico's torture.

The Duchess of Malfi (1612–13): (originally published as The Tragedy of the Dutchesse of Malfy) is a Jacobean revenge tragedy. Loosely based on events that occurred between 1508 and 1513 surrounding Giovanna d'Aragona, Duchess of Amalfi. Webster's principal source was in William Painter's The Palace of Pleasure (1567), which was a translation of François de Belleforest's French adaptation of Matteo Bandello's Novelle (1554). Act III, Scene IV is a mime scene. Act V, Scene iii, features an important theatrical device, echo, which seems to emanate from the grave of the Duchess. The 18th-century play The Fatal Secret by Lewis Theobald is a reworking of this play imposing Aristotle's 'unities' and a happy ending on the plot. PLOT: The Duchess of Malfi, a widow, secretly marries her steward, Antonio, defying her brothers Ferdinand and the Cardinal, who forbid her remarriage to protect their status. The brothers hire Bosola to spy on her, and he discovers her pregnancy. Ferdinand, descending into madness, disowns her. The Duchess attempts to flee, sending Antonio away with their eldest son, but she and her two younger children are captured and killed by Bosola under Ferdinand's orders. (Ferdinand’s famous line: “Cover her face: mine eyes dazzle: she died young.”. This line is used in the novel Queen of the Damned by Anne Rice, and in Sleeping Murder). Bosola, remorseful, seeks revenge. He accidentally kills Antonio, then murders the Cardinal. In the ensuing chaos, Ferdinand and Bosola fatally wound each other. The play ends with Antonio’s surviving son inheriting the Malfi estate, though his legitimacy is questionable.

The Devil's Law Case (1616–20): a tragicomedy. First edition gives the play the subtitle When Women Go to Law, the Devil is Full of Business. Webster dedicated the play to Sir Thomas Finch, Baronet. PLOT: Romelio, a wealthy and arrogant merchant in Naples, opposes the marriage of his sister Jolenta to the nobleman Contarino, preferring a match with Ercole, a Spanish noble. Jolenta loves Contarino, but Romelio's manipulations lead to a duel between Contarino and Ercole, where both are wounded. Believing Contarino dead, Romelio plots to pass off his illegitimate child as Jolenta's, intending to secure both inheritances. Meanwhile, their mother, Leonora, disgusted by Romelio's actions, falsely claims in court that Romelio is illegitimate to strip him of his estates. Contarino, who survives the attempted murder by Romelio, disrupts a planned duel between Romelio and Ercole by revealing his survival. In the end, Romelio is forced to restore Contarino's fortune, marry the nun he impregnated, and Jolenta is left unmarried. Leonora, Jolenta, and the nun are sentenced to build a monastery as penance, while the subplot of Julio's reckless behavior blends with the main story. The play ends with Romelio's downfall and a bitter reflection on society.

Anything for a Quiet Life (1621): a city comedy co-written with Middleton. PLOT: Sir Francis Cressingham marries a much younger woman, Lady Cressingham, soon after his first wife’s death, drawing criticism from his friend Lord Beaufort. Lady Cressingham pressures Sir Francis to sell his lands and stop practicing alchemy, which he reluctantly agrees to. Meanwhile, Young Cressingham (Sir Francis's son) and Young Franklin, struggling financially, con cloth merchant Walter Camlet. Knavesbe, a corrupt lawyer, schemes to advance his career by prostituting his wife to Beaufort, but she outsmarts them both. Camlet’s wife, suspecting infidelity, leaves him, leading Camlet to send Sir Francis’s children back to their father. Lady Cressingham’s demands strain the family, but she later reveals her harshness was a test to teach thriftiness. In the end, Sir Francis regains his lands, reconciles with his wife, and all misunderstandings are cleared up, with Beaufort inviting everyone to dinner.

A Cure for a Cuckold (1624–25) comedy co-written with William Rowley. PLOT: The play begins at the wedding of Annabel, daughter of Woodroff, to Bonvile. Lessingham, in love with Clare, receives a message from her demanding he kill his closest friend to prove his loyalty. When only Bonvile offers to duel, Lessingham reveals him as the target, causing Bonvile to end their friendship. Meanwhile, Annabel follows Bonvile to Calais, where she meets Rochfield, a would-be thief. After Annabel's kindness, Rochfield joins Woodroff's trading voyage, becomes a hero, and gains wealth. Back in England, Clare admits she loved Bonvile, causing a series of misunderstandings. Woodroff resolves the conflicts between Annabel, Bonvile, Clare, Lessingham, and Rochfield. In the subplot, Compass returns home to find his wife Urse with an illegitimate child. Compass forgives her, but the child's biological father, Franckford, disputes paternity. Woodroff settles the matter, and Compass and Urse renew their vows. The play contrasts the rigid honor of the main plot with the forgiveness in the subplot.

Appius and Virginia (1625–27): It is the third and least famous of his tragedies. It was revived during the Restoration era, in an adaptation by Thomas Betterton called The Roman Virgin, or The Unjust Judge. PLOT: The play is set in ancient Rome during the Decemvirate (451-449 BCE). Appius Claudius, initially feigning modesty, covets a position among the Decemviri and manipulates his way into power. He lusts after Virginia, daughter of Virginius, who is away commanding the army. Appius, with the help of his ally Marcus Claudius, fabricates evidence to claim that Virginia is a bond-slave rather than Virginius's daughter. Virginia is seized and Appius stages a false trial, ruling in Marcus's favor. Virginius returns to Rome and, in a desperate attempt to protect his daughter from Appius’s advances, kills her in court. Virginius then rallies his soldiers and returns to Rome. The Senate imprisons Appius and Marcus. Icilius, who is initially imprisoned, joins Virginius in seeking justice. Virginius shows Virginia's body to the public, inciting their anger against Appius. Appius commits suicide, while Marcus is executed. The play ends with Virginius and Icilius confronting the aftermath of their struggle for justice and honor.

Thomas Middleton (1580 – 1627)

A prolific writer of masques and pageants. His friendship with Thomas Dekker brought him into conflict with Ben Jonson and George Chapman in the War of the Theatres. Jonson's play The Staple of News indulges in a slur on Middleton's great success, A Game at Chess. In his plays there is no moral disorder. His most successful collaborations are with William Rowley.

PLAYS:

The Phoenix (1603–1604): a city comedy performed by the Children of Paul's. Middleton's earliest surviving play. PLOT: In the Duke of Ferrara's court, the virtuous yet inexperienced Phoenix is sent to travel, but he decides to stay and observe his own kingdom. His servant Fidelio's mother, Castiza, is mistreated by her new husband, the Captain. The Duke's scheming advisor Proditor and corrupt lawyer Tangle complicate matters with their own plots. Phoenix and Fidelio, disguised as commoners, intervene to help Castiza escape the Captain. At Justice Falso's house, he plots to marry his niece, who is Fidelio's beloved, and is also the father of the Jeweller's wife. Falso's corruption is exposed when Phoenix and Fidelio discover that Falso is abusing his position and attempting incest. In the final act, Phoenix reveals Proditor's conspiracy to kill the Duke, implicates Falso for his crimes, and denounces the corrupt characters. Proditor is banished, Falso loses his position, and the mad lawyer Tangle is cured. The play ends with justice restored, and Phoenix is ready to take over as Duke.

The Honest Whore, Part 1 (1604) (co-written with Thomas Dekker); Michaelmas Term (1604)

A Trick to Catch the Old One (1605): a Jacobean city comedy. Philip Massinger drew on it as one of the sources for his own play, A New Way to Pay Old Debts. PLOT: Theodorus Witgood, in debt to his avaricious uncle Pecunius Lucre, loves Joyce, niece of rival merchant Walkadine Hoard. Witgood convinces his former mistress to pose as a wealthy widow, attracting Lucre's support and creditors' leniency. Rival suitors, including Hoard, vie for the "widow." Witgood advises the mistress to accept Hoard's proposal, leading to a scheme where Lucre agrees to restore Witgood's estates if she resists Hoard. Witgood’s creditors, angered by his deception, arrest him, but he tricks Hoard into paying his debts. Witgood marries Joyce secretly, and at Hoard's wedding to the "widow," it is revealed that she is Witgood's former mistress. The play concludes with Witgood and his new wife embracing a more virtuous life. 

A Mad World, My Masters (1605): city comedy. The title had been used by a pamphleteer, Nicholas Breton, in 1603, and was later the origin for the title of Stanley Kramer's 1963 film, It's a Mad, Mad, Mad, Mad World. Source: a chapbook titled The Merry Conceited Jests of George Peele. PLOT: Richard Follywit plans to exploit his wealthy but miserly grandfather, Sir Bounteous Progress, by disguising himself as 'Lord Owemuch' to gain from his hospitality. Follywit and his cohorts rob Sir Bounteous’s home and disguise the robbery as an attack on themselves. Meanwhile, Penitent Brothel, who loves Mistress Hairbrain, hires 'The Courtesan' to corrupt her. The Courtesan, pretending illness, manipulates Mistress Hairbrain to meet Penitent. Follywit, now posing as a ridiculous prostitute, tricks Sir Bounteous into a sexual rendezvous and steals valuables. Penitent, realizing his affair with Mistress Hairbrain is with a demon, ends the relationship. Follywit marries the Courtesan, and at Sir Bounteous’s feast, he reveals his bride, exposing his final prank. Bounteous laughs at the clever deceit and toasts the marriage, recognizing Follywit's successful ruse.

Timon of Athens (1606) (co-written with Shakespeare); The Revenger's Tragedy (1606)

Your Five Gallants (1607): a Jacobean city comedy. The five "gallants" of the play's title are frauds, poseurs, and con men—a pickpocket, pimp, pawnbroker, cheat, and whoremonger—who compete with the protagonist, Fitzgrave, for the affections of Katherine, a wealthy orphan. (The five conspire to woo Katherine together; the one who wins her will help out the others.) Fitzgrave manipulates them into exposing their own crimes and vices through a masque. Fitzgrave marries Katherine, while the "gallants" marry the five prostitutes who are their shadows in the play.

The Bloody Banquet (1608–1609) (co-written with Dekker): a revenge tragedy. The play draws its plot from Pan His Syrinx (1584, 1597) by William Warner. PLOT: Lapyrus betrays Lydia by marrying Eurymone and attacking his uncle's kingdom. The Cilician Tyrant takes over Lydia, exiling the Old King and allowing his son, Tymethes, to remain. Tymethes loves Amphridote, the Tyrant's daughter, while the Tyrant’s new queen secretly loves Tymethes. Lapyrus, disguised as a beggar, helps the Old Queen of Lydia. The Old Queen is later rescued by shepherds, and Lapyrus vows to care for her. The Tyrant’s queen arranges secret meetings with Tymethes but is betrayed by Mazeres, who attempts to poison Tymethes. Tymethes is ultimately killed, and the Young Queen is forced to eat his remains. The Old King, Lapyrus, and allies, disguised as pilgrims, overthrow the Tyrant, who is killed along with the Young Queen. The Old King is restored to his throne, and he is reunited with the Old Queen.

No Wit, No Help Like a Woman's (1611): a Jacobean tragicomic play. Borrowed the main plot from a 1589 play, a comedy by Giambattista Della Porta titled La Sorella ("The Sister"). PLOT: Master Sandfield nearly kills Philip Twilight, believing he's courting Jane Sunset. Savourwit reveals Philip is already married and the ruse involving ransom money and mistaken identity. Savourwit schemes to marry Grace Twilight to Sandfield and Jane Sunset to Philip. Lady Goldenfleece's suitors, including Sir Gilbert Lambston and Weatherwise, are introduced. Disguised Mistress Low-water tricks Lady Goldenfleece’s suitors and Beveril falls for Lady Goldenfleece. Philip reconciles with his mother, Lady Twilight, after confessing his deceit. The 'Gallant Gentleman' (Mistress Low-water) reveals the marriage contract with Lady Goldenfleece is void due to existing marriage, and Lady Goldenfleece decides to marry Beveril. Mistakes are resolved, and Philip and Sandfield end up with their true loves.

The Second Maiden's Tragedy (1611): A Chaste Maid in Cheapside (1613): a city comedy follows Moll Yellowhammer, who loves Touchwood Junior but is betrothed to Sir Walter Whorehound. Sir Walter also has affairs with Allwit's wife and promises Moll's brother Tim a fake "landed niece" from Wales. Touchwood Senior, needing to avoid more pregnancies, gives a fertility potion to the childless Kixes. Moll's attempt to elope fails, and she falls ill. Sir Walter repents, but the news of Lady Kix's pregnancy ruins his plans. At Moll's "funeral," she and Touchwood Junior reveal they’re alive, marry, and everyone celebrates. Sir Walter is imprisoned for debt.

More Dissemblers Besides Women (1614):  tragicomedy. Cited as "the only play of the period to feature a pregnant female page”. PLOT:  The Duchess, committed to a vow of abstinence, falls for General Andrugio despite her promise. Lactantio, who secretly loves Aurelia, hides his relationship to inherit from his uncle, the Lord Cardinal. Aurelia, pressured to marry an old governor, disguises herself as a gypsy to escape. The Duchess pretends to love Lactantio to win the Cardinal's favor but eventually releases Andrugio and Aurelia to be together. Lactantio's former mistress, disguised as a page, reveals her pregnancy, leading to Lactantio’s disinheritance. The play ends with the Duchess providing a dowry for Lactantio's marriage to the page.

The Widow (1615–16); The Witch (1616): a revenge play modelled on Macbeth. (Charles Lamb made famous comparative study of Middleton’s witches with Macbeth’s witches); A Fair Quarrel (1616) (co-written with Rowley); The Old Law (1618–19) (co-written with Rowley); Hengist, King of Kent (1620)

Women Beware Women (1621): the one tragedy that he wrote by himself. The octavo text of the play is prefaced by a commendatory poem by Nathaniel Richards. Middleton based the plot of his play on actual events. Bianca Cappello was first the mistress and then the second wife and Duchess of Francesco I de' Medici, Grand Duke of Tuscany. T. S. Eliot, a student of Jacobean drama, refers to the Women Beware Women chess game in The Waste Land, Part II, line 137. PLOT: follows Bianca, who elopes with the poor Leantio and is locked up by his mother. While imprisoned, the Duke of Florence, aided by the widow Livia (playing chess with mother), rapes Bianca (one of the great moments in Jacobean drama). Disillusioned with Leantio and his poverty, Bianca becomes the Duke's mistress. Livia's brother, Hippolito, is in love with his niece Isabella, who is set to marry the foolish Ward. Livia deceitfully informs Isabella that she is illegitimate, leading Isabella and Hippolito into an affair. Livia, in turn, becomes Leantio's mistress. The story spirals into tragedy as Hippolito learns of Livia's affair and kills Leantio. Livia, grieving, reveals Isabella's true relation to Hippolito. During a masque celebrating Bianca's marriage to the Duke, chaos erupts: Hippolito is killed by poisoned arrows, Isabella and Livia kill each other, and Bianca accidentally poisons the Duke, then herself.

Anything for a Quiet Life (1621) (co-written with John Webster); The Changeling (1622) (co-written with Rowley): a Jacobean tragedy. PLOT: Beatrice-Joanna, betrothed to Alonzo, loves Alsemero. She convinces De Flores, who secretly loves her, to murder Alonzo. De Flores kills Alonzo and takes a finger with a diamond ring. Beatrice marries Alsemero but is later forced by De Flores to submit to him, and she arranges for her maid Diaphanta to sleep with Alsemero instead. The subplot involves Alibius’s madhouse where Franciscus and Antonio pretend to be madmen to gain access to Alibius’s wife, Isabella. Their schemes result in comic misunderstandings and conflicts. In the climax, Beatrice and De Flores are caught in their deceit. Alsemero discovers Beatrice’s treachery, and De Flores kills her before taking his own life (Beatrice’s dying words to her father are echoed in Eliot’s “Gerontion”). The play ends with a reflective epilogue where Alsemero comments on the impossibility of comforting those who have lost loved ones, urging the audience to applaud the "replacement" of their sorrow.

The Nice Valour (1622); The Spanish Gypsy (1623) (co-written with Rowley, Dekker and John Ford): PLOT: Roderigo kidnaps and rapes Clara, who later identifies him by a crucifix. Lewys, Roderigo's friend and Clara's suitor, is horrified by the crime. Meanwhile, Don John, disguised as a gypsy, courts Pretiosa. Clara's father, Don Fernando, learns of the crime and confronts Roderigo, who unknowingly marries Clara. In the end, identities are revealed, families are reunited, and Roderigo vows to atone. Don John marries Pretiosa, who is revealed to be Fernando's long-lost daughter.

A Game at Chess (1624): a comic satirical play. The play is satirical of King James, and it was shut down after only nine days. Was produced just after the breaking off of negotiations with Spain for marrying Prince Charles to the Infanta. PLOT: In the Prologue, the Ghost of Ignatius Loyola observes a chess game symbolizing the Catholic (Black House) versus Protestant (White House) struggle and expresses his ambition for global domination. The Black House schemes to corrupt and manipulate, including the Black Queen's Pawn's failed seduction of the White Queen's Pawn and the Black Bishop's Pawn’s attempt to rape her. The Black Knight Gondomar covers up the scandal and deceives others, including the Fat Bishop of Spalato, who switches sides for power. The White Queen's Pawn is wrongly punished for slander. Later, the Fat Bishop rejoins the Black House, and various deceptions continue. The Black Queen's Pawn tricks the Black Bishop's Pawn, while the White Knight and White Duke infiltrate the Black House. In the end, the White Knight reveals the Black House’s corruption and claims victory, sending all remaining Black House pieces to "the bag."

Francis Beaumont (1584 –1616) 

Most famous for his collaborations with John Fletcher. Wrote about 52 plays with him.  In 1605, Beaumont wrote commendatory verses to Jonson's Volpone.

PLAYS by Beaumont alone:

The Knight of the Burning Pestle (performed 1607; printed 1613): a comedy play in five acts.  It is the earliest whole parody (or pastiche) play in English. The play is a satire on chivalric romances in general, similar to Don Quixote, and a parody of Thomas Heywood's The Four Prentices of London and Thomas Dekker's The Shoemaker's Holiday. It breaks the fourth wall from its outset. PLOT: In London, as "The London Merchant" is about to be performed, a Citizen and his Wife interrupt, demanding a play of their choosing, starring their apprentice Rafe as a knight errant. Meanwhile, in the original play, Jasper, a merchant's apprentice, loves his master's daughter, Luce, who must escape marriage to Humphrey. Jasper's mother abandons his father, Old Merrythought, to support her younger son, Michael. After faking an elopement with Luce, Jasper hides in a coffin to trick Luce's father, eventually winning her hand. Rafe's knightly adventures culminate in a heroic death, satisfying the Citizen and his Wife.

The Masque of the Inner Temple and Gray's Inn (performed 1613; printed 1613?): a masque.

PLAYS With Fletcher:

The Woman Hater or, The Hungry Courtier, comedy (1606; 1607): One of the earliest of their collaborations. The song "Come, Sleep" from Act III has been anthologized under the title "Sleep". PLOT: The Duke of Milan, troubled by his love for Oriana, sister to Count Valore, struggles with insomnia. Meanwhile, courtier Lazarillo hunts for a rare fish, which passes through multiple hands, including Gondarino, a woman-hating general. Oriana defies Valore and seeks adventure, ending up at Gondarino's house during a hailstorm. The Duke suspects an affair between Gondarino and Oriana, while Gondarino slanders her. Oriana is sent to a brothel, but proves her virtue when tested. The Duke marries her, and Gondarino is humiliated. Lazarillo marries a prostitute and finally enjoys his long-sought feast.

Cupid's Revenge, tragedy (c. 1607–12; 1615): Source is Arcadia of Sir Philip Sidney, the Duke in Cupid's Revenge is a blend of Sidney's King of Lycia and King of Iberia. PLOT: The play portrays Leontius, the Duke of Lycia, suppressing the customary worship of the god Cupid, the patron deity of the land, in response to the pleadings of his son and daughter, Leucippus and Hisdaspes. In revenge, Cupid (who functions as a chorus in the play, comparable to the choric figures in the tragedies of Seneca or the personification of Revenge in Kyd's The Spanish Tragedy) oversees the ruin and death of the royal family and their retainers through some very unwise amorous entanglements. As he is dying in the play's bloody final scene, Leucippus reverses his father's edict against Cupid.

Philaster, or Love Lies a-Bleeding, tragicomedy (c. 1609; 1620): 1st tragicomedies of Beaumont and Fletcher. PLOT: The play is set in Sicily, ruled by a king who fears Philaster, the rightful heir. The king plans to marry his daughter, Arethusa, to a Spanish prince, Pharamond, but Arethusa loves Philaster. Arethusa exposes Pharamond's affair with Megra, leading Pharamond to falsely accuse her of infidelity with Bellario, Philaster's page. Deceived, Philaster wounds Arethusa but both survive. Philaster is sentenced to death, but citizens rebel. Bellario is revealed as Euphrasia, a woman in love with Philaster. Pharamond retreats, and Philaster is restored as the rightful king.

The Maid's Tragedy, tragedy (c. 1609; 1619): sex tragedy. PLOT: Melantius, a general, returns to Rhodes expecting to see his friend Amintor marry Aspatia. However, the King has forced Amintor to marry Melantius's sister, Evadne, instead. Aspatia is devastated by this betrayal. On their wedding night, Evadne reveals to Amintor that the King has made her his mistress, and this marriage is a cover for her dishonor. Amintor agrees to keep the secret, but Melantius discovers the truth and convinces Evadne to kill the King. Evadne kills the King, and his brother Lysippus becomes the new ruler. Aspatia, heartbroken, disguises herself as a man and provokes Amintor into a duel, allowing herself to be killed. Evadne then confesses her crime to Amintor and kills herself. Overcome with grief, Amintor also takes his own life. In the end, Melantius attempts suicide but is restrained, vowing to starve himself instead. The new King, Lysippus, pledges to rule wisely

A King and No King, tragicomedy (1611; 1619):  John Dryden was an admirer of A King and No King; his own play Love Triumphant (1694) bears a strong resemblance to the Beaumont/Fletcher work. Also influenced Mary Pix’s The Double Distress (1701). PLOT: Arbaces, King of Iberia, returns from war with Tigranes, the defeated Armenian king, intending to marry his sister Panthea to Tigranes. Arbaces discovers that his mother, Arane, has plotted his assassination, but the regent Gobrius has thwarted it. Tigranes' fiancée, Spaconia, follows him, hoping to prevent the marriage. Arbaces becomes infatuated with Panthea and struggles with incestuous desires, while Panthea remains virtuous. In his desperation, Arbaces considers murdering Gobrius, raping Panthea, and then committing suicide. Tigranes, despite his love for Panthea, remains faithful to Spaconia. The crisis is resolved when it is revealed that Arbaces is actually Gobrius’s son and not Panthea’s brother. The whole situation was a ruse to secure a rightful heir. Arbaces marries Panthea but abdicates the throne. His flawed character is attributed to his non-royal lineage. Comic relief comes from the cowardly Bessus and his cronies, who comically mishandle dueling customs.

The Captain, comedy (c. 1609–12; 1647)

The Scornful Lady, comedy (c. 1613; 1616): Thomas Shadwell borrowed from the Beaumont/Fletcher play for his own The Woman-Captain (1680).

Love's Pilgrimage, tragicomedy (c. 1615–16; 1647): its opening scene contains material from Ben Jonson's 1629 comedy The New Inn. The plot of the play derives from Las dos Doncellas, one of the Novelas ejemplares of Miguel de Cervantes.

The Noble Gentleman, comedy (licensed 1626; 1647): Thomas D'Urfey's adapted it in his play called A Fool's Preferment. PLOT:  Monsieur Mount-Marine, a naive French gentleman aspiring to become a great courtier, is the target of a clever ruse by his sensible wife. To curb the negative effects of his ambition, she and her friends deceive him into believing he has been rapidly promoted through the social ranks—from knight to baron to earl, and finally to duke. This fictitious rise is accompanied by a corresponding, fictitious decline in his wealth. By the end of the play, Mount-Marine is convinced that he remains the Duke of Burgundy, though he must keep this title secret. Simultaneously, Chatillion, another character driven mad by love, imagines himself as a claimant to the French throne and perceives imaginary dangers around him. His delusions mirror the absurdity of Mount-Marine’s situation, highlighting the farcical nature of their respective predicaments.

John Fletcher (1579 – 1625)

Remains an important transitional figure between the Elizabethan popular tradition and the popular drama of the Restoration. His mastery is most notable in two dramatic types, tragicomedy and comedy of manners. Fletcher’s cousins: Giles Fletcher (Poem “Christ’s Victory and Triumph”); Phineas Fletcher (Poem “The Purple Island” or “Isle of Man”)

SOLO PLAYS:

The Faithful Shepherdess, pastoral (written 1608–09; printed 1609?): Contains Fletcher's famous and often-quoted address "To the Reader," which includes his definition of tragicomedy. Fletcher described his play as a "pastoral tragicomedy." The first edition contained commendatory poems by Ben Jonson, George Chapman, Francis Beaumont, and Nathan Field, and dedications by Fletcher to Sir Walter Aston, Sir Robert Townshend, and Sir William Skipwith. Adaptation of Italian writer Guarini’s famous tragicomedy Ill Pastor Fido. PLOT: Clorin, a virgin shepherdess and skilled healer, lives near the grave of her first love. She helps various couples through their erotic turmoil. In the first storyline, the shepherd Perigot loves the pure shepherdess Amoret, but the scheming Amarillis disguises herself as Amoret to seduce Perigot. Perigot, deceived, stabs Amoret, who is saved by the river god. Misunderstandings lead Perigot to stab Amoret again. Clorin heals Amoret and cleanses Perigot of his guilt. In the second storyline, the lustful shepherdess Cloe seeks any lover, initially failing with the restrained Daphnis but succeeding with Alexis. The Sullen Shepherd attacks Alexis, and Cloe flees. Clorin heals Alexis and purges Cloe of her desire. In the third storyline, Thenot is in love with Clorin but desires her only for her constancy to her deceased lover. Clorin pretends to love him, causing Thenot to lose interest and flee. By the end, Amarillis repents and is pardoned, the Sullen Shepherd is banished, and the couples—Perigot and Amoret, Cloe and Alexis—commit to faithful love.

Valentinian, tragedy (1610–14; 1647): a revenge tragedy based on the life of the Roman emperor Valentinian III.  His assassin in the play is based on Petronius Maximus (r. 455), Valentinian's short-reigning successor.  An adaptation under the same title by the poet and playwright John Wilmot, 2nd Earl of Rochester . PLOT: Fletcher portrays Valentinian’s empire as decadent and collapsing, his soldiers mutinous. Valentinian rapes the virtuous Lucina; she then commits suicide. Lucina's husband, the upright soldier Maximus, devotes himself to obtaining revenge against the emperor, though his friend Aecius tries to dissuade him. Maximus finally succeeds as Valentinian dies a painful and drawn-out death by poison. Maximus is crowned by the Roman Senate for overthrowing the tyrant, only to die himself soon after.

Monsieur Thomas, comedy (c. 1610–16; 1639)

The Woman's Prize, or The Tamer Tamed, comedy (c. 1611; 1647): The play is a counterpart to Shakespeare's The Taming of the Shrew, in which (as the subtitle indicates) the gender tables are turned and Petruchio the "tamer" is "tamed" by his second wife Maria, whom he marries after the death of Katherine, the "shrew" in Shakespeare's text. Fletcher borrowed the name, age, and nuptial desire of Moroso from the character Morose in Ben Jonson's comedy The Silent Woman (1609). Fletcher's classical source for his play was Lysistrata. PLOT: Petruchio's marriage to Katherine ends with her death, and he marries Maria, who resists his control even more. Maria refuses to consummate their marriage (this refusal shows the influence of Aristophanes' play Lysistrata) and leads a women's abstention movement. At their country estate, Maria continues her defiance, and Petruchio's attempt to feign illness fails when she walls him up. When Petruchio returns, he finds Maria pretending madness and flirting with his friends. She encourages his departure, hoping it will improve him. Petruchio's final attempt to gain Maria's sympathy by pretending to be dead backfires as she mourns his foolishness. She declares she has tamed him, and they agree to start anew. In the subplot, Livia protests an arranged marriage to Moroso and marries her love, Roland, achieving her goal.

Bonduca, tragedy (1611–14; 1647)

The Chances, comedy (c. 1613–25; 1647): borrows from La Señora Cornelia, one of the Novelas ejemplares. An unusual and rather modern-seeming approach for the opening of this play, Fletcher forces the audience to piece together the plot through a series of short action scenes. (There are fully eleven scenes in Act I.). PLOT: In Bologna, Don John and Don Frederick, two Spanish gentlemen, seek the famous beauty Costantia. John inadvertently receives a baby from a mysterious woman and takes it to his lodging, while Frederick mistakenly helps the actual Costantia, who is under threat. The city is in turmoil due to a conflict between the Duke of Ferrara and Petrucchio, Costantia's brother. John aids the Duke in the skirmish, discovering that Costantia's child is the Duke's son, and that Petrucchio seeks revenge for the Duke's seduction of his sister. Confusion ensues as Costantia and the baby go missing, and the friends suspect each other of dishonesty. They overhear a conversation about another Costantia, leading to further misunderstandings. The true Costantia is found, along with the baby, and a fraudulent conjuring act reveals the truth. All parties are reconciled, and the play ends happily.

Wit Without Money, comedy (c. 1614; 1639): Written mostly in prose instead of verse.  Herman Melville chose one of the epigraphs in his collection The Encantadas from this play.  John Dryden wrote a Prologue for its 1672 revival. PLOT: Valentine, a young gentleman who has squandered his estate, disregards his Uncle's advice to marry a wealthy woman to restore his fortunes. His irresponsible lifestyle has also harmed his younger brother Francisco’s annuity. Valentine, who mocks marriage and women, is involved with suitors Fountain, Bellamore, and Hairbrain, who are courting the wealthy widow Lady Hartwell. Meanwhile, Lady Hartwell’s sister Isabella falls for Francisco. To prevent their match, Lady Hartwell plans to leave London but is delayed when Valentine gets her coachman drunk. Their verbal sparring turns into mutual interest. Isabella secretly sends money to Francisco, who eventually discovers her identity and they confess their feelings. Similarly, Valentine and Lady Hartwell, initially at odds, begin to court and agree to marry. By the end of the play, both couples—Valentine with Lady Hartwell and Francisco with Isabella—are happily united.

The Mad Lover, tragicomedy (acted 5 January 1617; 1647)

The Loyal Subject, tragicomedy (licensed 1618; revised 1633; 1647):  Fletcher modeled his play on an earlier work by Thomas Heywood titled The Royal King and the Loyal Subject. PLOT: In Muscovy, the Duke dismisses his loyal general, Archas, after Archas corrects his military mistakes. With a Tartar invasion looming, the Duke recalls Archas, who is falsely accused by the evil counsellor Boroskie of hiding a treasure and plotting for the throne. The Duke seizes the treasure and orders Archas's daughters to court; one outsmarts the Duke and ends up married to him. Archas is tortured based on false charges, but his son Theodor rescues him. The Duke repents, punishes Boroskie, and faces a soldier rebellion that Archas quells. The Duke's soldiers consider joining the Tartars, but Archas stops them and is willing to execute Theodor for treason before the Duke intervenes. It is later revealed that Archas’s younger son is disguised as Alinda, a servant to the Duke’s sister.

The Humorous Lieutenant, tragicomedy (c. 1619; 1647); Women Pleased, tragicomedy (c. 1619–23; 1647)

The Island Princess, tragicomedy (c. 1620; 1647): Fletcher's sources were two books on exploration, L'histoire de Ruis Dias, et de Quixaire, Princess des Moloques, a novella by Le Seigneur de Bellan (1615). The play was adapted four times, by an anonymous author, by Nahum Tate, by Thomas d'Urfey, and again by Peter Anthony Motteux. PLOT: In a political intrigue on Tidore, Princess Quisara challenges her suitors to rescue her brother, the King, who is imprisoned by the Governor of Ternate. Armusia, a new arrival, successfully rescues the King using gunpowder and clever strategy. Though Quisara is initially displeased by the forced marriage, she is eventually won over by Armusia's nobility. Armusia, despite facing hostility and a duel with Ruy Dias, proves his worth and charm. The Governor of Ternate, disguised as a holy man, incites conflict and manipulates Quisara into demanding Armusia’s conversion to native religion, leading to his imprisonment. Ruy Dias and the Portuguese rescue Armusia, expose the Governor’s disguise, and help him reclaim his place. Quisara, impressed by Armusia's courage and unwavering faith, converts to Christianity and agrees to marry him.

The Wild Goose Chase, comedy (c. 1621; 1652):  Edmund Gosse called it "one of the brightest and most coherent of Fletcher's comedies”. The play is prefaced by five commendatory poems, including a 54-line encomium by Richard Lovelace. The name of Fletcher's protagonist, Mirabel, was adopted by William Congreve for his hero in The Way of the World (1700); and Sir Richard Steele employed it for a character in The Spectator. PLOT: In Paris, Oriana seeks to hold Mirabel to a marriage promise he made three years prior, but he dismisses her. Mirabel's father, La Castre, introduces him to wealthy sisters Rosalura and Lillia-Bianca, whom Mirabel finds unappealing. Pinac and Belleur, Mirabel’s friends, court the sisters with mixed success. Lugier stages deceptions to humble Mirabel and help Oriana. Mirabel is fooled by several tricks but eventually agrees to marry Oriana after being misled about a false inheritance. Belleur and Pinac also end up marrying Rosalura and Lillia-Bianca. The play concludes with all three couples preparing for marriage, with Belleur humorously commenting on their future travels. Belleur has the closing line, a dirty joke: "No more for Italy; for the low countries, I."

PLAYS With Rowley: The Maid in the Mill (licensed 1623; 1647): The two playwrights took their main plot from Leonard Digges's translation of Gerardo. PLOT: In a Spanish meadow, siblings Lisauro and Ismenia, along with Terzo and Aminta, encounter Antonio and his friend Martino. Antonio is the nephew of Julio, who is feuding with Bellides, Ismenia's father. The men draw swords but are persuaded to part peacefully by Ismenia, who has fallen for Antonio. She invites him to court her, and Antonio is interested. Martino, initially cynical, decides to pursue Ismenia as well. The play introduces Franio, an old miller, and his family. His son, Bustopha, is a fat-clown actor miscast in a local pageant. Ismenia, Aminta, and Florimel, the miller's virtuous daughter, play goddesses in the pageant. During the event, Florimel is kidnapped by Count Otrante, who attempts to seduce her. Despite his threats, Florimel resists and uses psychological manipulation to repel him. King Philip, passing through the area, aids Franio in recovering Florimel. It is revealed that Florimel is actually Julio’s long-lost daughter. Her noble status makes her a suitable match for Otrante, who accepts her as his wife. With this resolution, Antonio and Ismenia, along with Martino and Aminta, end up together, resolving the play’s conflicts and matching three couples.

PLAYS With Field: Four Plays, or Moral Representations, in One, morality (c. 1608–13; 1647): composed of a quartet of short plays; it takes the form of an Induction that sets up a frame play, followed by four plays-within-a-play, titled The Triumph of Honor, The Triumph of Love, The Triumph of Death, and The Triumph of Time. PLOT; Set during the 1497 wedding festivities of Emanuel I of Portugal and Isabella of Aragon (misidentified as "Isabella of Castile"), the play presents four "triumphs" before the royal couple. The Triumph of Honor: Roman general Martius defeats Sophocles of Athens. Sophocles and his wife Dorigen display honor, contrasting with the dishonorable Nicodemus. (The sources are novel 5 of Day 10 in the Decameron of Boccaccio, and Chaucer's Franklin's Tale.) The Triumph of Love: In Milan, the Duke’s family—his wife, sons Gerard and Ferdinand, and Gerard’s mistress Violante—overcome generational and class conflicts through mock deaths and Cupid’s influence. (The source is also from Boccaccio's Decameron, novel 8 of day 5.) The Triumph of Death: Lavall, heir of the Duke of Anjou, forsakes his wife Gabriella for Hellena. He faces a spirit reproaching his sins and dies miserable and unforgiven. (The source is Painter's Palace of Pleasure, novella 42 of book 1.) The Triumph of Time: Classical deities and personifications, including Jupiter and Mercury, depict how humanity can transcend death through "Industry and the Arts." Emanuel and Isabella comment on each triumph and Emanuel returns at the end to complete the frame play.

PLAY With Shirley: The Night Walker, or The Little Thief, comedy (c. 1611; 1640): "a play of Fletcher's, corrected by Shirley....". PLOT: Maria is coerced by her mother, the Lady, into marrying the miserly Justice Algrip, though she loves Frank Heartlove. At the wedding feast, Jack Wildbrain convinces Heartlove to attempt to seduce Maria, but Maria resists and threatens to harm herself. They encounter Justice Algrip and Snap, pretending to be Maria’s ghost, hints at hidden truths. Maria revives, and Lurcher and Snap flee. He acknowledges his prior engagement, nullifying his marriage to Maria, and restores Lurcher's fortunes. Maria is reunited with Heartlove, Snap is revealed as Lurcher’s sister, and she accepts Justice Algrip’s renewed proposal. Wildbrain is forgiven by the Lady.

Was Fletcher’s assistant.  T. S. Eliot's essay on Massinger includes the famous line, "Immature poets imitate; mature poets steal...." is an examination of William Shakespeare's influence on Massinger. His comedies are in the tradition of the Jonsonian comedy of humours with hints from Fletcher. T.S. Eliot describes his tragedies as “dreary”.

The Maid of Honour, tragicomedy (c. 1621; printed 1632): called "an improbable and escapist drama".  A passage in this play served as a source for Andrew Marvell's "The Definition of Love,". PLOT: The play is set in Palermo and Siena, Italy, where King Roberto of Sicily refuses to support his ally, Ferdinand, Duke of Urbino, in his aggressive assault on Siena led by General Gonzaga. When French forces are defeated, Sforza seeks peace with Emperor Charles V but orders his brother-in-law Francisco to kill Marcelia if he doesn't return. Francisco makes advances toward Marcelia, leading to tension when Sforza returns. Manipulated by Francisco and rumors, Sforza kills Marcelia in a jealous rage. Francisco, seeking revenge for past wrongs, poisons Sforza, leading to the Duke's tragic death.

Haunted by visions, Domitian is eventually assassinated by Parthenius, Stephano, and others, including Domitia.

John Ford (1586 – c. 1639) 

Interest in frustrated and illicit love. His plays often show the influence of Robert Burton's The Anatomy of Melancholy. His Fame's Memorial is an elegy of 1169 lines on Charles Blount, 1st Earl of Devonshire. Honour Triumphant is a prose pamphlet, a verbal fantasia written in connection with the jousts planned for the summer 1606 visit of King Christian IV of Denmark. His non-dramatic literary works—the long religious poem Christ's Bloody Sweat (1613), and two prose essays published as pamphlets, The Golden Mean (1613) and A Line of Life (1620). Known for his collaborations with Dekker and Rowley. After 1626 Ford made the transition to sole author and wrote a further eight surviving plays:

The Queen (1627; printed 1653); The Lover's Melancholy (licensed 1628; printed 1629): regarded as "Ford's first unaided drama,". Based on Robert Burton's The Anatomy of Melancholy. PLOT: In the play, nobleman Meleander's daughters are Eroclea and Cleophila. The ruler of Cyprus falls for Eroclea, hiding her away, which drives Palador, the ruler’s son, into despair. Meleander is accused of treason and becomes ill, cared for by Cleophila. When the ruler dies, Palador becomes the new ruler but Eroclea remains missing. Meleander’s nephew Menaphon returns with friend Parthenophill, who is revealed to be Eroclea in disguise. This revelation cures both Palador and Meleander. Cleophila marries Amethus, and Thamasta, who loved Parthenophill, marries Menaphon. Comic relief is provided by Rhetias and two foolish courtiers, Pelias and Cuculus.

The Broken Heart (1629; printed 1633): The text is preceded by the motto "Fide Honor," an anagram for "John Forde,". The volume was dedicated to William Lord Craven, Baron of Hampsteed-Marshall. PLOT: In Classical Greece, the play centers on Amyclas, King of Laconia, his daughter Calantha, and their court. Young Spartan general Ithocles disrupts his sister Penthea's marriage to Orgilus, insisting she marry the noble Bassanes instead. Bassanes proves a cruel, jealous husband, imprisoning Penthea. Orgilus, disguised in Sparta while feigning a journey to Athens, seeks revenge. Ithocles, recognizing his wrongs and planning a marriage between his friend Prophilus and Orgilus’s sister Euphrania, proposes to Calantha, who accepts, rejecting her cousin Nearchos of Argos. Penthea dies by starvation; Orgilus kills Ithocles with a mechanical chair just before Ithocles's wedding to Calantha. At the prenuptial banquet, Calantha, despite learning of the deaths of her father, Penthea, and Ithocles, continues dancing. Afterward, she condemns Orgilus, appoints Nearchos as her successor, and dies of a broken heart.

'Tis Pity She's a Whore (1631; printed 1633): about the incestuous love of a brother and sister. Ford dedicated the play to John Mordaunt, 1st Earl of Peterborough and Baron of Turvey. PLOT: Giovanni, back from Bologna, reveals his incestuous desire for his sister Annabella to Friar Bonaventura, who tries but fails to dissuade him. Annabella, courted by Bergetto, Grimaldi, and Soranzo, eventually reciprocates Giovanni's feelings, and their relationship is encouraged by Annabella's tutoress, Putana. Hippolita, scorned by Soranzo, teams up with Vasques to murder him. Richardetto, in disguise, also seeks revenge and manipulates Grimaldi into attacking Soranzo with a poisoned sword. Grimaldi mistakenly kills Bergetto instead. Annabella, now pregnant, marries Soranzo. During their wedding masque, Hippolita attempts to poison Soranzo but is killed by Vasques, who reveals his loyalty to Soranzo. Giovanni, dismissing danger, attends Soranzo’s feast, where he reveals his sister’s heart, kills Soranzo, and is wounded by Vasques. The cardinal orders executions and confiscation of property, while Richardetto reveals his identity, ending with the cardinal lamenting Annabella's fate "who could not say, 'Tis pity she's a whore?"

Love's Sacrifice (1632; printed 1633): one of Ford's three surviving solo tragedies, the others being The Broken Heart and 'Tis Pity She's a Whore. Ford dedicated the play to his cousin John Ford of Gray's Inn, "my truest friend, my worthiest kinsman." based the main plot of the play on the life of Carlo Gesualdo, Prince of Venosa, who murdered his first wife Maria D'Avolos after catching her with her lover.  Ford probably drew upon Henry Peacham's The Compleat Gentleman as his source.  T. S. Eliot wrote that it is "disfigured by all the faults of which Ford was capable,". The poet Richard Crashaw refers to two of Ford's plays in a couplet in his Delights of the Muses (1646): “Thou cheat'st us, Ford: mak'st one seem two by art:/What is Love's Sacrifice but the Broken Heart?” PLOT:  Duke Phillippo Caraffa of Pavia falls in love with and marries Bianca, the beautiful daughter of a Milanese gentleman whom he saw while hunting. Caraffa’s close friend, Fernando, also falls for Bianca, and though she initially rejects him, she eventually reciprocates his feelings. Fiormonda, Caraffa's widowed sister, has long harbored unrequited love for Fernando. Seeing the burgeoning attraction between Fernando and Bianca, she informs Caraffa and manipulates him through her scheming ally, the villain D’Avolos. Caraffa, driven by jealousy and anger, causes a tragic climax where Fernando and Bianca are killed (though their affair is never physically consummated), and Caraffa subsequently takes his own life. The play also features a secondary plot about Ferentes, a profligate courtier, and a comic third subplot about Mauruccio, depicting a satirical view of love.

The Fancies Chaste and Noble (1636; printed 1638): notable for its treatment of the then-fashionable topic of Platonic love.  Ford dedicated the play to Lord Randal MacDonnell, 1st Marquess of Antrim and Lord Viscount Dunluck. PLOT: In the play, Octavio, Marquis of Siena, runs a Platonic academy called the "Bower of Fancies" for his nieces and wards—Clarella, Silvia, and Floria—whom he is rumored to have as paramours. Livio and his sister Castamela are involved in a subplot where Castamela is brought to court by Octavio’s nephew, Troylo-Savelli, to separate her from her suitor Romanello so that Troylo-Savelli can marry her. In a secondary plot, Fabricio sells his wife Flavia to Julio, but Flavia, who still loves Fabricio, must navigate her new life and fend off advances from noblemen Camillo and Vespucci. The comic subplot involves the barber Secco, his marriage to the older Morosa, and the humorous antics of the page Nitido and Spadone.

James Shirley (1596 –1666)

Charles Lamb called him “the last of a great race”. His career of play writing extended from 1625 to the suppression of stage plays by Parliament in 1642. Wrote tragedies of Italianate intrigueand villainy, tragicomedies in the Fltecher tradition and comedy of manners. His best known tragedy is The Cardinal and most popular comedy is The Lady of Pleasure. His first poem Echo, or the Unfortunate Lovers. Shirley's Poems (1646) contained the epyllion Narcissus and the masque The Triumph of Beauty. His Contention of Ajax and Ulysses (short comedic Caroline era stage play) closes with the well-known lyric "The Glories of our Blood and State." Shirley published an English grammar written in poetry, titled Rudiments of Grammar: The Rules Composed in English Verse for the Greater Benefit and Delight of Young Beginners (1656).

TRAGEDIES:

The Maid's Revenge (licensed 1626; first printed 1639): his earliest extant tragedy. Based his plot on the seventh story in the collection by John Reynolds called The Triumphs of God's Revenge Against the Crying and Execrable Sin of Murder. PLOT: In the Portuguese setting of Avero, the play revolves around two noble families. Elderly lord Gaspar de Vilarezo has a son, Sebastiano, and two daughters, Catalina and Berinthia. Antonio, a close friend of Sebastiano, loves Berinthia. However, Gaspar insists Catalina must marry before Berinthia can entertain suitors. Antonio pretends to court Catalina to be near Berinthia, leading Catalina to fall for him. Discovering the deception, Catalina locks up Berinthia, plans her kidnapping and poisoning, but Antonio intervenes, rescuing Berinthia and taking her to his castle. Sebastiano, though initially enraged, accepts Antonio’s explanation and stays to court Castabella. Catalina convinces Gaspar to order Sebastiano to kill Antonio, resulting in a duel where Antonio is slain. In revenge, Berinthia kills Sebastiano, poisons Catalina, and then takes her own life. The play also includes comic elements through the character of the Count de Montenegro.

Love's Cruelty (licensed 1631; printed 1640): a tragedy. Shirley based his plot on material from two sources: novel 36 of the Heptameron of Marguerite of Navarre, and novel 6, decade 3, of the Hecatomithi of Cinthio. PLOT: In Ferrara, Clariana and Bellamente are engaged. Clariana, in disguise, visits Bellamente's friend Hippolito, who locks her in his room. When delayed, Hippolito sends Bellamente to release her; Bellamente is surprised but accepts her explanation, and they marry. However, Clariana later commits adultery with Hippolito. A servant informs Bellamente, who forgives them to hide his own dishonor. The Duke of Ferrara, pursuing Eubella, employs Hippolito as his advocate. Hippolito, feeling guilty and charmed by Eubella, proposes marriage. The Duke, feeling guilty himself, approves the marriage. Clariana, seeking to disrupt it, lures Hippolito to her chamber but is rejected. They are caught, and Clariana stabs Hippolito, who wounds her in return. Clariana confesses to Bellamente and dies, followed by Hippolito, who sees Eubella one last time. Bellamente dies of shock, and the Duke marries Eubella.

The Cardinal (licensed 1641; printed 1652): The play was published in Six New Plays. PLOT: The play revolves around the Duchess, who is pursued by Columbo, the Cardinal's nephew. Despite the King's decree, the Duchess secretly loves Alvarez. Columbo kills Alvarez during a masque, leading to a chain of revenge. Hernando, seeking the Duchess's hand, kills Columbo in a duel. CITY COMEDIES set in 1630s London:

Love Tricks, or the School of Complement (licensed 1625; first printed under its subtitle, 1631): The volume was dedicated to William Tresham. The main plot of the play, about the loves of Infortunio and Selina, derives from the tale of Phylotus and Emilia in the eighth novel of Barnabe Rich's Farewell to the Military Profession. PLOT: Infortunio loves Selina, who is set to marry the elderly Rufaldo. Driven mad by his unrequited love, Infortunio wanders the countryside. Selina, realizing her love for Infortunio, disguises herself as a shepherd and flees to the forest. Her brother Antonio disguises himself as Selina to take her place at the wedding and disrupts the ceremony, humiliating Rufaldo. Antonio's real goal is to marry Hilaria, Rufaldo's daughter, whom Rufaldo wants to marry off to the wealthy but foolish Bubulcus. Bubulcus is arrested after pretending to kill Antonio in a duel. In the forest, Selina lives with shepherds, including her long-lost sister Felice, who escaped an unwanted marriage. Felice helps restore Infortunio's sanity, and Gasparo, Felice's former love, also appears, leading to reunions and revelations. Antonio's deception is uncovered when Cornelio, Selina's and Antonio's father, and Rufaldo come to see the shepherds' rustic festivities. The play concludes with Infortunio and Selina, Antonio and Hilaria, and Gasparo and Felice happily paired. The plot is interspersed with comic scenes, notably the "school of complement," where Gasparo and Gorgon teach elaborate courtly language, leading to humorous interactions with the mad Infortunio.

Richard Brome (c. 1590? – 1652)

English dramatist of the Caroline era. His plays strongly influenced by Jonsonian comedy (Brome was not a tragedian). He was one of the Sons of Ben.

The City Wit, or the Woman Wears the Breeches (performed 1629; printed 1653): city comedy. classed among his best works. Restoration playwright George Powell's comedy A Very Good Wife, staged and printed in 1693, borrowed heavily from The City Wit and from Brome's The Court Beggar. PLOT: Master Crasy, a London merchant, faces financial ruin and hosts a dinner to reconcile with his debtors and creditors. However, his mother-in-law, Pyannet Sneakup, ruins the plan, leading to further chaos. Crasy pretends to leave London but stays in disguise, robbing Sarpego and masquerading as a doctor, "Pulsefeel," to spy on his wife, Josina, who shows no remorse. Crasy also partners with Mistress Tryman (actually his apprentice Jeremy in disguise) and Crack in various schemes. In the end, Crasy regains his fortune and reconciles with Josina, while everyone else is left embarrassed.

The Northern Lass (performed 1629; printed 1632): one of his earliest successes. The first edition contains prefatory verses praising the play and its author, written by Ben Jonson, John Ford, and Thomas Dekker. The poem by Jonson begins with the lines: “I had you for a servant once, Dick Brome”. The second edition of 1663 added the subtitle A Nest of Fools. PLOT: Sir Philip Luckless plans to marry the wealthy widow Mistress Audrey Fitchow, despite his cousin Triedwell's objections. Triedwell's attempt to break the engagement fails, and Fitchow even attracts him. Meanwhile, Luckless mistakenly believes a letter from Constance, the niece of Judge Sir Paul Squelch, is from a loose woman, causing confusion. At the wedding feast, a disguised masque reveals Luckless’s mistake, angering Fitchow, who refuses to consummate the marriage. Constance falls into melancholy, and a series of deceptions ensue, leading Widgine to elope with the wrong Constance. This allows Fitchow to finalize her divorce, freeing Sir Philip to marry the "good" Constance. The play ends with most characters content, including Fitchow, who is pacified. 

The Queen's Exchange (performed 1629–30; printed 1657); The Novella (performed 1632; printed 1653); The Weeding of Covent Garden (performed 1633; printed 1659)

The Sparagus Garden (performed 1635; printed 1640): "topographical comedy". Greatest success of Brome's career. PLOT: The play revolves around feuding neighbors Touchwood and Striker, who are both justices of the peace. Their quarrel contrasts with the love between Touchwood's son, Sam, and Striker's granddaughter, Annabelle. When Touchwood forbids their marriage, Sam, with the help of his friends Gilbert Goldwire and Walter Chamlet, deceives both families. Annabelle’s father, Sir Hugh Moneylack, scams a naive countryman, Tim Hoyden, with his partners. Walter's uncle, Sir Arthur Cautious, is tricked into a betrothal with Annabelle. On the wedding day, Annabelle pretends to be pregnant, shocking Sir Arthur, who offers money to avoid the marriage. Sam steps in, and both Touchwood and Striker accept the union. It's revealed that Tim is the long-lost son of Touchwood and Striker's late sister, ending the feud. Annabelle's pregnancy is revealed as a ruse, and peace is restored.

The Antipodes (performed 1638; printed 1640): one of the best Caroline comedies – "gay, imaginative, and spirited...;". In the first edition, Brome dedicated the play to his patron, William Seymour, 2nd Duke of Somerset. Brome relied on The Travels of Sir John Mandeville; Mandeville himself is mentioned more than once in the play. The subject of sex is central to the play's concerns. At the end of the play, Brome includes Cupid and love as an essential part of his recipe for social sanity. PLOT: The play begins with Anthony Blaze welcoming Master Joyless to London. Joyless, an older man, is troubled by his jealousy over his young wife, Diana, and his son Peregrine’s obsession with travel, which has left his wife Martha deeply unhappy. Blaze proposes a cure through Doctor Hughball and the mysterious nobleman Letoy. Letoy, a wealthy aristocrat with unconventional ways, uses a play within a play to treat the Joyless family’s issues. Peregrine is convinced he’s in the Antipodes and eventually crowns himself king, leading to a humorous yet threatening reflection of English society. Peregrine and Martha finally consummate their marriage, restoring his sanity. Letoy reveals that Diana is his daughter, explaining his earlier behavior. The play ends with a masque where Harmony triumphs over discord, bringing wit, love, wine, and health to the characters.

The Court Beggar (performed1640; printed 1653); The New Academy, or The New Exchange (registered 1640; printed 1659); The Queen and Concubine (performed 1635–39?; printed 1659): Brome's best tragicomedy.  Wrote only three tragicomedies. (Along with The Queen and Concubine, the others are The Lovesick Court and The Queen's Exchange.). Adapted the plot from Penelope's Web (1587), a prose romance by Robert Greene. PLOT: Set in Sicily, the play follows King Gonzago, who, after being saved in battle by General Sforza, grows jealous of Sforza's popularity and imprisons him, replacing him with Petruccio. Gonzago then takes Sforza's daughter, Alinda, as his mistress and falsely accuses his queen, Eulalia, of adultery with Sforza, leading to Eulalia's exile. Courtiers Lodovico and Horatio respond differently: Lodovico is loyal, while Horatio is a sycophant. Eulalia, guided by a spiritual vision, becomes a healer in the countryside. Gonzago orders Sforza's execution, but Petruccio secretly spares him. Alinda’s mental state deteriorates, and the king eventually regrets his actions, restoring Eulalia as queen. Alinda repents and retires to a religious life, while Gonzago abdicates in favor of his son and retires to a monastery.

MINOR WRITERS:

Thomas Randolph (1605 –1635) was an English poet and dramatist, recognised by his mentor Ben Jonson as being a promising writer of comedy. His earliest printed work is Aristippus, Or, The Joviall Philosopher. Presented in a private shew, To which is added, The Conceited Pedlar (1630), a gay interlude burlesquing a lecture in philosophy. The Jealous Lovers, a drama; The Muse's Looking-Glass, hardly a drama. Amyntas, or The Impossible Dowry, a pastoral printed in 1638.

William Cartwright (1611 – 1643): was an English poet, dramatist and churchman. Listed among the 17th century dramatists known as the Sons of Ben. His plays include: The Ordinary (1635) The Royal Slave (1636); The Lady Errant (acted, 1635–1636; printed, 1651); The Siege, or Love's Convert (printed 1651).

Sir William Davenant (1606 –1668): career spanned both the Caroline and Restoration eras. Was the unofficial poet laureate before Dryden.

On 2 September 1642, just after the First English Civil War had begun, the Long Parliament ordered the closure of all London theatres. The closure was the culmination of the rising anti-theatrical sentiment among Puritans, and along with William Prynne's Histriomastix (1633), its text was the most notorious attack on theatre in English history. On 24 January 1643, the actors responded to the suppression of the theatre by writing a pamphlet to Parliament titled "The Actors remonstrance or complaint for the silencing of their profession, and banishment from their severall play-houses", in which they also state, "wee have purged our stages of all obscene and scurrilous jests". In 1660, after the English Restoration brought King Charles II to effective power in England, the theatrical ban was lifted.  Under a new licensing system, two London theatres with royal patents were opened: the King's Company and the Duke's Company.
