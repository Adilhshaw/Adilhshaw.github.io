a)  Religio Laici Or A Layman's Faith (1682): In heroic couplets, response to translation of the Histoire critique due vieux testament by the French cleric Father Richard Simon which aimed to undermine Protestantism asking Christians to base their faith on the history and traditions of the Roman Catholic Church than on the Bible as it be compromised. Protestantism prioritises the authority of the Bible over the traditions and rituals of the Catholic Church. In 10 sections.'Common quiet is Mankind's concern'.

The Hind and the Panther: A Poem, in Three Parts (1687) Accession of Roman Catholic king James II in 1685; he converted; presents some evidence for thinking that Dryden became a Catholic from genuine conviction rather than political time-serving, three parts in which the Roman Catholic church appears as "A milk-white Hind, immortal and unchanged",[4] the Church of England as a panther, the Independents as a bear, the Presbyterians as a wolf, the Quakers as a hare, the Socinians as a fox, the Freethinkers as an ape, and the Anabaptists as a boar; the second part deals with the controversial topics of church authority and transubstantiation; and the third part argues that the Crown and the Anglican and Catholic Churches should form a united front against the Nonconformist churches and the Whigs.

XIV.                    RestorationComedies of Manners (unimportant)

a)  His first play The Wild Gallant appeared in 1663, and was not successful

b) Marriage a la Mode (1672) is most successful on Restoration comedy of manners. Sources: John Fletcher’s plays Beggars' Bush and its subplot from Rule a Wife and Have a Wife. Dryden's best comedic endeavour. The play contains two songs, "Why Should a Foolish Marriage Vow" by Robert Smith and "Whilst Alexis Lay Pressed" by Nicholas Staggins. Set in Sicily, Palmyra and Leonidas are raised by Hermogenes who love each other. The usurper-king Polydamas recognizes him and he tells him Leonidas is his son. So, now can’t marry Palmyra. Hermogenes reveals he lied and actually Palmyra is his daughter. Taken; again can’t marry. He then reveals Leonidas is s/o real king displaced by Polydamas. So, revolts, wins, established king. The second storyline: Rhodophil and his friend Palamede. Palamede has fallen in love with Rhodophil's wife Doralice and Rhodophil is in love with Palamede's fiancée Melantha. Finally discover and stick to their partners as they feel they should respect tradition and morality.

c)  Others: The Assignation (1672) and Mr. Limberham; or, The Kind Keeper (1678). The Rival Ladies (1664); Sir Martin Mar-all (1667); An Evening's Love (1668); Troilus and Cressida (1679)

 

The Indian Queen (1664) and The Indian Emperor (1665) are set in Mexico.

Annus Mirabilis: The Year of Wonders (1667) a lengthy historical poem which described the English defeat of the Dutch naval fleet in Battle of Lowestoft;  Four Days Battle of June 1666, and finally the victory of the St. James's Day Battle a month later; and the Great Fire of London in 1666 and Great Plague. 304 decasyllabic quatrain (ten syllables rhyming abab). Influence of style:  Sir John Davies' poem Nosce Teipsum in 1599 uses it; revived by William Davenant in his poem Gondibert, which was published in 1651. Each quatrain should contain a full stop, which A. W. Ward believes causes the verse to become "prosy". Theatres re-closed in 1665 due to plague. (historical, he says, not epic) The fault (wit writing) : in a quatrain, first two lines are descriptive, last two lines add a simile to make poetic the whole.

Essay of Dramatick Poesie (1665): The treatise is a dialogue among four speakers: Sir Robert Howard [Crites], Charles Sackville (then Lord Buckhurst) [Eugenius], Sir Charles Sedley [Lisedeius], and Dryden himself (Neander). Take a barge downriver towards the noise from the battle, concluding the English have triumphed, begin a dialogue on the advances made by modern civilization. Agree to measure progress by comparing ancient arts with modern, esp.  "dramatic poesy". Crites upheld Merit of classical drama vs. modern drama (Eugenius); French drama better: Lisideius vs. Neander Shakespeare "the greatest soul, ancient or modern".Rhymein plays are an improvement upon blank verse drama proposition that Neander, despite having defended the Elizabethans, Crites (defends the blank verse tradition of Elizabethan drama). Neander says that Aristotle demands a verbally artful ("lively") imitation of nature, while Crites thinks that dramatic imitation ceases to be "just" when it departs from ordinary speech—i.e. prose or blank verse.

 To Crites' argument that the plots of classical drama are more "just," Eugenius retorts that modern plots are more "lively" due to variety. Lisideius: French plots carefully preserve Aristotle's unities vs.Neander replies that English dramatists like Ben Jonson also kept the unities but that they preferred to develop character and motive .

Later: "Defence of An Essay of Dramatic Poesy" (1669)

 

 

Both parts of Dryden’s The Conquest of Granada by the Spaniards (1670) are set in Spain.

Aureng-zebe (1675) Its prologue denounces the use of rhyme in serious drama. Based loosely on the figures of Aurangzeb (Aureng-zebe), the then-reigning Mughal Emperor of India; his brother, Murad Baksh (Morat); and their father, Shah Jahan (Emperor) last drama in rhymed verse considered his best tragic work. The aging emperor, a stereotypical weak king, finds his throne challenged by several of his sons the loyal Aureng-Zebe being an exception. In armed conflict, the hero defeats two rebellious brothers. The emperor falls passionately in love with Indamora (with whom Auregzeb is in love), and the Empress Nourmahal, Aureng-Zebe’s stepmother and the “evil woman” of the play, has a strong passion for her stepson Aurangzeb. Confronted with news of his father’s love for Indamora and his placing her under arrest, the hero accepts the challenge involving both his love and honor. In the final battle, Aureng-Zebe leads the emperor’s forces to victory, and Morat, mortally wounded, manages to prevent his mother from murdering Indamora. Her violent passion frustrated, Nourmahal poisons herself, and the Emperor grants Aureng-Zebe both the state and Indamora.
