CHAPTER 14

Scottish Literature to 1700

Pre-1700: Scotland independent; culturally tied to Europe (especially France) rather than England.

English cultural dominance began post-Union of the Crowns (1603) and Parliaments (1707).

Linguistic and cultural ties existed between Lowland Scotland and Northern England (shared “Inglis” language).

Early medieval Scotland:

  • Spoken languages: Celtic, Norse and Anglo-Saxon (Northumbrian dialect).
  • Literary “Scots” (called “Inglis” until 15th century) evolved from Northern Middle English.
  • Middle Scots (15th century): distinct literary language.
  • Gaelic remained confined to central, northern and western Scotland.

Medieval Scottish Literature:

  • Many works lost; some mentioned in The Complaint of Scotland (1549) and Dunbar’s “Lament for the Makars” (c. 1500).
  • Thomas the Rhymer (13th century): linked to romance Sir Tristrem but remains a semi-mythical figure.

John Barbour (c. 1320–95) Bruce: Historical romance on Robert the Bruce’s fight for independence. Themes: patriotism, freedom and heroism (e.g., “Fredome is a noble thing…”).

Andrew Wyntoun (c. 1400) Oryginale Chronykil of Scotland: Historical verse; less literary merit than Barbour.

Blind Harry (15th century) Wallace: poem in decasyllabic couplets wriiten in later part of 15th century Popular romance; celebrates William Wallace; less historically accurate. Inspired later writers, including Robert Burns.

The “Scottish Chaucerians”:  King James I, Robert Henryson, William Dunbar and Gavin Douglas

James I (1394 - 1437)

The Kingis Quair ("The King's Book"): a 15th-century Early Scots poem attributed to James I of Scotland, is semi-autobiographical, recounting his 1406 capture en route to France and 18-year imprisonment by Henry IV, Henry V and Henry VI of England. The poem begins with the sleepless narrator reading Boethius' Consolation of Philosophy, reflecting on misfortune and writing about his life of misery. At 12, he was captured during a sea voyage and imprisoned, feeling utterly isolated. Birds outside his window rekindle his connection to the world, leading him to notice a beautiful woman and fall in love—paralleling James’s release through marriage to Joan Beaufort, likely referenced as "flour jonettis." (reminiscences here of the imprisoned knights falling in love with Emily in Chaucer’s Knight’s Tale). Despondent after she departs, he dreams of goddesses: Venus defers to Minerva, who deems his love pure and advises wisdom, while Fortune reveals her wheel’s instability, pinching his ear before he awakens. A turtle dove reassures him with a message symbolizing good fortune, leading to his happiness with his beloved and increased wisdom. The poem ends with thanks to those who helped his fortune, a dedication to Chaucer and Gower and penultimate stanza repeats first line, "heigh in the hevynnis figure circulere," echoing celestial spheres. Written in rhyme royal (ABABBCC), the form is linked to Chaucer and the French chant royal.

Robert Henryson

Major Middle Scots poet, skilled in rhyme royal. Born early 15th century, dead by 1508 (His death mentioned in William Dunbar, Lament for the Makaris, lines 81–2: “hes done roune (has whispered in private) with Maister Robert Henrysoun”). Schoolmaster at Dunfermline. Poet Seamus Heaney adapted Henryson's fables and they were published by Faber and Faber in 2010.

The Morall Fabillis of Esope the Phrygian: Cycle of 13 narrative poems based on European fables. Explores moral dilemmas using animal characters reflecting human psychology. Symmetrical structure: 7 Aesopic fables (from Romulus manuscripts) and 6 beast epic stories (2 groups of 3). Central poem: dream vision where narrator meets Aesop, features The Lion and the Mouse at exact center. Each fabill has a taill (tale) and moralitas. Four fabillis include a prolog.

1st tale innit: “The Taill of the Cok and the Jasp” adapts Aesop’s fable "The Cock and the Jewel" with a prologue and moralitas. The prologue defends poetry’s moral purpose, contrasts fiction with dry scholasticism and addresses educated peers, defending Scots over Latin. The fable, set in a midden, features a vivid cock who rejects the valuable jasp, claiming it suits royalty, not his simple needs. A rhetorical climax urges the jasp to seek a royal home, after which the cock departs. Stanza 18 highlights the jasp’s protective powers, questioning the cock’s choice. The moralitas critiques rejecting wisdom (the jasp), lamenting humanity’s failure to value it.

2nd tale innit: “The Taill of the Uponlandis Mous and the Burges Mous” (aka “The Twa Mice”) Middle Scots adaptation of Aesop's The Town Mouse and the Country Mouse. Ironic contrast of urban and rural life. Town mouse visits country mouse; finds simple food unsatisfactory: “This rude dyat and I can not accord”. Town mouse invites country mouse to town for better, abundant food.  In town, food is plentiful but twice interrupted by house inhabitants. Feast interrupted by Gib (cat), country mouse flees: “Almichtie God, keip me fra sic ane ffeist!” Country mouse, preferring peace, returns home to enjoy simple fare.

3rd tale innit: “The Taill of Schir Chanticleir and the Foxe” (aka “Cock and Fox”). First fable based on Reynardian sources, not Aesopian; closest Aesop match: "The Dog, the Cock and the Fox." Direct source: Geoffrey Chaucer's "Nun's Priest's Tale" (Canterbury Tales). Lowrence (fox) enters farmyard, tricks Chanticleir (cockerel) with flattery, claims friendship with Chanticleir's dead father. Chanticleir, fooled, sings with eyes closed; Lowrence seizes him, drags him to the woods. Widow faints upon discovering the theft; hens (Pertok, Sprutok, Toppok) react differently to Chanticleir's loss. Pertok mourns; Sprutok criticizes Chanticleir's behavior, urges moving on; Toppok preaches a sermon on his failure to repent. Chanticleir escapes when the widow calls the dogs; Lowrence, tired, is tricked by Chanticleir's plea, allowing him to flee. Chanticleir returns to the widow's home, and his three wives' reactions are left ambiguous. Moralitas: focuses on pride and flattery's dangers, using alliteration. "Fy, puft-up pryde! Thow is full poysonabill!" warns against pride and flattery. Fox represents flatterers.

Last tale innit: “The Paddock and the Mouse”. Expansion of Aesop’s “The Frog and the Mouse”. Mouse wants to cross a stream to reach food on the other side. Paddock offers help, explaining her ability to swim. Mouse doubts the paddock’s intentions based on her appearance. Mouse cites the proverb: "A man’s morals are shown in his face." Paddock counters with "Don’t judge by looks" and examples of deceptive appearances. Mouse agrees, but fears losing freedom and life, demands a sworn oath for help. They tie themselves together with string and begin swimming. Paddock plans to drown the mouse by swimming downward. Mouse struggles, alerting a nearby kite. Kite grabs both, kills and disembowels them, but they don’t satisfy its hunger.

The Tale of Orpheus and Eurydice: Adapts Greek myth of Orpheus and Eurydice. Based on Ovid's Metamorphoses and Virgil's Georgics. Orpheus, a legendary musician, tries to bring back his wife Eurydice from the Underworld. Henryson expands and develops the story with his own interpretations. Elegiac tone with refrain: “Quair art thou gone, my love Euridices.”

The Testament of Cresseid: 616 lines. Imagines the tragic fate of Cressida from Troilus and Criseyde (Chaucer's version left it untold). Winter opening: “The Northin wind had purifyit the Air / And causit me remufe aganis my will.” Cresseid, after a romance with Diomedes, is abandoned, destitute and rejected by Greek soldiers. Returns to her father Calchas (keeper of Venus’ temple), hides in an oratory, curses Venus and Cupid. Gods punish her for blasphemy, inflicting leprosy, disfiguring and blinding her. Becomes a social outcast, joins a leper colony and is encouraged by another leper to beg for alms. Cresseid, now a leper, mourns her fate: “To leif upon, for all mirth in this eird / Is fra me gane, sic is my wickit weird”. When Troilus passes by, he doesn’t recognize her, but gives her all his wealth, moved by an unknown impulse. Cresseid, also not recognizing him, is told of his identity and regrets her past behavior. Writes her testament, dies soon after. Her epitaph: “Lo, fair Ladyis, Crisseid, of Troyis toun, / Sumtyme countit the flour of Womanheid”. Henryson’s portrayal is objective, expressing sympathy for Cresseid’s misfortune rather than societal condemnation. The poem uses the rhyme royal (ABABBCC) in seven-line stanzas, except for Cresseid's complaint, which uses a nine-line AABAABBAB rhyme scheme.

Robene and Makyne: Pastoral ballad; lively, humorous dialogue.

The Bludy Serk: Religious allegory; vivid, powerful imagery.

William Dunbar (1459/1460 – by 1530)

Scottish makar (court poet) active during the late 15th and early 16th centuries. Associated with the royal court of King James IV. Referenced Chaucer as “rose of rethoris all” and Gower Lydgate as “ surguit lippis and tongis aureate”. Inspired the Scottish Literary Renaissance in the mid-20th century, with poets like Hugh MacDiarmid and Sydney Goodsir Smith looking to Dunbar as a model. MacDiarmid said they had to go "back to Dunbar" for poetic inspiration.

Religious works:

  • Ordained priest in the Roman Catholic Church.
  • Rorate Celi Desuper (Nativity), Of the Passioun of Christ (Passion), Done is a Battell on the Dragon Blak (Resurrection).
  • The Table of Confession (discusses sin and confession).

Secular moral works:

  • Of Deming (morality).
  • Trilogy: Of Discretioun in Asking, Of Discretioun in Geving, Of Discretioun in Taking.

Satirical works:

  • Flyting of Dumbar and Kennedie (poetic insult exchange with Walter Kennedy).
  • The Fenyeit Freir of Tungland (satirizes a false friar).
  • To the Merchantis of Edinburgh (urges civic pride in Edinburgh).
  • Tydings Fra The Sessioun (criticizes corruption in the Court of Session).
  • The Treatise of Mr. Andro Kennedy (satirizes a court member).
  • The Dregy of Dunbar, The Twa Cummeris (preserved in the Bannatyne Manuscript).

Personal poems:

  • Schir, Ye Have Mony Servitouris (compares his value to the king and country).
  • Quone Mony Benefices Vakit (requests a church office).
  • On His Heid-Ake (attempt to excuse lack of productivity due to a migraine).

Notable works:

“The Thrissil and the Rois”: Poem written for King James IV's wedding to Margaret Tudor in August 1503. Form: Dream vision; Margaret symbolized as a rose, James as lion, eagle, thistle.  Allegory of James' self-image and kingdom. Praise for Margaret's beauty, virtue, and noble birth. Written in rhyme royal stanzas, with Latin/French-inspired aureate vocabulary. Begins with description of Spring: March and April pass with varying winds and silver showers. Birds’ songs wake Dunbar, who seeks the garden from his dream but finds it vanished, feeling "half-frightened."  Dunbar writes the poem, reflecting on May 9th, the ninth of May.

“The Goldyn Targe”: extended dream allegory in courtly love tradition.

“Ane Ballat of Our Lady”: hymn to the Blessed Virgin. “Hale, sterne superne! Hale, in eterne … Hodiern, modern, sempitern, Angelicall regyne!” Ritual celebration and heartfelt appeal in a single poem.

“Meditatioun In Wyntir” reflects on Winter’s depressive effect, with themes of ambition, aging and mortality. The poet feels lost, "Nature all curage me denyis," and finds no joy in music or poetry. Possibly inspired by Dunbar’s dissatisfaction with the royal court. The poem has 10 stanzas, each with 5 lines.

The poet struggles with insomnia, "sleip may I nocht," and doubts. Four personifications visit: Despair urges securing a livelihood, Patience calls for hope, Fortune is unappeasable, Prudence accuses inconsistency and Age reminds of life's reckoning. Death appears, saying there is no escape, “Undir this lyntall sall thow lowt.” Despite wealth or love, nothing distracts from dark thoughts. Only the shortening nights bring hope, “It dois my spreit sum pairt confort, Cum, lustie Symmer with thi flowris.”

"I that in Heill wes and Gladnes" (also "The Lament for the Makars"): a danse macabre poem that repeats the refrain "timor mortis conturbat me" (fear of death troubles me) and mourns the deaths of notable poets (makars). Composed around 1505, it lists poets from the 14th-15th centuries, including Geoffrey Chaucer and Walter Kennedy. The list reflects Scottish literary culture, with many names attested only through Dunbar’s work, such as Lydgate, Gower, Barbour, Blind Hary and others. Some poets, like Patrick Johnston, were still alive at the time of composition. The poem highlights the broader scope of Scottish literary tradition beyond surviving works.

“The Tua Mariit Wemen and the Wedo”: 15th/16th-century narrative poem in alliterative verse. The narrator overhears a conversation between three women discussing marriage and sexuality.

The first wife is unhappy with her older, unattractive, but wealthy and generous husband. The second wife is married to a vain, lecherous young man and desires a new lover.

The widow, married twice, advises her friends to appear gentle while being ruthless. Her first husband was impotent and she had a lover who fathered her son. She persuaded him to leave property to the child. Her second husband, a wealthy merchant, was dominated by her and she convinced him to bequeath property to her son. After his death, she took a secret lover.

The widow encourages her friends to learn from her and the younger wives praise her. The narrator ends with the question: “Which would you choose for your wife, if you were to marry one?”

“Ane Dance in the Queen's Chalmer”:  humorous Scots poem. Describes a dance in Margaret Tudor's chamber, wife of James IV of Scotland. Various courtiers and their dance moves are described comically. Refrain: "A merrier dance might no man see." Dancers include Master Robert Schaw, a physician who provided medicinal recipes to the queen's apothecary, William Foular. Schaw likely served women of the court. Dunbar includes himself as a clumsy dancer, shedding a slipper or panton. Poem satirizes court figures, appealing to a court audience.

"He Is Na Dog, He Is a Lam": addressed to Queen Margaret Tudor, continues from his earlier work "Of James Dog" (with a refrain: “Madam, ye heff a dangerous dog”) where he criticized her servant, James Dog, as "a dangerous dog." In this poem, Dunbar pretends to renounce his harsh opinion, praising James Dog as helpful and comparing him to a lamb. However, this praise is insincere, as Dunbar quickly adds new insults, mocking Dog's wife and wishing harm upon her. Despite the mock sympathy, he reaffirms Dog’s obedience before ending with a mix of praise and mockery. Refrain: “He is na dog, he is a lam”.

“The Petition of The Gray Horse, Auld Dunbar”: a poem in which Dunbar appeals to King James IV of Scotland, requesting a new gown for Christmas. He employs an equine metaphor, presenting himself as an old, worn-out horse in need of a horsecloth to keep warm during Yule, symbolizing his long, loyal, but poorly rewarded service to the King. The poem opens with a refrain to the King: "Schir, lat it never in toune be tald, That I suld be ane Yowllis yald."

“The Resurrection of Christ”: Poem depicts Christ’s victory over Satan and hell. Refrain: “Surrexit Dominus de sepulchro.”

“The Flyting of Dunbar and Kennedie”: the earliest Scottish flyting poem, a contest of verbal insults likely performed before James IV. Dunbar insults Kennedy in 3 stanzas, Kennedy replies in 3, then Dunbar attacks in 25 stanzas, with Kennedy responding in 38. The insults mix scatological, political, and personal jabs, including accusations of theft, treason and heresy. Both poets mock each other’s origins, appearance, and poetic skill.

Gavin Douglas (c. 1474 – 1522)

Scottish bishop, makar, translator. Potic career belongs to pre- Flodden period. Main Achievement: Eneados – full, faithful translation of Virgil's Aeneid into Middle Scots rhyming couplets, first successful vernacular translation in any Anglic language. He was the first to call his language “Scottis” rather than “Inglis”.

Works:

The Palice of Honour (1501): Dream-allegory, 2000+ lines, nine-line stanzas. Describes poet's journey through courts to a palace. Draws from Romaunt of the Rose, The House of Fame. Dedicated to James IV, promoting virtue and honor. Concludes with a “ballad of honour” in 3-line stanzas.

King Hart: Allegorical poem, human life represented by King Heart and his courtiers. 900+ lines, eight-line stanzas. Doubtful authorship. Dealing with the heart's (or soul's) adventures with Dame Pleasance and others: in the end, Age knocks at the gate, Youthheid and others flee and King Hart prepares for death.

Conscience: Short, four-stanza poem. Explores the loss of "conscience" through wordplay on "science" and "ens".

Eneados: Scots translation of Virgil's Aeneid, completed 1513. First full translation of classical poem into modern Germanic language. Includes 13th book by Mapheus Vegius. Each of the 13 books introduced with original verse prologue.  Prologues touch on semi-autobiographical matters, varied styles. Translation begins: "The batalis and the man I wil discrive, Fra Troys boundis first that fugitive..."

Sir David Lyndsay of the Mount (c. 1486 – c. 1555)

Scottish knight, poet, herald. Held the highest heraldic office of Lyon King of Arms. Renowned as a poet reflecting the spirit of the Renaissance, specifically as a makar.  Known for satirical works exposing corruption, influencing the Reformation. Allan Ramsay noted Lindsay’s sharper impact on reform than Knox: “Sir David’s satires helped our nation…than all the pelts of Knox”. Focused on state of Scotland, especially post-Flodden instability. Close relationship with King James V shaped personal tone in his poetry.He describes the Tower of Babel in Dialog: "The shadow of that hyddeous strength [the Tower of Babel], sax myle and more it is of length." This line is the motto for C. S. Lewis’s That Hideous Strength and the novel’s title is derived from it.

Appears as a major character in Nigel Tranter’s James V trilogy: The Riven Realm (1984), James by the Grace of God (1985), Rough Wooing (1987).

Featured as a character in Sir Walter Scott's epic poem Marmion. Lyndsay of the Mount is a major character in John Arden’s play Armstrong's Last Goodnight, set in 16th-century Scotland.

The Dreme (1528): It is his earliest surviving poem and is an allegorical lament on the misgovernment of the realm. 1134 lines. A dream allegory written in Middle Scots verse, addressing political corruption and societal decay in Scotland. It opens with a reflective January seaside walk and introduces John the Common Weill, a figure symbolizing the Scottish people, who laments being "disherisit" (disinherited), "bot perysit" (destroyed) and neglected. He criticizes the lack of leadership, stating, “Few to me that takis tent,” and describes Scotland’s decline through "unthrift, sweirnes, falset, povertie and stryfe." The work adopts a larger cosmic structure, including visions of the earth, planets, heaven and hell, culminating in the Complaint of John the Common Weill, which vividly portrays Scotland’s ruin. It concludes with Lindsay’s plea to James V to “rewle thy realme in unitie and peace.”

Ane Dialog betwix Experience and ane Courteour of the Miserabyll Estait of the World (6333 lines)

The Complaynt of Schir David Lyndsay (1529): a humorous, personal poem written in octosyllabic couplets, structured as a "begging letter" addressed to King James V. It opens with affectionate childhood memories of Lyndsay's service to the young king, recalling how he carried him on his back and shoulders: “I bure thy grace upon my bak… strydlingis on my nek.” Lyndsay nostalgically recounts James's first words, “Pa, Da Lyn: upon the lute,” and describes playing “twenty spryngis, perqueir” (songs by heart) for him. The poem critiques the bad advisers surrounding James and offers advice for better governance, blending satire and humor with personal appeals for financial support.

The Testament of the Papyngo: 1190 lines in rhyme royal, is an allegorical “complaint” as the parrot's last words. Critiques clergy's greed, laziness, hypocrisy, using birds as characters. Offers panoramic view of Scottish history with vivid imagery, satire. Includes elegy on James IV, reflecting on his death.

Ane Dialog Betwix Experience and ane Courteour: didactic poem in octosyllabic couplets with stanzaic variations. Examines Scotland’s woes across past, present and future.

Ane Satire of the Thrie Estaits: over 4000 lines. Play first performed in 1540 and revived successfully at the Edinburgh Festival in 1948, is a moral-allegorical play blending rhyming couplets with varied verse forms. The plot follows Rex Humanitas (King Humanity), who is seduced by Lady Sensuality and influenced by Flattery, Falsehood and Deceit, who adopt hypocritical names. Verity reproaches the King, Chastity laments her exile and Diligence and Solace deliberate on solutions. Divine Correction expels Sensuality and the King receives Good Counsel, Verity and Chastity. Good Counsel advises on ruling justly and Diligence summons the Three Estates. An interlude features a Poor Man confronting a corrupt Pardoner, a comic yet scathing critique of Church corruption. In part two, John the Common Weal highlights struggle of the common man with dignity. The play critiques Church abuses, social inequality and human folly.

The Historie of ane Nobil and Wailyeand Squyer William Meldrum (Squire Meldrum): a lively verse romance in sprightly octosyllables, recounts the titular character's adventures, based on real figures.

MEDIEVAL SCOTTISH POETRY: Anonymous poetry includes colorful realism in European lyrical forms. Examples: “The Wowing of Jok and Jynny”, “Christis Kirk on the Grene”, “Peblis to the Play”. Attributed to James I or James V (likely James V).

REFORMATION AND LITERATURE: Scottish Reformation (1560) was more disruptive than in England. Early Protestant work: Gude and Godlie Ballatis (1567). Innit four sections: Catechism, spiritual hymns, Scriptural ballads and Psalms adapted from popular tunes.

        

RELIGIOUS AND POLITICAL CONTEXT:

1542: James V’s death left an infant Mary Queen of Scots amid political and religious turmoil.

1560: Parliament established Protestantism under John Knox.

1561: Mary returned to rule, celebrated by poet Alexander Scott in “Ane New Yeir Gift to the Quene Mary”.

BELOW SCOTTISH COURT POETS:

Alexander Scott (1520–1582/1583)

Scottish court poet. Court culture persisted until 1603 (James VI became James I of England). Wrote 36 short poems; notable works:

  • Ane New Yeir Gift to Quene Mary.
  • The Rondel of Love.
  • Justing at the Drum (satire).

Poems preserved in Bannatyne Manuscript (1568), compiled by George Bannatyne. Poetry in Bannatyne Manuscript (1568).

William Montgomerie (ca. 1545–ca. 1611)

Court Poet and Songwriter: Known for blending Scots poetry with European courtly traditions.

The Cherry and the Slae: Form: 114 stanzas in an intricate structure, first used in “The Bankis of Helicon” (likely Montgomerie’s). Revival of the stanza in the 18th century; admired for musicality and flexibility. Central dilemma: Should the poet seek the perfect cherry (ideal love) or settle for the sloe (practical choice)? Moral allegory: Characters like Hope, Courage, Reason and Experience engage in debates, offering philosophical reflections. Vibrant natural descriptions (“About ane bank, quhair birdis on bewis…”).

“The Solsequium” (The Marigold): Compares the marigold (solsequium), which droops without the sun, to the poet’s devotion to his love. Key Lines: “Like as the dumb solsequium, with care ourcome,

Dois sorrow, when the sun goes out of sicht…”

“Flyting of Montgomerie and Polwart” and “Sweet hairt, rejoice in mind” a love song.

John Stewart of Baldynneis (c. 1545–c. 1605)

Writer, courtier at Scottish Court. Member of Castalian Band (James VI’s literary circle). Active during Scottish Renaissance period. Associated with promoting vernacular Scots literature. Known for abridged version of Orlando Furioso in decasyllabic quatrains and for verse like “Of Ane Symmer House”.

William Fowler (c. 1560 – 1612)

Scottish poet or makar (royal bard), writer, courtier and translator. Known for his sonnet sequence The Tarantula of Love

Mark Alexander Boyd (1562–1601)

Scottish poet, soldier of fortune. Remembered for Sonnet of Venus and Cupid (in Scots). Attributed to Boyd by Arthur Quiller-Couch (1900). Ezra Pound praised it as "the most beautiful sonnet in the language." Wrote in Latin, Greek, French. Famous sonnet begins: "Fra bank to bank, fra wood to wood I rin."

Alexander Hume (1560–1609)

Presbyterian poet opposing Court poetry’s frivolity.

Hymns or Sacred Songs (1599): devotional, serious, in Anglicized Scots includes “Of the Day Estivall”, a vivid summer day depiction.

Thanks for the Deliverance of the Sicke

Ane Epistle to Maister Gilbert Montcrief

UNION OF THE CROWNS (1603):

James VI became James I of England, shifting literary focus to English.

Court poets of early 17th century (e.g., Robert Aytoun, David Murray, Robert Ker, William Alexander) adopted English literary conventions.

William Drummond of Hawthornden (1585–1649)

First publication: Teares on the Death of Meliades (1613), elegy for Henry, Prince of Wales. Influenced by Spenser and Sidney’s pastoralism. Published Mausoleum (1613), anthology of elegies by Chapman, Wither, etc. Forth Feasting (1617): Panegyric on King James's visit to Scotland in heroic couplets. In 1618 began correspondence with Michael Drayton, lasted 13 years. Hosted Ben Jonson for a fortnight, part of his literary circle. Flowers of Sion (1623) and Cypresse Grove (essay on death's folly, explores "the All"). Featured in Jean Findlay’s novel The Queen's Lender (2022).

James Graham, Marquis of Montrose (1612–1650)

Scottish equivalent of Philip Sidney; loyal to English Cavalier tradition.  Initially joined Covenanters in the Wars of the Three Kingdoms. Supported King Charles I in the English Civil War. Known as the Great Montrose. Sentenced to death: hanging, beheading, quartering. Reputation evolved from traitor to romantic hero. Inspired works by Walter Scott and John Buchan. Final poem, written before execution, uses Scots word “airth” (direction)

DECLINE OF SCOTS LITERARY LANGUAGE:

Loss of a Court diminished Scots as a literary medium.

English became the primary language for serious writing; Scots became a vernacular.

Robert Sempill of Beltrees (1595–1668)

Known for: “The Life and Death of the Piper of Kilbarchan, or the Epitaph of Habbie Simson” (Habbie Simson): Humorous, vernacular mock-elegy for entertainment. Revived an old stanza form influential in 18th-century poetry. Lacked the depth of earlier Scots poetry (e.g., Dunbar, Henryson).

EARLY DEVELOPMENT OF PROSE:

Scottish prose developed later than poetry. Latin was the dominant literary language in Scotland for historians and moralists up until the 15th century.

15TH CENTURY PROSE:

First Known Literary Prose: The Craft of Deyng (mid-15th century), a treatise on dying, simple and direct.

Translations:

Gilbert Hay: Translated French works on knighthood, warfare and government.

John Gau: Translated Richt Vay to the Kingdom of Heuine (1533), a Protestant work from the German via Dutch, published in Sweden.

Murdoch Nisbet: Translated the New Testament into Scots (~1530), influenced by Wycliffe’s work; more philological than literary.

16TH CENTURY PROSE:

John Bellenden: Translated Livy and Hector Boece’s History of Scotland (1530s). His prose is functional, workmanlike, without literary adornment.

The Complaynt of Scotland (1540): Author unknown. Written in a mixture of Scots and Latin terms, dedicated to Queen Mother Marie of Guise. Describes Scotland’s social and political state. Features a “Monolog Recreatif” with detailed descriptions of nature, animals and a pastoral dialogue, reflecting a shift towards more localized, domestic themes.

HISTORICAL PROSE:

David Lyndsay of Pitscottie: Historie and Cronicles of Scotland (1575), a lively and anecdotal continuation of Boece’s Latin history, written in Scots.

REFORMATION IMPACT:

The Protestant Reformation shifted Scots literary focus toward Protestant England and away from Catholic France. The Geneva Bible gained prominence among Scottish Protestants.

John Knox: History of the Reformation (written in the 1560s, published posthumously in 1644). His prose shows a clear trend toward more Anglicized forms of Scots, reflecting the broader shift in language usage during the Reformation.

Ninian Winzet: Catholic polemicist who challenged Knox’s ideas with a forceful, colloquial Scots style. Represented the Catholic response to the Protestant Reformation in Scotland.

END OF THE SCOTS LITERARY PROSE TRADITION:

By the 1600s, Scottish prose writing had largely been subsumed by English forms.

Scottish Literary Prose had virtually ceased by the middle of the 17th century, with the rise of English as the literary language.
