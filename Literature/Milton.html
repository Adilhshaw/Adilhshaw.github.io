"I was born in London of an honest family; ... My father destined me from a child to the pursuits of literature; and my appetite for knowledge was so voracious, tbat, from twelve years of age, I hardly ever left my studies, or went to bed before midnight." So Milton tells us in his Second Defence of the English People

Samuel Johnson, in Lives of the Most Eminent English Poets, writes of him: “It appears in all his writings that he had the usual concomitant of great abilities, a lofty and steady confidence in himself, perhaps not without some contempt of others; for scarcely any man ever wrote so much, and praised so few. Of his praise he was very frugal; as he set its value high, and considered his mention of a name as a security against the waste of time, and a certain preservative from oblivion.”

Goes to St. Paul’s then to Christ’s College, Cambridge in 1625 (B.A. in 1629 and M.A. in 1632). After retiring from university, he resides in the house in Horton, Buckinghamshire for 6 years (until 1638). He takes his own time to prepare to be the great poet he was to be. In 1638 he travels to Europe, especially Italy (the Italian tour known as “great Wanderjahre of literary history”. He returns in 1639 during First Bishop War (second in 1640). Then settled in London. Grows total blind by 1652 (in left eye in 1644)

Prolusions

He delivered 2 prolusions before his college audience which he sees for the most part as hostile. On the first prolusions, he attacks scholastic philosophy as giving birth to trivial matter. Geography, too, is recommended as an alternative subject; how much better it would be, he told his fellow students, if instead of wasting time on useless philosophical disputation they toured the whole earth with the help of a map and saw the places made famous by history and literature. Milton in this prolusion often talks in strains strongly reminiscent of Francis Bacon, whose attack in The Advancement of Learning on the barrenness and mere verbalism of scholastic philosophy had obviously impressed him.

In the second prolusions, gives air to his love for music and order by referencing theories of Pythagoras and Platonic notion of music of spheres. He was rusticated from university Iin the second year) due to his quarrel with tutor on curriculum.

In a prolusion written in 1628 titled “Vacation Exercise” (in Latin) he declares “hail, native language, that by sinews weak / Didst move my first endeavouring tongue to speak.” He makes commitment to write in English” “They service in some graver subject use”. To honour great gods “Not those new-fangled toys and trimming slight” (probably a dig at the Metaphysical school).

“The lady of Christ’s”: in  Brief Lives of John Aubrey, he mentions that "His complexion exceeding faire—he was so faire that they called him the Lady of Christ's College."

Pslams and Latin Ovidian Elegies beginning at the age of 15.

Latin Poems

  1. “Ode on the Death of a Fair Infant Dying of a Cough” (1628) inadequately integrates themes of Christianity and mythology. This was his first formal poem. It was occasioned by the death of his nice which he laments but consoles his sister towards the end by stating that she has sent a gift to heaven.
  2. Eligia Prima (1626) his first Latin elegy written at the age of 17 (not an elegy except in the structure) as a verse letter to Charles Diodati. His last work was going to be a Latin elegy titled Epitaphum Damonis. Ovidian influence. The elegy portrays him by the side of Thames happily as he’s cmntent with his life of reading, theatre-going. The poem contains the eloquent description of “Alma Venus”.
  3. “On the Death of the beadle of Cambridge University,” (1626)  this follows serious poems on the death of Bishops of Winchester and Ely.
  4. “In Quintum Novembris” (“On the Fifth of November”) is a mock heroic poem on Guy Fawkes’ Gunpowder plot.
  5. Other Latin poems include his “Fourth Elegy” (1627) for tutor Thomas Young in which he challenges the notion that nature decays overtime. Knowledge is eternal. ; ‘Seventh Elegy” (on spring and love; his most sensual poetry here). When he was to publish these poems in 1645, appends apology for wantonness to them. “Fifth Elegy” (1628) is his finest one. Titled “In Adventum Veris” (“On Coming of Spring”) Ovidian verse.
  6. “Nativity ode” (“On the Morning of Christ’s Nativity”; his 6th elegy) written in  1629 is his first successful poem, a verse letter to Charles Diodati. “This is the month, and this is the happy morn.” Contains an introduction in rhyme royal and hymn and is seen as a offering that hebrings to infant Christ. Engs with the end of Magi’s journey. Christ’s  birth brings peace and end to war (as in the hush of the 4th stanza). Christ’s arrival is seen as that of a Pan as he brings harmony and music of spheres.
  7. “On May Morning”
  8. ‘Mansus” his Latin poems wrote to pay tribute to his friend Joh Baptista Manso who was patron of Torquato Taso. Manso also wrote poems for Milton, one prefaced to his 1645 volume.  
  9. A Latin elegy on Charles Diodati as he found he had died after he returned to England and now mourns because he planned to share with him the plan of his epic.

Italian Sonnets

Written as Petrarchan lover to one Emilia. Defends Italian as the language of love in a canzone.

L'Allegro and Il Penseroso (published 1645 in Poems) written shortly after leaving Cambridge. L'Allegro (“happy man”) begins with mock-violent dismissal of “Melancholy” shows a spring day of happiness in a man’s life in the country full of pastoral activities that stand for something symbolic. The narrator invokes Mirth/Euphrosyne and her divine parentage. Near the end of the poem, the narrator requests from Mirth to be immersed in the poetry and the pleasures that Mirth is able to produce. The final lines of the poem frame a response to questions posed in Elizabethan poetry, including Christopher Marlowe's "Come live with me and be my love": “These delights, if thou canst give, / Mirth with thee, I mean to live.”

Il Penseroso ("the thinker") Recounts the same sprig day in the speaker’s life. A slower and darker poem with subtle and somber theme and imagery. The speaker of this reflective ode dispels "vain deluding Joys" from his mind in a ten-line prelude, before invoking "divinest Melancholy" to inspire his future verses. The melancholic mood is idealised by the speaker as a means by which to "attain / To something like prophetic strain," invokes a Melancholy goddess, veiled in black. “These pleasures Melancholy give, / And I with thee will choose to live.”

“How soon hath Time, the subtle thief of youth” (1631 sonnet) talks of his maturity as written in his 23rd birthday.

Also wrote two conceited epitaphs in Ben Jonson’s style

"Epitaph on the admirable Dramaticke Poet, W. Shakespeare" (his first poem to appear in print as attached to Second Folio 1632)

Horton Poems

Arcades subtitled "Part of an Entertainment presented to the Countess Dowager of Derby at Harefield by some noble persons of her family, who appear on the scene in pastoral habit .... " Music by Henry Lawes especially the part in which “the Genius of the Wood” addresses the Countess. For once here, Milton can be seen as a courtly poet.

Comus (published 1637 anonymously) “A Mask Presented at Ludlow castle” written for earl of Bridgewater’s inauguration as Lord President of Wales. Presented on Michaelmas, it upheld virtue, temperance and chastity. While Comus, the enchanter, s/o Bacchus and Circe is enjoying his dance party with his mates “What hath night to do with sleep?” He is disturbed by footsteps of Lady who is looking for her brothers. The two brothers with a sister (Lady) in a forest reside.  The two brothers are away fetching food and water. Comus tells her he can take her to them and she agrees to follow. Lady is being tempted by Comus for sensual pleasures but fails; then Necromancy attempted, fails. She’s bound to a chair but uses her recta ratio (freedom of mind) to get free. The brothers are led by Sabrina and the attendant spirit to help her. Opens with speech of attendant spirit. Epigraph from Virgil’s Ecologues: “Alas, what wretchedness have I brought upon myself! / I have let loose the south wind upon my flowers…” Influenced by Book II of Faerie Queen, William Browne’s Inner Temple Masque, Jonson’s Pleasure Reconciled to Virtue (masque), Peele’s Old Wives Tale, Fletcher’s The Faithful Shapherdess.

“On Time” time vs. eternity with experiment in verse paragraph

“Upon the Circumcision”'

“At a Solemn Music” heavenly music of spheres wins over earthly music.

Lycidas (1638; singed as J.M) a pastoral elegy occasioned by the death of Edward King who drowned in Irish sea. He argues death can cut short fame; digression on fame, true and ephemeral: true fame lies in heaven only.” As Calliope (muse of poetry) couldn’t save Orpheus: “fame is of the other world”. Apollo and Phoebus answer him and he realises inevitability of death. River Cam and Cambridge then mourn the death of the son while unworthiess (and priests as well, an attack on Anglicanism) dwell the scene, yet he consoles with a vision towards future: “tomorrow to fresh woods and pastures new.” It has a double ending: first, the heavenly fame seen as the end,  and Lycidas’ place in heaven, and secondly the hope of a new morrow which is sifferent from Keats in his “When I have fears that I may cease to be” (which was to stand alone until fame and love melt to a nothingness).Samel Jonson attacks the pastoral style of the poem.

During 1637, in a letter to Charles Diodati he says he is waiting ‘Growig my wings and preparing for a flight…”

“3 species of liberty” According to him in his Second Defence he informs he was targeting in his writings these three regions that confer happiness of social life:

  1. Religious

Anti-Prelatical Pamphlets (Anti Episcopy: he was on Puritan side) composed with his “left hand”.

Bishop Hall had published, in December, 1641, a moderate enough defense of a limited episcopacy, under the title, A Humble Remonstrance to the High Court of Parliament. Young and a group of his fellow ministers wrote a reply (the authors' combined initials formed the word "Smectymnuus," under which name the pamphlet appeared), and this reply was in turn answered both by Hall and by James Ussher, Archbishop of Armagh. Milton Joined in on Hall’s side, his first pamphlet:

  1. Of Reformation in England and the Causes that have hitherto hindered it (1641), appearing just before the replies to Smectymnuus by Hall and Ussher and not directly referring to the controversy;
  2. Of Prelatical Episcopacy, and whether it may be deduced from the Apostolical Times, by virtue of those Testimonies which are alleged to that purpose in some late treatises; one whereof goes under the name of lames, Archbishop of Armagh (1641).
  3. Animadversions upon the Remonstrant's Defence against Smectymnuus (1641)
  4. The Reason of Church Government Urged against Prelaty (1642) the first to be published with his name, he defends the Presbyterian form of church government against episcopal.  Regrets the fact that advancement of knowledge required controversy. Confesses the denegatory quality of this kind of writing but says necessitated by God. Then says he will unite learnings from foreign lands to serve the arts in his native tongue. Prepares the ground for his Epic and says follows models of Homer, Tasso and Virgil and Book of Job to mould a Chritstian hero. Sees whether Tragedy or lyric or Epic would suit his mission better.
  5. An Apology against a Pamphlet called a Modest Confutation of the Animadversions upon the Remonstrant against Smectymnuus (1642) In the same month as the latter appeared a pamphlet on the other side, whose title is also instructive: A Modest Confutation of a Slanderous and Scurrilous Libel entitled Animadversions upon the Remonstrant's Defence against Smectymnuus. He charts ecclesiastical history from Henry VIII and points 3 hinderers to reformation: antiquarians, libertines and politicians.  To him God’s word alone is proper guide.

II. Domestic

Divorce Pamphlets (4) married Mar Powell in 1642, left him soon till reconciliation in 1645. Wrote the sonnet “I did but prompt the age to quit their clogs,” to register the reaction to his pamphlets.

  1. The Doctrine and Discipline of Divorce (1643): marriage is a companionship, spiritual, physical, intellectual but if not, there must be provisions of release. Virtuous man with zero experience and not the lusty ones mostly ends up with a dumb partner and.  Marriage is “the cheerful conversation of man with woman.”

Licensing order, 1643 produces: (Compare Licencing Act 1737)

Aeropagitica (1644) although he supported Presbyterians, they passed this act which he resists against. It deals with the freedom of press. The title from the speech of Isocrates in Athena in 5th BC. It is the name of the hill in Athens and he pays tribute to it. After the fall, original truth cannot be found apiece. Only few do but they too arrive at pieces of it. Truth will prevail error only in an open conflict. Moreover, Truth is not single: it is strong and doesn’t need protection via policies and it has shapes more than one. Takes a potshot at the parliamentary censors as having no capacity to judge a writer who is industrious and has summoned all energy plus consulted friends and previous writers only to ne challenged by a nobody. Truth can only be known by falsehood. A virtuous man shut  off from the world of evil isn’t as virtuous. It is full of patriotism as well.

He had joined Cromwell group that finally led to execution of Charles I.

III. Civil

  1. Of Education (1644) in a letter form addressed to Samuel Hartlieb. 130 students to be taught by 20 teachers: first trained in concrete subjects and not abstract logic and rhetoric, which are Latin and Greek (agriculture, geometry, astronomy, geography medicine and natural history). Then classical writers on ethics and economics and by then they’ll have learned Italian. Then Hebrew (Syrian dialect) and biblical studies and church history. Then choice histories, heroic poems and Attic tragedies. Later on rhetoric and logic and also regular exercises and walks. Music also. For him education repairs mistakes of our first parents.

Post-Execution Pamphlets (3rd series of pamphlets)

The Tenure of Kings and Magistrates designed to prove-in the words of its subtitle-"that it is lawful, and hath been held so through all ages, for any who have the power, to call to account a tyrant, or wicked king, and after due conviction, to depose, and put him to death, if the ordinary magistrates have neglected or denied to do it. And that they who of late so much blame deposing, are the men that did it themselves." People are free to choose and reject the king. Against divine right and absolutism.

1649, Latin Secretary to the Council of State.

Eikonoklastes (1649) autobiography of Chalrles I released. publication of Eikon Basilike, "the true portraiture of his Sacred Majesty in his solitudes and sufferings" in the form of a record of his supposed self-communings during his last years (though actually invented by Bishop Gauden). Milton undertook to destroy the effect of this dangerously popular work, with its sentimental, idealizing picture of the King, and in ("the Image-breaker"), published in October, 1649, he produced a stinging attack on the royal character as revealed in Eikon Basilike. Breaks Bishop Gauden’s image of a saint with a tyrant. He begins thus: “To descant on the misfortunes of a person fallen from so high a dignity, who hath also paid his final debt both to nature and his faults, is neither of itself a thing commendable, nor the intention of this discourse" and then attacks his character. He says that the book supposed to be his autobiography was a plagiarism of Sidney’s Arcadia and to think a man in his hours of death would take recourse to such an amorous book (while it has its merits but not when the hour demands religious devotion) itself talks of his character.

3 Defences 

Defence of the English People (1651) Latin title: Joannis Miltoni Angli pro Populo Anglicano Defensio contra Claudu anonymi, alias Salmasii, Defensionem Regiam. He replied to Defensio Regia pro Carolo written by French Claude Saumaise, generally known as Salmasius, commissioned by exiled Charles II as against regicide. Criticises the defence and author’s grammar etc. and the author leaves court, ashamed.

Second Defence of the English People (1654) Latin title: Joannis Miltoni Angli pro Populo Anglicano Defensio. Reply to Regii Sanguinis Clamor ad Coelum, .adversus Parricidas Anglicanos,(“ The Cry of the King's Blood to Heaven against the English Parricides”) that he mistook to be by Alexander More. It has autobiographical passages also. Once, in such a passage says he left his career hanging for the service of his nation (pamphleteering).

"John Milton's Defence of Himself against Alexander More," Latin title Joannis Miltoni pro se Defensio contra Alexandrum Morum (1655) against the attack by More. He attacked his language as well: "language of unwashed foulness, words naked and indelicate." In response, Milton says the precedent for such naked and plain language set by Sallust, Herodotus, Seneca, Plutarch, "the gravest of authors. "If you charge me with obscenity, you “will you have to charge with indecency and obscenity Erasmus, ... Thomas More.”

Pamphlets after Cromwell’s Death

A Treatise of Civil Power in Ecclesiastical Causes (1658) defending Protestant cause and each man with his Bible leading his own way to salvation as another man cannot direct him to the same.

Considerations touching the likeliest means to Remove Hirelings out of the Church, (1659) after Cromwell's son, Richard Cromwell, had given up the attempt to carry on his father's position and the Rump Parliament (what remained of the Long Parliament after the dismissal of the Presbyterians in "Pride's Purge" in 1648) was recalled by the army to consider the position. His dream of an ideal commonwealth and plan for an almost unsalaried clergy drawn only from those truly eager for spiritual service, instead of a class of ecclesiastics.

Ready and Easy Way to Establish a Free Commonwealth (1659; second edition on the eve of Restoration, 1660) Now is the opportunity to do away with this rule and establish government elected by qualified electorate. Planned for a single parliament. But in second edition he is nostalgic and giving up in the conclusion as he talks of “expiring liberty.”

Of True Religion, Heresy, Toleration, and the growth of Popery (1673) he’s in favour of toleration of all creeds based on honest interpretation of God's word, however mutually different, and against that one species of Christianity that imposes a man-made tradition on all.

Paradise Lost

He had different plans for writing his masterpiece: he thought of plot like Roman, British and historical English tale in epic or pay. Then he thought of Adam Unparadiz’s a play in five acts before choosing the epic form. In terms of measure, he defended his choice of “heroic verse without rime” and attacks use of rhyme as well.

Book 1: description of fallen angels beginning to recover.

Book 2: Shows “corruption optimi pessimal” (the corruption of the best becomes the worst) because evil has the attraction, and if one has the necessary rhetoric and beauty, then it becomes all the more dangerous.

Book 3: Takes place in heaven and is very weak poetically. God’s straightforward logical language is ineffective and the poetry is destroyed.

Book 4: first account of simple and innocent life in Eden begins with Vee’s words “Sweet is the breath of morn”. Satan enters Eden. He synthesises different mythologies, Biblical, Jewish, Paga, but doesn’t, unlike Spenser, mingle them. Book 4 is a prime example of his usage of pagan mythology. Mathew Arnold uses lines about Prosperin and Ceres as touchstone to great poetry.

Book 5:  Raphael is received by Adam. He gives the account of war in Heaven, the plot for part of this book and completely of Book 6.

Book 6: war in Heaven.

Book 7: account of creation by Raphael.

Book 8: Adam tells Raphael of his experiences post creation.

Book 9: great book. We see difference of opinuion between adam and Eve as she decides to go gardening farther; Adam letting her do it; hint of her being tempted by Satan as a toad; then serpent under forbidden tree; Adam too eats the fruit.

Book 10: changes after fall are reflected on Earth and in Hell.

Book 11: Michael tells Adam of the future history of world; Cain; Christ’s appearance making fall a “fortunate fall”; flood; Adam’s, and by extension Milton’s, belief in private virtue (“paradise within”) and their taking leave to go to Earth and work together.

Paradise Regained (1671) “brief epic” in 4 books taken from Luke.

Christ is tempted in the wilderness by Satan to uphold public life of fame; he rejects it and succeeds in regaining what Adam had lost. Fletcher’s Christ’s Victory and Triumph an influence.

Book 1: Christ spends 40 days in the wilderness without food; Satan decides to find this man out; he confronts him as an old rural pauper who wants him to turn stone into bread but Christ refuses. First temptation, temptation to distrust and not to gluttony, begins as Jesus points that he knows he is Satan. But fails and Jesus left alone in wilderness.

Book 2: Christ’s disciples wonder where he is; the Belial suggests to failed Satan to “set women in his eye”. Second temptation, to luxury and sensuality as Satan enters with magnificent garments inviting for grand banquet. Satan chides him about being powerless upon which he exclaims that he who “reigns within himself” “is more a king.”

Book 3: Then he tempts him with Fame of public life over private one; Patience and temperance over glory are true virtues. He now says that to fight for right cause, like Israel against Roman yoke, is good. Tempts him to take David’s throne. He says no, everything will happen at its appointed time.

Book 4: Satan builds arguments of Greek and Roman civilisations are magnificent and ideal. Compassion is shown for Greek philosophers who are great but “Sion’s songs” are better than these philosophers. He carries Jesus back to wilderness to frighten him with night; then to a pinnacle of temple to frighten him but he doesn’t call for help for his personal safety and temptation is over and he returns home “private return’d”.

Samson Agonistes (1671) modelled on ancient models of Greek tragedy, Daiches sees it as “the only successful Greek tragedy in English”. Follows plot: 24 hours; verse form as well. Milton says tragedy is the “gravest, moralest and most profitable.” Dialogue between Samson and people who visit him. During the conversations intervened by Chorus, he grows characters like penitence, confidence in God’s glory and whatever he has in store, and finally he pulls down the temple as Philistines are having a festivity. Visited by people like Manoa, father (tempts him with despair as he tells him he had warned him about marrying a Philistine; he grows self-reproaching and almost despairs), Dalila, wife (who tempts him with physical love and sex; she has revealed his power in his locks to  Philistines although she never knew they will blind him; she isn’t lying and says she prefers a blind Samson to a seeing one), Harapha, the giant Philistine (who taunts him with indignities and Samson says he deserves much worse for his past sins; later chases him with pretended motion towards him), Philistine officer (summons Samson for a performance at exhibition at the feast of Dagon; first he negates then seeing his hair grow and strength return agree with divine impulse; ) Then we return to Manao who is hopeful of restoring Samson and his sight but interrupted by the scene of destruction. Which was not suicide and didn’t kill himself “willingly” and his father concludes “Samson hath quit himself / Like Samson” a hero.

He says he won’t despair and so does the chorus: “All is best”. “Eyeless in Gaza, at the mill with slaves”
