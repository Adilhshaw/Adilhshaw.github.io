iii. “Mrs Warren's Profession” (1893) a problem play; was banned in England for its exposure of the brothel industry. In the preface, Shaw defines his aim as showing “prostitution is caused, not by female depravity and male licentiousness, but simply by underpaying, undervaluing, and maltreating women so shamefully that the poorer of them are forced to resort to prostitution .... Society, and not any individual is the villain of the piece.”

Elements of the play were borrowed from Shaw's 1882 novel Cashel Byron's Profession which is about a man who becomes a boxer due to limited employment opportunities. Shows prostitution was not caused by moral failure but by economic necessity. Says inspiration: Yvette by Guy de Maupassant.

Vivie Warren, a modern young woman, has just graduated from the University of Cambridge with honours in Mathematics is available for suitors. Her mother Mrs. Warren (prostitute, now madam i.e head of brothel) arranges for her to meet her friend Mr. Praed. Comes with business partner, Sir George Crofts, who is attracted to Vivie. She is romantically involved with Frank Gardner; His father, Reverend Samuel Gardner, has a history with Vivie's mother; Vivie and Frank half-siblings. Mrs. Warren successfully justifies to her daughter how she chose her particular profession in order to support her daughter and give her the opportunities she never had. Her business is a chain of brothels across Europe.But the reconciliation ends, finds mother continues business though she no longer needs to. She takes an office job, dumps Frank, disowns her mother, and Mrs. Warren is left heartbroken, having looked forward to growing old with her daughter.

The Apple Cart: A Political Extravaganza (1928) a satirical comedy in which political philosophies are expounded by the characters, often in lengthy monologues. The King, Magnus “defeats an attempt by his popularly elected Prime Minister to deprive him of the right to influence public opinion through the press and the platform: in short, to reduce him to a cipher. The King's reply is that rather than be a cipher he will abandon his throne and take his obviously very rosy chance of becoming a popularly elected Prime Minister himself” (preface). The plot follows the fictional English King Magnus as he spars with, and ultimately outwits, Prime Minister Proteus and his cabinet, who seek to strip the monarchy of its remaining political influence. Magnus opposes the corporation "Breakages, Limited", which controls politicians and impedes technical progress.

J. M Synge (1871 to 1909)

On Yeats’s behest, spends summers from 1898 to 1903 on Aron Island. Richard Ellmann, the biographer of Yeats and James Joyce, stated that Synge "built a fantastic drama out of Irish life. Yeats wrote "In Memory of Major Robert Gregory" in his memory. He was immersed in the socialist literature of William Morris. Yeats said that Synge was "the greatest dramatic genius of Ireland".  Joseph O'Connor wrote a novel, Ghost Light (2010), loosely based on Synge's relationship with Molly Allgood.

Kottabos: A College Miscellany (1899) his first poem influenced by Wordworth.
