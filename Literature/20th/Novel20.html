The Time Machine (1895) post-apocalyptic science fiction

A frame story of the narrator recounting adventures of the Time Traveller. Time Machine is interpreted in modern times as a commentary on the increasing inequality and class divisions of Wells' era, two separate human species: the fair, childlike Eloi, and the savage, simian Morlocks, distant descendants of the contemporary upper and lower classes respectively. The protagonist is a Victorian English scientist identified by a narrator simply as the Time Traveller. He recounts the Traveller's lecture to his weekly dinner guests; demonstrates a tabletop model machine for travelling through the fourth dimension. In the new narrative, the Time Traveller tests his device and stops in A.D. 802,701, where he meets the Eloi, a society of small, elegant, childlike adults with fruit-based diet. Feels communism has at last been achieved; time machine missing; Morlocks, ape-like troglodytes who live in darkness underground and surface only at night. Speculates that the human race has evolved into two species: the favoured aristocracy has become the intellectually degraded Eloi, and their mechanical servants have become the brutal light-fearing Morlocks; intelligence is the result of and response to danger; saves an Eloi named Weena from drowning  "The Palace of Green Porcelain"; Go to Sphinx where machine hidden; Morlocks attack; Traveller escapes Weena and the pursuing Morlocks are lost in the fire he set; he travels further ahead to roughly 30 million years from his own time. Dying Earth rotation gradually ceases and the sun grow larger, redder, and dimmer, relates his adventures evidence two strange white flowers.

Grouping 

Literary critics have followed Bennett in dividing his novels into groups. The literary scholar Kurt Koenigsberger proposes three categories. In the first are the long narratives – "freestanding, monumental artefacts" – Anna of the Five Towns, The Old Wives' Tale, Clayhanger and Riceyman Steps, which "have been held in high critical regard since their publication". Koenigsberger writes that the "Fantasias" such as The Grand Babylon Hotel (1902), Teresa of Watling Street (1904) and The City of Pleasure (1907), have "mostly passed from public attention along with the 'modern' conditions they exploit". His third group includes "Idyllic Diversions" or "Stories of Adventure", including Helen with the High Hand (1910), The Card (1911), and The Regent (1913), which "have sustained some enduring critical and popular interest, not least for their amusing treatment of cosmopolitanism and provinciality"

  • England's Pleasant Land (1940) --- The play is a historical pageant that explores the changes in the English countryside over the centuries and reflects on the impact of these changes on the land and its people. The play is divided into several acts, each depicting a different period in English history. The central theme of the play is the tension between progress and preservation, questioning whether modern advancements will destroy the natural beauty of England or help to preserve it for future generations

Important story summaries:

"The Sisters" (1904) –was Joyce's first published work of fiction using the pseudonym Stephen Dædalus. A first-person narrative, a young boy waits outside Father James Flynn's home, anticipating the priest's death. After the priest dies, a family friend criticizes Flynn and their relationship. The boy dreams of Flynn, recalling his death from "paralysis." He feels unexpectedly freed rather than mournful, despite Flynn's significant influence on him. The next day, he and his aunt visit Flynn's home, where his sisters, Eliza and Nannie, recount the priest's decline, including his overwhelming duties, a broken chalice, and his later odd behavior in the confession-box.

"Araby" – a first-person narrative, the reader experiences the drab North Richmond Street through the eyes of a boy enchanted by the neighborhood's magic despite its bleakness. The boys play in the dim streets, their imaginations vivid as they explore the area's dark corners and odors. The boy is captivated by Mangan's sister, beginning to idealize her romantically and obsess over her. On learning that she cannot attend the Araby bazaar, he promises to bring her a gift. However, when he arrives at the bazaar, it is mostly closed and disappointing. He sees a young woman at a stall, more interested in her conversation than in serving him, shattering his romantic illusions about both the bazaar and Mangan's sister. This realization leaves him feeling disillusioned and self-aware

"Eveline" (1904) – follows a nineteen-year-old woman named Eveline. As she sits by her window, she reflects on her difficult life: her deceased mother and brother, her distant remaining brother Harry, and her oppressive father. Her job offers little satisfaction. Eveline dreams of escaping with her sailor lover, Frank, to Buenos Aires. However, the memory of a melody associated with her mother's death and a promise to care for her home paralyze her at the dock. Despite Frank's romantic promises, Eveline is unable to leave Dublin. Joyce uses Eveline's paralysis to illustrate the broader sense of stagnation felt by Dubliners. Critics debate Frank's intentions and whether the journey to Buenos Aires signifies a genuine escape or exploitation.

"Clay" – Maria, a beloved, middle-aged woman working at a Dublin rescue mission, eagerly heads to Joe’s house for a holiday gathering. After a tea with her laundress colleagues, she buys cakes for the Halloween party but accidentally leaves them on the tram, possibly due to her flirtatious interaction with a drunken elderly man. At Joe’s home, she joins in a traditional Halloween game where the objects have prophetic meanings. Maria fails to pick the ring, symbolizing marriage, and instead selects a lump of clay and then a prayer book, suggesting a life devoted to spiritual service. After some wine, Maria sings “I Dreamt That I Dwelt in Marble Halls” but makes a mistake by repeating the first verse and omitting the second, which yearns for romantic suitors she has never had. The evening ends with Joe, moved by Maria’s song, asking his wife for the corkscrew.

"The Dead" – described by T. S. Eliot as "one of the greatest short stories ever written". It was later adapted into a one-act play by Hugh Leonard. The story follows Gabriel Conroy, a teacher and book reviewer, as he attends his aunts Kate and Julia Morkan's Christmas party with his wife, Gretta. Gabriel arrives late and has an awkward encounter with Lily, the caretaker’s daughter, and is concerned about his speech, which he fears is too academic. He also faces criticism from Miss Ivors, an Irish nationalist, for writing for a Unionist newspaper, which leaves him unsettled. During dinner, Gabriel reflects on the snow outside and delivers his speech, praising Irish hospitality. As the party ends, Gabriel and Gretta leave for a hotel, but Gabriel's romantic expectations are dashed when Gretta shows little interest. She reveals that the song "The Lass of Aughrim" reminds her of Michael Furey, a deceased former lover from Galway, whose death she believes was caused by his visit to her while sick. Gabriel is stunned by this revelation and contemplates the impact of the dead on the living. The story concludes with Gabriel reflecting on the snow and the inevitability of death, feeling a profound connection to all living and dead as the snow falls.

A Portrait of the Artist as a Young Man (1916) first novel.  Stephen Dedalus rebels against the Catholic and Irish conventions. Began in 1904 as Stephen Hero, Joyce abandoned it in 1907 and condensed 25 chaps to 5. The novel serialised in the English literary magazine The Egoist in 1914 and 1915 by Pound. Epigraph, Ovid, "And he turned his mind to unknown arts." The novel opens with young Stephen Dedalus listening to his father's fairy tale. His early memories are a mix of sensory experiences - from a candy seller to his mother's scent and his governess Dante's brushes. At Clongowes school, Stephen longs for the comfort of study hall while playing outside. He falls ill and spends time in the infirmary, where he learns of Parnell's death. During winter holidays, he overhears a heated family argument about Parnell and the Catholic Church. Back at school, Stephen is bewildered by sexual incidents involving classmates. He experiences unjust punishment when a teacher beats him for sitting out due to broken glasses. Stephen complains to the rector and gains support from his peers. Years later, Stephen spends a summer in Blackrock with his friend Aubrey, reading "The Count of Monte Cristo" and exploring. Financial troubles force his family to move to Dublin, where he develops a crush on Emma Clery, even writing her a love poem. At Belvedere, Stephen excels academically but faces trouble for his controversial essays. He acts in a school play and takes a melancholy trip to Cork with his father. Despite winning a large sum for academic excellence, Stephen remains restless and begins visiting prostitutes, battling guilt and shame. A religious retreat at school intensifies Stephen's guilt, leading him to confess and temporarily embrace piety. When offered the possibility of priesthood, Stephen realizes it's not his calling. Instead, he applies to the University of Dublin and has an epiphany about becoming a writer while observing a girl on the shore. At university, Stephen becomes known for his aesthetic theories, often discussing them with friends, especially Cranly. He continues to think about Emma and writes her another poem. The novel concludes with diary entries detailing Stephen's excitement about leaving Ireland to pursue his destiny as a writer, showcasing his artistic and intellectual growth throughout the narrative.

Finnegans Wake (1939) final work Joyce declared that "every syllable can be justified". Serialized in 1928 in journals The Transatlantic Review and transition (sic), under the title "fragments from Work in Progress". Giambattista Vico's cyclical view of history: Finnegans Wake opens with the words "riverrun, past Eve and Adam's, from swerve of shore to bend of bay, brings us by a commodius vicus of recirculation back to Howth Castle and Environs" and ends "A way a lone a last a loved a long the". Written over a period of seventeen years. Consists of 17 chapters, divided into four Parts or Books. Part I contains eight chapters, Parts II and III each contain four, and Part IV consists of only one short chapter. About the lives of the Earwicker family, comprising the father HCE, the mother ALP, and their three children Shem the Penman, Shaun the Postman, and Issy.

Part I In Finnegans Wake, Joyce opens with Finnegan's fall and transformation into the modern pub owner H.C.E. The book cycles back to its beginning with the final sentence linking to the start. The first chapter (I.1) introduces Finnegan's fall while building a wall, his corpse vanishing before the wake, and his resurrection when whiskey spills on him. The chapter concludes with HCE sailing into Dublin Bay. Chapter I.2 describes HCE's rise to prominence, only to be brought low by a spreading rumor about a scandalous incident in Phoenix Park. Chapters I.2 to I.4 trace this rumor's evolution, including HCE's trial under the name Festy King. His wife ALP's letter becomes central, leading to I.5, where it's analyzed. The letter, intended for delivery by Shaun, ends up in a midden, and Chapter I.6 provides character details through riddles. In the last chapters of Part I, Shem the Penman (I.7) is portrayed negatively by Shaun but defended by ALP. Chapter I.8, celebrated for its river names, depicts a conversation between two washerwomen about ALP and HCE's issues, ending with a request for a Tale of Shem or Shaun. Part II shifts focus to HCE and ALP's children: Shem, Shaun, and Issy. II.1 introduces a pantomime program and a guessing game, leading to Shem's disgrace and Shaun's success with the girls. HCE calls the children inside. II.2 shows the children studying and engaging in Euclid exercises, with Shem's assistance leading to Shaun's realization and a conflict. The chapter ends with a night letter to HCE and ALP. II.3 depicts HCE in the pub, with radio and TV broadcasts of stories about HCE's past and his symbolic replacement by his children. HCE confesses his sins, and the pub closes as he transforms into Rory O'Connor. II.4 follows a dream sequence where four old men observe Tristan and Isolde's journey, reflecting on their own lives. Part III focuses on Shaun the Post delivering ALP's letter. III.1 features Shaun answering questions about the letter while floating in a barrel. III.2 and III.3 depict Shaun's transformation from a sermon-giver to a medium through which HCE speaks. The section concludes with a bedroom scene in III.4, where Mr. and Mrs. Porter are interrupted by their child, and their coitus ends with the rooster crowing. Part IV consists of one chapter with unrelated vignettes, starting with a call for dawn. It includes "Saint Kevin," "Berkely and Patrick," and "The Revered Letter." ALP delivers her final monologue, urging HCE to rise and reflecting on their past. The book concludes with ALP disappearing into the ocean, and the final fragment connects to the book's beginning, completing the cyclical narrative

David Herbert Lawrence (1885-1930) (Taken from Azra’s notes 20th Century Early novel)

English novelist, short story writer

He described his voluntary exile as a "savage enough pilgrimage".

His first published work was a group of his early poems in the "English Review" in 1909, and in 1910 appeared his early short story and then his first novel "The White Peacock".

It was in 1910 that he broke his engagement with Jessie Chambers (identified generally with Mir-jam in "Sons and Lovers"), became engaged to Louis Burrows, witnessed his mother's death and began the first version of "Sons and Lovers", which was published in 1913.

1912 Lawrence wrote the first of his so-called "mining plays", The Daughter-in-Law, written in Nottingham dialect.

He showed his indebtedness to Freud in his works written in reply to the Austrian physician and psychopathologist: "Psychoanalysis and the Unconscious" (1921) and "Fantasia of the Unconscious" (1922).

He made his defense of "Lady Chatterley's Lover" first in "À Propos of Lady Chatterley's Lover" (at first entitled "My Skirmish with Jolly Roger") and then in a pamphlet "Pornography and Obscenity" (1929).

Two notebooks of Lawrence's unprinted verse were posthumously published as Last Poems and More Pansies. These contain two of Lawrence's most famous poems about death, "Bavarian Gentians" and "The Ship of Death"

 

NOVELS:

The White Peacock (1911) first novel. 1910 on the title page. Started the novel in 1906 and then rewrote it three times. The early versions had the working title of Laetitia. Maurice Greiffenhagen's 1891 painting "An Idyll" inspired the novel. The novel is set in Nethermere (fictional name for real-life Eastwood) and is narrated by Cyril Beardsall, whose sister Laetitia (Lettie) is involved in a love triangle with two young men, George and Leslie Temple. She eventually marries Leslie, even though she feels sexually drawn to George. Spurned by Lettie, George marries the conventional Meg. Both his and Lettie's marriages end in unhappiness, as George slides into alcoholism at the novel's close

The Trespasser (1912) Originally it was titled the Saga of Siegmund and drew upon the experiences of a friend of Lawrence, Helen Corke, and her adulterous relationship with a married man that ended with his suicide. Lawrence worked from Corke's diary, with her permission, but also urged her to publish; which she did in 1933 as Neutral Ground. Set mostly on the Isle of Wight, it tells the story of Siegmund, a married man with children, and his adulterous affair with Helena.

Sons and Lovers (1913) The novel is dedicated to Edward Garnett. He allowed Garnett to cut roughly 100 pages from the text. Lawrence rewrote the work four times. Before publication the work was usually titled Paul Morel. Summary: Part I Gertrude Coppard, the refined daughter of a "good old burgher family," meets Walter Morel, a rough-hewn miner, at a Christmas dance. They fall into a whirlwind romance marked by physical passion. However, Gertrude soon realizes the difficulties of living off Walter’s meager salary in a rented house. The couple fights and drifts apart, with Walter retreating to the pub after work each day. Gradually, Mrs. Morel's affections shift to her sons, starting with the eldest, William. As a boy, William is so attached to his mother that he does not enjoy the fair without her. As he grows older, he defends her against his father's occasional violence. Eventually, he leaves their Nottinghamshire home for a job in London, where he begins to rise into the middle class. He becomes engaged but detests his fiancée's superficiality. When William dies, Mrs. Morel is heartbroken. Her love shifts to her second son, Paul, especially after he catches pneumonia. Part II Paul is both repulsed by and drawn to his mother. Afraid to leave her but needing to experience love, he falls into a relationship with Miriam, a farmer's daughter who attends his church. They take long walks and have intellectual conversations about books, but Paul resists a deeper relationship, partly because his mother disapproves. At Miriam's family's farm, Paul meets Clara Dawes, a young woman with feminist sympathies who is separated from her husband, Baxter. After pressuring Miriam into a physical relationship, which he finds unsatisfying, Paul breaks with her and grows more intimate with Clara, who is more passionate physically. However, even Clara cannot hold him, and he returns to his mother. When his mother dies soon after, Paul is left alone.

The Rainbow (1915) Its sequel is Women in Love. Prosecuted in an obscenity trial at Bow Street Magistrates' Court in 1915, as a result of which 1,011 copies were seized and burnt. Was banned in the UK for 11 years. Follows three generations of the Brangwen family, farmers and craftsmen in the east Midlands of England, from the 1840s to 1905. The story begins with Tom Brangwen, a farmer who marries Lydia, a Polish refugee. It then shifts to Lydia's daughter, Anna, and her turbulent relationship with her husband, Will. The final and most prominent part of the book focuses on Ursula, Anna and Will's daughter, who struggles to find fulfillment in an increasingly industrialized and materialistic society. Ursula experiences a same-sex relationship and a passionate, ultimately doomed affair with Anton Skrebensky, a British soldier of Polish descent. The book concludes with Ursula envisioning a rainbow as a symbol of a hopeful future for humanity.

Women in Love (1920) follows the Brangwen sisters, Gudrun and Ursula. Gudrun, an artist, has a destructive relationship with industrialist Gerald Crich, while Ursula develops a love for Rupert Birkin. Their relationships are marked by societal and personal conflicts. At a party, Gerald's sister drowns, and his father dies soon after. During a holiday in the Austrian Alps, Gudrun befriends artist Loerke, which angers Gerald. In a fit of rage, Gerald attempts to strangle Gudrun, then retreats to the mountains, where he dies from exposure. Birkin is deeply affected by Gerald's death, reflecting on their lost friendship and his commitment to Ursula. The novel explores British society before World War I and ends in the Tyrolean Alps. Ursula is inspired by Lawrence's wife, Frieda, Gudrun by Katherine Mansfield, Birkin by Lawrence, and Crich by Mansfield's husband, John Middleton Murry. The book stirred criticism for its portrayal of love, and denounced as chauvinistic and centred upon the phallus by the philosopher Simone de Beauvoir in The Second Sex.

The Lost Girl (1920) Alvina Houghton, the daughter of a widowed Midlands draper, comes of age just as her father’s business is failing. In a desperate attempt to regain his fortune and secure his daughter’s proper upbringing, James Houghton buys a theater. Among the traveling performers he employs is Ciccio, a sensual Italian who immediately captures Alvina’s attention. Fleeing with him to Naples, she leaves her safe world behind and enters one of sexual awakening, desire, and fleeting freedom.

Aaron's Rod (1922) is a picaresque novel. The title refers to the rod of Aaron in the Old Testament, Moses' brother who built the Golden Calf in the desert for the worship of the Israelites. The rod, his divine symbol of authority and independence, finds its echo in the flute of Aaron Sisson. British writer, Reginald Turner, was the model for the character "little Algy Constable" in Aaron's Rod.  John Middleton Murry praised the novel in his periodical "Nation and Athenaeum":

"To read 'Aaron's Rod' is to drink of a fountain of life.... 'Aaron's Rod' is the most important thing that has happened to English literature since the war. To my mind it is much more important than 'Ulysses'." SUMMARY: Aaron Sisson, a checkweighman and secretary to the Miners Union for a colliery in Nottinghamshire, and amateur flautist from the English Midlands, leaves his stale marriage and family to pursue a career as a professional musician in Italy. He befriends Rawdon Lilly, a Lawrence-like writer, who helps him recover from an illness in London. In Florence, Aaron joins intellectual circles, engages in political debates, and has an affair with an aristocratic lady. The novel ends with an anarchist or fascist explosion destroying Aaron's flute, symbolizing his lost potential.

Kangaroo (1923) is a ‘strongly auto-biographical’ novel set in Australia and follows Richard Lovat Somers, an English writer, and his German wife, Harriet, who travel to New South Wales in the early 1920s. The novel explores Richard’s engagement with two political movements: the right-wing Digger movement led by Benjamin Cooley (nicknamed “Kangaroo”) and the socialist movement led by Willie Struthers. Initially intrigued, Richard rejects both due to their extreme ideologies. The novel, written during Lawrence's stay in Thirroul, features vivid Australian landscapes and reflects on Richard and Harriet’s complex marriage, along with Lawrence’s disillusionment with fringe politics and his desire to distance himself from humanity’s entanglements. It was written in six and a half weeks during Lawrence and his wife Frieda’s eleven week stay in the NSW coastal town of Thirroul in 1922. The novel includes a chapter ("Nightmare").

The Boy in the Bush (1924), coauthored with M.L. (Mollie or Molly) Skinner. It is derived from a story in a manuscript given to Lawrence by Mollie Skinner, entitled The House of Ellis. The story follows Jack Grant, a young Englishman who arrives in Australia after being expelled from his public school and agricultural college. He navigates the rugged Australian landscape, engaging in activities like hunting, farming, and gold mining.

The Plumed Serpent (1926) political novel. SUMMARY: After Easter, Irish tourist Kate Leslie and her American cousin Owen Rhys visit Mexico, where Kate is initially excited by a bullfight but leaves in disgust after witnessing the violence. She meets Mexican General Don Cipriano and later encounters political discussions about the Mexican Revolution. Kate also reads about a religious movement in Sayula led by Ramón Carrasco, who aspires to replace Christian worship with that of Aztec gods. Kate decides to stay in Mexico and explores the Men of Quetzalcoatl, founded by Ramón. Despite initially being wary of his plans to replace Christian symbols with Aztec ones, Kate begins to sympathize with Ramón. Cipriano proposes marriage to Kate, suggesting she become a goddess alongside him and Ramón. The new president’s unpopular policies lead to rebellion and church opposition against Ramón. WORKS:

He collaborated with Joseph Conrad on three novels, The Inheritors (1901), Romance (1903) and The Nature of a Crime (1924, although written much earlier). 

Inheritors: An Extravagant Story (1901) is a quasi-science fiction novel written before WWI, predicting themes of corruption and the 20th-century impact on British aristocracy. The "fourth dimension" metaphor depicts a shift from traditional values to modern expediency. Arthur, an aspiring writer, meets a woman claiming to be from the Fourth Dimension, aiming to "inherit the earth." She infiltrates his aristocratic family, gaining political influence and becoming increasingly desirable to Arthur. Arthur, compromising his ideals, joins the Fourth Dimensionists, who exploit his weaknesses for their agenda. He gets a job writing for a journal led by Fox, a Fourth Dimensionist, and becomes embroiled in power dynamics. Manipulated by the woman, Arthur believes he can impress her. Fox entrusts him with editorial power briefly, but Arthur chooses not to stop the presses, undermining Fox and betraying allies like Edward Churchill, Callan, and Fox. Realizing he was used and seeing no future, Arthur has a breakdown at his Club, known as "the one they got at."

Romance (1903) The story follows the young protagonist, John Kemp, who becomes entangled in a series of adventures after being falsely accused of a crime. He escapes to the Caribbean, where he encounters pirates, revolutionaries, and a beautiful woman named Seraphina.

The Nature of a Crime (1924) is a collaborative novel written and published in 1909 by authors Joseph Conrad and Ford Madox Ford. The text did not acquire acclaim until after Conrad's death in 1924, when Ford brought the text to light in his essay "Joseph Conrad: A Personal Remembrance". The Nature of a Crime is likely influenced by major events from the life of Joseph Conrad, most notably his suicide attempt made at the age of 20. This novel is a series of letters from an unnamed narrator, a lawyer, to an unnamed love interest. The narrator confesses to gambling away money entrusted to him by his client, Edward Burden, who is unaware of the misuse. As Edward’s wedding approaches, the narrator struggles with his impending indictment and expresses his hidden emotions and love for the married woman. The narrator reflects on life, God's relationship with man, gender dynamics, female empowerment, and suicide. He plans to commit suicide due to his unrequited love and the fear of imprisonment. However, he is ultimately saved from despair when Edward and his fiancée Miss Annie Averie announce a year-long honeymoon, giving him time to fix the financial issues. 

Ford's Tudor trilogy, The Fifth Queen, includes The Fifth Queen: And How She Came to Court (1906), Privy Seal (1907), and The Fifth Queen Crowned (1908), that presents a highly fictionalised account of Katharine Howard's arrival at the Court of Henry VIII, her eventual marriage to the king, and her death. Ford defined the trilogy as 'nothing more than a tour de force, a fake more or less genuine in inspiration and workmanship, but none the less a fake'. Joseph Conrad described it as "the swansong of the historical romance"

PLOT SUMMARY OF TRILOGY: The Fifth Queen trilogy features an omniscient narrator. Katharine Howard, a devout, impoverished young noblewoman, is introduced alongside her fiery cousin, Thomas Culpeper. By chance, she catches the attention of King Henry VIII. With help from her old Latin tutor and spy for Thomas Cromwell, Nicholas Udal, she becomes a lady-in-waiting for Lady Mary. In Henry VIII's court, Katharine navigates a web of calculating and spying members, rising in prominence. She becomes involved with the King and is used by Cromwell, Bishop Gardiner, Thomas Cranmer, and Nicholas Throckmorton. Her association with Throckmorton, suspected of complicity in Wyatt's Rebellion, briefly implicates her in January 1554. Katharine's forthrightness, devotion to the Old Faith, learning, youth, and beauty attract the King, contrasting with historical views of her as flighty and flirtatious.

First Novel: Katharine Howard, a devout young Catholic noblewoman in her mid-teens, arrives in London with her cousin, Thomas Culpepper. Henry VIII is disappointed with his new wife, Anne of Cleves, and takes notice of Katharine's forthrightness and devotion to the Catholic Church. Despite her past with Culpepper, she gains favor at court. The novel portrays the chaos in England with Catholics and Protestants clashing, and Thomas Cromwell plotting behind the scenes. Katharine gradually gains acceptance in court amid the political and religious turmoil.

Second Novel: Focuses on Thomas Cromwell's plotting to derail Katharine Howard’s rise at court. Cromwell is aware of Henry's dissatisfaction with Anne of Cleves and aims to prevent Katharine, a Catholic, from becoming queen. Despite his efforts, Katharine wins Henry’s heart through her goodness and innocence, not guile. The novel highlights the political machinations and espionage within Henry's court.

Third Novel: Chronicles the plot to remove Katharine Howard, now queen, orchestrated by Thomas Cranmer after Cromwell’s execution. Katharine’s saintly character and attempts to reconcile Lady Mary with her family contrast with historical accounts. Her kindness and innocence make her less interesting and historically inaccurate. The struggle between Catholicism and Protestantism continues, with even her Catholic supporters turning against her. The novel culminates in Katharine’s tragic downfall, painting her as an overly idealized figure.

Ladies Whose Bright Eyes (1911) initially published under "Ford Madox Hueffer" and later as "Ford Madox Ford." The novel was inspired by Mark Twain’s A Connecticut Yankee in King Arthur’s Court and reflects Ford’s personal circumstances, including his attempt to divorce his wife, Elsie Martindale Hueffer, to marry Violet Hunt. Mr. Sorrell, a modern man, is transported to the Middle Ages. Unlike Twain's Hank Morgan, Sorrell makes only a half-hearted effort to introduce modern technology like guns or printing. His attempts fail due to his lack of practical knowledge. Instead, his golden cross causes him to be mistaken for a Greek miracle-worker, granting him hospitality and romantic attention from medieval women. Sorrell's presence inspires medieval ladies to engage in tournaments, and he himself tries to become a knight. The novel contrasts the romanticized medieval past with its more mundane and power-driven aspects, depicting cathedrals as crowded and commercial rather than serene. Just as Sorrell adapts to his medieval life, he is abruptly returned to the twentieth century, having gained insight and wisdom from his historical experience.

The Good Soldier: A Tale of Passion (1915) set before World War I. It tells the tragic story of Edward Ashburnham and his seemingly perfect marriage, using flashbacks and a non-chronological narrative. The unreliable narrator reveals a different reality than expected. Loosely based on Ford's own experiences with adultery, the novel's original title was The Saddest Story, changed to The Good Soldier during World War I. Narrated by John Dowell, follows the dissolving relationships of two couples: John and Florence Dowell, and Edward and Leonora Ashburnham. The novel begins with the line: "This is the saddest story I have ever heard." For nine years, the couples maintain a facade of friendship while seeking treatment for heart ailments at a spa. Florence’s ailment is a pretense to avoid intimacy with John, allowing her to have an affair with an artist named Jimmy. Edward's marriage to Leonora is marred by his infidelities and her control over their finances. Florence commits suicide upon realizing Edward’s growing affection for their ward, Nancy, and that John has discovered her affair. Edward, tormented by his love for Nancy and unable to maintain chastity, sends her to India. Leonora sabotages Edward's last hope by revealing his sordid past to Nancy, who then becomes catatonic upon learning of his death. The novel ends with Dowell caring for Nancy in Edward's estate, lamenting that no one got what they wanted. He sympathizes with Edward, seeing himself as a disengaged voyeur who failed to participate in life.

In the "Dedicatory Letter to Stella Ford" that prefaces the novel, Ford reports that a friend pronounced The Good Soldier "the finest French novel in the English language!"

His tetralogy, Parade's End (1924 to 1928) includes Some Do Not (1924), No More Parades (1925), A Man Could Stand Up (1926), and Last Post (1928). The novels chronicle the life of Christopher Tietjens, "the last Tory", a brilliant government statistician from a wealthy landowning family who serves in the British Army during the First World War. His wife Sylvia is a flippant socialite who seems intent on ruining him through her sexual promiscuity. Tietjens may or may not be the father of his wife's child. Meanwhile, his incipient affair with Valentine Wannop, a high-spirited pacifist and women's suffragist, has not been consummated, despite what all their friends believe. The setting is mainly England and the Western Front of the First World War, in which Ford had served as an officer in the Welch Regiment, a life he vividly depicts. the books were combined into one volume as Parade's End in 1950.

PLOT SUMMARY OF THIS TETROLOGY:

Some Do Not (1924) follows Christopher Tietjens and Vincent Macmaster on a transformative weekend in Rye around 1912. Tietjens, a sharp-witted government statistician, grapples with his disastrous marriage to Sylvia, who plans to return to him after an affair. Macmaster, aspiring to be a critic, becomes infatuated with Mrs. Duchemin during a visit to her home. The weekend includes a round of golf with General Campion and a chaotic encounter with Suffragettes, where Tietjens meets Valentine Wannop. They fall in love during a night drive while hiding a Suffragette.

Part II, set during World War I, sees Tietjens back in London, recovering from shell shock. He helps Mrs. Wannop write propaganda articles, while Sylvia suspects an affair between Tietjens and Valentine. Macmaster, now married to Mrs. Duchemin (Mr. Duchemin having died), hosts literary parties, maintaining secrecy about his marriage. Tietjens confronts his brother Mark about rumors damaging his reputation. Valentine tries to reconcile the brothers and plans to meet Tietjens before he returns to war, but their meeting doesn't happen. The novel ends with Tietjens reflecting on the day and heading back to the front, facing probable death.

The cancelled ending, published later, depicts Sylvia's failed attempt to seduce Tietjens and her retreat to a convent, which Tietjens recalls in the next novel, No More Parades.

No More Parades (1925) Captain Christopher Tietjens, with Sergeant-Major Cowley, struggles to move 2,994 troops from Rouen to the front amid countermanded orders, inadequate supplies, a French railway strike, and British Garrison Police interference. Shell-shocked Captain McKechnie complicates matters. A German shelling kills O Nine Morgan. Tietjens reflects on his unfaithful wife, Sylvia, after the "All-clear" signal. He distracts McKechnie by writing a sonnet. Colonel Levin arrives to inform Tietjens that Sylvia has come to see him, reminding him of his love for Valentine Wannop. Tietjens, disgusted by Levin's help offer, recalls Sylvia's departure to a convent, thinking it ended their marriage and freed him for Valentine. Sylvia leaves without speaking to him. Tietjens settles his troops for the night, handling police harassment of Canadian volunteers and other issues. Despite excelling in his duties, Tietjens faces inevitable deployment to the front, foreshadowed by Morgan's death.

A Man Could Stand Up (1926) Part I On Armistice Day, November 11, 1918, Valentine Wannop, at her school, is interrupted by a call from Edith Ethel Duchemin (now Lady Macmaster) informing her that Christopher Tietjens is back in London and needs help. Despite the call’s malicious undertones and background noise, Valentine decides to reunite with Tietjens if he still wants her. Part II The scene shifts to the front in April 1918, where Tietjens, amidst less intense bombardments compared to previous novels, reflects on the death of O Nine Morgan and his evolving character. He contemplates retreating from his burdens to live with Valentine and pursue a new life selling antiques. Part III Valentine visits Gray's Inn to meet Tietjens, but Mrs. Wannop, Christopher's father’s old friend’s wife, tries to manipulate them into staying apart. As Valentine learns about Tietjens' war experiences from his unit members, and Tietjens reveals his psychological struggles to Mrs. Wannop, their reunion culminates in a charged, celebratory dance reflecting the tumult of Armistice Day.

Last Post (1928) Set in the post-WWI years, Last Post explores the aftermath of the conflict and the shattered certainties of the time. Part I Christopher Tietjens, now an antiques dealer, lives with Valentine Wannop, his brother Mark, and Mark's French wife, Marie Léonie, in West Sussex. The first part delves into their lives, highlighting past incidents and evolving interpretations. Part II The focus shifts to threats facing the family: Christopher's estranged wife Sylvia, their son, and an American tenant at Groby. Mark's perspective dominates, as he is bedridden in an outdoor shelter, while other viewpoints include Marie Léonie, Valentine, and Sylvia. The novel examines Sylvia's ongoing malice, which disrupts the Tietjens' lives, including financial woes due to Christopher's partner and a feud with Mark, fueled by Sylvia’s false claims. Christopher's absence, as he tries to resolve these issues in York, is felt through the perspectives of others. The novel concludes with Sylvia's retreat, a shift in her attitude, and the poignant death of Mark Tietjens.

NOVELS:

Tarr (1918) first appeared in serial form in The Egoist in 1916-1917. Set in the Bohemianism milieu of pre-war Paris, it presents two artists, the Englishman Tarr and the German Kreisler, and their struggles with money, women and various art and social situations. Tarr, possibly based on the author Lewis, criticizes fellow artists and mistreats his German not-quite-fiancée Bertha, to whom he is cruel and deceitful. Kreisler, even worse, is an untalented artist who refuses to return to Germany, sponges money from others, and behaves atrociously at social events. After receiving an ultimatum from his father to return home, Kreisler meets Tarr, who attempts to befriend him. However, Kreisler rebuffs Tarr and ultimately victimizes Bertha, already mistreated by Tarr, leading to a tragic conclusion.

The Childermass (1928) first of intended tetralogy, The Human Age, of which two more volumes appeared long afterwards in 1955 - Monstre Gai and Malign Fiesta. The trilogy is set in a fantastic region beyond the grave. Two pilgrims from earth, Pullman and Satters, are present at an absurd tribunal outside a city, where a grotesque examiner and his rebellious examinees confront each other with nightmare dialectic. The men get inside the city in Monstre Gai to discover that it is under attack from Hell, whither they flee in Malign Fiesta.

The Apes of God (1930) The novel The Apes of God is a satire of London's 1926 literary and artistic scene, targeting figures like the Sitwells, Gertrude Stein, James Joyce, Virginia Woolf, and the Bloomsbury group. It follows the simpleton Dan Boleyn as he navigates the art world under the misguided guidance of Horace Zagreus, an infatuated sixty-year-old albino who mistakenly believes Dan is a genius. The "Apes of God" are superficial imitators of true creators, characterized as prosperous frauds. Zagreus himself is merely imitating Pierpoint, a painter-turned-philosopher who never appears in the novel but is the source of all societal ideas depicted. The novel's longest chapter, 'Lord Osmund's Lenten Party,' parodies a fancy-dress party held by the Sitwell-like Finnian Shaw family. The novel's critique of England builds on Lewis's ideas from his 1926 book, The Art of Being Ruled.

Snooty Baronet (1932)

The Revenge for Love (1937) The novel is set in Spain during the Spanish Civil War and England. Percy Hardcaster, a communist propagandist, is imprisoned in Spain after being caught with dynamite. He plans an escape with help from a guard, but it is foiled, resulting in the guard's death and Hardcaster's injury. Later, Hardcaster, now with a wooden leg, is released and returns to England, where Sean O’Hara hosts a party in his honor. The party introduces various characters, including the talentless painter Victor Stamp and his wife, Margot, who struggle financially and become targets for shady schemes involving art forgery and political intrigue related to Spain. Jack Druze, an accountant fascinated by the art world, helps artist Tristram Phipps with his finances and becomes infatuated with Tristram’s wife, Gillian. Tristram is a naive and struggling artist, also dragged into the forgery plot. The story satirizes talentless artists, political activists, and the bourgeois, with characters like Margot and her friend Agnes showcasing their ignorance and superficiality. The novel, much like The Apes of God, offers brutal satire and a plot intertwined with the murky politics of the Spanish Civil War.

The Vulgar Streak (1941) Lewis stated that this novel explores ruthlessness and excessive willpower, traits found in Napoleon’s and Hitler’s armies, and in literary characters like Julien Sorel and Raskolnikov. Vincent Penhale, the protagonist, shares with these characters a desire to escape his lower-class roots. Set in September 1938 during Chamberlain's negotiations with Hitler, the story begins with Penhale in Venice with his friend Martin Penny-Smythe. They meet April Marrow and her mother, and Penhale gets engaged to April despite Mrs. Marrow's reluctance due to his mysterious background. Although Penhale appears middle-class, we learn he is actually from a working-class family, having adopted a middle-class accent and manners through effort. He is an actor and dress designer who inherited suspicious money. Throughout the story, his shady past and questionable actions surface. Back in London, Penhale visits his working-class family, except for his sister Madeleine, whom he tries to help ascend socially. The novel highlights the rigid British class system and the challenges of escaping one's class. Penhale's deceit and struggle with his true identity are central themes. Critics have mixed views on the novel, which, while more conventional in style, is not considered one of Lewis's greatest works but is among his most readable.

Waugh received the James Tait Black Memorial Prize for Men at Arms in 1952.

NOVELS

The Temple at Thatch was an unpublished novel. After his friend Harold Acton commented unfavourably he burned the manuscript. The story was evidently semi-autobiographical, based on Waugh's Oxford experiences. The protagonist was an undergraduate and the work's main themes were madness and black magic.

Decline and Fall (1928) first published novel. The novel's title is a contraction of Edward Gibbon's The History of the Decline and Fall of the Roman Empire. The title alludes also to the German philosopher Oswald Spengler's The Decline of the West. The novel was dedicated to Harold Acton, "in homage and affection". The novel is a social satire that uses black humor to critique British society in the 1920s. Paul Pennyfeather, a modest theology student, is expelled from Oxford after a prank by the Bollinger Club. This forces him to teach at a private school in Wales, Llanabba, run by Dr. Fagan, where he finds the other teachers to be failures. Paul falls for Margot Beste-Chetwynde, a wealthy widow, and becomes her son's tutor. Unaware of her illicit activities, he agrees to help her by flying to Marseille to bribe police. His college friend Potts, now an investigator, shadows him. Paul is arrested on his wedding day for his involvement and sentenced to seven years in prison, where he meets former Llanabba staff. Margot marries a minister who arranges Paul's escape from prison, faking his death. Paul then resumes his studies at Oxford, disguised and using a different identity. The novel ends with him listening to the Bollinger Club's shouts, just as it began.

Vile Bodies (1930) It satirises the bright young things, the rich young people partying in London after World War I. Original title Bright Young Things. The book was dedicated Bryan Guinness and his wife Diana. Adam Symes plans to marry Nina Blount but his manuscript is seized by customs. His friend Agatha Runcible is strip-searched, sparking a media frenzy. Adam informs Nina they can't marry and negotiates a new contract with his publisher. Winning £1,000, Adam gives it to a drunk major to bet, but the major vanishes. At a party, Adam, Nina, and Agatha visit the Prime Minister's daughter's home. Agatha's expulsion leads to a scandal, toppling the government. Nina's father offers Adam a £1,000 cheque, but it's signed by Charlie Chaplin. At Margot Metroland's party, Simon Balcairn, a gossip columnist, crashes it and later commits suicide. Adam takes his job, inventing stories. Nina's friend Ginger Littlejohn becomes a dashing figure in his columns. Adam seeks money from Nina's father but fails. While Adam is away, his friends write his column, leading to his dismissal. At motor races, he sees the major with his winnings, who then disappears. Agatha crashes and later dies. Nina gets engaged to Ginger; Adam agrees if Ginger pays him £100. Ginger and Nina honeymoon in France, but Ginger can't join Nina for Christmas. Adam pretends to be her husband. War breaks out, and Adam finds himself on a battlefield in France, where he meets the major, now a general, with his winnings. They drink champagne with an evangelist's angel while Adam falls asleep.

Black Mischief (1932) expanded from a novella, 'Seth'. The novel is dedicated to the Lygon sisters. Editor of the Catholic journal The Tablet, Ernest Oldmeadow, launched a violent attack: the novel was "a disgrace to anybody professing the Catholic name". The novel chronicles the efforts of the British-educated Emperor Seth, assisted by a fellow Oxford graduate, Basil Seal, to modernise his Empire, the fictional African island of Azania, located in the Indian Ocean off the eastern coast of Africa. After winning a civil war and losing his father to cannibalistic soldiers, Seth, the Oxford-educated emperor of Azania, aims to modernize his country. He enlists his college friend Basil Seal to head the Ministry of Modernization, with help from Armenian shopkeeper Krikor Yokoumian. Basil integrates into Debra Dowa's society, mingling with various officials like General Connolly, Viscount Boaz, the Earl of Ngumo, and British diplomat Sir Samson Courteney. The French consul, Monsieur Ballon, suspects British schemes and plots against Seth. Despite rapid modernization efforts, Seth's launch of a new currency triggers his downfall. Ballon orchestrates a coup during a "Birth Control Parade," replacing Seth with his senile uncle Achon, who dies soon after coronation. Seth, hiding, is killed by his own Minister, who is then killed on Basil's orders. At Seth's funeral, Basil unknowingly consumes his girlfriend Prudence Courteney, whose plane crashed during evacuation. With no heir, the League of Nations takes over Azania. Basil returns to England, now a serious and changed man.

A Handful of Dust (1934) a satirical novel. Tony Last, an English country squire, joins an expedition to the Brazilian jungle after his illusions are shattered. He becomes the prisoner of a maniac in a remote outpost. Waugh incorporated autobiographical elements from his recent desertion by his wife and his travels to South America. Tony lives with his wife, Brenda, and their son, John Andrew, at Hetton Abbey. Tony is content, but Brenda is bored and begins an affair with John Beaver. Brenda spends time in London and convinces Tony to finance a flat. When John Andrew dies in a riding accident, Brenda, initially thinking John Beaver died, reveals her true feelings. She asks Tony for a divorce to marry Beaver, demanding £2,000 a year, which would force Tony to sell Hetton. Devastated, Tony refuses and decides to travel. Tony meets explorer Dr. Messinger and joins an expedition in the Amazon rainforest. Messinger proves incompetent, and the natives abandon them. Messinger is killed seeking help, leaving Tony ill and stranded. He is rescued by Mr. Todd, who forces Tony to read Dickens to him indefinitely. In England, Tony is presumed dead. His cousins inherit Hetton Abbey and erect a memorial, while Brenda marries Tony's friend. (Tony's fate in the jungle was first used by Waugh in a short story, published in 1933 under the title "The Man Who Liked Dickens").

Brideshead Revisited: The Sacred and Profane Memories of Captain Charles Ryder (1945) It follows, from the 1920s to the early 1940s, the life and romances of Charles Ryder, especially his friendship with the Flytes, a family of wealthy English Catholics who live in a palatial mansion, Brideshead Castle. Ryder has relationships with two of the Flytes: Lord Sebastian and Lady Julia. The novel explores themes including Catholicism and nostalgia for the age of English aristocracy. The novel is divided into three parts, framed by a prologue and epilogue. Prologue: During WWII, Charles Ryder and his battalion are stationed at Brideshead, prompting his memories. Et In Arcadia Ego: In 1923, Charles, an Oxford history student, befriends Lord Sebastian Flyte, the younger son of Marquess of Marchmain. Charles meets Sebastian's eccentric friends and family at Brideshead Castle, including his sister Julia and the haughty Anthony Blanche. Sebastian's Catholic family surprises the agnostic Charles. Brideshead Deserted: Sebastian's drinking problem worsens, and Charles falls out with Lady Marchmain, leaving Brideshead. Julia marries Canadian businessman Rex Mottram, causing family sorrow due to his divorcee status. Sebastian drifts into alcoholism and poor health in Morocco, where Charles finds him in a monastery but too ill to return. Charles returns to London, paints Marchmain House, and learns more about the Flyte family from Cordelia. A Twitch Upon the Thread: Charles becomes a successful architectural painter, marries Celia, and fathers two children, but their marriage deteriorates. Julia separates from Rex, and she and Charles plan to marry. Cordelia brings news of Sebastian's decline. Lord Marchmain returns to Brideshead to die, names Julia heir, but his deathbed return to faith leads Julia to end her relationship with Charles. Epilogue: In 1943 or 1944, Charles, now a loveless army officer, is stationed at a damaged Brideshead. The chapel has been reopened for soldiers, symbolizing enduring faith and efforts.

Sword of Honour (1952–1961) Second World War trilogy. Three novels are: Men at Arms (1952), Officers and Gentlemen (1955) and Unconditional Surrender (1961). SUMMARIES: Men at Arms (1952) Guy Crouchback, heir of a declining aristocratic English Roman Catholic family, returns to England at the start of WWII after his failed marriage and years spent in Italy. He believes that the evils of modernity, evident in the Soviet Union and Nazi Germany, now clearly present a real enemy. He joins the Royal Corps of Halberdiers and trains as an officer, experiencing chaotic postings around Britain. Guy meets Brigadier Ben Ritchie-Hook, a fiery officer, and Apthorpe, an eccentric fellow officer. Guy observes their humorous conflict over a portable toilet. Before active service, Guy unsuccessfully tries to seduce his ex-wife Virginia. He and Ritchie-Hook then participate in the Dakar Expedition. Apthorpe dies in Freetown, and Guy, implicated in his death due to a gift of whisky, is sent home in disgrace. Officers and Gentlemen (1955) After being sent back to the UK in disgrace, Guy Crouchback joins a commando brigade training in Scotland under his old friend Tommy Blackhouse, the man for whom his wife Virginia left him. Among the trainees is Ivor Claire, whom Guy admires. With help from Colonel "Jumbo" Trotter, Guy learns to navigate military protocols. He is posted to Cairo and becomes involved in the chaotic evacuation of Crete, where he meets Corporal-Major Ludovic. They escape by boat but run out of fuel, and the captain mysteriously disappears, likely due to Ludovic. They eventually reach Egypt, and Ludovic, seen as a hero, is promoted. Recovering in hospital, Guy is aided by Mrs. Stitch, who tries to protect Claire from desertion charges. She arranges for Guy's extended return to England to avoid compromising Claire's cover story. Back in England, Guy seeks a new role. Unconditional Surrender (1961) The title is a reference to the Allied demand for an Unconditional Surrender of the Axis, made in the same period in which the plot is set, but which gets an ironical meaning when applied to the protagonist's tangled personal life. The novel has also been published under the title The End of the Battle. Guy Crouchback spends 1941-1943 in Britain, mostly in desk jobs. He turns 40 and feels the futility of the war as Britain allies with the Soviet Union. Virginia, now impoverished, becomes pregnant by Trimmer, a former hairdresser turned media war hero, and seeks an abortion but then decides to find a husband instead. Guy is chosen for parachute training, commanded by the paranoid Ludovic, who fears exposure for past crimes. Injured during training, Guy contacts Jumbo Trotter and returns to live with his Uncle Peregrine. Now financially secure after his father's death, Guy remarries Virginia. Virginia, living with Uncle Peregrine, gives birth to Trimmer's son, Gervase. Guy is posted to Yugoslavia, where he is disillusioned by the Partisans and learns of his friend's Communist loyalties. While Guy is abroad, a German doodlebug kills Peregrine and Virginia, but spares Gervase. Guy attempts acts of salvation in Yugoslavia, which backfire. Post-war, he marries Domenica Plessington, but they remain childless in the revised ending, though some versions mention them having two sons.

Henry Graham Greene (1904 – 1991) (Taken from Azra’s notes 20th Century 30s and 40s)

English novelist. Greene was awarded the 1968 Shakespeare Prize and the 1981 Jerusalem Prize.

William Golding called Greene "the ultimate chronicler of twentieth-century man's consciousness and anxiety" 

He was literary editor for "The Spectator" in London in 1940—41

He began his career as a novelist with "Babbling April" in 1926 but did not gain recognition till his fourth novel, "Stamboul Train" (1932) which he called an "entertainment". He originally divided his fiction into two genres which he described as "entertainments" and "novels". The last book termed an entertainment was Our Man in Havana  

The major event in his life was his conversion to Roman Catholicism in 1927.

Greene describes Virginia Woolf and E.M. Forster as characters who wander "like cardboard symbols through a world that is paper-thin."

He shaped the Catholic novel, influenced by François Mauriac's theme of "La misère de l'homme sans Dieu." In a review of Mauriac's La Pharisienne, reprinted in Collected Essays (1969), Greene lamented that "with the death of James, the religious sense was lost to the English novel."

His The Power and the Glory won the 1941 Hawthornden Prize

His The Heart of the Matter won the 1948 James Tait Black Memorial Prize.

In 1986, he was awarded Britain's Order of Merit.

Made several suicide attempts by Russian roulette and by taking aspirin before going swimming in the school pool.

NOVELS

The Man Within (1929) first novel. The title is taken from a sentence in Thomas Browne's Religio Medici: “There's another man within me that's angry with me”. Derides the book as hopelessly romantic. About Francis Andrews, a reluctant smuggler, who betrays his colleagues, and the aftermath of his betrayal. Andrews flees from smugglers after a battle with customs officials. He finds refuge with Elizabeth, a woman living alone after her partner's recent death. Despite local gossip about Elizabeth's character, Andrews decides to testify against the smugglers at their trial in Lewes, where he is scorned as a traitor. The smugglers are acquitted and vow revenge on Andrews, targeting Elizabeth. Upon returning to the cottage, Andrews learns that Elizabeth has committed suicide after being attacked. Carlyon, the smuggler leader, confronts Andrews, who chooses to take the blame for Elizabeth's death to protect his father and tells Carlyon to leave.

Stamboul Train (1932) Set on a train journey from Ostend to Istanbul. Was renamed Orient Express when it was published in the United States. Classed as an "entertainment". The epigraph given is from George Santayana. Portrayal of antisemitism. The novel centers on passengers aboard a luxury train traveling from Ostend to Istanbul. Mabel Warren and Janet Pardoe board in Cologne, while Josef Grünlich joins in Vienna. Carleton Myatt, a businessman heading to Istanbul to address concerns about his agent, faces antisemitism and, feeling sympathy for the ailing dancer Coral Musker, upgrades her to first class. Coral falls in love with Myatt and disappears at Subotica, leading Myatt to attempt a rescue, which fails, and he narrowly escapes after rescuing Grünlich. Dr. Czinner, an escaped communist leader with a forged British passport, plans a failed revolution in Belgrade. He is recognized by Warren, who is stranded in Vienna after her bag is stolen by Grünlich. At the Yugoslav border, Czinner is arrested, along with Grünlich and Coral. During a court martial, Czinner is sentenced to death, Grünlich to imprisonment, and Coral to deportation. Grünlich escapes, while Czinner is wounded and dies in hiding with Coral’s help. Warren eventually returns to Cologne with Coral, who collapses from a heart condition. Upon arrival in Istanbul, Myatt discovers that Janet Pardoe is the niece of rival businessman Stein and plans to marry her, securing a business deal and confirming his contract to take over Stein’s currant business, despite his brief encounter with Coral and the literary aspirations of Cockney novelist Savory. (J. B. Priestley, suspecting that Savory was based on him, threatened a libel suit. As a result, Greene had to revise and replace about twenty pages of the book)

It's a Battlefield (1934) described it as his "first overtly political novel". Its theme, said Greene, is "the injustice of man's justice." He ranked it as a “novel”. The title It's a Battlefield is explained by the epigraph, which Greene took from the account of the battle of Inkerman in Alexander Kinglake's The Invasion of the Crimea. The novel follows the intersecting lives surrounding bus driver Drover, who is scheduled to hang after knifing a policeman during a demonstration. Drover's Communist colleagues want him to die to gain party support, while his wife Milly begins an affair with Drover’s brother Conrad, who is tormented by guilt. The Assistant Commissioner of Metropolitan Police, newly appointed after working in the Far East, is involved in deciding whether Drover should be reprieved. Milly visits the policeman's widow, and her sister Kay has affairs with Surrogate, a Communist economist, and Jules, a worker at a Soho café. Both Surrogate and the Assistant Commissioner seek the help of society hostess Caroline Bury. Conrad, feeling compelled to act, attempts to blackmail a pawnbroker for a revolver and shoots at the Assistant Commissioner with blanks before being hit by a car. Unbeknownst to everyone, the Home Secretary has already granted Drover a reprieve. (The character named Surrogate was modelled after John Middleton Murry. Lady Caroline Bury was inspired by Lady Ottoline Morrell.)

England Made Me (1935) was republished as The Shipwrecked. Set in Stockholm, the novel follows Anthony Farrant, a charming but failed bodyguard working for the dubious financier Krogh, inspired by Ivar Kreuger. Anthony, in his thirties, charming and broke, is back in London from the Far East after being fired from every job, kicked out of every club and left by every woman. His twin sister Kate comes over from Stockholm to rescue him. She is secretary and mistress to Krogh, a self-made millionaire who owns a multinational financial empire. Despite Kate's hopes, Anthony fails both her and Krogh by neglecting his duties and chasing women. When he betrays Krogh by leaking private information to the press, Krogh’s old associate Hall kills Anthony and disposes of the body. Kate, now without her brother, lover, or job, decides to move on to Copenhagen, while Krogh's fraudulent empire persists.

A Gun for Sale (1936) The novel was published and filmed in the United States under the title This Gun for Hire. Raven, an English assassin, is hired to kill a government minister in Czechoslovakia to provoke a European war. After completing the job, he returns to London and receives his payment in stolen cash from his contact, "Cholmondeley." Discovering the deception, Raven is furious and hunted by the police who believe he is the thief. Without a ticket, he follows Cholmondeley onto a train heading to Nottwich. On the train, Raven encounters Anne Crowder, a chorus girl traveling to start work in a pantomime. Her fiancé, Mather, is the detective leading the search for Raven. At Nottwich, Raven uses Anne’s ticket to disembark and, recognizing him, Anne is taken to an empty house where Raven plans to kill her. However, Anne escapes and chooses not to report the incident out of sympathy for Raven, instead going to the theatre to start her job. At the theatre, Anne is courted by Davis, who is revealed to be Cholmondeley. After dinner, Davis takes Anne to a house and, realizing she knows too much, smothers her, though he does not kill her. The house owner steals Anne's handbag, which Raven later finds her with when he breaks into the house. Anne, weakened but alive, agrees to cooperate with Raven to get revenge on Davis/Cholmondeley. While in hiding, Raven and Anne discuss Raven's past, including his mother’s suicide, providing context for his actions and foreshadowing his own end. The police, led by Mather, surround their hideout, but Anne creates a diversion allowing Raven to escape. He disguises himself as a student participating in a gas-mask drill to evade capture. Disguised, Raven locates Davis, who is unmasked as Sir Marcus, a corrupt industrialist. Raven forces Davis and Marcus to his hideout, explains his motives, and kills them. Raven is then shot and killed by the police, ending his violent quest for revenge. 

Brighton Rock (1938) a murder thriller set in 1930s Brighton. There is an incidental link between this novel A Gun for Sale, as the murder of gang boss Kite, mentioned in A Gun For Sale, allows the seventeen-year-old sociopath Pinkie to take over his gang, setting the events of Brighton Rock in motion. Kite's murder, caused by a report from Charles "Fred" Hale in the Daily Messenger, leads Hale to Brighton to distribute cards for a newspaper competition. Realizing Pinkie's mob is hunting him, Hale meets Ida Arnold by chance in a pub and on the Palace Pier but is snatched away without her noticing. To confuse the police, Pinkie has Spicer distribute Hale's cards and tries to retrieve one from the café where sixteen-year-old Rose works. Since Rose saw Spicer, Pinkie courts her to marry her and prevent her from testifying against him, though he despises her. In London, Ida reads about Hale's death, deemed heart failure, and goes to Brighton to investigate, recruiting help and financing her trip with a lucky bet. Her inquiries uncover the story despite Rose warning Pinkie. Pinkie meets mobster Colleoni, pretending to reconsider joining him to get Colleoni to murder Spicer. Both escape a razor attack at the races, but Pinkie later kills Spicer and implicates solicitor Prewitt in the cover-up. Prewitt helps Pinkie and Rose marry, and Pinkie learns Rose knew of his crimes but didn't mind. Feeling trapped, Pinkie proposes a fake suicide pact to Rose. Ida tails them with a policeman, sensing danger. Pinkie's attack misfires, blinding him, and he runs off a cliff to his death. Rose later confesses she wanted to follow Pinkie into damnation. The priest tells her that Pinkie's love shows there was good in him. She looks forward to playing a recording from their wedding, unaware it contains his declaration of hatred.

The Power and the Glory (1940) also published as The Labyrinthine Ways. Title is an allusion to the doxology often recited at the end of the Lord's Prayer: "For thine is the kingdom, the power, and the glory, forever and ever, amen." About a renegade Catholic 'whisky priest' (a term coined by Greene) living in the Mexican state of Tabasco in the 1930s, a time when the Mexican government was attempting to suppress the Catholic Church. During the period when Catholicism was outlawed in Mexico, Tabasco enforced the ban rigorously, while many other states followed a don't-ask-don't-tell policy. In Tabasco, priests were forced to renounce their faith, marry, and receive pensions, or face execution if they refused. The story follows a fugitive priest who returns to his former parish town after years and tries to minister to the marginalized people despite many challenges, including prohibition hindering the Mass. The priest, a "whisky priest" with personal failings, meets his daughter but feels no repentance, only a deep love and a desire to save her from damnation. His main antagonist is a morally strict police lieutenant who takes hostages and shoots them if the priest is sheltered. The lieutenant's pursuit is partly fueled by bad experiences with the church in his youth. Despite feeling unworthy, the priest remains on the run to serve the poor. To avoid the lieutenant's wrath, he attempts to flee to a neighboring state. He twice encounters the lieutenant but isn't recognized. Near death after a perilous journey, he is nursed back to health by Protestant landowners and plans to reach the local capital. The priest meets a mestizo, a Judas figure, who lures him to hear the confession of a dying man over the border, suspecting a trap but feeling duty-bound. He is captured by the lieutenant, who, despite admiring him, insists on his execution. On the eve of the execution, the lieutenant tries to enlist the renegade Padre José to hear the priest's confession, but José's wife thwarts the effort. The lieutenant believes he has eradicated all priests, but in the final chapter, a new covert priest arrives. A faithful Catholic woman, previously shown reading pious tracts to her children, agrees to harbor the new priest, adding the protagonist to her repertoire of Christian martyrs.

The Heart of the Matter (1948) The book's title appears halfway through the novel: "If one knew, he wondered, the facts, would one have to feel pity even for the planets? If one reached what they called the heart of the matter?". Major Scobie lives on the west coast of Africa during WWII, responsible for local security. His wife Louise, unhappy and solitary, loves literature but struggles to make friends. Scobie feels responsible for her misery but does not love her. Their only child, Catherine, died years earlier. Both are devout Catholics. The oppressive climate adds to the novel's tension. Scobie is passed over for promotion, upsetting Louise, who then asks to move to South Africa. Meanwhile, a new inspector, Wilson, arrives. He and Louise form a friendship, which Wilson mistakes for love. Scobie inspects local ships for smuggled diamonds. On a Portuguese ship, he finds a letter addressed to Germany, confiscates it, but destroys it after finding it innocuous. Scobie is called to investigate the suicide of Inspector Pemberton, suspecting involvement of a black marketeer named Yusef. Yusef denies it but warns of a new inspector searching for diamonds. Scobie dreams of suicide but dismisses it due to the damnation it would bring. Unable to secure a loan for Louise's passage to South Africa, Scobie accepts money from Yusef. After a shipwreck, survivors arrive, including Helen Rolt, whom Scobie feels drawn to. They begin an affair, despite his guilt over committing adultery. Yusef blackmails Scobie with a letter to Helen, forcing him to smuggle diamonds. Louise returns, and Scobie struggles to hide the affair. Unable to confess, he commits sacrilege by receiving communion. Yusef's servant delivers a "gift" to Scobie, which he refuses. Scobie's servant, Ali, witnesses the affair and the gift. Yusef arranges Ali's murder, which Scobie blames himself for. Seeing no way out, Scobie fakes a heart ailment, gets sleeping pills, and commits suicide, knowing it means damnation. Helen continues her dreary existence. Louise, realizing Scobie's death was a suicide, refuses to marry Wilson but remains uncertain. In the final chapter, Louise discusses Scobie's suicide with a priest, who advises that no one can know what is in a person's heart or about God's mercy.

The End of the Affair (1951) is the fourth and final of Greene's "Catholic novels" tetralogy, following Brighton Rock, The Power and the Glory, and The Heart of the Matter. Greene's own affair with Catherine Walston formed the basis for this novel. The British edition of the novel is dedicated to "C" while the American version is made out to "Catherine". The novel centers on Maurice Bendrix, a writer in wartime London, and Sarah Miles, the wife of a civil servant. Bendrix, based on Greene himself, reflects on writing, while Sarah is modeled after Greene's lover, Catherine Walston. Bendrix and Sarah's love affair is intense but troubled by his jealousy and her refusal to divorce her husband, Henry. After a bomb nearly kills Bendrix during a tryst, Sarah abruptly ends the affair. Wracked with jealousy, Bendrix hires a detective to uncover Sarah's new lover and discovers through her diary that she had vowed to God to end the affair if Bendrix survived the bombing. Sarah's faith and struggles are detailed, and after her sudden death, miraculous events suggest a deeper meaning to her beliefs. By the novel's end, Bendrix also comes to believe in God.

The Quiet American (1955)

Our Man in Havana (1958) novel set in Cuba. uses the novel to mock intelligence services, especially the British MI6, and their willingness to believe reports from their local informants. Garbo was the main inspiration for Wormold, the protagonist of Our Man in Havana. The novel, a black comedy, is set in Havana during the Fulgencio Batista regime. James Wormold, a vacuum cleaner retailer, is recruited by Hawthorne for MI6. Living with his materialistic and manipulative daughter Milly, Wormold needs extra income and accepts the espionage job. Lacking real information, he fabricates reports and creates a fictitious network of agents, some named after real people. He confides only in his friend, Dr. Hasselbacher. Wormold sends sketches of vacuum cleaner parts as "secret military installations," which London believes. They send him a secretary, Beatrice Severn, and a radio assistant. Beatrice insists on contacting his "agents," leading to real-life complications when people sharing his fictitious agents' names are threatened or killed. London warns of a plot to poison Wormold at a luncheon, validating his fabricated intelligence. Dr. Hasselbacher warns him at the event, and Wormold avoids the poisoned whisky, which kills a dachshund. In retaliation, Carter, a fellow vacuum cleaner salesman, kills Dr. Hasselbacher. Captain Segura, a local military officer in love with Milly, has a list of spies. Wormold tricks Segura into a drunken state, steals the list, and kills Carter in a brothel shootout. The list turns out blank when sent to London. Wormold confesses to Beatrice, who admires him. Segura gets Wormold deported by reporting him to MI6. Wormold and Beatrice are summoned to London. To avoid scandal, MI6 gives Wormold a teaching post and recommends him for the Order of the British Empire. Beatrice and Wormold decide to marry, with Milly going to a Swiss finishing school, funded by Wormold's espionage earnings.

Lucky Jim (1954) Amis's first novel and won the 1955 Somerset Maugham Award for fiction. The novel follows the academic and romantic tribulations of the eponymous James (Jim) Dixon, a reluctant history lecturer at an unnamed provincial English university. Dedicated to Philip Larkin. Jim Dixon is a lecturer in medieval history at a red brick university in the English Midlands. Worried about losing his probationary position, he tries to maintain a good relationship with his absent-minded head of department, Professor Welch. He must also ensure the publication of his first scholarly article, only to find it has been translated into Italian and passed off as someone else's work. Dixon struggles with his on-again off-again girlfriend, Margaret Peel, who uses emotional blackmail to keep him in a sexless limbo. During a musical weekend at Professor Welch's, Dixon drunkenly burns a hole in the sheets with a cigarette. Dixon meets Christine Callaghan, the girlfriend of Welch's son, Bertrand. Despite a bad start, Dixon is attracted to Christine. Bertrand, using Christine to secure a job from her uncle, becomes upset by Dixon's growing closeness to her. Dixon and Christine kiss and make a date, but decide not to see each other again due to guilt over their respective relationships. During Dixon's public lecture on "Merrie England," he drinks too much and mocks the university culture before passing out. Welch informs him that his employment will not be extended, but Christine's uncle offers him a job in London. Dixon learns from Margaret's ex-boyfriend that her suicide attempt was faked for emotional blackmail. Freed from Margaret, Dixon meets Christine, who reveals she is leaving Bertrand. They decide to leave for London together, walking off arm in arm and outraging the Welches.

That Uncertain Feeling (1955) comic novel. A satire on life and culture in a Welsh seaside town, the story concerns a married librarian who begins an affair with the bored wife of a local bigwig. Amis, an English incomer to Swansea in real life, mocks Wales's devotion to culture and learning as false and pretentious. In The Old Devils, one of the central characters, the writer Alun Weaver, is portrayed as a "stage-Taffy"; Weaver is the memoirist of a fictional Welsh poet based loosely on Dylan Thomas.

I Like It Here (1958) The 'here' of the title is England, and the novel is about going away from here, to abroad. Amis called it merely 'experience with style sauce'. Amis's alter ego Garnet Bowen is 'ludicrously sent off to Portugal in search of a reclusive writer named Wulfstan Strether'

Take a Girl Like You (1960) comic novel. Jenny Bunn, 20, moves from Northern England to a town near London to teach primary school. She's beautiful but holds traditional values, including "no sex before marriage." She rents a room from Dick and Martha Thompson, sharing the house with Anna, another lodger. Patrick Standish, 30, teaches at a local private school. He quickly asks Jenny out and tries to seduce her on their first date, but Jenny resists. Patrick struggles with Jenny's commitment to virginity until marriage. The story follows Jenny's new life as a teacher and Patrick's work and social life, including his friendship with flatmate Graham and the wealthy Julian Ormerod. After a period of dating, Patrick gives Jenny an ultimatum: have sex or end the relationship. Jenny agrees but doesn't show up. Patrick then sleeps with his headmaster's schoolgirl daughter. At a party at Julian's house, Patrick takes advantage of a drunk Jenny. Initially upset, Jenny later accepts what happened. The story ends without a clear "happily ever after." Years later, Amis wrote a sequel, "Difficulties with Girls," featuring Patrick and Jenny married but still facing challenges.

The Anti-Death League (1966) Set in England, it follows the lives of characters working in and around a fictional British Army camp where a secret weapon is being tested. Cathy and Max are discharged, Max returns to duty, and Cathy becomes a barmaid. Cathy and James fall in love. Brian suspects a spy and meets Dr. Best at Lady Lucy's. Willie finds a trio-sonata manuscript. Best attempts to rape Lucy but is stopped by James. Dr. Best enters a restricted area, observes weapon unloading, and evades capture. A stolen weapon is fired, destroying a priory. Jagger arrives at the camp. The stolen weapon was found in the hospital. Brian, Max, and Jagger confront Dr. Best, who tries to commit them but is taken into custody. A spy is discovered and killed by Jagger during a firefight. James, distressed, is supported by Lucy and Willie. Cathy tells James about her successful treatment and love, aiding his recovery. The novel ends with a concert of Roughead's music and a poignant twist. The Green Man (1969) "three genres of novel in one": ghost story, moral fable, and comic novel. The novel is set in and around The Green Man, an inn between London and Cambridge owned by 53-year-old Maurice Allington. Maurice lives there with his second wife, teenage daughter, and 80-year-old father. The inn, dating back to the 14th century, is haunted by the ghost of 17th-century owner Thomas Underhill, a Cambridge scholar involved in the occult and linked to two unsolved murders, including his wife's. Plot: Maurice Allington faces several challenges: his father's death from a stroke, a drinking problem leading to hallucinations, and an affair with his doctor's wife. His attempt to seduce his wife and mistress into a ménage à trois backfires when the women become interested in each other. Maurice begins to see ghosts around the inn, including Underhill and his wife, but his family dismisses these as hallucinations caused by stress and drinking. Maurice investigates Underhill's past at All Saints' College and discovers Underhill's record of using black arts. Maurice also recalls a drunken conversation with Underhill, who asks for help in his scheme, involving a silver figurine from his grave. Maurice is visited by a young man, revealed to be God, who warns him against Underhill and gives him a silver crucifix as a counter to the figurine. At the midnight meeting, Underhill shows Maurice a terrifying holographic pornography show. Maurice realizes Underhill's plan to harm his daughter Amy and uses the crucifix to defeat Underhill. In the final scene, Maurice destroys the figurine and exorcises Underhill. His wife leaves him, but he plans a fresh start with his daughter, recognizing his enduring flaws.

Girl, 20 (1971) The novel's anti-hero is Sir Roy Vandervane, a late middle-aged orchestral conductor and composer. He is a committed philanderer who also has pretensions to be at the vanguard of 1960s counter-culture. The novel is narrated by his friend and colleague Douglas Yandell, whose love life is hardly less complex. Sylvia is Sir Roy's (much) younger girlfriend with wisdom and guile far beyond her years. The plot describes the interactions of the aforementioned and of wife Kitty and daughter Penny.

The Alteration (1976) alternate history novel set in a parallel universe in which the Reformation did not take place. It won the John W. Campbell Memorial Award in 1977. The main character, ten-year-old Hubert Anvil, is a chorister at St George's Basilica, Coverley. His teachers and the Church hierarchy, up to the Pope, decide that his superb voice is too precious to lose to puberty, so Hubert must undergo castration. He tries to escape but is caught. His mother has an affair with the family chaplain, and his brother Anthony is a liberal dissident against repressive church policies. In this alternate history, Arthur Tudor and Catherine of Aragon had a son, Stephen II of England, leading to a papal crusade restoring him to the throne and making Coverley the ecclesiastical capital. The Protestant Reformation didn't happen; Martin Luther reconciled with the Catholic Church and became Pope Germanian I. Anti-Semitism is rampant, and Jews must wear yellow stars. Thomas More is Pope Hadrian VII, and Protestantism is confined to the Republic of New England. England dominates the British Isles, and the Convocation of clergy rules with the Holy Office's totalitarian control. The novel opens with the funeral of Stephen III, describing the political and religious figures present, highlighting the alternate historical landscape, including a Christian/Muslim cold war. Pope John XXIV uses the war as a means to control Europe's population. William Shakespeare's works are suppressed, while other artists like Mozart and Beethoven submit to religious authority. Hubert's book collection includes altered versions of famous works, reflecting the clerical domination. Science is banned, with limited technology allowed. Airships connect Europe with other continents, and high-speed trains are used within Europe. The political scene mirrors real 1970s Britain with figures like Lord Stansgate and Officers Paul Foot and Corin Redgrave representing the feared Holy Office. Pope John XXIV resembles Harold Wilson, and his Secretary of State is Enrico Berlinguer. Opera lovers will recognize castrati Federicus Mirabilis and Lupigradus Viaventosa as tributes to singers Fritz Wunderlich and Wolfgang Windgassen.

Jake's Thing (1978) is a satirical novel. The novel follows the life of Jacques 'Jake' Richardson, a 59-year-old Oxford don who struggles to overcome the loss of his libido. 

The Old Devils (1986) It won the Booker Prize. Alun Weaver, a writer of modest celebrity, returns to his native Wales with his wife, Rhiannon, sometime girlfriend of Weaver's old acquaintance Peter Thomas. Alun begins associating with a group of former friends, including Peter, all of whom have continued to live locally while he was away. Drinking daily in the pub with the men, he cuckolds most of them. A poet, Brydan, is a thinly disguised parody of Dylan Thomas. It was considered Amis's masterpiece by his son, Martin Amis, who wrote, "it stands comparison with any English novel of the century."

The Folks That Live on the Hill (1990) The novel's protagonist Harry Caldecote, a retired librarian, lives in the fictional Shepherd's Hill area of North London. Harry is twice divorced and lives with his sister Clare. The novel describes the interaction of Harry's extended family, son Piers, brother Freddie, sister-in-law Desiree and Fiona and Bunty, who are relatives of his former wives, from the viewpoint of the principal characters. Amis lampoons the attitudes and lifestyles of the North London chattering classes.

The Russian Girl (1992) comedy novel. Set in the early nineties the novel describes in comic detail events set in train by the arrival in London of Russian poet Anna Danilova. Richard Vaisey, Anna's sponsor and soon-to-be lover is a middle-aged academic, a professor of Slavonic studies not so happily married to wife Cordelia. Anna's youth and charm is what attracts Richard, not her poetry which he regards as doggerel. Anna attempts to portray her would-be oligarch brother, in legal trouble in Russia, as a dissident in order to elicit support from the London literati. Comedy of course ensues in Cordelia's response to Richard's infatuation and in the interaction of the latter with other minor characters.

In Their Wisdom (1974) was shortlisted for the Booker Prize: The story centers around the death of Sir Lewis Eliot, a prominent figure, and the subsequent legal battle over his will. The Comforters (1957) Muriel Spark, a Catholic convert, took the title from the Biblical story "The comforters of Job," believing they were demons. The plot idea came after Spark's mental breakdown, during which she believed TS Eliot's poems contained secret codes. She recovered with better nutrition and a rest cure funded by writers, including Graham Greene. TS Eliot reassured her there were no codes in his work. The central character, Caroline Rose, is a novelist who has recently converted to Catholicism. After returning from a retreat, she starts hearing voices and a typewriter, with the words matching her thoughts. Meanwhile, her boyfriend Laurence, staying with his grandmother in Sussex, discovers she is smuggling diamonds baked into loaves of bread.

Robinson (1958) set in 1954 and begins with 29 people on a plane bound for Azores and then Lisbon that crashes on a remote island in the North Atlantic, killing all the crew and most of the passengers. The narrator, January Marlow, and two other survivors, are nursed by Robinson, a mysterious loner already in residence on the island. As the novel progresses, their physical health returns, but the mental health of the characters is tested by the extreme circumstances. For rescue, they await the coming of the boat that will collect the pomegranate crop, the first contact with civilisation since their disappearance. A young boy called Miguel and a cat called Bluebell flesh out the cast list.

Memento Mori (1959) The title (Latin for "remember you must die"), references a common trope. The plot revolves around a group of elderly upper-class Britons, centered on Dame Lettie Colston, a retired prison reform committee member. Key characters include her brother Godfrey, a retired brewing company head; his wife Charmian, a successful novelist; and Charmian's former maid Jean Taylor, now in a nursing home. Dame Lettie starts receiving calls saying, "Remember you must die," and it turns out the entire group is receiving these calls. Each person describes the caller differently and reacts uniquely: Lettie with paranoia, Godfrey with anger, and Charmian with acceptance. Inspector Mortimer, a retired policeman, investigates but never catches the caller, believed by some to be Death itself. Another plot involves the estate of Lisa Brooke, who had an affair with Godfrey and forced Guy Leet, who has arthritis, to marry her. After Lisa dies, a dispute arises between her secret husband Guy, her siblings, and her housekeeper Mrs. Pettigrew, who has a dubious will in her favor. Mrs. Pettigrew eventually dominates Charmian and blackmails Godfrey. It’s revealed that Lisa had married Matthew O'Brien, who is in an asylum, making her marriage to Guy void. When O'Brien dies, the estate passes to Mrs. Pettigrew. Retired sociologist Alec Warner, previously romantically involved with Jean and Lettie, documents the group's interactions for his gerontology study, but loses his notes in a fire and feels devastated. Alec frequently visits Jean in the nursing home, providing insight into institutional aging. Lettie loses her housekeeper Gwen due to paranoia, disconnects her phone, and is ultimately killed in a home invasion. The novel ends with the deaths of most major characters and a look at the remaining survivors' twilight years.

Discovers his dead father had sunk from being a respected history teacher and had died in the Ramsgard workhouse. He comes to believe that Squire Urquhart is “the embodiment of evil” and his planned book dangerously immoral. He becomes involved with two women: "Gerda, the child of nature, whom he marries, and the more intellectual and complex Christie", the younger daughter of Malakite whom he doesn’t marry as she would destroy his 'mythology". "The landscape and the emotional intimacies of his relationships in Dorset manage to attack [Wolf’s] inner life as no physical or personal contact in London had done". At the end faces the loss of his "mythology" and questions how human beings can "go on living, when their live-illusion was destroyed". Thinks of suicide but the novel ends with Wolf having "a kind of vision" involving a field of golden buttercups and realizing "that traditional morality" the kind his "mythology" operated under "is too simple.   • A Glastonbury Romance (1932): "probably the longest undivided novel in English". The action begins in Norfolk with the funeral Canon William Crow and the reading of his will, and where the romance between the cousins John and Mary Crow begins. William' Crow's secretary-valet, John Geard, has inherited most of the deceased's wealth. John rejoins Mary. A central aspect of A Glastonbury Romance is the attempt by John Geard, ex-minister, who becomes the mayor, to restore Glastonbury to its medieval glory as a place of religious pilgrimage. On the other hand, the Glastonbury industrialist Philip Crow, along with John and Mary Crow, and Tom Barter, all whom are from Norfolk, view the myths and legends of the town with contempt. takes on the task of organizing a pageant for Geard. Owen Evans is a devoted student of Welsh mythology. They try to revive Welsh mythology through pageants. Finally the novel concludes with the a flooding of the low lying country surrounding Glastonbury, so that it becomes once again the legendary Isle of Avalon. This leads the death of Geard, and ends his ambitious plans for Glastonbury. In the novel's final pages there is a panegyric to "the great goddess Cybele"
  • Weymouth Sands (1934) title of the English version was changed to Jobber Skald and the time changed from "The present" to "190-" because of a lawsuit. Another major character, the novelist's father Uryen Quirm believes that he is "the incarnation of a Welsh god". He tries "to reawake the old gods once worshipped" at Maiden Castle, but he fails in this, just as his son fails in his relationship with Wizzie. 

Some writers have seen Powys as a forebear of the modern animal rights movement for his condemnation of animal cruelty, especially vivisection, a theme also found in Weymouth Sands (1934).

Porius (1951): Set in the Dark Ages during a week of autumn 499 AD, this novel is, in part, a bildungsroman, with the adventures of the eponymous protagonist Porius, heir to the throne of Edeyrnion, in North Wales, at its centre. The novel draws from both Arthurian legend and Welsh history and mythology, with Myrddin (Merlin) as another major character. The invasion of Wales by the Saxons and the rise of the new religion of Christianity are central themes. It takes place in the time of the mythic King Arthur (499 CE). The events take place in the week of "October 18, to October 25, A.D. 499", during a historical period when, Powys claims, "There appears to be an absolute blank, as far as documentary evidence goes, with regard to the history of Britain". This was in fact a time of major transition in the history of Britain, with the replacing of Roman traditions with Saxon rule and the conversion of the British to Christianity. The main plot follows the various experiences of Porius, the heir to the throne of Edeyrnion, the novel's eponymous protagonist, and his struggle to gain freedom from the influence of his parents. This in particular involves resolving his divided loyalties between Rome and the indigenous peoples of Wales. Porius himself not only has Roman, Brythonic, ancestors but an ancestor who was an aboriginal giant as well as relatives amongst the Forest people. A major climax in the novel comes when Porius mates with the young giantess, he names Creiddylad, one of two surviving Cewri, or giants, the true aboriginals of Britain. This is immediately followed by the violent deaths of Creiddyladd and her father.

 NOVELS:

Pied Piper of Lovers (1935) is largely autobiographical and focuses on the protagonist's childhood in India and maturation in London. Walsh Clifton, an Anglo-Indian, loses his mother during childbirth. Raised in India by his father, John Clifton, and Aunt Brenda, Walsh is torn between his Indian roots and colonial identity. He feels more at ease with Indians, though they are underdeveloped in the novel, and is uneasy around Europeans, especially Christians. Walsh's sense of superiority over Indians is challenged alongside traditional European masculinity. His awareness of mortality grows, symbolized by an ankle bone in a pyre and his grandmother's death obsession. Sent to England for education, Walsh grapples with being English or Indian, linked to paternal/masculine and maternal/feminine identities, respectively. This part covers his school experiences, developing sexuality, and his father's death in India. Walsh has homosexual encounters, significant dreams, and comments on his readings. In the final part, Walsh lives a bohemian life in Soho, earning from his inheritance and composing jazz. He eventually rejects this lifestyle, ending the novel with Ruth, his terminally ill first love, diagnosed by the doctor who delivered him and witnessed his mother's death.

Panic Spring (1937) under the pseudonym Charles Norden. Set on the fictional Greek island of Mavrodaphne, named after the black grapes and wine of Patras, the novel features expatriates from Britain and Russia. Each chapter is written from a different character's perspective. The novel alludes to global political and financial crises, with references to the Berlin Olympics and Greek unrest. PLOT:  Marlowe is stranded in Brindisi during Greek political strife and is taken to the island of Mavrodaphne by the boatman Christ, who works for the wealthy businessman Rumanades. Marlowe, a disillusioned schoolteacher, meets Gordon and Walsh from Durrell's previous novel, "Pied Piper of Lovers." In the third chapter, Rumanades' history, including his capitalist successes and failed marriage, is narrated, culminating in a fireworks display. Marlowe then moves into one of Rumanades' villas and meets Francis and Fonvisin, becoming interested in Quietism. Subsequent chapters focus on the individual narratives of Walsh, Fonvisin, and Francis. In the tenth chapter, a gramophone concert leads to a cliffside conversation between Francis, Marlowe, and Walsh. Marlowe begins writing on Quietism, and Francis is called back to London but stays for Rumanades' farewell celebration. Rumanades dies of a fever, leading to the departure of the expatriate community due to the ensuing turmoil.

The Black Book (1938) Lawrence Lucifer, now on a Greek island, reflects on his formative year at the Regina Hotel in England through a disjointed, dreamlike narrative exploring dying English culture. Overwhelmed by details and ideas, Lawrence is unable to act to improve society or himself, using mythic and literary motifs such as birth, death, and crucifixion. His encounters include Tarquin, a writer struggling with his sexual identity; Lobo, a Peruvian cartographer forming relationships with women, including Miss Smith, an African woman Lawrence helps read Chaucer; Chamberlain, who prefers carnal experiences with his wife, Dinah; and Morgan, a Welsh war veteran and hotel custodian. Key figures are an unnamed woman Lawrence addresses often and Herbert “Death” Gregory, whose diary Lawrence consults. Gregory’s story involves his relationship with Grace, a tubercular woman he marries and then resents until her death, after which he seeks a conventional life with Kate, a widowed waitress. Lawrence rejects Gregory’s “quaint suicide,” leaves England, and moves to Greece with his unnamed lover. The novel ends with Lawrence overlooking the Ionian Sea, recording his experiences and waiting for meaning to emerge from his self-exile.

The Alexandria Quartet (1962) a tetralogy of overlapping novels. The first three books present three perspectives on a single set of events and characters in Alexandria, Egypt, before and during the Second World War. The fourth book is set six years later. The four novels are:

Justine (1957) is narrated by an impoverished Irish writer and schoolmaster, referred to as "Darley" in later novels. From a remote Greek island, he recounts his time in 1930s Alexandria and his tragic romance with Justine, a beautiful, mysterious Jewish woman married to wealthy Egyptian Copt, Nessim. Alexandria is depicted as an exotic city of cultural and religious interactions, mirroring Justine's complex character. The narrator and Justine's secretive affair becomes increasingly desperate and dangerous, with the narrator fearing Nessim may try to kill him. The story alludes to another novel by Justine's former husband and includes her diary, revealing her many lovers, complex sexuality, and perpetual angst. Two of Constantin Cavafy's poems, "The City" and "The God Abandons Antony", translated by Durrell, are included as "workpoints" in the Appendix

Balthazar (1958) initially titled the book Justine II in his drafts. The book is dedicated to Durrell's mother: "these memorials of an unforgotten city". Both the epigraphs are from de Sade's Justine. The book opens with the Narrator on a Greek island with Nessim's illegitimate daughter from Melissa, now around four or six years old. Unlike the light tone of Durrell's "Prospero's Cell," this narrative is darker. Nature descriptions, a hallmark of Durrell's prose, evoke solitude and have a "prose-painting" quality foreshadowing "Clea." Part One The story quickly undermines the "facts" of "Justine." Balthazar arrives with the Inter-Linear, a heavily annotated manuscript that reveals hidden secrets. The Narrator reminisces about Alexandria, recalling its poetic and diverse inhabitants. Part Two This section, told mostly by Balthazar, focuses on the novelist Pursewarden, modeled after Wyndham Lewis, and recounts Scobie's tragic death—a depiction of a hate crime against a homosexual. After Scobie's death, his neighborhood ransacks his house, leading to deaths and poisonings from his bootleg arak. Part Three This part covers a murder during Alexandria's carnival, which occurred during Darley's affair with Justine but wasn't mentioned in "Justine." Part Four Balthazar reveals that while Darley was entangled with Justine and sought comfort with Melissa, the person who truly loved him was Clea.

Mountolive (1958) is the only third person narrative in the series, and it is also the most overtly political. The novel begins with young David Mountolive's affair with Leila Hosnani on the Hosnani estate, recalling his maturation and career as a diplomat. Returning to Egypt, Mountolive recontextualizes events from "Justine" and "Balthazar" and retains Pursewarden as his chief political adviser. Mountolive introduces a Coptic gunrunning plot supporting Zionism. Pursewarden kills himself, and Nessim is warned to curb his brother Narouz's dangerous rhetoric. The novel ends with a wake for Narouz, the Pasha pretending to believe he is implicated to continue receiving bribes from Nessim, and Mountolive preparing to leave Egypt, disillusioned.

Clea (1960) The book begins with Darley living on a remote Greek island with Nessim's illegitimate daughter, now six years old, marking the time since "Justine." Darley has spent this period maturing, thanks to £500 left to him by the writer Pursewarden, who killed himself. Mnemjian arrives with a message from Nessim and news of the Hosnani family's fall from prosperity. They return to Alexandria, now under nightly bombardment from WWII. Darley reminisces about the past and reconnects with Clea, resuming their romance, now free from the interference of Justine and Melissa.

The Revolt of Aphrodite (1974) consists of two novels. Genre of dystopic fiction. The two novels are:

  • Tunc (1968)
  • Nunquam (1970)

The protagonist, Felix Charlock, finds himself increasingly tied to the multinational corporation Merlins, which is most often simply called The Firm. He is both contractually and maritally tied to the Firm. His rebellion and attempt to escape leads to the death of his son and his own incarceration in a mental asylum. His lover at the beginning of the novel, Iolanthe, is likewise captured by the Firm through its net of contracts and capitalist acquisitions, ultimately leading to her death. Monsieur (1974) won the 1974 James Tait Black Memorial Prize. Five characters, including Durrell ("D"), claim authorship of the book. In the first section "Outremer," the protagonist and chief narrator Bruce Drexel, a diplomat like Durrell, returns to Provence after the suicide of his lover, Piers de Nogaret. Drexel, married to Piers' sister Sylvie who is institutionalized, revisits Avignon with his friend Toby for the funeral, reminiscing about his past with Piers and Sylvie. "Macabru" recounts Bruce, Piers, and Sylvie's journey to Egypt years earlier, where they join a Gnostic cult led by Akkad. They travel to the oasis Macabru and take a long Nile River journey. In "Sutcliffe, or the Venetian Documents," Robin Sutcliffe, a character in Aubrey Blanford's novel, narrates his misadventures in Venice and his failed marriage to Pia, Bruce's sister, suggesting the previous sections might be fictional. "Life with Toby" returns to Bruce and Toby in Avignon, discussing the Knights Templar and Gnostic themes, interrupted by "The Green Notebook," which contains Sutcliffe's fragmentary notes. "Dinner at Quartilla's" reintroduces Blanford, claiming to have written the novel. He dines with the long-dead Duchess Tu. The novel ends with an Envoi, listing character connections without final resolution.

Livia (1978) the protagonist Aubrey Blanford, introduced in "Monsieur," travels to Avignon to stay with his friends Sam and Hilary at their inherited chateau. They restore the property and enjoy an idyllic boat trip. The novel reveals "Monsieur" as the fictional work of Rob Sutcliffe, Blanford's alter ego, and introduces the real-life characters behind the fictional creations. Set in Provence before WWII, "Livia" opens with the death of Constance and ends with a debauch hosted by Prince Hassad as war looms. The friends, including Lord Galen, enjoy a last summer before the onset of Nazism. Galen, initially convinced by Hitler to invest in his plans, later regrets his decision and escapes Germany with Hassad, suffering financial losses. "Livia" retells "Monsieur" from a new perspective, focusing on Blanford, his wife Livia, and her sister Constance, mirroring the relationships in "Monsieur." Blanford's fictional author Sutcliffe remains a key figure, and the single-word titles of the five books are Blanford's choice, while Sutcliffe prefers alternative titles. 

Constance or Solitary Practices (1982) was nominated for the Booker Prize in 1982. The novel is set during World War II, from the Nazi invasion of Poland in 1939 to the Allied invasion of Normandy in June 1944, with locations including Avignon, Geneva, Poland, and Egypt. The story continues in Avignon, focusing on Constance's growing relationship with her husband Sam. As war approaches, their European group from "Livia" disbands. Aubrey Blanford moves to Egypt for a job offered by Prince Hassad. During a visit from Sam, now a soldier, a picnic ends in disaster with Sam's death and Blanford's injury from friendly fire. Constance relocates to Geneva, where she learns of Sam's death. She later returns to Vichy-controlled France, living in the house of Tu Duc, where her sister Livia, now disfigured and suicidal, joins her. After Livia's suicide, Constance goes back to Geneva and starts an affair with Sebastian Affad, Prince Hassad's aide. Affad returns to Alexandria, facing disgrace among his Gnostic sect. Durrell then introduces characters from "Monsieur," including its author Robin Sutcliffe, and Bruce Drexel. Cameo appearances from "The Alexandria Quartet" characters, such as David Mountolive, Maskelyne, Balthazar, Pursewarden, and Melissa, who has an affair with Affad, add to the interwoven narrative.

Sebastian or Ruling Passions (1983) A letter informs the Egyptian Sebastian Affad that he will die; a mix up has caused major ructions within the Gnostic sect in Egypt. Affad is called back to Egypt for admonishment. Before leaving Switzerland, however, he has asked Constance to use her psychiatric skills to treat his son, who has become autistic. She is gradually successful in working with the boy. After Affad returns to Switzerland from Egypt, the couple renew their relationship. The psychopath Mnemidis intervenes. After escaping from the institution where Constance works, he goes to her flat, intending to kill her, but murders Affad instead. The book finishes in a surreal manner. Affad seems virtually to disappear from Constance's memory; and two chapters give conflicting accounts of Constance's action upon the death of her boss Schwartz. Unexpectedly Sylvie reappears, for the first time since Monsieur. (She is said to be a fictional creation of author Aubrey Blanford, also introduced in the first novel as a character). She begins an affair with Constance.

Quinx, or The Ripper's Tale (1985) The surviving characters include "two novelists, a psychoanalyst, a German double agent, a Cambridge-educated gypsy, a Jewish lord, a schizophrenic young woman and an Egyptian prince." The ex-Nazi double agent Smirgel provides information about the location of the Templar treasure, which has long been sought by Lord Galen. Gypsies are congregating to take part in a Camargue festival. The climax of the book is set below the Pont du Gard, an ancient Roman monument where the characters expect to find the treasure.
