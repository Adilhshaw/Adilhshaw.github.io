Grouping

Literary critics have followed Bennett in dividing his novels into groups. The literary scholar Kurt Koenigsberger proposes three categories. In the first are the long narratives – "freestanding, monumental artefacts" – Anna of the Five Towns, The Old Wives' Tale, Clayhanger and Riceyman Steps, which "have been held in high critical regard since their publication". Koenigsberger writes that the "Fantasias" such as The Grand Babylon Hotel (1902), Teresa of Watling Street (1904) and The City of Pleasure (1907), have "mostly passed from public attention along with the 'modern' conditions they exploit". His third group includes "Idyllic Diversions" or "Stories of Adventure", including Helen with the High Hand (1910), The Card (1911), and The Regent (1913), which "have sustained some enduring critical and popular interest, not least for their amusing treatment of cosmopolitanism and provinciality"

Elizabeth and her German Garden (1898) ---originally published anonymously

is a semi-autobiographical novel. The story is narrated through the journal entries of Elizabeth, a young Englishwoman who has moved to Germany with her husband, referred to as the "Man of Wrath." The novel explores themes of nature, self-discovery, and the constraints of domestic life, all while showcasing Elizabeth's deep affection for her garden. The novel is set in a picturesque but neglected garden surrounding Elizabeth's home in Germany. The garden serves as a central character in the story, representing both a physical space for growth and a metaphor for Elizabeth's personal journey. The narrative begins in early May. The novel begins with Elizabeth expressing her love for her garden, which she describes as a wilderness in need of care. She reflects on her lack of gardening knowledge but is determined to learn and make the space beautiful. As she tends to her garden, she experiences moments of joy and guilt, particularly when her husband expresses his displeasure at her neglect of domestic duties. Throughout the summer, Elizabeth grapples with the challenges of having visitors in her home, which disrupts her connection to the garden. She finds it difficult to enjoy her sanctuary when surrounded by people who do not share her passion for nature. The presence of guests, who often criticize her gardening efforts, adds to her frustration and longing for solitude. As the seasons change, Elizabeth's relationship with her garden deepens. The novel culminates in a series of introspective moments where the narrator contemplates her identity and the nature of happiness. Christine (1917) (only novel written under the pseudonym Alice Cholmondeley) ---- It is written in the style of a compilation of letters from Christine, an English girl studying in Germany, to her mother in Britain. It covers the period of May–August 1914. In the letters Christine is a witness to the mood in Germany leading up World War I. The book was initially marketed as non-fiction. British propaganda in World War I used Christine, a series of letters written by Elizabeth von Arnim, pen name Alice Cholmondeley, to promote Anti-German sentiment propaganda. The novel utilized the rhetorical framework laid out by Aristotle that focused on pathos, ethos, and logos as a means to persuade the audience.  The letters also follow the rhetorical framework of Ronald Reid, particularly the use of ethnocentrism, which is the creation of an “us” by way of constructing a definitive “them.” The novel's foreword begins with a sentimental summary of the supposed origin of the letters. Christine introduced the British and American public to the German mindset leading into World War I, depicting Germans of all ages as conforming to the nation's war aims. She describes their developing bloodlust for wealth through war with France and Russia, noting their madness, drunken excitement, mindless marching, and callousness, labeling them as "abject, greedy, and pitiful" slaves. An article in Nation suggested that if her accounts were accurate, it would erase distinctions between the attitudes of the German people and their government. The book, believed to be written by an unbiased woman, helped rally American support for the war by reinforcing stereotypes of Germans.

The Unbearable Bassington  (1912) --- a novel is accounted Saki’s best work. It is a satirical novel set in Edwardian society. The story follows the life of Comus Bassington, a young man from a wealthy family who is bored with his privileged life and seeks out new experiences. Comus is a charming and witty character, but also selfish and manipulative. He spends his time pursuing pleasure and causing mischief, often at the expense of others. As the story progresses, Comus becomes increasingly disillusioned with his life and begins to question his values and beliefs. He falls in love with a woman who challenges him to be a better person, but ultimately chooses to follow his own desires rather than doing what is right. The plot is basically that his mother keeps trying to arrange things for Comus; a job as a secretary or an advantageous marriage, only for Comus to spoil things by selfishness or an unwillingness to be guided by another.

When William Came: A Story of London Under the Hohenzollerns  (1913) --- is a novel set several years in what was then the future, after a war between Germany and Great Britain in which the former won. The "William" of the book's title is German Emperor Kaiser Wilhelm II. The book chronicles life in London under German occupation and the changes that come with a foreign army's invasion and triumph. Like Robert Erskine Childers's novel The Riddle of the Sands (1903), it predicts the Great War (in which Saki would be killed) and is an example of invasion literature, a literary genre which flourished at the beginning of the 20th century as tensions between the European great powers increased.

Other Stories:

"The Interlopers": a story about two men, Georg Znaeym and Ulrich von Gradwitz, whose families have fought over a forest in the eastern Carpathian Mountains for generations. Ulrich's family legally owns the land and so considers Georg an interloper when he hunts in the forest. But Georg, believing that the forest rightfully belongs to his family, hunts there often and believes that Ulrich is the real interloper for trying to stop him. One winter night, Ulrich catches Georg hunting in the forest. Neither man can shoot the other without warning, as they would soil their family's honour, so they hesitate to acknowledge one another. In an "act of God", a tree branch suddenly falls on each of them, trapping them both under a log. Gradually they realize the futility of their quarrel, become friends and end the feud. They then call out for their men's assistance and, after a brief period, Ulrich makes out nine or ten figures approaching over a hill. The story ends with Ulrich's realization that the approaching figures on the hill are actually hungry wolves. The wolves who hunt in packs as opposed to rivalries, it seems, are the true owners of the forest, while both humans are interlopers.

"Gabriel-Ernest": starts with a warning: "There is a wild beast in your woods …" Gabriel, a naked boy sunbathing by the river, is "adopted" by well-meaning townspeople. Lovely and charming, but also rather vague and distant, he seems bemused by his "benefactors." Asked how he managed by himself in the woods, he replies that he hunts "on four legs," which they take to mean that he has a dog. The climax comes when a small child disappears while walking home from Sunday school. A pursuit ensues, but Gabriel and the child disappear near a river. The only items found are Gabriel's clothes, and the two are never seen again. The story includes many of the author's favourite themes: good intentions gone awry, the banality of polite society, the attraction of the sinister, and the allure of the wild and the forbidden. There is also a recognition of basic decency, upheld when the story's protagonist 'flatly refuses' to subscribe to a Gabriel-Ernest memorial, for his supposedly gallant attempt to save a drowning child, and drowning himself, as well. Gabriel-Ernest was actually a werewolf who had eaten the child, then run off.

"The Toys of Peace": Preferring not to give her young sons toy soldiers or guns, and having taken away their toy depicting the Siege of Adrianople, Eleanor instructs her brother Harvey to give them innovative "peace toys" as an Easter present. When the packages are opened young Bertie shouts "It's a fort!" and is disappointed when his uncle replies "It's a municipal dustbin." The boys are initially baffled as to how to obtain any enjoyment from models of a school of art and a public library, or from little figures of John Stuart Mill, Felicia Hemans and Sir John Herschel. Youthful inventiveness finds a way, however, as the boys combine their history lessons on Louis XIV with a lurid and violent play-story about the invasion of Britain and the storming of the Young Women's Christian Association. The end of the story has Harvey reporting failure to Eleanor, explaining "We have begun too late," not realising he was doomed to failure whenever he had begun.

"The Bull": Tom Yorkfield, a farmer, receives a visit from his half-brother Laurence. Tom has no great liking for Laurence or respect for his profession as a painter of animals. Tom shows Laurence his prize bull and expects him to be impressed, but Laurence nonchalantly tells Tom that he has sold a painting of a different bull, which Tom has seen and does not like, for three hundred pounds. Tom is angry that a mere picture of a bull should be worth more than his real bull. This and Laurence's condescending attitude give him the urge to strike him. Laurence, running away across the field, is attacked by the bull, but is saved by Tom from serious injury. Tom, looking after Laurence as he recovers, feels no more rancour because he knows that, however valuable Laurence's painting might be, only a real bull like his can attack someone.

Dorothy Miller Richardson (1873-1957) British novelist

Author of Pilgrimage, a sequence of 13 semi-autobiographical novels published between 1915 and 1967

Earliest modernist novelists to use stream of consciousness as a narrative technique. In a review of Pointed Roofs (The Egoist April 1918), May Sinclair first applied the term "stream of consciousness" in her discussion of Richardson's stylistic innovations. She brought the stream-of-consciousness method into English fiction out of determination to match the masculine novel of objective reality with a feminine subjectivity. Pointed Roofs was the first volume of Pilgrimage, the first complete stream of consciousness novel published in English.

Her relationship with H. G. Wells led to pregnancy and a miscarriage. Dorothy Richardson portrays H. G. Wells as Hypo Wilson, a manipulator of other people in his own interests.

Virginia Woolf in 1923 noted, that Richardson "has invented, or, if she has not invented, developed and applied to her own uses, a sentence which we might call the psychological sentence of the feminine gender."

John Cowper Powys, writing in 1931, saw Richardson as a "pioneer in a completely new direction" because she has created in her protagonist Miriam the first woman character who embodies the female "quest for the essence of human experience"

She was the author of numerous articles in periodicals such as Adelphi and Vanity Fair. She began her literary career reviewing for the vegetarian journal, Crank. Between 1912 and 1921, she wrote a regular column, ‘Comments by a Layman’, for the Dental Record. She translated eight books into English from French and German.

WORKS:

The Quakers Past and Present (1914) delves into the historical context surrounding the birth of Quakerism, emerging about a century after the decentralization of theological authority in Europe. It discusses how the Reformers' dream of a reformed church led to the rise of Protestant communions with differing doctrinal interpretations, eventually paving the way for the Quaker movement. It highlights the early challenges faced by Quakers in England, including persecution and martyrdom, as they advocated for religious liberty and self-governing congregations. Furthermore, explores the unique beliefs and practices of Quakerism, such as the emphasis on individual interpretation through the concept of the "Inner Light." It discusses the role of key figures like George Fox and Robert Barclay in shaping Quaker theology and community life. The text also touches upon the mystical and contemplative aspects of Quakerism, noting the enduring influence of mysticism within the Quaker tradition. Outlines the three main phases of Quaker history in England: the period of expansion and establishment, the quiet cultivation of Quaker method, and the modern evangelistic revival. It highlights the pivotal role of individuals like Margaret Fell in supporting and sustaining the early Quaker movement. The text concludes by discussing the contemporary relevance of Quakerism, particularly in terms of its engagement with modern thought and philosophy, as well as its ongoing missionary work and intellectual pursuits.

Journey to Paradise: Short Stories and Autobiographical Sketches (1989) is a collection that showcases her pioneering use of the “stream of consciousness” technique. This book includes 13 short stories published between 1919 and 1949, along with a selection of autobiographical sketches.

Pilgrimage (1938) is a novel sequence from the first half of the 20th century. It comprises 13 volumes, including a final posthumous volume.  Richardson's own term for the volumes was "chapters". Miriam Henderson, the central character in the Pilgrimage novel sequence, is based on the author's own life between 1891 and 1915. The first novel in Dorothy Richardson's "Pilgrimage" series, Pointed Roofs (1915), is set in 1893 and follows 17-year-old Miriam Henderson, who teaches English at a finishing school in Hanover due to her father's financial problems. In Backwater (1916), Miriam works as a governess in a North London middle-class school. Honeycomb (1917) depicts Miriam working for the Corrie family and ends with her mother's suicide, mirroring Richardson's own life events.

The Tunnel (1919) shows Miriam starting an independent life in Bloomsbury as a dental surgery receptionist. Interim (1920) continues Miriam's story in her lodgings, with a serialized publication alongside Joyce's Ulysses. In Deadlock (1921), Miriam delves into philosophical inquiries, influenced by her interactions with Michael Shatov, based on Benjamin Grad, a fellow lodger who proposed to Richardson.

Revolving Lights (1923) maintains Miriam's friendship with Shatov, despite rejecting his marriage proposal. The Trap (1925) depicts Miriam's unsuccessful flat-sharing with Miss Holland. Oberland (1928) details Miriam's holiday in the Swiss Alps, celebrating the joy of travel.

Dawn's Left Hand (1931) centers on Miriam's affair with Hypo Wilson, resulting in a pregnancy and miscarriage, and explores her relationship with Amabel, based on suffragette Veronica Leslie-Jones. Clear Horizon (1935) continues Miriam's relationship with Amabel. Dimple Hill (1938) was the last volume published in Richardson's lifetime, included in a four-volume collected edition.

After Richardson's death, three chapters of "A Work in Progress" were published in 1946, and the incomplete 13th chapter, March Moonlight, was published in 1967. This final chapter introduces Mr. Noble, reflecting Richardson's meeting and subsequent marriage to Alan Odle.

London Street Games (1916) ---is a lively collection of the games and rhymes recited by children in London streets around the time of the early 20th century. Described by Douglas as a “breathless catalogue,” the book captures the inventiveness and energy of London children, listing around 950 different games, verses, and songs. It serves as a vivid memorial to the playful spirit of the era and the streets that harbored these activities.  Some children had toys such as skipping ropes, marbles, bats and balls. However, many had to invent games with whatever items were to hand. Cherry pips, caps, boots, buttons, stones and lengths of rope were all temporarily turned into playthings and then later discarded or returned to their original use. In London Street Games, Douglas lists 30 different games that could be played with a boy’s cap alone!

South Wind (1917) --- is a novel set on the fictional Mediterranean island of Nepenthe, a thinly veiled depiction of Capri. The story unfolds over twelve days and follows Thomas Heard, a bishop returning to England from his diocese in Africa. Upon arriving on Nepenthe, Bishop Heard encounters a diverse cast of characters, including expatriates, freethinkers, eccentrics, politicians, and zealots. The island’s languorous atmosphere and the influence of the south wind, the Sirocco, gradually erode his moral rigidity. The novel explores themes of philosophical hedonism, moral relativism, and the clash between conventional morality and the island’s more relaxed, indulgent lifestyle. Key events include Bishop Heard’s moral transformation, a volcanic eruption symbolizing the upheaval in his moral and spiritual life, and incidents of murder and art forgery that add intrigue and highlight the island’s complex social dynamics.

Siren Land (1911) --- is a travelogue that explores the rich cultural and historical landscape of southern Italy, particularly around the Bay of Naples. Douglas weaves his personal experiences and observations with classical myths and local folklore, creating an evocative picture of the area’s enchanting beauty and enduring allure. The book opens with a discussion on the ancestry of Sirens, blending scholarly insights with Douglas’s characteristic mock-erudite style. Subsequent chapters delve into various topics, including the Roman Emperor Tiberius’s retirement to Capri, the early appreciation of Capri’s Blue Grotto, and the cemeteries and burial chapels of the region2. Douglas also muses on leisure, a subject dear to his heart, and provides an ironic hagiography in "The Life of Sister Serafina". The final chapter, “The Headland of Minerva,” marks the end of summer and reflects on the vast amount still left to explore and write about. "A Defence of Cosmetics" (1896) ----is an essay, published in the first edition of The Yellow Book in April 1894, when Beerbohm was 21. It established his reputation and later appeared in his first book, "The Works of Max Beerbohm," as "The Pervasion of Rouge." Originally intended to publish a satire on Oscar Wilde, "A Peep into the Past," Beerbohm's essay on cosmetics was chosen instead. In the essay, Beerbohm argued that make-up was becoming essential for both women and some men in the 1890s. He claimed, "most women are not as young as they are painted" and predicted cosmetics would become beloved by all, signaling the end of natural beauty. The essay, ironically defending Decadence, caused a sensation and was condemned as "decadent." Wilde, appreciating Beerbohm's wise but ironic manner, commented that "The gods bestowed on Max the gift of perpetual old age"

He collaborated with Joseph Conrad on three novels, The Inheritors (1901), Romance (1903) and The Nature of a Crime (1924, although written much earlier).

Inheritors: An Extravagant Story (1901) is a quasi-science fiction novel written before WWI, predicting themes of corruption and the 20th-century impact on British aristocracy. The "fourth dimension" metaphor depicts a shift from traditional values to modern expediency. Arthur, an aspiring writer, meets a woman claiming to be from the Fourth Dimension, aiming to "inherit the earth." She infiltrates his aristocratic family, gaining political influence and becoming increasingly desirable to Arthur. Arthur, compromising his ideals, joins the Fourth Dimensionists, who exploit his weaknesses for their agenda. He gets a job writing for a journal led by Fox, a Fourth Dimensionist, and becomes embroiled in power dynamics. Manipulated by the woman, Arthur believes he can impress her. Fox entrusts him with editorial power briefly, but Arthur chooses not to stop the presses, undermining Fox and betraying allies like Edward Churchill, Callan, and Fox. Realizing he was used and seeing no future, Arthur has a breakdown at his Club, known as "the one they got at."

Romance (1903) The story follows the young protagonist, John Kemp, who becomes entangled in a series of adventures after being falsely accused of a crime. He escapes to the Caribbean, where he encounters pirates, revolutionaries, and a beautiful woman named Seraphina.

The Nature of a Crime (1924) is a collaborative novel written and published in 1909 by authors Joseph Conrad and Ford Madox Ford. The text did not acquire acclaim until after Conrad's death in 1924, when Ford brought the text to light in his essay "Joseph Conrad: A Personal Remembrance". The Nature of a Crime is likely influenced by major events from the life of Joseph Conrad, most notably his suicide attempt made at the age of 20. This novel is a series of letters from an unnamed narrator, a lawyer, to an unnamed love interest. The narrator confesses to gambling away money entrusted to him by his client, Edward Burden, who is unaware of the misuse. As Edward’s wedding approaches, the narrator struggles with his impending indictment and expresses his hidden emotions and love for the married woman. The narrator reflects on life, God's relationship with man, gender dynamics, female empowerment, and suicide. He plans to commit suicide due to his unrequited love and the fear of imprisonment. However, he is ultimately saved from despair when Edward and his fiancée Miss Annie Averie announce a year-long honeymoon, giving him time to fix the financial issues.

Ford's Tudor trilogy, The Fifth Queen, includes The Fifth Queen: And How She Came to Court (1906), Privy Seal (1907), and The Fifth Queen Crowned (1908), that presents a highly fictionalised account of Katharine Howard's arrival at the Court of Henry VIII, her eventual marriage to the king, and her death. Ford defined the trilogy as 'nothing more than a tour de force, a fake more or less genuine in inspiration and workmanship, but none the less a fake'. Joseph Conrad described it as "the swansong of the historical romance"

PLOT SUMMARY OF TRILOGY: The Fifth Queen trilogy features an omniscient narrator. Katharine Howard, a devout, impoverished young noblewoman, is introduced alongside her fiery cousin, Thomas Culpeper. By chance, she catches the attention of King Henry VIII. With help from her old Latin tutor and spy for Thomas Cromwell, Nicholas Udal, she becomes a lady-in-waiting for Lady Mary. In Henry VIII's court, Katharine navigates a web of calculating and spying members, rising in prominence. She becomes involved with the King and is used by Cromwell, Bishop Gardiner, Thomas Cranmer, and Nicholas Throckmorton. Her association with Throckmorton, suspected of complicity in Wyatt's Rebellion, briefly implicates her in January 1554. Katharine's forthrightness, devotion to the Old Faith, learning, youth, and beauty attract the King, contrasting with historical views of her as flighty and flirtatious.

First Novel: Katharine Howard, a devout young Catholic noblewoman in her mid-teens, arrives in London with her cousin, Thomas Culpepper. Henry VIII is disappointed with his new wife, Anne of Cleves, and takes notice of Katharine's forthrightness and devotion to the Catholic Church. Despite her past with Culpepper, she gains favor at court. The novel portrays the chaos in England with Catholics and Protestants clashing, and Thomas Cromwell plotting behind the scenes. Katharine gradually gains acceptance in court amid the political and religious turmoil.

Second Novel: Focuses on Thomas Cromwell's plotting to derail Katharine Howard’s rise at court. Cromwell is aware of Henry's dissatisfaction with Anne of Cleves and aims to prevent Katharine, a Catholic, from becoming queen. Despite his efforts, Katharine wins Henry’s heart through her goodness and innocence, not guile. The novel highlights the political machinations and espionage within Henry's court.

Third Novel: Chronicles the plot to remove Katharine Howard, now queen, orchestrated by Thomas Cranmer after Cromwell’s execution. Katharine’s saintly character and attempts to reconcile Lady Mary with her family contrast with historical accounts. Her kindness and innocence make her less interesting and historically inaccurate. The struggle between Catholicism and Protestantism continues, with even her Catholic supporters turning against her. The novel culminates in Katharine’s tragic downfall, painting her as an overly idealized figure.

Ladies Whose Bright Eyes (1911) initially published under "Ford Madox Hueffer" and later as "Ford Madox Ford." The novel was inspired by Mark Twain’s A Connecticut Yankee in King Arthur’s Court and reflects Ford’s personal circumstances, including his attempt to divorce his wife, Elsie Martindale Hueffer, to marry Violet Hunt. Mr. Sorrell, a modern man, is transported to the Middle Ages. Unlike Twain's Hank Morgan, Sorrell makes only a half-hearted effort to introduce modern technology like guns or printing. His attempts fail due to his lack of practical knowledge. Instead, his golden cross causes him to be mistaken for a Greek miracle-worker, granting him hospitality and romantic attention from medieval women. Sorrell's presence inspires medieval ladies to engage in tournaments, and he himself tries to become a knight. The novel contrasts the romanticized medieval past with its more mundane and power-driven aspects, depicting cathedrals as crowded and commercial rather than serene. Just as Sorrell adapts to his medieval life, he is abruptly returned to the twentieth century, having gained insight and wisdom from his historical experience.

The Good Soldier: A Tale of Passion (1915) set before World War I. It tells the tragic story of Edward Ashburnham and his seemingly perfect marriage, using flashbacks and a non-chronological narrative. The unreliable narrator reveals a different reality than expected. Loosely based on Ford's own experiences with adultery, the novel's original title was The Saddest Story, changed to The Good Soldier during World War I. Narrated by John Dowell, follows the dissolving relationships of two couples: John and Florence Dowell, and Edward and Leonora Ashburnham. The novel begins with the line: "This is the saddest story I have ever heard." For nine years, the couples maintain a facade of friendship while seeking treatment for heart ailments at a spa. Florence’s ailment is a pretense to avoid intimacy with John, allowing her to have an affair with an artist named Jimmy. Edward's marriage to Leonora is marred by his infidelities and her control over their finances. Florence commits suicide upon realizing Edward’s growing affection for their ward, Nancy, and that John has discovered her affair. Edward, tormented by his love for Nancy and unable to maintain chastity, sends her to India. Leonora sabotages Edward's last hope by revealing his sordid past to Nancy, who then becomes catatonic upon learning of his death. The novel ends with Dowell caring for Nancy in Edward's estate, lamenting that no one got what they wanted. He sympathizes with Edward, seeing himself as a disengaged voyeur who failed to participate in life.

In the "Dedicatory Letter to Stella Ford" that prefaces the novel, Ford reports that a friend pronounced The Good Soldier "the finest French novel in the English language!"

His tetralogy, Parade's End (1924 to 1928) includes Some Do Not (1924), No More Parades (1925), A Man Could Stand Up (1926), and Last Post (1928). The novels chronicle the life of Christopher Tietjens, "the last Tory", a brilliant government statistician from a wealthy landowning family who serves in the British Army during the First World War. His wife Sylvia is a flippant socialite who seems intent on ruining him through her sexual promiscuity. Tietjens may or may not be the father of his wife's child. Meanwhile, his incipient affair with Valentine Wannop, a high-spirited pacifist and women's suffragist, has not been consummated, despite what all their friends believe. The setting is mainly England and the Western Front of the First World War, in which Ford had served as an officer in the Welch Regiment, a life he vividly depicts. the books were combined into one volume as Parade's End in 1950.

Some Do Not (1924) follows Christopher Tietjens and Vincent Macmaster on a transformative weekend in Rye around 1912. Tietjens, a sharp-witted government statistician, grapples with his disastrous marriage to Sylvia, who plans to return to him after an affair. Macmaster, aspiring to be a critic, becomes infatuated with Mrs. Duchemin during a visit to her home. The weekend includes a round of golf with General Campion and a chaotic encounter with Suffragettes, where Tietjens meets Valentine Wannop. They fall in love during a night drive while hiding a Suffragette.

Part II, set during World War I, sees Tietjens back in London, recovering from shell shock. He helps Mrs. Wannop write propaganda articles, while Sylvia suspects an affair between Tietjens and Valentine. Macmaster, now married to Mrs. Duchemin (Mr. Duchemin having died), hosts literary parties, maintaining secrecy about his marriage. Tietjens confronts his brother Mark about rumors damaging his reputation. Valentine tries to reconcile the brothers and plans to meet Tietjens before he returns to war, but their meeting doesn't happen. The novel ends with Tietjens reflecting on the day and heading back to the front, facing probable death.

The cancelled ending, published later, depicts Sylvia's failed attempt to seduce Tietjens and her retreat to a convent, which Tietjens recalls in the next novel, No More Parades.

No More Parades (1925) Captain Christopher Tietjens, with Sergeant-Major Cowley, struggles to move 2,994 troops from Rouen to the front amid countermanded orders, inadequate supplies, a French railway strike, and British Garrison Police interference. Shell-shocked Captain McKechnie complicates matters. A German shelling kills O Nine Morgan. Tietjens reflects on his unfaithful wife, Sylvia, after the "All-clear" signal. He distracts McKechnie by writing a sonnet. Colonel Levin arrives to inform Tietjens that Sylvia has come to see him, reminding him of his love for Valentine Wannop. Tietjens, disgusted by Levin's help offer, recalls Sylvia's departure to a convent, thinking it ended their marriage and freed him for Valentine. Sylvia leaves without speaking to him. Tietjens settles his troops for the night, handling police harassment of Canadian volunteers and other issues. Despite excelling in his duties, Tietjens faces inevitable deployment to the front, foreshadowed by Morgan's death.

A Man Could Stand Up (1926) Part I On Armistice Day, November 11, 1918, Valentine Wannop, at her school, is interrupted by a call from Edith Ethel Duchemin (now Lady Macmaster) informing her that Christopher Tietjens is back in London and needs help. Despite the call’s malicious undertones and background noise, Valentine decides to reunite with Tietjens if he still wants her. Part II The scene shifts to the front in April 1918, where Tietjens, amidst less intense bombardments compared to previous novels, reflects on the death of O Nine Morgan and his evolving character. He contemplates retreating from his burdens to live with Valentine and pursue a new life selling antiques. Part III Valentine visits Gray's Inn to meet Tietjens, but Mrs. Wannop, Christopher's father’s old friend’s wife, tries to manipulate them into staying apart. As Valentine learns about Tietjens' war experiences from his unit members, and Tietjens reveals his psychological struggles to Mrs. Wannop, their reunion culminates in a charged, celebratory dance reflecting the tumult of Armistice Day.

Last Post (1928) Set in the post-WWI years, Last Post explores the aftermath of the conflict and the shattered certainties of the time. Part I Christopher Tietjens, now an antiques dealer, lives with Valentine Wannop, his brother Mark, and Mark's French wife, Marie Léonie, in West Sussex. Important story summaries:

"The Sisters" (1904) –was Joyce's first published work of fiction using the pseudonym Stephen Dædalus. A first-person narrative, a young boy waits outside Father James Flynn's home, anticipating the priest's death. After the priest dies, a family friend criticizes Flynn and their relationship. The boy dreams of Flynn, recalling his death from "paralysis." He feels unexpectedly freed rather than mournful, despite Flynn's significant influence on him. The next day, he and his aunt visit Flynn's home, where his sisters, Eliza and Nannie, recount the priest's decline, including his overwhelming duties, a broken chalice, and his later odd behavior in the confession-box.

"Araby" – a first-person narrative, the reader experiences the drab North Richmond Street through the eyes of a boy enchanted by the neighborhood's magic despite its bleakness. The boys play in the dim streets, their imaginations vivid as they explore the area's dark corners and odors. The boy is captivated by Mangan's sister, beginning to idealize her romantically and obsess over her. On learning that she cannot attend the Araby bazaar, he promises to bring her a gift. However, when he arrives at the bazaar, it is mostly closed and disappointing. He sees a young woman at a stall, more interested in her conversation than in serving him, shattering his romantic illusions about both the bazaar and Mangan's sister. This realization leaves him feeling disillusioned and self-aware

"Eveline" (1904) – follows a nineteen-year-old woman named Eveline. As she sits by her window, she reflects on her difficult life: her deceased mother and brother, her distant remaining brother Harry, and her oppressive father. Her job offers little satisfaction. Eveline dreams of escaping with her sailor lover, Frank, to Buenos Aires. However, the memory of a melody associated with her mother's death and a promise to care for her home paralyze her at the dock. Despite Frank's romantic promises, Eveline is unable to leave Dublin. Joyce uses Eveline's paralysis to illustrate the broader sense of stagnation felt by Dubliners. Critics debate Frank's intentions and whether the journey to Buenos Aires signifies a genuine escape or exploitation.

"Clay" – Maria, a beloved, middle-aged woman working at a Dublin rescue mission, eagerly heads to Joe’s house for a holiday gathering. After a tea with her laundress colleagues, she buys cakes for the Halloween party but accidentally leaves them on the tram, possibly due to her flirtatious interaction with a drunken elderly man. At Joe’s home, she joins in a traditional Halloween game where the objects have prophetic meanings. Maria fails to pick the ring, symbolizing marriage, and instead selects a lump of clay and then a prayer book, suggesting a life devoted to spiritual service. After some wine, Maria sings “I Dreamt That I Dwelt in Marble Halls” but makes a mistake by repeating the first verse and omitting the second, which yearns for romantic suitors she has never had. The evening ends with Joe, moved by Maria’s song, asking his wife for the corkscrew.

"The Dead" – described by T. S. Eliot as "one of the greatest short stories ever written". It was later adapted into a one-act play by Hugh Leonard. The story follows Gabriel Conroy, a teacher and book reviewer, as he attends his aunts Kate and Julia Morkan's Christmas party with his wife, Gretta. Gabriel arrives late and has an awkward encounter with Lily, the caretaker’s daughter, and is concerned about his speech, which he fears is too academic. He also faces criticism from Miss Ivors, an Irish nationalist, for writing for a Unionist newspaper, which leaves him unsettled. During dinner, Gabriel reflects on the snow outside and delivers his speech, praising Irish hospitality. As the party ends, Gabriel and Gretta leave for a hotel, but Gabriel's romantic expectations are dashed when Gretta shows little interest. She reveals that the song "The Lass of Aughrim" reminds her of Michael Furey, a deceased former lover from Galway, whose death she believes was caused by his visit to her while sick. Gabriel is stunned by this revelation and contemplates the impact of the dead on the living. The story concludes with Gabriel reflecting on the snow and the inevitability of death, feeling a profound connection to all living and dead as the snow falls.

A Portrait of the Artist as a Young Man (1916) first novel.  Stephen Dedalus rebels against the Catholic and Irish conventions. Began in 1904 as Stephen Hero, Joyce abandoned it in 1907 and condensed 25 chaps to 5. The novel serialised in the English literary magazine The Egoist in 1914 and 1915 by Pound. Epigraph, Ovid, "And he turned his mind to unknown arts." The novel opens with young Stephen Dedalus listening to his father's fairy tale. His early memories are a mix of sensory experiences - from a candy seller to his mother's scent and his governess Dante's brushes. At Clongowes school, Stephen longs for the comfort of study hall while playing outside. He falls ill and spends time in the infirmary, where he learns of Parnell's death. During winter holidays, he overhears a heated family argument about Parnell and the Catholic Church. Back at school, Stephen is bewildered by sexual incidents involving classmates. He experiences unjust punishment when a teacher beats him for sitting out due to broken glasses. Stephen complains to the rector and gains support from his peers. Years later, Stephen spends a summer in Blackrock with his friend Aubrey, reading "The Count of Monte Cristo" and exploring. Financial troubles force his family to move to Dublin, where he develops a crush on Emma Clery, even writing her a love poem. At Belvedere, Stephen excels academically but faces trouble for his controversial essays. He acts in a school play and takes a melancholy trip to Cork with his father. Despite winning a large sum for academic excellence, Stephen remains restless and begins visiting prostitutes, battling guilt and shame. A religious retreat at school intensifies Stephen's guilt, leading him to confess and temporarily embrace piety. When offered the possibility of priesthood, Stephen realizes it's not his calling. Instead, he applies to the University of Dublin and has an epiphany about becoming a writer while observing a girl on the shore. At university, Stephen becomes known for his aesthetic theories, often discussing them with friends, especially Cranly. He continues to think about Emma and writes her another poem. The novel concludes with diary entries detailing Stephen's excitement about leaving Ireland to pursue his destiny as a writer, showcasing his artistic and intellectual growth throughout the narrative.

Finnegans Wake (1939) final work Joyce declared that "every syllable can be justified". Serialized in 1928 in journals The Transatlantic Review and transition (sic), under the title "fragments from Work in Progress". Giambattista Vico's cyclical view of history : Finnegans Wake opens with the words "riverrun, past Eve and Adam's, from swerve of shore to bend of bay, brings us by a commodius vicus of recirculation back to Howth Castle and Environs" and ends "A way a lone a last a loved a long the". Written over a period of seventeen years. Consists of 17 chapters, divided into four Parts or Books. Part I contains eight chapters, Parts II and III each contain four, and Part IV consists of only one short chapter. About the lives of the Earwicker family, comprising the father HCE, the mother ALP, and their three children Shem the Penman, Shaun the Postman, and Issy.

Part I In Finnegans Wake, Joyce opens with Finnegan's fall and transformation into the modern pub owner H.C.E. The book cycles back to its beginning with the final sentence linking to the start. The first chapter (I.1) introduces Finnegan's fall while building a wall, his corpse vanishing before the wake, and his resurrection when whiskey spills on him. The chapter concludes with HCE sailing into Dublin Bay. Chapter I.2 describes HCE's rise to prominence, only to be brought low by a spreading rumor about a scandalous incident in Phoenix Park. Chapters I.2 to I.4 trace this rumor's evolution, including HCE's trial under the name Festy King. His wife ALP's letter becomes central, leading to I.5, where it's analyzed. The letter, intended for delivery by Shaun, ends up in a midden, and Chapter I.6 provides character details through riddles. In the last chapters of Part I, Shem the Penman (I.7) is portrayed negatively by Shaun but defended by ALP. Chapter I.8, celebrated for its river names, depicts a conversation between two washerwomen about ALP and HCE's issues, ending with a request for a Tale of Shem or Shaun. Part II  shifts focus to HCE and ALP's children: Shem, Shaun, and Issy. II.1 introduces a pantomime program and a guessing game, leading to Shem's disgrace and Shaun's success with the girls. HCE calls the children inside. II.2 shows the children studying and engaging in Euclid exercises, with Shem's assistance leading to Shaun's realization and a conflict. The chapter ends with a night letter to HCE and ALP. II.3 depicts HCE in the pub, with radio and TV broadcasts of stories about HCE's past and his symbolic replacement by his children. HCE confesses his sins, and the pub closes as he transforms into Rory O'Connor. II.4 follows a dream sequence where four old men observe Tristan and Isolde's journey, reflecting on their own lives. Part III focuses on Shaun the Post delivering ALP's letter. III.1 features Shaun answering questions about the letter while floating in a barrel. III.2 and III.3 depict Shaun's transformation from a sermon-giver to a medium through which HCE speaks. The section concludes with a bedroom scene in III.4, where Mr. and Mrs. Porter are interrupted by their child, and their coitus ends with the rooster crowing. Part IV consists of one chapter with unrelated vignettes, starting with a call for dawn. It includes "Saint Kevin," "Berkely and Patrick," and "The Revered Letter." ALP delivers her final monologue, urging HCE to rise and reflecting on their past. The book concludes with ALP disappearing into the ocean, and the final fragment connects to the book's beginning, completing the cyclical narrative

David Herbert Lawrence (1885-1930)

English novelist, short story writer

He described his voluntary exile as a "savage enough pilgrimage".

His first published work was a group of his early poems in the "English Review" in 1909, and in 1910 appeared his early short story and then his first novel "The White Peacock".

It was in 1910 that he broke his engagement with Jessie Chambers (identified generally with Mir-jam in "Sons and Lovers"), became engaged to Louis Burrows, witnessed his mother's death and began the first version of "Sons and Lovers", which was published in 1913.

1912 Lawrence wrote the first of his so-called "mining plays", The Daughter-in-Law, written in Nottingham dialect.

He made his defense of "Lady Chatterley's Lover" first in "À Propos of Lady Chatterley's Lover" (at first entitled "My Skirmish with Jolly Roger") and then in a pamphlet "Pornography and Obscenity" (1929).

NOVELS:

The White Peacock (1911) first novel. 1910 on the title page. Started the novel in 1906 and then rewrote it three times. The early versions had the working title of Laetitia. Maurice Greiffenhagen's 1891 painting "An Idyll" inspired the novel. The novel is set in Nethermere (fictional name for real-life Eastwood) and is narrated by Cyril Beardsall, whose sister Laetitia (Lettie) is involved in a love triangle with two young men, George and Leslie Temple. She eventually marries Leslie, even though she feels sexually drawn to George. Spurned by Lettie, George marries the conventional Meg. Both his and Lettie's marriages end in unhappiness, as George slides into alcoholism at the novel's close

The Trespasser (1912) Originally it was titled the Saga of Siegmund and drew upon the experiences of a friend of Lawrence, Helen Corke, and her adulterous relationship with a married man that ended with his suicide. Lawrence worked from Corke's diary, with her permission, but also urged her to publish; which she did in 1933 as Neutral Ground. Set mostly on the Isle of Wight, it tells the story of Siegmund, a married man with children, and his adulterous affair with Helena.

Sons and Lovers (1913) The novel is dedicated to Edward Garnett. He allowed Garnett to cut roughly 100 pages from the text. Lawrence rewrote the work four times. Before publication the work was usually titled Paul Morel. Summary: Part I Gertrude Coppard, the refined daughter of a "good old burgher family," meets Walter Morel, a rough-hewn miner, at a Christmas dance. They fall into a whirlwind romance marked by physical passion. However, Gertrude soon realizes the difficulties of living off Walter’s meager salary in a rented house. The couple fights and drifts apart, with Walter retreating to the pub after work each day. Gradually, Mrs. Morel's affections shift to her sons, starting with the eldest, William. As a boy, William is so attached to his mother that he does not enjoy the fair without her. As he grows older, he defends her against his father's occasional violence. Eventually, he leaves their Nottinghamshire home for a job in London, where he begins to rise into the middle class. He becomes engaged but detests his fiancée's superficiality. When William dies, Mrs. Morel is heartbroken. Her love shifts to her second son, Paul, especially after he catches pneumonia. Part II Paul is both repulsed by and drawn to his mother. Afraid to leave her but needing to experience love, he falls into a relationship with Miriam, a farmer's daughter who attends his church. They take long walks and have intellectual conversations about books, but Paul resists a deeper relationship, partly because his mother disapproves. At Miriam's family's farm, Paul meets Clara Dawes, a young woman with feminist sympathies who is separated from her husband, Baxter. After pressuring Miriam into a physical relationship, which he finds unsatisfying, Paul breaks with her and grows more intimate with Clara, who is more passionate physically. However, even Clara cannot hold him, and he returns to his mother. When his mother dies soon after, Paul is left alone.

The Rainbow (1915) Its sequel is Women in Love. Prosecuted in an obscenity trial at Bow Street Magistrates' Court in 1915, as a result of which 1,011 copies were seized and burnt. Was banned in the UK for 11 years. Follows three generations of the Brangwen family, farmers and craftsmen in the east Midlands of England, from the 1840s to 1905. The story begins with Tom Brangwen, a farmer who marries Lydia, a Polish refugee. It then shifts to Lydia's daughter, Anna, and her turbulent relationship with her husband, Will. The final and most prominent part of the book focuses on Ursula, Anna and Will's daughter, who struggles to find fulfillment in an increasingly industrialized and materialistic society. Ursula experiences a same-sex relationship and a passionate, ultimately doomed affair with Anton Skrebensky, a British soldier of Polish descent. The book concludes with Ursula envisioning a rainbow as a symbol of a hopeful future for humanity.

Women in Love (1920) follows the Brangwen sisters, Gudrun and Ursula. Gudrun, an artist, has a destructive relationship with industrialist Gerald Crich, while Ursula develops a love for Rupert Birkin. Their relationships are marked by societal and personal conflicts. At a party, Gerald's sister drowns, and his father dies soon after. During a holiday in the Austrian Alps, Gudrun befriends artist Loerke, which angers Gerald. In a fit of rage, Gerald attempts to strangle Gudrun, then retreats to the mountains, where he dies from exposure. Birkin is deeply affected by Gerald's death, reflecting on their lost friendship and his commitment to Ursula. The novel explores British society before World War I and ends in the Tyrolean Alps. Ursula is inspired by Lawrence's wife, Frieda, Gudrun by Katherine Mansfield, Birkin by Lawrence, and Crich by Mansfield's husband, John Middleton Murry. The book stirred criticism for its portrayal of love, and denounced as chauvinistic and centred upon the phallus by the philosopher Simone de Beauvoir in The Second Sex.

The Lost Girl (1920) Alvina Houghton, the daughter of a widowed Midlands draper, comes of age just as her father’s business is failing. In a desperate attempt to regain his fortune and secure his daughter’s proper upbringing, James Houghton buys a theater. Among the traveling performers he employs is Ciccio, a sensual Italian who immediately captures Alvina’s attention. Fleeing with him to Naples, she leaves her safe world behind and enters one of sexual awakening, desire, and fleeting freedom.

Aaron's Rod (1922) is a picaresque novel. The title refers to the rod of Aaron in the Old Testament, Moses' brother who built the Golden Calf in the desert for the worship of the Israelites. The rod, his divine symbol of authority and independence, finds its echo in the flute of Aaron Sisson. British writer, Reginald Turner, was the model for the character "little Algy Constable" in Aaron's Rod.  John Middleton Murry praised the novel in his periodical "Nation and Athenaeum":

"To read 'Aaron's Rod' is to drink of a fountain of life.... 'Aaron's Rod' is the most important thing that has happened to English literature since the war. To my mind it is much more important than 'Ulysses'." SUMMARY: Aaron Sisson, a checkweighman and secretary to the Miners Union for a colliery in Nottinghamshire, and amateur flautist from the English Midlands, leaves his stale marriage and family to pursue a career as a professional musician in Italy. He befriends Rawdon Lilly, a Lawrence-like writer, who helps him recover from an illness in London. In Florence, Aaron joins intellectual circles, engages in political debates, and has an affair with an aristocratic lady. The novel ends with an anarchist or fascist explosion destroying Aaron's flute, symbolizing his lost potential.

Kangaroo (1923) is a ‘strongly auto-biographical’ novel set in Australia and follows Richard Lovat Somers, an English writer, and his German wife, Harriet, who travel to New South Wales in the early 1920s. The novel explores Richard’s engagement with two political movements: the right-wing Digger movement led by Benjamin Cooley (nicknamed “Kangaroo”) and the socialist movement led by Willie Struthers. Initially intrigued, Richard rejects both due to their extreme ideologies. The novel, written during Lawrence's stay in Thirroul, features vivid Australian landscapes and reflects on Richard and Harriet’s complex marriage, along with Lawrence’s disillusionment with fringe politics and his desire to distance himself from humanity’s entanglements. It was written in six and a half weeks during Lawrence and his wife Frieda’s eleven week stay in the NSW coastal town of Thirroul in 1922. The novel includes a chapter ("Nightmare").

The Boy in the Bush (1924), coauthored with M.L. (Mollie or Molly) Skinner. It is derived from a story in a manuscript given to Lawrence by Mollie Skinner, entitled The House of Ellis. The story follows Jack Grant, a young Englishman who arrives in Australia after being expelled from his public school and agricultural college. He navigates the rugged Australian landscape, engaging in activities like hunting, farming, and gold mining.

The Plumed Serpent (1926) political novel. SUMMARY: After Easter, Irish tourist Kate Leslie and her American cousin Owen Rhys visit Mexico, where Kate is initially excited by a bullfight but leaves in disgust after witnessing the violence. She meets Mexican General Don Cipriano and later encounters political discussions about the Mexican Revolution. Kate also reads about a religious movement in Sayula led by Ramón Carrasco, who aspires to replace Christian worship with that of Aztec gods. Kate decides to stay in Mexico and explores the Men of Quetzalcoatl, founded by Ramón. Despite initially being wary of his plans to replace Christian symbols with Aztec ones, Kate begins to sympathize with Ramón. Cipriano proposes marriage to Kate, suggesting she become a goddess alongside him and Ramón. The new president’s unpopular policies lead to rebellion and church opposition against Ramón. Ramón and Cipriano attempt to remove Christian imagery from churches, leading to further unrest. Kate becomes involved in the conflict, supports Ramón's movement, and marries Cipriano, adopting the name "Malintzi." The novel concludes with Mexico in religious turmoil, the Catholic Church outlawed, and Quetzalcoatl worship declared the official religion. Kate remains conflicted about her future in Mexico

Lady Chatterley's Lover (1928) last novel. First published privately in 1928, in Italy, and in 1929, in France. Lawrence once considered calling the novel John Thomas and Lady Jane. According to some critics, the fling of Lady Ottoline Morrell with "Tiger", a young stonemason who came to carve plinths for her garden statues, also influenced the story. SUMMARY:  Constance and Hilda, daughters of Sir Malcolm Reid, enjoyed a carefree youth in Dresden. World War I ends their idyll; their lovers die, and they return to London. Hilda marries, and Constance weds Clifford Chatterley, an aristocrat left paralyzed by the war. They settle at Wragby Hall, where Constance cares for Clifford and struggles with the grim mining town nearby. Clifford's writing attracts intellectuals, but Constance finds their discussions unfulfilling. Her father suggests an affair to relieve her stress, and Clifford agrees. Constance briefly has an affair with playwright Michaelis, which ends poorly. She then meets Oliver Mellors, Wragby's gamekeeper, and they begin a passionate relationship. Hilda forces Clifford to hire a nurse, Ivy Bolton, easing Constance's burden. Constance and Mellors' relationship deepens, with her considering having his child. Clifford remains oblivious, focusing on mining innovations. Constance's father proposes a trip to Venice, providing cover for her to conceive Mellors' child. In Venice, she learns she is pregnant and reconnects with Duncan Forbes. Clifford fires Mellors after his vengeful wife spreads rumors. Constance reunites with Mellors in London, planning to divorce their spouses and be together. The novel ends with Mellors waiting hopefully on a farm for their future.  The Escaped Cock (1929) republished as The Man Who Died. Short novel originally written in two parts. The story is a recasting of the resurrection of Christ narrated in the New Testament. The man who survives his crucifixion comes to celebrate his bodily existence and sensuality.

The Prussian Officer and Other Stories (1914) a collection of early short stories. "The Prussian Officer" tells of a captain who, having wasted his youth gambling, remains single and focused on his military career.
