George Saintsbury disagreed with Bridges, and stated that Milton had simply been using standard extra-metrical liberties, but Bridges was able to answer this objection by showing that every single instance in the poem of such a variation from the norm could be explained by his natural definition of elision; this would be extremely unlikely to be the case if the poet had simply been allowing himself extra-metrical variations as described by Saintsbury. Bridges took the very restricted range of Milton's variations to be a proof of his thesis. Bridges goes on to talk about Recession of Accent and lists six "rules" for accentual verse. He states "These 'laws' are merely the tabulation of what my ear finds in English stressed or accentual verse". These rules being:

  • the stress governs the rhythm
  • the stresses must all be true speech-stresses
  • a stress has more carrying power over the syllable next to it, than it has over a syllable removed from it by an intervening syllable
  • stress has a peculiarly strong attraction towards verbal unity and for its own proclitics and enclitics
  • a stress will not carry a heavy syllable which is removed from it by another syllable. Here Bridges cites several lines from Shelley which violate this rule, such as: "Each and all like ministering angels were."
  • a stress will not carry more than one heavy or two light syllables on the same side of it

Inventions: 1. Curtal sonnet: a sonnet of ten-and-a-half lines. It has precisely the structure of a Petrarchan sonnet in which each component is three-quarters of its original length. The octave of a sonnet becomes a sestet and the sestet a quatrain plus an additional "tail piece". That is, the first eight lines of a sonnet are translated into the first six lines of a curtal sonnet and the last six lines of a sonnet are translated into the last four and a half lines of a curtal sonnet. Used in 3 poems, viz. "Pied Beauty", "Peace", and "Ash Boughs". He gives a mathematical explanation of it in the preface to his Poems (1876–89).

2. Sprung rhythm: Known for his concept of sprung rhythm. Hopkins saw sprung rhythm as a way to escape the constraints of running rhythm, which he said inevitably pushed poetry written in it to become "same and tame". It is designed to imitate the rhythm of natural speech. It is constructed from feet in which the first syllable is stressed and may be followed by a variable number of unstressed syllables. Sprung rhythm may be classed as a form of accentual verse, as it is stress-timed, rather than syllable-timed. “The Windhover” is an example.

3. Inscape and Instress and: concepts about individuality and uniqueness taken from the ideas of the medieval philosopher Duns Scotus. Inscape has been rendered variously as: external design, aesthetic conception, intrinsic beauty, the intrinsic form of a thing, a form perceived in nature, the individual self, the expression of the inner core of individuality, the peculiar inner nature of things and persons, expressed in form and gesture, and an essence or identity embodied in a thing. Instress is when human recognizes the inscape of other beings in an act that Hopkins calls instress. It is the apprehension of an object in an intense thrust of energy toward it that enables one to realize specific distinctiveness. Ultimately, the instress of inscape leads one to Christ, for the individual identity of any object is the stamp of divine creation on it. An example is his poem "As kingfishers catch fire".

"In Praise of Limestone": First published in Horizon in July 1948, his visit to Ischia, an island in the Gulf of Naples influenced it. Auden's limestone landscape has been interpreted as an allegory of Mediterranean civilization and of the human body. As a topographical poem (in which he goes back to his native Yorkshire), it describes a landscape and infuses it with meaning. The Shield of Achilles (1952) and the title work of a collection published in 1955. The stanzas with longer lines describe the scenes of a barren and impersonal modern world that Hephaestus creates in Auden's version. Dame Edith Louisa Sitwell (1887 to 1964)

eldest of the three literary Sitwells: Osbert (1892–1969) and Sacheverell (1897–1988). Met Siegfried Sassoon in 1918 and became friends; upon a suggestion by someone of marrying her, he said, "I don't think poets should marry one another."

“The Drowned Suns” in the Daily Mirror in 1913 her first poem. Between 1916 and 1921 she edited Wheels, an annual poetic anthology with brother.

Façade series of poems she started publishing in 1918 contains poems like Said King Pompey, The Octogenarian, "I do like to be beside the Seaside", When Sir Beelzebub. Saw a published version in Façade and Other Poems, 1920–1935 (1950).

1929, she published Gold Coast Customs, a poem about the artificiality of human behaviour and the barbarism that lies beneath the surface. Some famous poems: Street Songs (1942), The Song of the Cold (1945), The Shadow of Cain (1947), "Still Falls the Rain", about the London Blitz, The Bee-Keeper.

Sitwell's only novel, I Live Under a Black Sun, based on the life of Jonathan Swift, was published in 1937. Though primarily biographical fiction, it includes thematic treatments on mourning and melancholia, and political allegory.

Wrote two books about Queen Elizabeth I of England: Fanfare for Elizabeth (1946) and The Queens and the Hive (1962). Also wrote prose English Eccentrics (1933) and Victoria of England (1936).

Anthony Powell who dedicated his novel, What's Become of Waring, to her. She also helped to publish the poetry of Wilfred Owen after his death.
